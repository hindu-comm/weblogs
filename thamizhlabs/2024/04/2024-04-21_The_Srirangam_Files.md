# The Srirangam Files

### The untold story of Tyranny of a Mad Sultan-to-Be

Everything about Srirangam is massive. The largest living temple complex in the world boasts a perimeter of 10,710 feet, with colossal walls that run for 32,592 feet and a Gopuram that’s 236 feet tall. Unfortunately, Srirangam was also the site of ***one of the largest massacres in South India***, with 12,000 civilians being butchered by a trained army, at the orders of a Sultan-to-be.

No mainstream history book or academic textbook will mention this event; Probably because it’d raise an uncomfortable debate that not many are ready to confront forthrightly. Thankfully though, our glorious temples have not just been preservers of our tradition, but also of our collective civilisational memory.

This event has been recorded in Srirangam temple’s *Koil Ozhugu* – the temple’s authentic documentation of it’s own history. The data has also been corroborated by multiple inscriptions, notes by travelers like Ibn Batuta, and also by Guru Parampara tradition. This is the unraveling of ‘*The Srirangam Files*’, an event glossed over by secular historians but an event that remains fresh in the collective memory of the bereaved.

*Go back 701 years to 1323 CE.* It was the Panguni month, late March. Panguni month’s temple festivities had started, and devotees thronged to the Srirangam temple with devotion, in a celebratory mood. They were blissfully unaware of the fact that Ulugh Khan, the Sultan-to-be, who was camping 13km away at Kannanur Koppam (Samayapuram) had completely different plans.

Why was Ulugh Khan stationed at Kannanur Koppam at that time? Same reason as why Malik Khafur and Khusrau Khan had earlier visited Thamizhagam – to raid and plunder the wealth present in its magnificent temples. In addition to plunder, Ulugh Khan, son of the Delhi Sultan Ghyasuddin Tughlaq also wanted to subdue these Southern Kingdoms to be under the Sultanate’s ‘ever merciful’ watch. So, after winning over the Hoyasalas, the Sultan-to-be was camping at Kannanur, the second capital of Hoysalas. His next stop was – Mabar, also known as Madurai.

That’s when things took a wrong turn. As Ulugh Khan was getting ready to travel towards Madurai, he heard about the huge crowds gathering for the Panguni festivities at the Srirangam temple nearby.

> ‘*If Srirangam temple is as big as the Madurai temple, is it not a low
> hanging fruit? Why travel 142km to Mabar tomorrow, when Rangam is just
> 13km from here’,* Ulugh Khan was making mental calculations.

Meanwhile, seven days of Panguni festivities were done, and the temple authorities were gearing up for the 8^(th) day. Ofcourse, they heard about the Sultanate forces reaching Samayapuram. They were discussing if they must proceed with the next day’s event, in which the Azhagiya Manavala Perumal will be taken to the Varaha temple aka ‘Pandriazhvan Medu’. Given the proximity of the place to Samayapuram, some of them raised concerns. But the majority overlooked these objections and decided to proceed with the festivities. Why? Because they lacked *Shatrubodh*.

Historically, the region had witnessed plenty of battles between Tamil Hindu kings, and not one of them had let these battles affect the Srirangam temple or its temple festivals.

> To put it in the modern “secular” parlance, the Hindu Tamil kings had
> kept the state affairs separate from the religious affairs. And the
> temple authorities, who assumed that the Delhi Sultanate forces would
> also be similarly honorable or “secular”, were in for a shocker.

You see - it might have been a sacred temple festival for the devotees, but for the Sultan-to-be, it was an expedition - a chance not just to loot and ransack the temple’s wealth but also to strike fear at the hearts of idol worshipping *Kafirs*.

While devotees would happily recite Alwaar Pasurams on the Srirangam deity for **self-realization**, the Sultan-to-be would happily start an *all-war* proceeding to raid the Srirangam temple for **wealth-realization**.

While Srirangam maybe Bhooloka Vaikuntam – Heaven on Earth - for devotees, the Sultan-to-Be had his eyes only on *jannat* in the afterlife*.* And he’d be getting there only by following what his holy book prescribes.

His holy book’s verse 8:12 says: *When your Lord revealed to the angels: I am with you, therefore make firm those who believe. I will cast terror into the hearts of those who disbelieve. So, cut off their heads and even the tips of their fingers!*

So that is what Ulugh Khan did. On the 8^(th) day of Panguni festivities, before raiding Madurai, he took a pit stop at Srirangam and ordered his well trained army to butcher the 12,000 civilian ‘idol worshippers’ who had gathered at the Varaha Swami temple. Let that number sink in – 12,000 is almost half the capacity at Old Trafford cricket stadium.

The devotees – that included women and children - did not even know why they were being killed. They weren’t yet exposed to an ideology that identified them not as ‘worthy individuals’ but as ‘worthless infidels’. Every other war strategy book on the planet would call it dishonorable and cowardly to kill unarmed civilians, but for this ideology.

Koil Ozhugu documents this bloody event as ‘***Panniraayiravar Mudithiruththiya Pandriyazhvan Mettu Kalagam’, "an invasion at Pandriyazhvan Medu that took 12,000 heads".*** It took weeks before all those bodies could be disposed. The river Kaveri turned red with the blood of devotees!

Ulugh Khan who would eventually be coronated as Mohammed Bin Thuqlaq and be called as ‘The Mad Sultan’ later, had shown his early signs of madness by unleashing hell upon Bhooloka Vaikuntam.

The bad news reached the main Archakar Srirangarajanatha Vaduladesikar. *If these invaders can butcher even civilians, what would they do to our murthis,* he wondered. He immediately sealed the Moolasthanams of Lord Ranganathar and Ranganayaki Thayar with stone-walled screens. The Utsava Murti was to be saved by Pillai Lokacharya who had to secretly take the Murthi out through a dense jungle path.

Some versions state that the 12,000 civilians at the Kaveri river shore willingly gave themselves over to the invaders’ swords to delay the entry of Ulugh Khan’s forces into the temple premises, thus saving their murthis from mutilation, desecrecation and destruction. What a supreme sacrifice! It is upon this pedestal of pain that we continue to safeguard our traditions.

Not just the moolavars and the urchava murthis, but some precious manuscripts needed saving as well. Afterall, invaders burning libraries and manuscripts was not unheard of. Sri Vedanta Desikar saved the famous manuscript of 'Shruta Prakashika' by tying it into his Shikha and laid down among the dead bodies that night, pretending to be dead. By sunrise the next day, he managed to escape to Mysore.

Meanwhile, the elderly Pillai Lokacharya was exhausted by the constant travel and gave away his dear life in the protection of his deity who was dearer to him than his life. Pillai Lokacharya attained Paramapatham at a place called Jothishkudi near Madurai Anaimalai. He had done his duty to Dharma, and his disciples took over the baton of responsibility from him. They took the Perumal, even more secretively, through Tirunelveli and Aralvazmozhi to Kerala. After some years, Perumal went to Melkotte in Karnataka and finally to Tirupathi.

After decades of laying low, the courageous fightback finally happened in 1371 thanks to Vijayanagar empire. Gopanna Raya, who was a general of Vijayanagar king Kumara Kampanna, heard about the happenings at Srirangam Temple and decided to bring back Azhagiya Manavala Perumal. He went to Tirupathi, took Perumal with him to Ginjee, performed poojas, and then took the swami to Srirangam. Gopanna installed the Vigraham in the temple and restored all the rituals and pooja. This event is inscribed in the Srirangam temple.

Hopefully, a Dharmic filmmaker in the future will depict not just the tyranny but how it was overcome by the brave warriors of Vijayanagara kingdom.

Until then, we must pass on the story ourselves, for nobody else is doing it. We must light a lamp every Panguni in memory of the 12,000 Dharmic souls. We must take the baton of responsibility of protecting Dharma on ourselves and keep passing it on across generations.

Vedanta Desikar, a first hand wittness to this event, writes in his Abeedhi Sthavam (#22): “*Sri Ranganatha, these Turks who behave as if they are servants of Kali Purusha, increase the fear in the world. May you, armed with powerful weapons, destroy this fear*”

May we also pray to Ranganatha to protect us from the dark forces! He will always lead us from darkness to light.

**Key Takeaways:**

1.  Know what history is hidden from you, and why. What uncomfortable
    discussions would it spark, and whose interests are being protected,
    and why?

2.  In a war, you can only be as moral as your enemy. No valiant war
    book prescribes killing of unarmed civilians, but if it’s done, your
    primary responsibility lies in defending yourself and your
    civilization, whatever it takes. Whatever Deception it takes.
    Remember Pillai Lokacharya and Sri Vedanta Desikar

3.  Fightback against tyrants is a slow process and may take decades,
    even centuries. In the interim, keep sowing the seeds for the
    eventual rise of a Dharmic Vijayanagara Empire that can win against
    tyrants. Think in terms of decades, not years.
