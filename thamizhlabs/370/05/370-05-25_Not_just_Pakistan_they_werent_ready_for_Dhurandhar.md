When a non-left film is made badly (and many have been), the response from leftist reviewers is gleeful. Samrat Prithviraj. Ram Setu. Razakar. These films were practically gifts to leftist critics. The amateurishness of the filmmaking became a vessel to pour contempt into the idea itself. Because poor craftsmanship is a gift to the ideological critic. It allows them to mock the *idea* by mocking the *execution*. Its almost as if the left awaits such subpar films to use the messenger to also discredit its message.

But then came the wave of non-left films par excellence: films like Baahubali,*RRR*, *Kantara*, *Amaran*, *Chaava*. These films were hard to mock. The filmmaking was too good, the performances too committed, the emotional resonance too real. You couldn't laugh at them. You had to argue against them. And arguing against them meant engaging with what they were actually saying. But nobody, neither the left nor the Dharmics were prepared for the bulldozer that is Dhurandhar.

####  **Dhurandhar: The glitch in the leftist matrix**

You see, Bollywood was this place where secular films got made, where Pakistan and India were bhai-bhais, with both countries suffering only from the nastiness and pettiness of each country’s power-hungry politicians. Our spy thrillers were filled with male RAW agent falling in love with a female ISI agent, and then dancing in saffron bikinis and green tshirts at a party. They expected "Urduwood" to keep churning out these same *Aman ki Tamasha* tropes.

And yet here it was: a mainstream Hindi film, featuring Ranveer Singh (of all people) with superhit music, spirited performances, and superlative command over the craft, saying things that crores of Indians have thought but rarely seen said on screen with this level of confidence.

> A Paki scholar once said “If the Turks cease to be Muslims, they are
> still Turks. If the Iranians cease to be Muslim, they are still
> Persians. But if we cease to be Muslims, what are we? We are
> *Indians*” And nothing could be more insulting to them.

“Not India” is how Pakis can build an unique identity around. And no film except for Dhurandhar dared to show this with cinematic brilliance: the common Pakistani identity being constituted around anti-Indian hostility.

Crores of Indian saw for the first time how ISI wanted to bleed India by a thousand cuts. How our own government, in the aftermath of terror attacks, finds ways to point fingers at the victims. How the Indian intelligentsia has spent decades treating its own culture as suspect, its own civilisation's right to peaceful existence as somehow negotiable. These are not fringe ideas on the Indian right. But they had never quite been given this kind of cinematic form, in this kind of production, for these *many* audience.

This is why the reaction from the left was not the cheerful mockery they deploy against weaker films. It was the language of cancellation: "hate-filled," "biased," "propaganda," "saffronisation." The vocabulary of delegitimisation. Which is revealing, because these are the same people who champion Haider, Jai Bhim, Viduthalai, Jodha Akbar: films that are, on any honest reading, as agenda-driven, as emotionally manipulative, and as selectively told as anything they criticise. The double standard isn't incidental. It's the point.

#### Ideologues First, Filmmakers Next

Think about these three samples:

- Saif Ali Khan acts as a villain in Tanhaji, and after its release, he
  goes to an Anupama Chopra interview and says it has “questionable
  politics” and hopes such films don't get made! He goes on to add that
  "There was no concept of India till the British arrived", a statement
  he confidently makes, as a student of history.

- AR Rahman composes music for Chaava, that shows the brutality of
  Aurangzeb in full force, and also the brave Maratha pushback and
  concept of Swarajya. An year later, he goes to a BBC interviewer and
  calls the same film as **"divisive"** ,"cashed in on divisiveness"

- Kabir Khan, the director of Aman ki Tamasha ‘Ek Thaa Tiger’ fame said:
  *"I can forgive bad acting and sloppy scripts, but I can’t forgive bad
  politics."*

What does this tell about them? It is the fact that they are ideologues first, and filmmakers/technicians/performers next. They care less about their livelihoods on cinema and more about spreading ideas through cinema, that they think are politically correct. Only when the market refuses to fund such ideas, they reluctantly take up other work, and ensure their reluctance is stated on record, and their allegiance to their idea of political correctness is captured for eternity.

#### Cinema as Inception

Why do they do this? It is because, leftists, to their credit, understand that Cinema is the ultimate tool of mass hypnotism. As Cobb says so eloquently in Inception (2010), the most resilient parasite is an idea. Once an idea has taken hold of the brain, it’s almost impossible to eradicate. Leftists know the power of cinema in planting political ideas inside people's heads. Vetrimaaran understands this, and which is why he calls Dhurandhar as propaganda that is capable enough to influence public memory.

#### The Strategic Error: Dharmics Ceding the Cinema Space

There is a segment of the Dharmic Right that looks down on cinema. They call it "an appeal to base emotions," "waste of time”, “filled with promiscuous koothadis”. This is the same error in judgement kingmaker Kamaraj also made. He dismissed DMK that was full of playwrights, dialogue writers and screenwriters, and was playing “ideological politics” with Communists. But DMK usurped INC in Tamilnadu, and Congress has been reduced to a trivial player in the state ever since.

Cinema is politics by other means. The most effective means, even. Because unlike a policy argument, a film doesn't feel like an argument. It feels like an experience. It bypasses the rational defences. It plants things that don't feel like ideas but like memories, like felt truths. This is why Comradewood and Dravidawood built what they built.

Politics is “dirty” too, but would you suggest we cede the Parliament? Politics gave us the Ram Janmabhoomi Mandir, the abrogation of Article 370, and the Waqf Amendment Act. But **entertainment captures the mindspace** that decides which way the politics flows. This has been widely known since the time of Goebells. And Vetrimaaran is following the Goebells strategy of “Accuse your enemy of what you are doing” to the T. Vetrimaaran knows he makes propaganda films, but he won’t admit it, but calls Dhurandhar propaganda because game recognizes game. The more minds Dhar influences, the less it leaves for Vetrimaaran to capture. Its a zero-sum game.

Ten years ago, nobody expected a Chaava or a Dhurandhar from Bollywood. The reform of Comradewood and Dravidawood seems equally impossible today. But Bollywood’s transformation from Urduwood somehow happened. It happened because people showed up, made things, found audiences, kept going. Not because of a Masterplan from the delightful duo at the centre, but because enough people decided not to cede the space.

*Don’t blame the tool for how it’s being used against you. As actor Vijay says “Yaaru suttaalum Thupaaki sudum” (No matter who holds it, the gun will shoot)*

To sustain this momentum, the Dharmic ecosystem needs to change its tactical approach:

1.  **Stop being Moral Judges:** If an actor or technician aligns with
    Dharmic or National interests, **amplify them.** Don’t go digging
    into their personal lives to find reasons to “cancel” them. We don’t
    need saints; we need allies.

2.  **Embrace the Opportunists:** In a numbers game, you need both the
    “committed” and the “opportunists.” If the wind is blowing toward
    Dharmic narratives, and people jump on the bandwagon for profit—let
    them. It still serves the larger goal of reclaiming the cultural
    commons.

3.  **Support the Craft:** Don’t just support a film because it has the
    “right” message. Demand excellence. Encourage Dharmic writers,
    technicians, and showrunners to be the best in the business.

The era of being silent spectators to our own vilification is over. Cinema is the new Kurukshetra, and we are finally showing up to the fight with the right weapons.

Dhurandhar is not an endpoint. It is a proof of concept. The space exists. The audiences are there. The hunger is real. What Dhurandhar showed is that a well-made film arguing for civilisational self-respect can find an audience in modern India as big as any other. That is not a small thing.
