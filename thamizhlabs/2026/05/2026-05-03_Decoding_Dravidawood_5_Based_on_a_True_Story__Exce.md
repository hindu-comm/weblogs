# Decoding Dravidawood \#5: Based on a True Story. Except when it isn't.

### A biopic makes a promise to its audience: this person was real, this story is truth. Dravidawood has weaponised that promise by erasing identities, inserting ideology, and profiting from suffering

#### **The Contract**

When a movie starts with a card “based on a true story,” it is entering into a moral contract with you: it is promising that, while some details may be dramatized, the spine of the story and the identities at its heart are true and the events it portrays are close to reality. If you wanted pure imagination, you would’ve chosen a masala entertainer. When you pick biopic as a genre of film to watch, you are trusting that the film’s heart beats in sync with the lives it borrows from.

Dravidawood has learned to exploit this contract with remarkable sophistication. Not by cooking things up entirely, because 100% lies get refuted easily. But if you make strategic distortions, it will largely go unquestioned. A name changed here. A community erased there. A villain's identity quietly reassigned from religion A to Hindu. A real person's caste surname replaced with an EVR-ist identity. Or worse: Real people whose lives fuel the story are left uncredited and unpaid. Each individual change is defensible as "creative liberty." But like the Theseus’s ship, at one point, the accumulated pattern of those changes, across film after film, becomes something else entirely, without being noticed. Let’s dissect 4 such films, and also the 2 antidote films that properly dramatized things without ideological distortions when telling a real story.

#### **Irudhi Suttru: Stealing a life story, Keeping a National Award**

Irudhi Suttru (Saala Khadoos in Hindi) is a 2016 film directed by Sudha Kongara about a boxing coach who trains a young woman from a marginalised background to compete at the national level. It won the National Award. It put Kongara on the map. It was marketed as a film that is inspired from many true stories.

This “many” word is the trick is played by the director, because, this film’s core plot of two sisters from marginalised background, both wanting to be boxers, but the younger one succeeding, is exclusive to Thulasi Helen and her akka Saraswathi. Director Sudha had met with Thulasi during her script research and promised that she will be credited , allegedly offering even a role as a coach in her own biopic. But not only did Sudha not credit her, when confronted by Thulasi post-release, she issued mild threats with namedropping the Commissioner.

If you think this Thulasi is some attention seeking creditmonger trying to steal her 15 minutes of fame, you are mistaken. Sometimes known as “The Lady Muhammad Ali of India” because of her rapid footwork, Thulasi had once defeated Mary Kom.

All in all, she had won 30 medals in boxing, before a legal dispute about sexual harassment within the state boxing association in TN pushed her into a hiatus from the sport. Her life story was made into an award winning documentary ‘*Light Fly, Fly High*’ by Norwegian filmmakers. Thulasi perhaps thought Dravidawood filmmakers would be as honorable as Norwegians. But she only got to know Irudhi Suttru was based on her life after she watched its trailer on Youtube.

> stolen from me hurts more than being beaten at the boxing ring. People
> steal money, or bodies in the name of love, but stealing one’s life
> story hurts the most”.

She is now driving an auto rickshaw to lead her life, even as Sudha used the film’s fame to make multi-crore films next, including Parasakthi, the “biopic” based on anti-Hindi agitations, produced by Akash Baskaran who is related to DMK’s first family by marriage.

**The person whose life made the film possible continues to live a poor life: unacknowledged, uncompensated, unknown.**

#### **Soorarai Pottru: The Brahmin who was distorted as an EVR-ist**

Sudha Kongara’s next film was inspired by the life of Captain GR Gopinath (Gorur Ramaswamy Iyengar Gopinath), the founder of Air Deccan. Gopinath is a real, popular person, so obviously, her old tricks with Thulasi won’t fly with him. So Sudha had to officially buy the filmmaking rights for his “Simply Fly” book. Why worry when you have new tricks to pull in this Brahmin entrepreneur’s biopic.

In Soorarai Pottru(2020):

- Gopinath is renamed as Nedumaaran Raajangam and given a strong EVR‑ist
  ideological identity, who gets married with a black shirt in
  ritual-free, a “self-respect” wedding.

- The antagonist is named Paresh Goswami, characterized as a ruthless
  classist Brahmin, supposedly inspired by Naresh Goyal, founder of Jet
  Airways.

So a real Brahmin man’s entrepreneurial story is retained, but his Brahmin identity is stripped out and replaced with an EVR‑ist persona, while “Brahmin” is reserved for a casteist villain who sanitizes after a handshake with the hero, and won’t even like it if his janitors use the same restrooms as him. The hero supposedly breaks not just cost barriers in aviation, but caste barriers too. **Why force fit such an angle? Because to justify the EVR hammer, such unnecessary nails have to be invented in the plot.**

Imagine a Steve Jobs biopic where Jobs is renamed as a Dstock Pammal Suresh K Sammandham who says “EVR mattum illai-na, enaala Apple computer kandupudichurka mudiyaadhu” (If not for EVR, I would not’ve found Apple Computers), would you take such a biopic seriously? Nobody is going to question if a real EVR-ist entrepreneur’s biopic is made, but if you put the EVR sticker to a successful non-EVR-ist Brahmin’s story, this sticker model shall be questioned.

> **Is Soorarai Pottru the biopic of Gopinath at all? If 90% of a
> Theseus ship is replaced by different components, is it still the same
> ship?**

#### **Jai Bhim: Adding ideological masala to real ingredients**

Jai Bhim (2021), directed by TJ Gnanavel, tells the real story from the 90s, of Parvathi who fought a lonely legal battle post the custodial death of her husband Rajakannu. It is a more complex case, because the underlying injustice it depicts is real, the advocacy of advocate Chandru is real, and the suffering of the communities it represents is real. All of this deserves to be acknowledged before the critique begins.

But a film that claims to champion truth has a higher responsibility to truth than one that makes no such claim. And Jai Bhim made the claim loudly, repeatedly, and to enormous international acclaim. It even briefly displaced The Shawshank Redemption to become the \#1 ranked film in IMdB, with a 9.6 rating. So what’s wrong with Jai Bhim?

The distortions begin with the villain. The real sub-inspector whose custodial torture of Rajakannu in 1993 formed the basis of the case was a Christian, not a Hindu. In the film, the villain is given a Hindu name Gurumoorthy, complete with the Vanniyar community’s sacred motif Agni Kundam in the frame with a caption “with the caption of “Kshatriya Kula Manadu, Villupuram”. This is not an oversight. **It is a deliberate distortion to reassign villainy from one community to another, executed at the level of visual design, in a film that presents itself as a faithful account of real events.**

When the Vanniyar community protested and the manipulation became a legal case, the makers replaced the Agni motif with a calendar bearing the image of Goddess Lakshmi. Replacing one Hindu symbol with another, after the caste-targeting was exposed, while keeping the character’s Hindu identity intact. The correction was itself a continuation of the original distortion, but this also was tolerated for “creative liberty”

Chandru responds by saying: "No sir, if you're being so positive, there must be something wrong I'm doing." The implication, delivered to enormous multiplex audiences and amplified by Amazon Prime's social media handle before they deleted the post after backlash, is that Tamil Brahmin approval is a reliable indicator for redirection. Dstocks used this clip to spread an alleged EVR quote “Whatever a Brahmin supports, you must oppose it”. FYI, Brahmins eat by hand and sheet by their backsides, in case somebody wanted redirection.

Even if you can tolerate all of the above distortions as creative liberty, consider this: In the film’s climax, the hero Chandru is shown to hand Sengeni (Parvathi) a new house after her legal victory. Millions of audience would’ve believed that Parvathy finally got her well deserved justice/happy ending after watching the film. But in reality, 70 year old Parvathi lives in a hut, struggles to make ends meet, and cares for her two mentally challenged sons with no support system.

Amazon reportedly paid ₹45 crores for OTT rights. Kalaignar TV reportedly paid ₹10 crores for satellite rights. The film won international awards and ₹40 lakhs in prize money at festivals. The filmmakers used Parvathy’s real suffering, packaged it, sold it internationally, collected the awards and the money, but left her in the same hut she was in before the cameras arrived. Parvathi lamented that no one, including Suriya, who is also the producer of the film, had informed them that they were going to make a film focusing on her husband and herself. Tell me in good faith that this is not exploitation.

#### **Amaran: The Identity That Could Not Be Named**

Amaran (2024) tells the real story of braveheart Major Mukund Varadarajan and it is a 99% sincere and well-made and patriotic film that can make grown men cry. It just had one small distortion: it hides the fact that Major Mukund was a Tamil Brahmin. This is not a trivial biographical detail: it is part of who he was, part of the family and tradition that shaped the man who gave his life for his country. His wife Indhu Rebecca Varghese’s identity as an army wife from a Christian family is depicted accurately in the film, as it should be. But why obfuscate Major’s community? He even keeps calling his dad as “Naina” in the film, which is common in Telugu households, not Tamil.

The usual defence is: “A soldier belongs to the nation, not to a caste.” And this is absolutely true. No Indian braveheart should ever be reduced to their community. But this is a biopic isn’t it? How would people have reacted if he was shown as Mukund Menon from Kochi? Authentic portrayal of roots is important for a biopic, even when our bravehearts outgrow those identities. Director Rajkumar himself said that the film stuck to authentic portrayal of the war cry of Rajput Regiment that Mukund was part of: “Bolo Bajrangbali ki Jai”, despite pushback from “secular” Dstocks. So why miss this Tamil Brahmin detail?

The deeper reason is to protect the century old Dravidian maya that Tamil Brahmins do not engage in physical labour, do not sacrifice, do not bleed for their country. That they are soft-handed, pampered, removed from the real Tamil world of toil and struggle. EVR wrote in his 1938 Kudiyarasu essay that “Brahmins are like Jews of Germany, who do not indulge in physical toil and live by leeching other’s hardwork”

Portraying a TamBrahm as a physically strong Major who bravely laid down his life for this country would destroy this elaborate and carefully constructed myth. Dravidian ideology’s hegemony over Tamil cinema made that depiction impossible. Showing a Tamil Brahmin as a complete, admirable human being, with his community identity intact, is a political act that carries real professional risk for any filmmaker. The White Dwarfs of the Tamil cinematic universe won’t like it one bit.

#### The Hindi Template

Tamil cinema doesn’t usually make a lot of biopics. Older films were that of Tamil freedom fighters, or award films like “Bharathi”, “Kamarajar” which are ignored by the mainstream audience. This trend of making commercial biopics started when they realized Hindi biopics like “Chak de India” became successful at the BO. There was an additional thing Dravidawood picked up from Hindi - that it was possible to buy the rights to something, and then add your own ideological masala in the name of creative liberty and sell it to the masses.

Chak de India took “inspiration” from the story of Mir Ranjan Negi, the Hindu goalkeeper who was scapegoated after India's hockey loss to Pakistan in 1982. In the film though, he becomes Kabir Khan, a Muslim man carrying the burden of being suspected of match-fixing. This is the ideological masala added to the real recipe that makes audience gobble it up, without even realizing what’s real and what’s added later.

Films like Sherni, Fire, Netflix series like IC814 have all used the same identity swapping technique, and Dravidawood was inspired by this template.

#### **What Honest Biographical Commercial Cinema Looks Like**

Please don’t mistake this post as a demand for making commercial biopics in a boring documentary style. Its totally understandable that certain creative liberties are needed to make the biopic more interesting and engaging to the audience. But whether they are truly non-ideological creative liberties, made in good conscience, is the question.

The standard is not impossibly high. Rocketry: The Nambi Effect showed what is possible when a filmmaker commits to authentic portrayal. Nambi Narayanan is depicted as a practising Hindu, a scientist of extraordinary ability, and a man whose faith and professional excellence exist in the same frame without apology or embarrassment. It sure had creative liberties, but nothing ideological. Infact, criticisms came in from leftist critics for portraying Nambi’s household playing Subrapatham, because in their worldview, a scientist cannot practice faith in his house.

Dheeran Adhigaaram Ondru (2017) is, in craft terms, one of Tamil cinema's finest police procedurals. The story of the inter-state dacoits Bawaria Gang, who murdered and looted across nine Indian states for a decade from 1995 to 2005, eventually killing a sitting Tamil Nadu MLA, is told with urgency and authenticity. Post-release of the film, Jangid (the real Dheeran) appreciated the team for their realistic portrayal of events and praised the director's research for the film. The film took a huge creative liberty with an elaborate love story where the hero’s own wife was in danger by the gang. This is understandable for commercial reasons, there is nothing ideological about it.

Some may argue that the ideological distortion with identity swaps are not deliberate. To them, I submit Sirai (2025), which is based on a real story, and the writer Tamil himself admitted in an interview that they changed the admirable innocent protagonist from a Hindu to a Muzlim, so as to spread a positive message about this community to the masses. The film’s director Suresh was the AD of Vetrimaaran, who recently appreciated his protégé on stage for making this “much needed inclusive film in times such as this”. Director also stated on stage how he made “political corrections” to the film, putting himself in the shoes of his Guru Vetrimaaran. Tell me in good faith that this is merely creative liberty, and not ideological.

#### Conclusion

A biopic must be accountable to the people whose stories it claims to represent. It means not stealing a boxer’s story and calling it inspiration. It means not taking ₹55 crores from OTT platforms for a film about a woman’s suffering and leaving that woman in a hut. It means not erasing a martyr’s identity because it complicates your ideology. It means not inserting EVR-ism into an entrepreneur’s story to dishonestly claim credit for someone else’s achievement.

Dravidawood has built an extraordinary apparatus for appearing progressive while doing the opposite of these things. It has mastered the aesthetics of conscience without the substance of it. It has learned that the words “based on a true story” opens a door to audience trust that can then be walked through carrying whatever ideological cargo the filmmaker wishes to bring.

> The audience deserves better. The real people behind these stories:
> Thulasi, Parvathi, Major Mukund, Captain Gopinath, deserve better.
> Their lives are not raw material for an ideological project. Their
> stories are not vessels to be emptied of their actual content and
> refilled with someone else’s worldview.

They are real. What happened to them was real. The least a film can do is honour that reality. Even when, and especially when that reality is inconvenient for the story the filmmaker wanted to tell. **If 90% of a Theseus ship is replaced by different components, is it still the same ship?**
