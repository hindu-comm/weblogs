#### A confession

I am not an idealist. I do not live in a utopia where people imagine Tamil Nadu elections to work like American presidential debates where candidates make arguments and counterarguments on television, voters decide based on policy positions, rhetorical ability, and leadership acumen. I am also not a big-brained person who possesses “exclusive confidential data” from highly placed Soros sources to conclude that the Deep State or the Vatican or the Illuminati were unhappy with MKS and hence installed Joseph Vijay as their new “puppet” to directly control Tamil Nadu.

I am a normal person with normal intelligence, whose feet is firmly planted on the ground, who observes historical patterns and makes arguments based only on publicly available data. So read on, if you are interested in an educated common man’s perspective on how Vijay won over Tamil Nadu, what this result tells us about the Tamil psyche, and what other parties must understand if they hope to replicate anything resembling the Vijay model.

#### The rise and rise and rise of Vijay

Joseph Vijay Chandrasekhar, son of famous Tamil director SAC, was criticised as a nepo-kid for the longest time, especially by fans of Ajith, his main competitor. He was mocked for his looks, his mediocre acting in early films, his father’s handholding. But Vijay took all of it on his stride and transformed himself over a decade into a stylistic, dance-beast, action-hero of commercial Tamil cinema. At one point, he became the highest paid star in the industry, overtaking even Superstar Rajinikanth. So there is genuine truth to his political statement: “I left the peak of my career (Career’in Uccham) to come serve the people.”

But Vijay’s political rise did not begin in 2024 when he launched Thamizhaga Vettri Kazhagam. It began much earlier, in a deliberate, patient, decades-long process that most people were too busy mocking to notice. His father SAC, after associations with MGR, realised early that he could groom his son into the next MGR. This resulted in a strong district-wise Vijay fan clubs very similar to MGR’s district-level fan club infrastructure, which Vijay actively encouraged and maintained. He chose scripts carefully like MGR: generic enough to appeal broadly, always positioning him as the good guy who defeats the bad guys. He even made his 50th film Sura to appeal to fishermen who seem to have genuinely accepted Vijay (after MGR) as one of them. The film spectacularly bombed at the BO, but that didn’t stop Vijay.

Vijay reinvented himself as a classy and massy hero in Thuppaaki (2012) by playing Jagdish, a DIA army officer who defeats a sleeper cell leader in Mumbai. Since this nationalistic film became a superhit, he continued collaborating with Murugadoss for his Katthi (2014) which was more political. He played dual roles, one of which was comrade Jeevanantham who fights for a dry agricultural village against a Cola company stealing groundwater. He played another role of Kathiresan who gives a press meet where he questions the corruption in 2G spectrum alleged to the outgoing DMK telecom minister A Raja.

Comrade Jeevanandham

Vijay chose to push his political aspirations a bit far with his 2013 film “Thalaiva” (leader) with a poster “Time to lead”. This caught the attention of then CM Jayalalitha who perhaps interpreted this as a direct challenge to her leadership. While the film released globally on August 9, 2013, it was stalled in Tamil Nadu until August 20, 2013. Vijay had to release an emotional video message with folded hands, asking fans for patience and requesting support from the Chief Minister, in addition to removing the posters.

Thalaivaa: Time to Lead

After Jayalalitha's demise in 2016, the political messaging in his films grew bolder. *Mersal* (2017) featured a doctor (who is a MGR fan) delivering dialogues about the recently launched GST, Singapore's free healthcare, and the taxation of life-saving medicines: timely, politically charged, emotionally delivered. He also made a factually incorrect dialogue in the film that “While life saving medicines were taxed (with GST), alcohol that removes Mangalsutra of women was tax free”. In reality, alcohol still comes under through a combination of **State Excise Duty** and **Value Added Tax (VAT)**, rather than GST, which eventually makes the producers pay more tax as they can’t claim Input Tax Credit under GST. This time BJP called for removal of these scenes from the film, with BJP strongman H Raja calling Vijay as “Joseph Vijay” invoking his Christian name into the controversy. Vijay published a notice on his letterpad signing it for the first time as Joseph Vijay to reinforce that he is not ashamed of his identity. The film was a massive superhit, with an excellent second half flashback that had a colorful song “Aalaporaan Thamizhan” , meaning, the Tamil is going to rule. It was no doubt self-referential.

By 2018, Vijay made no qualms about making an explicit political film Sarkar, that was funded by Sun Pictures themselves. It is rumored that since Sarkar times, Vijay’s salary was the highest in the market, beating even Superstar Rajini’s - as Petta was also produced by the same studio. Vijay played the role of Sundar Ramasamy ( a corporate CEO like Sundar Pichai) who returns to India to vote, but has his vote stolen. In the second half, Vijay turns into a full time politician with a song “Oru Viral Puratchi” - one fingered revolution - where he throws away the freebies (TV, grinders and fans) given by the government. The film’s villain was allegedly named Komalavalli, which was Jayalalitha’s birth name. Ministers from ADMK accused Vijay of attempting to instigate viewers against the government by ridiculing its welfare schemes. Following threats of lawsuits from the government, the filmmakers agreed to cut scenes and mute the name wherever it appeared in the audio track.

It was clear that Vijay was positioning himself against all established parties:

- DMK (2G scene in Katthi, TV freebie scene in Sarkar)

- ADMK (Villain name and Mixie freebie in Sarkar)

- BJP (with signing himself as Joseph Vijay).

- He also positioned himself as a leftist comrade in Katthi, who was not
  anti-industry, but anti-exploitation.

#### The 2026 results that nobody saw coming

Lets cut to today. I don’t think people outside Tamil Nadu fully understand the scale of what Vijay has managed to achieve this week. Most people in Tamil Nadu themselves are shocked by the numbers (108/236). The experts said Vijay was no MGR and he proved them right. Even the mighty MGR, with decades of prior political experience, took five years after starting ADMK before becoming CM. Vijay did it in two.

They said you needed booth-level agents, organisational cadre strength, and last-mile campaigning across all 234 constituencies. Vijay gave 15 minute speech bytes that were emotional, rhetorical, deliberately repetitive. He barely covered even fifty percent of constituencies.

They said he would not find 233 candidates. He did. Except for KAS, almost 90% of them were debut candidates, unknowns fielded by a party that was two years old.

They said no party has ever won without coalitions: with caste-based, religion-based, or communist parties. Vijay went entirely alone. Not even smaller parties allied with him.

They said he was a work-from-home politician whose fandom was a social media illusion. But his fans turned voting day into a celebration moving mountains, crossing oceans to cast their vote.

They said he could never breach Chennai, the DMK fortress. He fielded a nobody against sitting CM MKS and won. All of Chennai has been breached. They said he would not cross double digits. He won three-digit seats. 108.

They called him Anil, a reference to a comment he had made about being a small squirrel helping ADMK win long ago. He became a giant-slaying dragon that breathes fire.

Rules are being rewritten. Expert psephologists are being humbled. Sitting ministers are being taken down by unknown debutantes.

An unexpected victory, perhaps even for Vijay, has happened. And the utter unbelievability of it is messing with everyone’s minds, which is why conspiracy theories are proliferating on the internet.

#### Is Vijay a Deep State puppet?

Let’s first address the elephant in the room. 99% Tamil intelligentsia did not see this election results coming. What followed was predictable in its pattern if not its specifics. Dstocks began calling TVK an RSS Trojan horse. RW commentators began calling it a Vatican/ Deep State secret operation. Both are ideology-driven narratives, not truthful analysis. You believe whichever one you believe because it comforts your existing prejudices, not because the evidence points there.

Just one day before the results, RW handles were brutally trolling pollsters like Pradeep, DravidaSishu, and AwakeHindu for predicting a TVK win. Now suddenly everyone is acting as if they have the Pope’s personal phone number and knew it all along as a Vatican plot. Funny how that works.

The Vatican-RSS binary is basically a glorified version of the “13 families that control the world” theory. The appeal of such theories is that they convert a genuinely surprising, multi-causal, multi-factored, complex democratic outcome into a simple story with a puppet master. It is very easy to do and very satisfying to believe, just like in novels and cinema. **The problem with such conspiracy theories is that they are always cooked up after the results, not before**. These theories are nothing but post-hoc rationalisation intended to spin a political narrative.

> What actually happened is this: TN has had a genuine political vacuum
> ever since the passing away of its powerful leaders MK and JJ. 9 years
> of cumulative public frustrations burst out at an unexpected opportune
> moment, channelled through a vessel named Vijay who had spent 30 years
> making himself trusted and beloved.

Better to engage with this actual reality instead of resorting to post-hoc fallacies. Sure, every politician receives questionable funding from varying power brokerage systems, but to single out TVK for it post-results, is merely coming from a place of judging Joseph Vijay for his name. Be eternally vigilant, but don’t believe everything you hear on the internet without application of the mind. If you do, then what’s the difference between you and the “Tharkuris” that you so despise? **Let ground reality be your compass and truth be your Dhruva Nakshatra always.**

#### The Tamil Psyche

To understand Vijay’s political rise, you first have to understand the Tamil psyche which comes from century old tradition of hero worship. Since the Sangam era, Nadu Kal (hero stones) were erected not just to memorialise fallen warriors but to worship them. Tamils worship their ancestors and the heroes of the past who protected their villages as Naataar Kadavuls (folk religious deities). Plenty of communities trace their lineage to legendary Tamil kingdoms, whether as rulers, warriors, or accountants. Whether these lineages are historically accurate is immaterial. What matters is that millions of people believe them to be true and that belief shapes how they relate to heroism, leadership, and the idea of the protector.

These Tamils grew up listening to Sthala Puranas and Karna Parambarai stories from their grandmothers, in their literature, in Koothus and dance skits. In village festivals, it is not uncommon for people to be consumed by the spirit of folk deities, heroes worshipped not just as historical figures but as living protectors.

This psyche eventually transfixed to moving images on screen, where their mental visual imagery became closely associated with their reality. In the darkness of a theatre, many are consumed by the spirits of heroism that’s manifest on screen, the presence of which is acknowledged by the commune with hoots, claps and whistles. The distinction between truth and fiction can get very blurry for a society with this psyche. When something on screen resonates deeply with them, they become emotionally invested in the hero. This is why there were instances reported in village theatres where the 5 anna and 10 anna tickets seated on the floor near the screen, tore it up in moments where MGR was in peril in the cinema. The hero on screen and the hero in the heart were not two different things.

> Political commentator Cho Ramaswamy captured it precisely: “It is not
> only the song, but the singer who is important for the public.”

**Tamil politics is personality-driven, not ideology-driven**. It always has been. Any analysis that ignores this is incomplete.

#### Is Hero Worship a feature or a bug?

Tamil Nadu’s relationship to film-star-turned-politicians is too often treated as a pathology to be corrected, a collective delusion that a better-educated electorate would eventually outgrow. This framing is wrong, both as analysis and as political strategy.

> **Hero worship is not a bug. It is a feature of a specific political
> culture with its own logic, its own history, and its own internal
> coherence.**

MGR did not win Tamil Nadu’s loyalty because voters were foolish. He won it because he spent decades playing the same character on screen: a man who fought for the poor, stood up to the powerful, never abandoned the weak, and then carried that character into public life with sufficient consistency that people believed it. This was no accident.

> MGR was inspired by Hollywood actor and Democratic political activist
> Gregory Peck who often portrayed protagonists with ‘fiber’ within a
> moral setting. MGR even met his inspiration Peck at the
> Waldorf-Astoria Hotel, New York.

So with such scripts, the emotional transfer happened on common man’s minds. It was a reasonable bet by people with limited information about political candidates, using the information they had access to: thirty years of watching this man be, on screen, exactly the kind of person they wanted representing them.

NTR did the same in Andhra Pradesh. He played gods and kings for decades, accumulated the emotional capital of that mythology, and converted it into political currency when he entered the arena. His Telugu Desam Party won its first election with a landslide that shocked every serious political analyst. The serious analysts had not accounted for what thirty years of playing Ram and Krishna does to a man’s relationship with an electorate that grew up watching him be those characters.

**People want to vote for someone they connect with, someone they trust, someone they believe to be competent.** In Tamil Nadu, this can come through cinema power, through extraordinary oratory: former DMK CMs Arignar Anna and MK were not actors but were orators of such force that people waited overnight to hear them speak, or through the Mr. Clean image of a Kamaraj. What it cannot come through, in Tamil Nadu, is pure policy argument delivered to a passive electorate. That is not how Tamil political trust is built. Even Kamaraj underestimated this “cinema” reality and could not win over DMK after he lost to them.

The question of whether a dancing, fighting film star is qualified for political office deserves to be asked honestly. But it deserves to be asked of film stars and career politicians **BOTH**. How many past MLAs in TN have backgrounds that would survive serious scrutiny? How many completed atleast schooling? The goonda who became an MLA through muscle and money is somehow more legitimate than the actor who built decades of genuine popular trust? The intellectual dislikes the actor because the actor is visible, popular, and operates through emotion. The goonda turned politician is less offensive to the intellectual’s sensibility because he operates behind the scenes and all his vices are not out there on gossip columns for public consumption.

The intelligentsia must get off its high horse and acknowledge reality: circles of power and fame are prone to vice. The era of honest men of character like Kamaraj is behind us. We are all forced to choose between different shades of grey. And yes, at some point you have to contend with the reality that your vote, that of an educated reader of essays, carries the exact same weight as that of a “Tharkuri” that you so detest. The name of this numbers game is *democrazy*, and if you choose to play it, you had better understand its rules. The election does not grade you on how you arrived at your choice. The only thing that matters, on counting day, is the number of votes against your symbol.

#### What DMK Understood that others don’t

DMK is not a mighty political force because it has a better ideology. It is a mighty force because it understood how Tamil minds actually work, and built an entire cultural apparatus to operate on those minds at scale.

The common people of Tamil Nadu are moved primarily by emotion. This is not an insult. This is a description of human beings. We are all more emotional than rational. The difference is that DMK built its entire political project around this reality while its opponents built theirs around the fantasy that Tamil voters could be won by superior argument alone.

DMK owns cinema. It owns street theatre. It owns mega serials. It owns reality shows. It owns every mass medium with genuine mindshare among ordinary Tamil people. It uses these mediums to plant ideas: sticky, emotional, difficult-to-dislodge ideas. Tamil versus Hindi. Aryan versus Dravidian. State versus Centre. Stepchild versus favoured child. These are not policy positions. They are feelings. And once a feeling that we are given stepmotherly treatment by the center takes root in a person’s mind, no amount of counter-argument can remove it. The idea was not placed there by logic but by emotions. Pathos is far more powerful than logos. **You cannot logos your way out of a pathos problem.**

A single episode of Neeya Naana can plant an idea into more Tamil minds than a thousand treatises and Twitter threads combined. This is not a criticism of those Tamil minds. It is a description of how human communication works at scale. This is why despite all the trolling, DMK’s political events are organised like reality shows with emotional background scores, film directors and actors performing alongside politicians, sentiment calibrated with production-house precision.

It took a Vijay tsunami, also driven by Pathos, to unseat DMK in 2026.

#### The Shepherd and the Sheep

A good shepherd must know what his sheep want. Not what he thinks they should want. Not what they would want if they were better educated or more rational. What they actually want, right now, as they are.

This does not mean pandering. It means meeting people where they are before trying to take them somewhere else. Earning trust before asking for change. Understanding that the path from where your audience is to where you want them to be is travelled in steps, not in a single leap from their current position to your ideal destination.

Thevar Magan film said it best, in the way that Tamil cinema sometimes says things better than any political philosopher: *Medhuva thaan ya varuvaan namma aalu.* Our man will come, but slowly. Incremental progress. Gradual trust-building. The long game played with patience rather than the short game played with contempt.

The non-left Tamil intellectual wants voters to arrive at the correct political conclusion immediately, fully formed, without requiring the emotional scaffolding that actually moves people. When voters do not oblige, the intellectual concludes they are stupid, ungrateful, or hopelessly manipulated.

> This is precisely the mistake that DMK also makes: calling voters
> stupid and ungrateful when DMK is not voted to power. The non-left, in
> its elite moments, makes exactly the same error from exactly the same
> place of pride. **Respecting the voter means accepting that the
> voter’s emotional relationship to politics is as legitimate as your
> intellectual one**.

#### The Dharmic Normie vs The Dharmic Intellectual

I know you are still not convinced, so let me share another perspective. A Dharmic intellectual knows the texts. He can cite the Vedanta. He can win the argument. He has probably won many arguments. But a Dharmic normie carries something additional: the emotional weight of his community.

He does not just know the right things: he cares about the people who haven’t arrived at those things yet, and he is willing to meet them where they are. He appeals to emotion first, builds trust second, and introduces logic only after the emotional connection has made the audience willing to receive it. This is not manipulative at all, this is the only communication that’s effective with emotional non-intellectual normal human beings.

Dharma needs not just Vedanta treatises. It needs Sthala Puranas too: the local stories, the accessible narratives, the forms that meet people in their own emotional language. The Sthala Purana does not replace the Vedanta. It is the Vedanta’s ambassador to people who would never read the Vedanta. The Dharmic intellectual will find discrepancies in multiple Sthala Puranas and use them to discredit the entire tradition. The Dharmic normie understands the immense value of the Sthala Purana in rooting a Puranic event to an individual’s local temple, in making the cosmic feel personal. A Dharmic normie believes that every Sthala Purana is true, because he uses his heart to feel its value, not his intellect to dissect its authenticity.

The common man learns more from a Puranic story than from an Upanishadic text, which is a far cry to comprehend for his capacity. Cinema plays the role of a Sthala Purana in a common man’s mind. A well-made film that resonates with Dharmic values reaches more Tamil minds in two hours than a decade of intellectual output reaches in its lifetime. Of what use is intellect if does not meet people where they are?

Vijay’s “Om Shakthi Parashakthi, Thee Mu Ka oru Theeya Shakthi” line can change more minds than any well-argued Substack article.

And let me add something irrational but important here: cinema is an art form, a medium with a life of its own. If you only want to use cinema for your purposes without truly loving it, the medium will sense it and resist it. Most RW cinema gets a bad reputation not because the ideology is wrong but because the filmmaker does not truly love the artistic medium as he merely wants to use it for furthering his agenda. The audience can feel the difference. Always.

#### Conclusion

Vijay won over Tamil Nadu because he understood instinctively, through 30 years of professional practice, how Tamil emotional lives work. He built trust one film at a time, and then converted it into the most durable currency in Tamil politics.

The non-left can resent this. It can wish that Tamil voters related to politics the way serious analysts do. It can continue to produce excellent arguments that reach small audiences and lose elections. Or it can learn the lesson.

Respect the medium. Engage with mass culture. Build emotional trust before asking for intellectual agreement. Meet people where they are. Carry the weight of your emotional kith and kin rather than abandoning them to the more emotionally intelligent opposition. Don’t do the same Stockist elitist mistake of cursing the voters.

Till date, Vijay has fished in the same Dravidian waters, with EVR as one of his leaders, following D culture of Jan 14 wishes of Tamil New Year, opposing Parandur airports and promising impractical freebies. He is still a political amateur who will most likely make plenty of administrative and policy mistakes, which he may learn from, or not.

A lot remains to be seen. Whether Vijay can remain truly secular in the genuine sense, not the performative Dravidian sense is an open question. The criticisms can wait. Not now. They will have their time.

But for today, we can all wish him well for singlehandedly putting a full stop to the Dravidian megafactory that had all the money, all the muscle, all the institutional control, and 50 years of political oxygen. He went alone, with debut candidates, with fifteen-minute speeches, with no caste coalition, no organisational machinery of consequence, and he won. Dstocks are calling this defeat worser than 1991, where they won merely 7 seats as an alliance.

For today, we can all breathe this fresh air of change, where our sacred Tamil Nadu shall be ruled by a non-Dravidian party after fifty long years.

V for Valiant. V for Victory. V for Vaagai. V for Vijay.
