# Decoding Dravidawood \#4: "Give us your daughters"

### Dravidawood does not hate all Brahmins equally. It despises Brahmin men. But Brahmin women? It loves them so very much.

Dravidawood has a long-standing consistent theme in their films: “to convert Brahmin women” by seemingly liberating them from their regressive, patriarchal, non-macho families. One film on this theme an anomaly. Two is a trend, three is a coincidence, four could be a pattern. But if you see 12 films across decades with the exact same template, would you start believing that this is an ideological project? Let’s dive in.

### **The Template**

Before listing films, it helps to name the pattern. Once you see it, you start spotting it everywhere.

Call it **The Dravidawood TamBrahm Woman Template**:

1.  **The heroine**

    - She is explicitly Tamil Brahmin: by name, surname, locality,
      school, college, job role or ritual.

    - She is innocent, smart, spirited, and has a lot of dreams.

2.  **Her community’s men and women**

    - The men are weak, cowardly, bigoted, hypocritical, or cruel
      caricatures.

    - The father is a casteist, regressive person who is an obstacle to
      her dreams

    - The fiancé or arranged groom is a passive, beta male, a laughable
      “comic relief” or a punching bag for the film’s hero.

    - The women are either poor victims/peddlers of patriarchy (mostly
      heroine’s mother), or needs saving (the akka/thangai in distress)
      or a helpful paati who helps the heroine break free from the
      shackles of patriarchy

3.  **Her community’s institutions**

    - Agraharam, temple, madapalli, school, marriage ritual: all are
      cages.

    - They exist to stunt her individuality, by enforcing rules on her
      and never support any of her dreams.

4.  **The liberator**

    - A Drav man, usually stripped of caste surname or clearly marked as
      “non-Brahmin” in name, background and speech.

    - He is “alpha”: macho, capable, protective, emotionally vivid,
      progressive, everything the Brahmin men are not.

5.  **The conclusion**

- Her turning away from community expectations and toward this liberator
  is framed as “coming of age” or “breaking free”.

- The ideology that blesses this is the Dravidian worldview. EVR blesses
  the woman overtly or covertly, his ideology is always in the
  Dravidawood air.’

Not every film ticks every box equally. But across the twelve, the **architecture is stable**.

#### Fab Four: Sethu, Kadhalikka Neramillai, Annapoorani, Meyaadha Maan 

1.  **Sethu(1999): The Alpha and the Agraharam**

Sethu (Chiyaan) is a violent campus strongman: he beats rivals, threatens opponents, terrorises his way through college politics. The film nevertheless codes him as fundamentally **righteous and compassionate**.

His love interest is Abitha Kuchalambal, explicitly called as “Agrahaarathu mami” by Chiyaan’s friends: fair, innocent, priest’s daughter, engaged to a Brahmin boy who also serves at the temple. Goes to same college as Chiyaan.

Look at how that fiancé is written:

- He is soft, passive, and broke.

- He fails to protect Abitha’s sister from dowry harassment.

- He cannot stop a brothel operating right in his agraharam.

But Chiyaan can: he thrashes the abusive husband, exposes the brothel to police, saves the honor of the damsel in distress (akka), walks into spaces Brahmin men in the film only snivel around. Chiyaan even threatens heroine’s fiance and he cows down.

Then comes the key line: the fiancé himself tells Abitha that Sethu is better than him in every way, and adds, “No shastra says a Brahmin woman cannot marry outside her community.”

That’s the ideological kill shot. A Brahmin man, in a Tamil film, invoking “scripture” to endorse a Brahmin woman walking away from Brahmin men. The worldview has won so thoroughly that it can put its own conclusion into the mouth of the “defeated” party.

Abitha ultimately dies by suicide when her father tries to marry her off again. Apparently, she cannot live without the man who stalked, kidnapped, and tied her up in an abandoned location. The film mourns it as tragic love. Step back, and it looks like the purest form of the **“alpha liberator” fantasy**: the poor TamBrahm woman deserves to be with an alpha Drav man. This film won National Award.

2.  **Kadhalikka Neramillai (2025): EVR’s LaLa land of enjaaiment w/o
    responsibility**

In Kiruthiga Udhayanidhi’s Kadhalikka Neramillai, the heroine Shriya is a modern day TamBrahm woman: working, independent, drinks, smokes, sleeps, but doesn’t believe in marriage.

Her TamBrahm family believes:

- Education spoils women and makes them arrogant

- Autonomy is suspect.

- When her engagement collapses, their first response is judgment, not
  care.

**3. Annapoorani (2023): From Madapalli to Meat**

Annapoorani updates the template for the OTT age: no rowdies, just food shows and ambition. But the structure is the same.

- Heroine is the daughter of the **Srirangam temple madapalli** cook: a
  man whose lineage and vegetarianism are both devotional acts, not
  lifestyle choices.

- Her dream is to be a star chef in a secular, non‑veg world.

- Her dad doesn’t support her dreams and arranges a wedding, but she
  elopes

The film’s key arguments:

- Through her progressive friend Farhan, we’re told **no god really says
  meat is a sin**.

- Immediately after, we see him offer **Namaz before cooking biryani**,
  and the film suggests this makes the dish taste better, while also
  claiming biryani “has no caste or religion”. This kinda secularism
  sells in the land of Paguthocrisy.

  Iyengar cook doing secular Namaz for tasty Biryani

The emotional pivot:

- Annapoorani eats chicken, runs away from her wedding with Farhan’s
  help, and is “freed” from the “Brahminical” kitchen into the
  cosmopolitan one.

- Her **Paati**, not her father, is the one who helps her escape and
  whispers, “Don’t get trapped like me.”

That grandmother trope is important. The older Brahmin woman recognises her life as a cage, and helps the younger one escape it. It is **women dismantling their own house from the inside**.

4.  **Avvai Shanmughi: Ideology hidden beneath genuine comedy**

Heroine is Janaki, daughter of a businessman Viswanathan Iyer who is a scheming man. But hero Pandiyan has no surname stated.

The Brahmin household staff are sketched in the same register. Sethurama Iyer (Delhi Ganesh) is an irresponsible flirt, and his so‑called girlfriend Kousi, the poor Tamil Brahmin cook, is caught stealing silver lamps. It is not the family that exposes her, but Pandiyan in his Avvai Shanmughi disguise: the “Dravidian” intruder who reveals the rot inside the conservative Brahmin bungalow.

Contrast this with the helpful Pasha (Nasser) who loses his job trying to help the hero. Shanmughi hires this Pasha in an Iyer kitchen by lying that he is a dumb Palavakam Swaminathan in a terrific comedy scene. When found out, he delivers the lecture that the Iyer leather businessman kills more animals(cows) for export than the cook does for food. It takes great ingenuity to make one laugh at oneself, without even making you realize the ideology behind.

#### The pattern in other films

1.  **Dasavatharam (2008)**

    Kamal’s rationalist scientist hero Govind Ramasamy Naicker meets the
    annoying “foxy Aryan” Aandaal (Asin), an Iyengar woman who rats him
    out every chance she gets. But the rational Naicker man saves her at
    every instant, from goondas to ex-CIA agents. After the tsunami,
    this cat-mouse duo fall in love. But why? The atheist-rationalist
    Naicker mocks her regressiveness throughout, then suddenly calls for
    “unity.” Why do they end up together? Because who is a better match
    to liberate the Iyengar girl than the atheist rationalist Dravidian
    hero?

2.  **Vedam Puthithu (1987)**

    A Thevar man’s son falls for a Tamil Brahmin priest’s daughter. He
    even learns the Vedas from the father so as to ogle at his daughter.
    They get found out and eventually the priest’s rigidity causes
    multiple deaths. The surviving Brahmin boy (brother of heroine) is
    orphaned, rejected by his own community, but is raised by the
    atheist Sathyaraj’s family, who give up meat to accommodate him. By
    the climax, the son eventually removes his sacred thread in disgust.
    The Brahmin boy’s final act of defiance against his own tradition is
    presented as enlightenment. The non Brahmin family’s generosity is
    presented as the true spirit of humanity. It is to be noted that the
    same anti-caste director Bharathiraja’s later film’s song
    “Thirupaachi Aruvaala” from Taj Mahal is caste-coded and still
    extremely popular among caste circles.

3.  **Meyaadha Maan**

    A North Madras man-child named Murali who repeatedly uses suicide
    threats after breakups falls for a Brahmin college-mate named Madhu.
    She postpones her arranged wedding with a useless NRI groom and
    somehow falls for this wastrel Murali because he remembers what
    color chudihar she used to wear years ago. She decides to stop her
    wedding by getting pregnant with Murali’s progeny and vomits right
    at the homa kundam on her arranged wedding day, signalling the
    pregnancy. Such desecration of sacred spaces pass off as comedy in
    Dravidawood. Their registered marriage promptly follows, a signal
    that it has replaced the ritual wedding.

4.  **Boat**

    A pre-independence era period film that features a casteist Brahmin
    mama, whose open-minded daughter falls in love with their fisherman
    and sings a Carnatic song full of North Madras slang to prove her
    love.

5.  **Chellame and Vairamuthu’s Lyrics**

    Perhaps the most explicit statement of the project appears not in a
    film’s screenplay but in its song lyrics. Vairamuthu wrote for
    Chellame: “Aariya Udhadugal Unnadhu / Dravida Udhadugal Ennudhu /
    Aariyam Dravidam Rendum Kalakkatume”: the Aryan lips are yours, the
    Dravidian lips are mine, let Aryan and Dravidian mix. The lyric is
    framed as romantic. It is also a precise statement of their
    ideological project: the Aryan woman and the Dravidian man, their
    union celebrated in song by the movement’s most celebrated poet.

#### Conclusion

Every one of these films is built around the same structural choice: the Tamil Brahmin woman as the site of ideological contest, her community’s men as the obstacle, and the Dravidian worldview as the liberation.

This is not a coincidence. Coincidences do not sustain themselves across decades and different directors. This is a project: conscious in some cases, absorbed and reproduced unconsciously in others, but consistent in its architecture regardless of who is directing or writing in any given decade.

The strategic logic is worth stating clearly. You cannot defeat a community from the outside without creating resistance. Communities under external attack become more cohesive, more defensive, more committed to their identity. But if you can make the community’s own daughters feel that their family is their captor and their tradition is their cage, the community does not resist. It slowly hollows. The men are dismissed as bigots. The institutions are abandoned as oppressive. The women carry the genetic and cultural continuity of the community, and if they are successfully turned against it, the community fades not through conflict but through attrition.

*Ideological change runs fastest when the daughter feels she must escape her mother to become herself.*

This is not a new strategy. It is as old as cultural warfare itself. What is remarkable about the Dravidawood version is how consistently and patiently it has been executed: not through any single mastermind but through an ideological ecosystem that has reproduced the same template across generations of filmmakers, writers, and lyricists who share a worldview and know, intuitively, what kind of story serves it.

> This is not just about TamBrahm women. Learn to read between the lines
> and know the pattern to educate your friends and family. If you are a
> woman seeking liberation, please understand if you are making your own
> decisions independently, or if you are being influenced by an
> environment that is motivated to “come for you”. Be wise, listen to
> all voices - both regressive and progressive and decide if you are
> taking your shots or letting your life become someone else’s
> ideological project. Your choice.
