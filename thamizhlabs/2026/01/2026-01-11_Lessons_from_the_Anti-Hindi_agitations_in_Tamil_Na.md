## Origins: Not EVR or Rajaji, but MKG set the fire ablaze!

British India was: Multilingual, Multicultural and Multireligious. Trying to unify it was quite a task.

Gandhi believed that a newly independent India needed a **single unifying instrument** to replace the imperialist’s language of English, which he saw as alienating Indians from their own land and instilling a sense of inferiority.

His solution was **Hindustani**: an amalgam of Hindi and Urdu.

He believed Hindustani:

- Was non-English

- Unified Hindus and Muslims

- Was acceptable to a majority of Indians

Gandhi envisioned Hindustani (Hindi, for all practical purposes) as a **Rashtra Bhasha** and **link language**, not as a replacement for regional languages. When a welcome address delivered to him in **Mayavaram in 1915** was read in English, Gandhi openly expressed dissatisfaction that it had not been delivered in Hindi.

Hindi’s role, as he articulated, was limited to enabling Indians speaking different languages to recognize their **organic relationship** with one another.

However, Gandhi also wrote in *Young India* that:

- Linguistic minorities should not expect the majority to learn their
  languages

- Non-Hindi speakers must learn Hindi for national integration

And Gandhi was not merely a Congress leader - **he was Congress**.

To fulfil Gandhi’s vision in Tamil Nadu, **Rajaji brought EVR into the Congress** and the duo collaborated on many of MKG’s pet projects - Khadi, Prohibition, Untouchability abolition and **HINDI PROMOTION!**

In **1922**, EVR’s own building in Erode housed the first Hindi teacher training college in Madras Presidency. Tamil scholar **Thiru. Vi. Ka.** wrote that *“Nayakkar (EVR) was the primary person to have imposed Hindi in Tamil Nadu.”*

Politics, however, does not preserve friendships.

EVR broke away from Congress to join the **Justice Party**, while Rajaji went on to defeat it and become **Premier of the Madras Presidency**.

## Phase 1: Compulsion and the First Revolt (1938–1940)

As premier of Madras Presidency, in **1938**, Rajaji issued an order making **Hindi mandatory** for Classes 6–9 in all schools of Madras Presidency.

The Madras Presidency then included: Tamil Nadu, Andhra, Karnataka, Kerala

Rajaji’s reasoning was pragmatic, even well-intentioned: If Hindi became the official language after Independence, the South should be prepared.

However, “making Hindi mandatory” proved to be a fatal miscalculation.

### The Protests

The order triggered the **first phase of anti-Hindi agitations**, led initially by Tamil scholars and activists. EVR joined later as the movement gathered momentum.

Key arguments made by Tamil scholars included:

- Multilingual societies like the USSR functioned without a single
  common language

- India did not need linguistic uniformity to thrive

### A Dangerous Turn

Unfortunately, resolutions passed by the **Justice Party Confederation in 1938** went beyond opposing Hindi. They included demands for a **separate Dravida Nadu**, free from what EVR framed as *Aryan dominance*.

This marked the emergence of EVR’s **racial-secessionist worldview**:

- Brahmins were classified as Aryans

- Non-Brahmins as Dravidians

- Dravida Nadu was a racial project to expel Brahmins and north Indian
  banias (merchants) while welcoming the Dalits, backwards and
  minorities from the north as their kin.

### State Repression and Withdrawal

Rajaji remained unfazed by it all. As with prohibition, he believed that the usage of an iron hand was justified if the end goal was for the “greater good”. Protests intensified. Two protestors **Thalamuthu** and **Natarajan** died in prison.

Figures like **Tagore** and **Jinnah** condemned the usage of criminal prosecution on language protestors. Rajaji did not relent.

What finally stopped him was **World War II**.

When India was dragged into the war without consent, all provincial premiers - including Rajaji, resigned by **October 1939**. By **1940**, the interim government withdrew compulsory Hindi from schools, due to pushback.

**Phase 1 ended in victory for the anti-Hindi imposition protestors.**

## Phase 2: Constitution, Caution, and Compromise (1948–1950)

The second phase of the movement began in **June 1948**, when Congress CM **Omandhoorar** proposed a law allowing one among Hindi, Sanskrit, Arabic, Kannada, Telugu, or Malayalam as a second language.

Opponents saw this as a **backdoor entry for Hindi**, given the lack of teachers for other languages. The newly renamed **Dravidar Kazhagam (DK)** with EVR at the helm, resolved that Hindi should not even be optional.

### Constitutional Intervention

During Constituent Assembly debates, **T.T. Krishnamachari** warned that aggressive Hindi push would strengthen separatist forces like the DK and urged restraint.

The result was a compromise:

- Hindi was designated Official Language

- English retained as associate official language for **15 years**

Madras Presidency once again withdrew Hindi mandates in schools.

**Phase 2 also ended in success.**

Meanwhile, in **1949**, **Annadurai split from the DK to form the DMK**. Despite bitter exchanges between guru and shishya, Hindi remained a common enemy.

## Phase 3: Symbols, Civil Disobedience, and Assurances (1952–1959)

In **1952**, the Nehru government began introducing Hindi on **railway station signboards**.

This triggered the **Defacing Hindi Campaign**.

- Annadurai defaced the Hindi lettering at **Erode Junction**

- EVR did so at **Trichy Junction**

- Karunanidhi defaced Hindi signage at the **Trichy post office**

Nehru dismissed the campaign as: “Childish nonsense”, “Extreme immaturity”, “No greater folly” to which Annadurai responded calmly: *“Let us forgive and forget (Nehru’s comments)”*

### The Long Phase

The agitation continued in various forms. In **1955**, the **Official Language Commission** was set up to review the progressive replacement of English by Hindi—reigniting fears of imposition.

Finally, on **7 August 1959**, Nehru assured in Parliament that:

- English would continue indefinitely

- Until non-Hindi-speaking people desired otherwise

The DMK withdrew agitation.

**Three phases. Three victories.**

## Phase 4: Violence, Betrayal, and Settlement (1964–1967)

The final phase was the most violent. This is also the period where Parasakthi film is set. This is where the tables turned from 1938. With Nehru dead and the **1965 deadline** approaching, Hindi was set to become the sole official language on the Republic Day of 1965, which DMK called as a black day!

Rajaji, the Hindi-imposer from 1938, had started supporting DMK in its anti-Hindi agitations by 1965. He famously remarked that the constitutional provision on Hindi should be ‘thrown into the sea'. He argued that the language war was between Hindi vs English, and not Hindi vs Tamil.  
  
Rajaji wrote in his magazine Swarajya that such laws only transform the 3 crore patriotic non-Hindi speaking Indian citizens into separatists. He even inaugurated anti-Hindi imposition conference led by PT Rajan!

EVR was pro-Congress and anti-DMK now (more so after Rajaji supported DMK). HE said that teardrops DMK are using a dead snake (Hindi imposition) to come to power.  
Imagine the prime face of anti-Hindi agit-prop EVR calling for protesting students to be actioned against, and even suggesting people to learn Hindi! This is a good moment to pause and introspect if the leaders you admire (be it EVR/Rajaji) truly embody “causes”, or are just playing their own selfish political games.

Unaware of power plays, in **1964**, a passionate anti-Hindi protesting common man **Chinnasamy’s self-immolation** ignited widespread protests in TN:

- College Students revolted

- DMK supporting students were snitched to the police by Congress
  supporting students

- Police brutality was unleashed - lathi charge, tear gas and firing
  followed

- At least **25 people died across seven locations**

The political outcome was decisive: **DMK swept to power in 1967,** becoming the first non-Congress party to win a state election with single majority.

The fourth phase is considered an ongoing one, with eternal vigilance against imposition.

## What Was This Anti-Hindi movement Really About?

### Takeaway 1: Linguistic Respect, Not Linguistic Supremacy

Tamils take pride in Tamil as an ancient classical language.  
But resisting Hindi defaulting is about **respect for diversity**, not hostility.

MKG’s ideas on English may have made sense back then, but is no longer relevant. Retaining English as a link language is no longer colonial - it is pragmatic, at least institutionally, within the educated circles and formalized workforce.

North Indians defaulting to Hindi in a group setting is not okay, as it is disrespectful to the linguistic diversity of our glorious nation.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Takeaway 2: This Was Not Merely Anti-Imposition

This movement was **not just anti-Hindi imposition**.  
It was often **explicitly anti-Hindi**, and at times **anti-North Indian**.

Examples abound:

- EVR calling Hindi a “cunning language”

- Bharathidasan calling it a harmful language

- Annadurai’s mockingly said Anna said “If we were to ask, ‘What grammar
  is there in Hindi? What is its literary flourish?’ after much effort,
  they would show us two epics - Tulsidas’s Ramayana and the All-India
  Railway Guide. They offer these two as the best literary works in
  their language.”

The hostility started by this movement continues to leave a bitter aftertaste in TN today in xenophobic slurs like “Vadakkan” (Tamil equivalent of Madrasi to denote a Northie), stereotyping (Pani Puri Sellers, Breeders like pigs), and even violence against migrants. The secessionist nature of this movement must not be forgotten.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Takeaway 3: Pro-Tamil ≠ Anti-Hindi

EVR himself urged people to oppose Hindi while learning English—not to protect Tamil, because he saw little worth protecting in Tamil anyways.

The antidote Poets like **Namakkal Ramalingam Pillai** argued the opposite:

> “Oh what can other languages do to our Tamil? Our grammarians were
> magnanimous enough to accommodate words from all languages. Those who
> oppose Hindi know not of the glory of Tamilnadu”

The idea that one must oppose Hindi to be pro-Tamil is merely a **Dravidian political compulsion**, not some moral obligation based on truth.

### Takeaway 4: English Benefited More Than Tamil

Ironically, the movement strengthened English more than Tamil. Even recently, in Class 10 TN board exams, 31,000 students have passed in English but failed in Tamil/mother tongue.

The average Tamil today takes pride (not shame) in not knowing to read or write in Tamil. Most Tamils cannot name a single Sangam Lit poet other than Thiruvalluvar. Most Tamils cannot write Tamil properly without spelling errors. தமிழ் இனி மெல்லச் சாகும், said an imaginary British police to Bharathi. Times are revealing he may perhaps be right.

Most CBSE/ICSE schools run by Dstock politicians teach Hindi. The rich have no problem in learning the most widely spoken language of India. The poor are denied access to Hindi in the name of “protecting” Tamil, while elites signal virtue with “Hindi Theriyadhu Poda” slogans. Elites who frown upon “Hindu khatre mein hai” narrative would happily buy into the “Tamil Hindi se khatre mein hai” narrative.

## TLDR; Conclusion

Do not default to Hindi by default in a social setting.  
Respect the linguistic diversity of this glorious nation.

We need unity, not uniformity, humanity not homogeneity.  
Use English as a link language.  
Learn as many languages as you wish.

Do not yield to political provocation.

**Pro-Tamil need not mean Anti-Hindi.**

*Vaazhga Tamil. Jai Hind.*
