#### **Meet Ramya, the Bad Girl**

The director Varsha Bharath had said she made Bad Girl to push back against the pressure on Tamil women to be pure, chaste, devoted, “godly” even. That pressure is real. Women in Tamil cinema have historically been flattened into archetypes: the self-sacrificing mother, the devoted wife (Jillunu oru Kaadhal), the fallen woman who must be redeemed (Maharaja) or punished (Padayappa, Mannan etc). These are typical male-centric films that portray women in a specific way to eventually make the men appear good. So when “Bad Girl” wants to tell the story of a flawed woman, from a female POV, it does make us sit up and watch.

> Even in the opening scenes, Ramya, the titular “Bad Girl” is scolded
> by her “PSBB-ish” school teacher that she has failed in MPC subjects
> “like a boy”. We have moved on from the era were women fought for the
> freedom to ***study*** in school like men. Now its about women
> fighting for the freedom to ***fail*** in school like men.

The film goes on to chronicle her story spanning roughly sixteen years, from Class 9 to her early thirties. And her defining trait/flaw is that she thinks of having a “boyfriend” as a proxy for winning in life. Or rather, those without BFs appear like uncool losers in her eyes.

Which is why, as early as Class 9 (age 14), she desperately gets committed to a guy Sidhu who wasn’t even originally interested in her. She broke up with him after she overheard him calling her an “item”. Breaking up with this bad boy was good for her self-respect. *Does Ramya learn from this though*? .

Cut to Class 11. A “bad boy” named Nalan Thanigachalam who was expelled from his previous school for doing pot, joins this “PSBB-ish” school. Despite warning from other girls, Ramya falls for Nalan’s veins, and gets into a secret relationship with him. This is now a pattern - does Ramya have a thing for “bad boys”?

> In a school excursion to Ooty, the duo steal private time together and
> Ramya wonders: how can something that feels this good be called bad by
> society? This is the film's central philosophical question.

The film never answers this question honestly, but I will. Wait for it.

Btw, Ramya’s mom Sundari is also a teacher in the same school. After a couple rat them out, Ramya-Nalan are grounded in their respective homes with no cell phones. Ramya is determined to elope with Nalan, and even waits opposite his house one night with packed bags. But the selfish dude doesn’t show up, breaking Ramya’s heat. Second betrayal. *Does Ramya learn from this though?*

Cut to a “Queen Mary-ish” college, Ramya is practically “living together“ in men’s hostel (despite being a Chennai resident) with her boyfriend Arjun Konda. He is called the most toxic man on campus by other women, but Ramya thinks she is lucky to be the girlfriend of the most “desirable” man on campus. She buys him thoughtful gifts, but he just trashes them. To Ramya, Arjun’s her world, but to him, she's just a side-chick, meant to be used. Weird that every sane person on campus realizes this, except for Ramya who's starstruck with Arjun. She realizes he has been cheating on her, so gets drunk, and “beats him up” on his graduation day in front of his family and friends, like a *female Arjun Reddy*. Why should boys have all the toxicity fun, right? *Does Ramya learn from this though?*

Cut to Ramya at 32 now. She works at some interior design company for bare minimum pay, and spends all her money on cigars and rent and a cat, which is in her recently broken up BF Irfan’s house. They lived together but “adulting” issues like leaving toilet seat on, grooming etc. ensured incompatibility and Ramya is now on the lookout for a new house. She is a proper single crazy cat lady now.

> The film does not judge her at all. But that’s the problem isn’t it?
> It should have judged her.

#### What’s wrong with Ramya?

Bad Girl is a film about a narcissist. Even her friends call it out. Her self-centered nature even when talking to her helpful pregnant friend is pukeworthy. Even critics sympathetic to the film acknowledge its portrait of a deeply self-involved woman.

But every time consequences loom, the film redirects blame outward.

- Her mother disciplines her after the school incident but she is
  portrayed as the **villain** of that chapter. Ramya threatens her mom
  (who had been nothing but kind and thoughtful to her) to off herself.

#### **The Two Questions the Film Never Asks**

If Bad Girl were truly a grown-up film about a flawed young woman, it would eventually arrive at two questions:

1.  **At what point does a recurring pattern of choices stop being
    “society’s fault” and start being yours?**

2.  **What is freedom, if it never demands responsibility?**

Ramya at thirty-two is not a liberated woman. She is a woman who has:

- Confused the **absence of structure** with the **presence of freedom**

- Paid for that confusion with the hurt of her family and friends

A truly radical ending would have looked like this:

- Ramya tells her mother: **“I am sorry.”**

- Not “I was wrong to desire”, but “I hurt you; I lied to you; I chose
  badly and blamed you; I see it now.”

Instead, we get a soft landing: an “I love you” without an “I’m sorry”. Even this, we’re told, felt “too commercial” or “too cringe” for the director Varsha, and had to loop it in only as a compromise. One can only imagine how uber-woke the original Varsha’s script might have been.

#### What Dravidam is this?

From the opening frame, the community target is unambiguous. Dronacharya Gurukulam. Sri Ramajayam on every board. A conservative Iyengar grandmother. An orthodox Brahmin family that hates ICM (As if other families are welcoming of ICM). From the word go, the film tries to drill these thoughts into the viewer’s heads:

> Your family is your cage. Your school is your prison. Your tradition
> is an iron chain you must break free from. Your community is
> “oppressing” you.

It is worth understanding the ideological lineage here. EVR, whose name appears on a wall (as Ee Ve Ra) at the exact moment Ramya "breaks free," held a particular view of women's liberation: that the institution of marriage was a patriarchal tool to enslave women, that fidelity (Karpu) was an Aryan import used to control Tamil women, and that women should not bear children as it would give them true freedom. “Enjoyment without responsibility” was EVR’s clarion call. The fact that EVR had a traditional housewife in Nagammai, and a 2nd wife Maniyammai who never remarried after his demise, goes on to show how leaders live wildly different lives than the ones they profess for their followers. EVR himself admitted that he “lost a slave” and “implemented nothing he preached” when his first wife passed away.

Across these films, the template is recognisable:

> **Take the Tamil Brahmin woman. Show her community’s men, family and
> institutions as her captors. Offer the Dravidian worldview as the door
> out of this cage.**

This is not a conspiracy. It is a **long-term cultural project**.

Its strategy is wise: don’t attack the community from the outside; **reshape how its own young women see their inheritance from the inside**. Ideological change runs faster when the **daughter** feels she must escape her **mother** to become herself. And with Brahmin women slowly taken out of the fold, the community fades into oblivion, slowly but surely.

#### **The Family Is Not the Enemy**

You will find the liberal types write about “born families” and “found families” to distinguish between a bad vs good family. It is common in leftist circles to bond over sharing stories of family trauma and regressive parents. Even in “Bad Girl” climax, Ramya lives alone with her cat, having shed her birth family's expectations, as a kind of hard-won sanctuary and as a “happy ending”.

Let us say this plainly, because the film will not: the family is not a conspiracy against women. It is the oldest and most durable institution of human civilisation, and it has survived because it serves needs that no substitute has yet successfully replaced: for children, for the elderly, for the adults within it who need both roots and a reason to grow beyond themselves. Cats do not require *you* to grow. But human babies do. It means you need a stable household to raise a baby. Cats are the perfect companions for someone who has not yet developed the capacity for the kind of love that **costs** something.

Marriage as an institution tamed the male and provided protection for the female in a fundamental way. Women who are uprooted from their families, values, roots become vulnerable and are easier to exploit for the predators. Plenty of credible scientists have spoken to this.

Yes, parents will be uncool conservatives who'd boringly quote flight prices and not say "Inga Irukku da America" like how a cool parent's supposed to be. But a parent would never lead their kids down the vicious path. Society would because it doesn't care about your wellbeing, it only cares about what it can get from you in the name of "progress". You're naive if you live for society's approval, instead of your parents or your own value systems. Fret not, society intends to show you how little it cares about you, very soon.

#### A friendly a̶d̶v̶i̶c̶e̶ chat from a ‘Boomer’

I want to speak directly here, not as a critic but as someone who has been through that fire. 16 to 24 is the most precarious phase of any person’s life. You are not quite an adult but you are convinced you are. Your parents feel like villains, your teachers feel like obstacles, your faith feels like a cage. Everyone your age seems to be doing things you’re not doing, and the FOMO is real and painful. That is not weakness. That is biology. What most people don’t tell you is that you are, in those years, almost entirely at the mercy of your hormone, and the rebellion you feel is more often than not not your deepest self speaking. It is a beta version of you, still in testing, still with bugs to fix. And everybody likes an alpha, not a beta!

After 25, an entire world outside of school/college circle opens up. The real world couldn't care less about your popularity or coolness quotient or your Instagram likes. The rules change in a jiffy. That close friend from college becomes a pixel on your feed. That introverted loser at the library who used to skip parties suddenly becomes the most sought-after groom/bride on matrimony sites. Stable and boring day jobs become the new cool thing. The world, my friend, changes its rules faster than you change partners in college.

You'll change too. Suddenly, Stack Overflow will seem more interesting than Snapchat. You’ll spend more time refreshing NIFTY than refreshing Instagram. You’ll find your old Taylor Swift playlist slightly cringe, and start worshipping Ilaiyaraaja. You'll enjoy Thirukural more than Twilight. You'll realize that teenage love was just a fickle feeling and rediscover that true love is a verb, not noun. Sounds unbelievable now, I know. Don’t take my word, even EVR said so: “Love is a casual, cheap thing. Who can NOT fall in love after seeing one another? To be enslaved to love is not apt.”

The only thing I’d ask you to trust is in the infinite potential of your future self. Do not take your beta version personality so seriously. The person you’re becoming is far richer than the “bad” boy/girl you see in the mirror today. Put on the developer hat, as patch updates to your personality are coming up every year. Hang in there, my friend. Life after 25 is no easy ride, but its calmer, kinder, and infinitely wiser and trust me, that’s the kind of cool you’ll never outgrow. In the world of Bad Girl, the baddest of all bad words is **responsibility**.

#### Conclusion

It is Dravidawood’s choice to willingly tell the stories of how innocent Brahmin women trapped in traditional cages of family and marriage and patriarchy break free to find the EVR sanctuary where egalitarian humans live with enjoyment but without responsibility. Ofcourse, this is only a fantasy being sold. Ask the many EVR-ists who’ve whistleblown about the sexual exploitations in such circles.

The West has experimented with sexual liberation and realized that feelings are fickle and the meaning of life is not to be found on this hedonistic treadmill. They are trying to course correct with Eastern wisdom, but some of us would still like to go the hedonistic way. Whether you want to compete on rising up the virtue ladder or descending down the vice paatala staircase, you are free to choose. Pro-choice!

A film like Bad Girl asks a central question: “how can something that feels this good be called bad by society?” Because vices always feel good, and virtues always feel bad, in the short term. In the long run, there are costs to vices which will never be shown on cinema. Films like bad girl will only ever show you the cool liberation in the pursuit of vices. The cost is edited out. The editing is the ideology.

Say whatever you want to sound cool and hip and progressive in your public life, but have the wisdom to be uncool, boring and regressive in your private life and choose the path of virtue. It is your choice at the end of the day, **just don’t let your life become someone’s social justice project.**
