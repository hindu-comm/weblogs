Kaala(2018), featuring Superstar Rajinikanth as hero, was the passionate pet project of director Pa.Ranjith. On the surface, Kaala appears to be a story about land rights - specifically the rights of Dharavi's slum dwellers to resist a redevelopment project by a powerful builder-politician nexus. This is an important theme as Asia's largest slum sits on costly real estate that the vultures have been eyeing for decades. The residents who live there have real grievances. A film championing their cause should, in principle, be welcome.

- **The True Interest:** The film is primarily a manifesto for a
  specific brand of identity politics.

- **The “Dravidawood” template:** Kaala exemplies how modern Tamil
  cinema should “redevelop” its “morality”- specifically, which symbols
  are holy/unholy and which communities are default villains.

Let’s dive deep.

### **Dravidawood’s template of Good and Evil**

In Ranjith’s world, a character’s name isn't just a label; it’s a moral verdict delivered before they speak a single word. These are meticulously chosen for ideological reasons.

**The villains**

Perumalsami: the betraying slum dweller. Pankaj **Patel**: the brutal police enforcer. Vishnu **Pai**: the greedy politician. Haridev **Abhyankar**: the megalomaniacal primary antagonist. Carefully chosen first and last names.

**The heroes**

Kaala. Zareena. Lenin (Kaala’s youngest son), explicitly hailed as a revolutionary by the superstar Kaala himself. *Sidenote*: Estimates of deaths during Vladimir Lenin's regime (1917–1924) range from 4 million to over 10 million through famine, civil war, and “Red Terror” campaign.

### **Dravidawood’s political map**

Ranjith doesn’t stop at names. He constructs an entire political geography around his ideological commitments.

The antagonist’s party is the Navbharat National Party (a proxy for the BJP) that has won all of Maharashtra except Dharavi. Dharavi is their “final frontier”. Haridev Abhyankar addresses his followers through a programme called “Dil Ki Awaaz,” a proxy for “Mann Ki Baat.” Right from his introduction, Hari is framed as a thinly veiled Modi (or Thackeray) surrogate, a Hindu nationalist who promotes cleanliness and digital connectivity, presented as villainous without ambiguity or nuance from frame one.

Villains are from NNP, a “Sanghi” party

The builder company planning to acquire the slum dwellers’ land is named “Manu Realty.” Their redevelopment plan involves renaming Dharavi as “Dandakaranya Nagar,” a reference drawn from the Ramayana’s forest. Both choices deploy Hindu tradition as a marker of oppression. This is deliberate framing, not incidental detail.

Villain Hari in all whites, standing for Manu Realty and Dandakaranya Nagar

The opposition, the righteous side: is the Oppressed People Republic Party. Their flag is the Indian tricolor with the saffron replaced by blue. Replacing saffron with blue is a political statement. The film is telling you which India it endorses, and it is not the one that includes that colour.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!EKgw!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F35da8214-d5aa-4d5a-99c7-7eda43b2f316_1199x537.jpeg)

Note the Oppressed People Republic Party’s poster

There is also a pointed irony in the film’s portrayal of Maharashtrians as an oppressive native community hostile to Tamil migrants in Mumbai. In reality, Mumbai has largely been welcoming to South Indians, including Tamils, barring some early Shiv Sena-era agitation that has since largely receded. More tellingly, the legislator representing Sion-Koliwada (the constituency that borders Dharavi) is himself a native of Pudukottai, Tamil Nadu. The oppression narrative the film leans on is considerably more complicated than Ranjith allows. In contrast, the largely welcoming state of Tamilnadu is now poisoned by vested interests who are hostile to North Indian laborers seeking a livelihood in TN, resulting in namecalling, dehumanizing and even public hacking. Politicians (you know who) openly denigrate them as Vadakkans, Pani Puri sellers, breeders like pigs etc. in their public speeches.

### **What is sacred and sacrilege in Dravidawood**

Kaala’s home is filled with Buddha idols. He meets his aides at Bhagavan Gautam Buddha Vihar. He offers dua at the local mosque. The film establishes him firmly as a man of spiritual range: open, inclusive, respectful of traditions beyond his own. Ranjith even seeds visual *Buddha Purnima eggs* throughout: Ambedkar’s portrait, Buddha’s image deliberately contrasted with a statue of Ram in an upper-caste household, the colour blue running through every frame of Kaala’s world: his wardrobe is only black or blue.

The villain’s spiritual life is rendered very differently. Hari Abhyankar is a devotee of Ram and Krishna. His home has the idol, the atmosphere of devotion, the rituals and in this same space, he conducts himself as a feudal patriarch: some guests stand, others sit on the floor, and everyone must touch his feet before leaving. He judges single mothers. He controls through hierarchy. The devotion and the domination share the same frame, deliberately. In one particularly telling scene, Hari refuses to touch the water in Kaala’s home: the untouchability instinct, externalised, made visual.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!eetH!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F467bcda7-a6a9-41f9-94d6-c0f9094ac57e_1200x503.jpeg)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!eetH!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F467bcda7-a6a9-41f9-94d6-c0f9094ac57e_1200x503.jpeg)

Ranjith also takes aim at the practice of charan sparsh (touching the feet of elders) as a marker of Hari’s authoritarian social order. But charan sparsh is not a Hindu practice in the narrow, sectarian sense Ranjith implies. It is a gesture of reverence found across traditions. In Theravadin Buddhism (practiced in Sri Lanka, Thailand, and Myanmar) prostration before one’s teacher, accompanied by the Pali invocation “Okāsa ahaṃ bhante vandāmi,” is a foundational act of receiving instruction. If Ranjith considers prostration inherently degrading, is he also making that argument about Theravadin Buddhism?

One more detail the film takes care to establish: Kaala’s wife openly states she would feed beef to Rajini, while looking down upon his son for being vegetarian. Meanwhile, halal dietary practice is treated with quiet respect. The hierarchy of whose food customs deserve dignity is quietly but clearly drawn.

### **Ravana Kanging**

Ranjith’s most ambitious move is to deliberately invert the Ramayana and glorify Ravana by identifying his hero with him. A copy of Raavana Kaaviyam, the Tamil literary work that rehabilitates Ravana as a tragic hero, sits on his desk. He is addressed as the leader of the Asura clan. The film’s hammer-and-sickle imagery appears in the animation flashback, mapping Marxist symbology onto this mythological framework without apology.

The climax brings it all together. It is intercut (inspired by the baptism sequence in The Godfather) with a Ramayana discourse at Hari Abhyankar’s home, while his men massacre the slum dwellers of Dharavi. Each death is framed as one of Ravana’s heads falling, only to be replaced by another rising.

The problem is that Ranjith is so in love with what he wants to say that he stops caring about how he says it. The Hindu epic is conscripted wholesale into a political argument: Ram reduced to a symbol for a political party, Ravana elevated as the symbol of electoral resistance, without the intellectual rigour that such an inversion demands.

The death of a conventional film villain is usually intercut with the slaying of a cinematic demon. But Ranjith attempts something more interesting here: he intercuts violence with the immersion of Ganpati, using a god's departure rather than a demon's defeat as his counterpoint.

### EVR kanging

Among the details of Ranjith's Dharavi set is a EVR chowk, complete with statue. Ranjith's reverence for EVR alongside Ambedkar is standard in Dravidian political culture. Anyone who has read EVR’s works know his stance with regards to the downtrodden, along with EVR’s view of Ambedkar questioning his integrity, years after his passing. Who EVR supported post the Kilvenmani massacre that killed Dalit farmers is also well known. Ranjith’s political education, the film suggests, has been more devoted to the iconography of these figures than to the full complexity of their histories. He has built a secular shrine for EVR-Ambedkar-Buddha-Ravana-Lenin in this film. Shrines don’t require you to read the difficult chapters.

### The Romance of Squalor

Strip away the ideology and what remains is a film with a real problem at its core: it has nothing constructive to say about Dharavi.

- **The Complexity Ignored:** Dharavi has a population density of **2.7
  lakh per sq km** and houses 20,000+ businesses. Redevelopment is a
  logistical and human nightmare that requires nuanced solutions.

- **The Wrong Villain:** The film blames the *idea* of development, but
  the real enemy is **corruption in housing allotment**. Ironically, the
  “digitization” the film mocks is one of the few tools that could
  actually stop middlemen from stealing units meant for the poor. Mumbai
  has seen older housing societies (four-story buildings with 250 sq ft
  apartments) successfully redeveloped into high-rises where each
  original resident received two larger apartments in return, with
  landscaped gardens and parking. Comparable transformations have
  happened in cities like Hong Kong.

- **Romanticizing Resistance:** By framing “resistance” as the only
  goal, the film offers no vision for a better future, effectively
  romanticizing squalor rather than solving for dignity. If Dharavi is
  solved, that would render “Inquilab” irrelevant isn’t it? Easy to
  guess whose interests the film is protecting.

  ### **The Closing Image**

  The film ends with a powerful closing image: a blue flag with the
  Buddhist Dharmachakra hoisted on top of a asbestos hut, even as the
  Indian tricolor is relegated to the sidelines, the flag flying above
  the tall skyscrapers in the back.

  It is a moment of total honesty: the film isn’t about land rights or
  anti-displacement: it’s about the displacement of national identity by
  a specific ideological one. Everything the film tried boils down to
  this final image.

  The film flopped. Ranjith blamed scheming casteists. The more honest
  explanation is simpler: a film buckled under the weight of its own
  agenda did not meet general audience’s normal screenviewing
  expectations.

  Kaala is a compromised film because its director was more interested
  in making a manifesto than a movie. That he used Dharavi and Superstar
  as his Trojan horse to carry his ideology has left an indelible Kaala
  mark on Rajinikanth, the nationalist.
