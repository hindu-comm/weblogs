Vishakapatnam has a fairly large Odiya population, most of them working in the Railways, Steel Plant and PSUs here. You’ll find Odia spoken in pockets of Vizag, especially in areas like Gajuwaka, Steel Plant, Railway Quarters. Festivals like Rath Yatra are celebrated with fervor, echoing Puri’s grandeur.

The city was part of the southern expanse of the Kalinga empire, which stretched from coastal Odisha into northern Andhra. This makes Vizag not just a neighbor, but a cultural sibling.Vizag’s coast was a gateway for ancient trade with Southeast Asia, much like Odisha’s ports. Historical sites like Sankaram, Thotlakonda, Pavurallakonda, Bavikonda near the city show the spread of Buddhist influence here.

If you have been to Andhra University College of Science, you would have seen this statue right in front of it. This is of Maharaja Vikram Deo Varma, the ruler of the princely state of Jeypore in Odisha. He was a polyglot fluent in **English, Odia, Telugu, and Sanskrit, wrote plays, poems, and essays**, earning the title *Sahitya Samrat* and an honorary D.Litt from Andhra Bharati Tirtha Research University.

In the 1930s, **Andhra University faced existential uncertainty**, with serious proposals to shift it to Guntur or Vijayawada due to lack of funds and infrastructure. Vikram Deo Varma stepped in with **a donation of ₹20 lakhs**, **land grants**, and a **recurring annual contribution of ₹1 lakh for 17 years**—a staggering commitment that sustained the university through its formative decades.

As **Pro-Chancellor**, he didn’t just fund the institution—he shaped its soul. He brought in **C.R. Reddy**, a visionary educationist, and **Dr. S. Radhakrishnan**, philosopher-statesman and future President of India, as Vice Chancellors.This wasn’t patronage—it was **curation of intellectual excellence**, placing Andhra University on the national map.

Built between **1917–1923** by **Maharaja Ram Chandra Dev IV of Jeypore**, Hawa Mahal on the Beach Road was designed as a **summer retreat and transit residence** for the royal family during their travels to Madras.The land was originally owned by the Raja of Salur and was carefully leveled to create a sea-facing estate above tide level.

Architect **Muppidi Venkat Rao**, who also designed Andhra University’s TLN Sabha Hall and other Vizag landmarks, gave it its distinctive **Indo-Gothic style**. In **1961**, **Jawaharlal Nehru** stayed at Hawa Mahal during his visit to Vizag to inaugurate **Jala Usha**, the **first ship built by Hindustan Shipyard.**

The royal family also built the Panduranga Temple near the Beach Road in 1971, that as consecrated by **Sant Kesav Das Ji**, a devotee of Panduranga Vithal, after a visionary dream of the deity dancing on Vizag’s shore.

The land was donated by the **Jeypore royal family**, specifically Maharaja Vikram Deo Varma’s daughter, who recognized the sanctity of the site. The temple houses **Lord Panduranga and Rukmini**, and later expanded to include shrines for **Shyam Baba, Prasanna Durga, and Kasi Visweswara**, becoming a vibrant **Smartha-Vaishnava fusion**.

Also the f**irst Krishna Chaitanya Math established in Andhra Pradesh**, consecrated in the **late 1960s** near Zilla Parishad junction. The land was donated by **Mani Deo**, daughter of Maharaja Vikram Deo Varma, and the Jeypore family funded much of its construction.

The Math introduced **early morning bhajans**, a novelty that drew locals into devotional rhythm, and later added a **Goshala** and a **Puja Mandir**. It became a hub for **Vaishnava devotion**, supported by Vizag’s Marwari and local communities, and still thrives today as a spiritual sanctuary.

The city has it's own Taj Mahal, the Samadhi of the Kurupam Maharani, Lakshmi Narasayamma Patta Mahadev, who passed away at a young age in 1901, during childbirth, along with her twins—a tragedy that shook the royal household.

Her husband, **Rajah Vairicherla Veerabhadra Raju**, the Zamindar of Kurupam, built a **mausoleum in Waltair** as a tribute to her memory. It’s known variously as the **Kurupam Tomb**, *Jnana Vilas*, *Prema Nivedana Rupam*, and most poetically, *Vizag’s Taj Mahal.*

The monument blends **Mughal, Rajasthani, and European architecture**, with **Bhagavad Gita shlokas in Telugu script** inside, and English inscriptions outside—one reads: *“Here lies the body of my dearest Lakshmi and the heart of Virbhadra Raju.”* A **marble statue** of the Maharani was commissioned from French sculptor Charles Desvergnes, though it was never installed. A model of it survives in a museum in France.

In **1914**, to commemorate **King Edward VII’s coronation**, the Rajah of Kurupam donated land and built Vizag’s first major market—**Kurupam Market**. It featured a **Tudor-style arch with a clock** sourced from the prestigious Gillett & Johnston of England, echoing the grandeur of Manchester and New York’s towers.Though the original structure has been lost to time, the market remains a bustling hub, with echoes of its regal past in the goldsmith lanes and Deepavali firework shops.

**Kurupam**, though in Andhra Pradesh, lies **right near the Odisha border**, and its cultural affiliations—language, architecture, and lineage—reflect a **hybrid legacy**.The Kurupam family’s ties to **Vizianagaram, Daspalla, Gangpur, and Chemudu** royal houses further blur the lines between Andhra and Odisha, making Vizag a true **civilizational confluence.**

The Jagannatha temple is a **spiritual and cultural beacon**, hosting a very grand **Rath Yatra** annually. The **Bahuda Jatra** procession winds through Vizag’s streets with chariots, drums, and thousands of devotees.

The **Chhera Pahanra** ritual—where the chariot is ceremonially swept—is performed by **members of the Daspalla royal family**, preserving centuries-old tradition. The temple’s location atop Daspalla Hills isn’t coincidental—it was **built on land owned by the Daspalla princely state**, making the entire neighborhood a royal endowment.

The **Daspalla Hotel**, a landmark near Jagadamba Junction, stands on **land originally owned by the Daspalla royals**. Though the hotel was later developed by entrepreneur M. Raghavendra Rao in the 1980s, the name honors the land’s regal provenance.

**Daspalla Chitralaya**, a popular cinema theater now a multiplex, also stood on **royal land**, serving as a cultural bridge between tradition and modernity.

Telugu-speaking **Patnaiks, Patros, and Mishras** with ancestral ties to Odisha have become part of Vizag’s social fabric, blending linguistic and devotional traditions.

The **Varaha Lakshmi Narasimha Temple** at Simhachalam was built in **1268 CE** by **Narasingha Deva I** of the **Eastern Ganga dynasty**, and consecrated by his son **Bhanudeva I**.

The temple’s **Kalinga architecture**—with its curvilinear shikhara, intricate carvings, and lion motifs—mirrors the style of Odisha’s great temples like **Lingaraj and Konark**. The **Gajapati rulers**, successors to the Eastern Gangas, continued patronage, embedding Odisha’s spiritual legacy into Vizag’s landscape.
