How often do you find an ancient fort, a modern thermal power plant, an industrial area, a cluster of toy makers and a reserve forest all within the same vicinity? Kondapalli, a suburb of Vijayawada is famous for it’s fort, the toys made here, as well as the Thermal power plant and a reserve forest.

</div>

The main attraction in Kondapalli is it’s Fort, built by Prolaya Vema Reddy during the 14th century CE, though as per some other historians it’s believed to have been built by Anna Vema Reddy, after he recaptured it from the Mudigonda Chalukyas. The fort passed through many kingdoms from the Reddy Rajulu to the Gajapatis to Vijayanagara to the Nizam and finally the East India Company.

<div class="captioned-image-container">

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!V1Di!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa2be5bbb-fff0-4e96-802f-9442bce13370_400x285.jpeg "undefined")

</div>

The fort itself is located on the Kondapalli hill range that runs for around 24 km between Vijayawada and Nandigama, forming an ecosystem, that includes the Indrakeeladri Hill and Gandhi Hill near the city. This also contains the Kondapalli Reserve Forest famous for a kind of light weight wood called as Ponuku, used in manufacture of the famed Kondapalli toys.

It also contains many medicinal plants like Nela Usiriki Chettu( Gooseberry trees), Adavi Mirapa( Wild Chillies), while geologically it contains rocks like charnockites, granites, dolerites.

The reserve forest contains many wild animals like leopards, sambar deer, jackals, civet cats, wild boars, monkeys and peacocks.

<div class="captioned-image-container">

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!XnqD!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd57dcd8c-d33b-4e3c-a5df-b7d8ebe3adc6_800x531.jpeg "undefined")

</div>

After the fort came under the Gajapatis , a fierce succession conflict erupted between Hamvira Deva and his younger brother Purushottama Deva. Hamvira, though the elder and a seasoned warrior, was bypassed for the throne.

In retaliation, he allied with the Bahmani Sultanate and seized power in 1472, ceding Kondapalli and Rajahmundry to the Bahmanis as part of the deal. But the tide turned in 1476 when Purushottama regrouped and defeated Hamvira, reclaiming the throne and the lost territories.

That same year, a famine in the Bahmani kingdom triggered unrest. The garrison at Kondapalli revolted, reportedly handing the fort back to Hamvira — referred to as “Hamer Oriya” in some chronicles.

Purushottama Deva once he became the king, tried to get back Kondapalli and Rajahmundry from the Bahmani Sultan III. But when he held siege over Rajamundry, for some unknown reason he signed a peace treaty with the Sultan, which resulted in souring of relations between Bahmani and Vijayanagara rulers.

Indeed, after the uneasy peace treaty with Bahmani Sultan III, Gajapati Purushottama Deva waited for the right moment. That came in 1481, when Sultan Mahammad’s death plunged the Bahmani Sultanate into chaos. Seizing the opportunity, Purushottama launched a decisive campaign against Mahmad Shah, the Sultan’s son, and successfully reclaimed Rajahmundry and Kondapalli Fort.

This victory not only restored key strategic territories but also reaffirmed the Gajapati dynasty’s resilience. Purushottama’s reign ended in 1497, and he was succeeded by his son Gajapati Prataprudra Deva, whose rule would face even more intense military challenges — from Krishnadevaraya of Vijayanagara, Hussain Shah of Bengal, and the Qutb Shahi dynasty of Golconda.

In 1509, Gajapati Prataprudra Deva started a war against Sri Krishnadevaraya , but he had to retreat to the north to defend an attack by Sultan Allauddin Hussan Shah of Bengal, due to which Rayalavaaru was able to easily occupy Kondapalli in 1515.

And finally the siege of Kondavidu in 1519, where Rayalavaaru, broke through the fort’s formidable defenses after a gruelling three months campaign. It wasnt just a military victory, the capture of Kondapalli and Kondavidu, gave Rayalavaru complete dominance over Coastal Andhra, as well as the East Coast ports.

He also married Jaganmohini, the daughter of Gajapati Prataprudra Deva, sealed peace between the two empires. And signed a treaty restoring lands up to the southern boundary of the Krishna river to the Gajapatis which included Kondapalli.

However after the treaty, the Gajapatis faced another formidable challenge between **1519 and 1525**, from **Sultan Quli Qutb-ul-Mulk**, the founder of the **Qutb Shahi dynasty of Golkonda**, as he launched invasions into the eastern territories. Despite resistance, the **final assault in 1531** brought **Kondapalli Fort under Golkonda’s control**.

<div class="captioned-image-container">

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zVor!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffff58546-99d3-444e-a1fc-df81f6bfade0_1503x929.png "Vijayawada diaries: Kondapalli Fort - a treasure house of medieval history  – Rashminotes")

</div>

Following **Gajapati Prataprudra Deva’s death in 1533**, his successor **Govinda Bidyadhar** continued the struggle. But recognizing the shifting power dynamics, he eventually **signed a treaty with the Sultan**, marking the end of Gajapati influence over Kondapalli.

<div class="captioned-image-container">

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!8Trp!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8fb4a8f4-7c5a-4372-aadf-49d5768d7cb0_1024x576.jpeg "undefined")

</div>

However after the **Mughals took control in the 17th century**, the fort became part of the Deccan’s shifting power dynamics. With the **decline of Mughal authority in the early 18th century**, **Nizam ul-Mulk**—founder of the **Asaf Jahi dynasty**—asserted independence and brought the region under **Hyderabad’s rule**.

Then came the pivotal moment in **1766**, when **Nizam Ali Khan** signed a **treaty of alliance** with the **British East India Company** which granted them control over certain territories, including Kondapalli.

In return, the Company agreed to **station troops at the fort** to aid the Nizam, at an annual cost of **£90,000**.That same year, **General Caillaud** reportedly **stormed Kondapalli Fort**, securing it for the British—marking the beginning of its use as a **military training base.**

Following the **1768 treaty**, the **Nizam of Hyderabad formally acknowledged the grant** made by **Mughal Emperor Shah Alam II** to the **British East India Company**, solidifying British claims over the region. In return, the Company extended a **friendship allowance of £50,000** to the Nizam—a diplomatic gesture that masked deeper strategic ambitions.

Then came the decisive moment in **1823**, when the East India Company **outright purchased the Northern Circars**—including Kondapalli—from the Nizam. This transaction marked the **end of native control** over the fort and surrounding territories.

From its origins as a **leisure retreat and business hub**, Kondapalli Fort had transformed into a **British military training base**, complete with barracks, an English cemetery, and strategic infrastructure.

<div class="captioned-image-container">

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!lWN1!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F799a140a-1059-453d-a15d-e057e0db82cd_555x417.jpeg "AP Heritage: Kondapalli Fort")

</div>

The fort, which has a very picturesque sight, has three successive entry gates.

The **Dargha Darwaja**, carved from a **single granite block**, stands as a solemn sentinel to history. Measuring **12 feet wide and 15 feet high**, it’s not just an entrance—it’s a memorial to **Gulab Shah**, whose tomb nearby lends the gate its name. His sacrifice in battle adds a poignant layer to the fort’s legacy.

On the opposite end, the **Golconda Darwaza** opens toward **Jagayyapeta village**, offering a strategic passage and a symbolic link to the fort’s later chapters under the Qutb Shahi dynasty. The **fortified walls**, with their **towers and battlements**, reflect centuries of defense strategy—from Reddi rulers to British garrisons.

At the far end of the fort there is the Tanisha Mahal or Palace, perched on a crest between two hills. This palace once served as a **command center and royal retreat**, with **multiple chambers on the ground floor**, likely used for administration, rest, and storage and a **grand hall on the upper floor**, possibly a durbar or ceremonial space, offering sweeping views of the surrounding hills.

Nearby, the **ruins of other structures**—granaries, barracks, and water tanks—still stand, weathered but proud. There’s even a **deep reservoir fed by a spring**, whose icy waters were once rumored to cause fever. And beyond that, an **old granary now inhabited by bats**, adding a touch of eerie charm to the fort’s mystique.

The **English barrack**, with its **eight spacious rooms** and an **annexed house**, stands as a stark reminder of the fort’s transformation into a **British military training base** after 1766. These quarters likely housed officers and troops stationed to support the Nizam under the East India Company’s treaty obligations.

Nearby, the **English cemetery** adds a solemn layer to the landscape. Though modest in scale, it holds the memorials of British personnel who lived—and died—within these rugged walls. Some records even mention it in 19th-century tomb listings from the Krishna District.

<div class="captioned-image-container">

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!qrq_!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F629e5679-631f-4ec9-9ea3-b00a70e32668_800x600.jpeg "undefined")

</div>

The famous Kondapalli toys are also made here at the Bommala Colony, registered as one of the GI mark handicrafts from Andhra Pradesh. They are manufactured by a community known as Aryakshatriyas who are said to have migrated from Rajasthan in 16th century and are mentioned in the *Brahmanda Purana.* They trace their lineage back to **Muktharishi**, a Rishi believed to be **blessed by Shiva** with divine mastery over arts and crafts.

The toys are made from soft wood known as *Tella Poniki* which are found in nearby Kondapalli Hills. Each toy part—head, limbs, trunk—is carved separately using chisels and then filed to smoothen edges.

Artisans use **makku**, a paste of tamarind seed powder and sawdust, to join parts and add fine details. The toys dried, sanded, and then painted using, **vegetable dyes** (like marigold and pomegranate), **oil and watercolors, enamel paints**, depending on the toy’s theme.

The **Dasavataram** set is the most popular , often featured in Navratri Golu and Bommala Koluvu displays. And the dancing dolls which move at the touch of a finger. As also depictions of rural life like bullock carts,village women, farmers, and animals like elephants and cows, while toys like Ambari elephants and village sets evoke festive and spiritual traditions.

Kondapalli is also an industrial hub with the Narla Tata Rao Thermal Power Station located here, as also Indian Railway’s second largest wagon workshop at Rayanapadu. HPCL, BPCL, GAIL, Indian Oil have their storage terminals here, as well as Reliance Industries and Lanco Infratech.

Heritage, industry, history, nature all meet together at Kondapalli a place worth visiting.
