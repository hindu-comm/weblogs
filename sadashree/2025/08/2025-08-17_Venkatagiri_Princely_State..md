Venkatagiri, located in the Tirupati district of Andhra Pradesh, was once a princely state under the Velugoti dynasty. Originally known as *Kalimili*, it rose to prominence in the 17th century when Recherla Venkatadri Naidu, a Nayak under the Vijayanagara Empire, established his rule there. The town later became the capital of the Venkatagiri Zamindari, which lasted until Indian independence.

</div>

The town is world-renowned for its **handloom cotton sarees**, known for their fine weave and elegant zari work. These sarees have earned a Geographical Indication (GI) tag, preserving their legacy as a symbol of Andhra’s textile heritage.

The **Venkatagiri estate** traces its origins to around **1208 CE**, founded by the formidable **Velugoti Recharla Bhetala Naidu**, also known as **Chevi Reddi**—a warrior of the **Recharla gothram** who earned high esteem under **Kakatiya ruler Ganapathi Deva.**

The Velugoti rulers, of the **Padmanayaka Velama** lineage, were originally military chiefs under the Kakatiyas and later Vijayanagara emperors. Over time, they evolved into **hereditary zamindars**, navigating shifting allegiances with the Mughals, Arcot Nawabs, and eventually the British.

</div>

Bhetala Naidu established his seat of power during the reign of **Ganapathi Deva Rai**, the grandfather of Prataparudra. He was rewarded with titles and land for his valor, and his capital was initially at **Velugodu** in present-day Kurnool before shifting to **Venkatagiri.**

After the **decline of the Vijayanagara Empire** post-Talikota (1565), many loyal Nayaka families reasserted their autonomy. Among them, **Velugoti Rayudappa Nayani** reestablished the **Venkatagiri kingdom in 1600**, anchoring it as a resilient polity in the Nellore region.

Yachama Naidu was one of the famous chiefs of this line during the late 16th and early 17th century. The son of of **Kasturi Ranga Nayaka**, a distinguished general of the Venkatagiri royal house, Yachama inherited a legacy of military excellence and political acumen.

He was a steadfast supporter of **Emperor Venkata II** and later **Sriranga II**, during a time of intense factionalism and imperial decline. He played a key role in **reclaiming territories from the Deccan Sultanates**, reinforcing imperial authority in contested regions. He also suppressed uprisings by the **Nayaks of Vellore and Madurai**, ensuring internal stability during a volatile period.

After Venkata II’s death, **Sriranga II** was imprisoned by the usurper **Jagga Raya** at **Vellore Fort**. Yachama Naidu orchestrated a daring rescue of **Sriranga’s young son, Rama Deva Raya**, smuggling him out disguised as a washerman’s child.

Though he couldn’t save Sriranga II and his family from eventual murder, his actions preserved the royal bloodline and set the stage for the **Battle of Toppur (1617)**—a decisive civil war that restored legitimacy to the throne.

Yachama’s leadership during the **Battle of Toppur** rallied loyalists and disillusioned nobles, leading to the defeat of Jagga Raya’s faction. He also seized the **Gobburi Estates** tht encompassed key coastal and inland regions: **Pulicat**, **Chengalpet**, and **Maduranthakam**—territories rich in trade, agriculture, and strategic access to the Coromandel Coast.

However, to ensure **centralized control**, these regions were later brought under the **direct administration of Vellore**, the imperial capital under **Rama Deva Raya**. With **Chandragiri** and **Penukonda** weakened, **Vellore** emerged as the new seat of power for the **Aravidu dynasty**.

By the **late 17th century**, the Venkatagiri rulers had transitioned from imperial Nayakas to **quasi-independent feudatories** under the **Nawab of Arcot**, who himself was a Mughal appointee in the Carnatic.

In **1695**, as a reward for their **military service to the Mughals**, the Velugoti family received a *sanad* (imperial charter) from the Mughal emperor—likely **Aurangzeb**—granting them **free jahir (tax-free tenure)** over **14 taluks** across four *sarcars* (administrative divisions).

These included taluks of Sarwepalli, Nellore, Rapur, Venkatagiri in Sarvepalli Circar, Sri Kalahasti,Satyaneru, Chenur, Gudur, Tirupati, Sagutur in Chandragiri Circar,Vishnukanchi, Karangudi, Mosaravaka in Kanchi Circar and Poonamallee in Thirupachur Circar.

These taluks were not just administrative units—they were **symbols of imperial trust**, **economic lifelines**, and **strategic footholds** across the eastern Deccan and Tamil country. The grant effectively **elevated the Venkatagiri rulers** into a **hereditary zamindari** with Mughal legitimacy, allowing them to collect revenue, maintain armed forces, and administer justice within their domains.

This *sanad* marked a shift from being **military vassals of Vijayanagara** to **landed aristocrats under Mughal suzerainty**, and later, **recognized zamindars under British rule**. It’s a fascinating example of how **regional dynasties adapted to imperial transitions** while preserving their core identity.

With the **annexation of Arcot by the British**, the Venkatagiri estate was formally absorbed into the **Madras Presidency**. It became a **revenue administrative zone**, with the **Venkatagiri Rajas** retaining titular authority as **zamindars** under the Permanent Settlement system.

With 736 villages and 600 odd hamlets, it was one of the **largest zamindaris** in the region, both in terms of land and socio-political influence.

The estate endured until **7 September 1949**, when it was formally abolished under the **Madras Estates (Abolition and Conversion into Ryotwari) Act, 1948**—a landmark reform that ended zamindari rule and redistributed land to cultivators.

A visionary and reformist, **Maharaja Sir Rajagopala Krishna Yachendra** was the **28th ruler** of Venkatagiri. He served as a **member of the Madras Legislative Council** from 1887 and was knighted with the **GCIE** and **Kaiser-i-Hind** honors.

He championed **education, infrastructure, and public welfare**, establishing schools, rest houses, and civic buildings like the **Indra Mahal** and **Victoria Jubilee Rest House.** His reign reflected a blend of **traditional authority and modern governance**, navigating colonial politics while preserving cultural identity.

By the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the **Venkatagiri Zamindari**—a legacy of martial valor turned landed aristocracy—was firmly embedded within the **Madras Presidency**, with its core in what is now **Nellore district** of Andhra Pradesh.

It covered Bay of Bengal and certain tracts of Nellore in the East, to Kalahasti Zamindari in the South, to the Eastern Ghats separating Nellore and Kadapa in the West to Kurnool, Guntur districts in the North.

The estate was divided into **two major divisions**, each with its own hierarchy and local governance. The Southern division covered taluks of Venkatagiri,Rapur, Sagutur, Mallam, Polur and Manubolu, while the Northern division covered Pellur, Podili, [Darsi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darsi), Kanigiri, Kocherlakota, and Marella.

Each division was headed by a *Peishkar*, and each Revenue Taluks were managed by a *Tahsildar* assisted by his *Sheristadar*, the chief clerk,Revenue Inspector, and other staff. The Dewan was the Chief Executive of the estate assisted by a manager.

In **1875**, the Rajas endowed a **permanent fund of ₹50,000** to support the Venkatagiri Rajahs' High School in Nellore which became a beacon of vernacular and Anglo education in Nellore.

The Rajah Venkatagiri Choultry, George Town built in the 18th century adjacent to older **Monegar Choultry was** endowed to **feed 100 poor persons daily and give alms to another 100**, with a **₹1 lakh investment in government securities** for its upkeep. It later merged into the present-day Monegar Choultry complex, continuing its mission of shelter and care.

In **1885**, the Raja donated **₹1 lakh** for construction of Victoria Gosha Hospital at Triplicane, addressing the critical need for **purdah-compliant medical care** for women. The hospital was part of the **Dufferin Fund** initiative and remains a vital institution today, now renamed as Kasturba Gandhi Hospital.

The **Veterinary Hospital in Chennai, founded by Raja Venugopala Krishna Yachendra** in the early 20th century, this was among the earliest veterinary care centers in the city, reflecting his progressive outlook. They also donated for a new stand at the Guindy Race Course named after them, and Madras Gymkana Club in Island Grounds.

The land on which the Hindu newspaper office now stands was once owned by Raja of Venkatagiri, as well as the Osborne House at Royapettah.

The **Venkatagiri sari tradition** flourished in the **early 1700s**, nurtured under the **Velugoti dynasty** of Nellore.It wasn’t just a craft—it was a **courtly expression**, woven for **queens, noblewomen, and zamindaris**, often as part of ceremonial attire or royal gifts. The **Bobbili and Pithapuram dynasties** also extended their patronage, helping the tradition spread across Andhra’s aristocratic circles.

These saris were originally woven from **fine muslin**, with **zari borders** and **minimalist elegance**—a style that mirrored the **dignity of the wearer**. The weavers, often from hereditary artisan families, were commissioned to produce **custom designs**, sometimes incorporating **family emblems or regional motifs**.
