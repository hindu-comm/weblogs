# From Sharmaji Ka Beta to Social Media

[](https://substack.com/@sadashree)

We grew up with one comparison:  
*“Sharmaji Ka Beta”* or in Telugu, *“Rao Gaari Abbayi.”*  
The mythical neighbor’s child who always scored higher, landed the better job, settled abroad.

But now?  
It’s not just one.  
It’s a thousand.  
Every scroll, every feed, every story — another “Sharmaji Ka Beta” living their best life.

That’s what social media is all about:

A hall of mirrors where everyone else seems smarter, richer, happier, where you’re left asking,

**“What about me?**

Open LinkedIn and it’s the same story:

- Someone mastered *all* the latest technologies.

- Someone quit their job, started selling Paav Bhaji, and is now earning
  crores.

- Someone else is writing a long thread about how they became
  “unstoppable.”

And then there’s you.  
Slogging 10–12 hours a day.  
No growth. No hike. No recognition.

You pause, stare at the screen, and whisper:  
**“What the f**\* am I doing?”\*\*

Maybe that’s the hidden truth of LinkedIn — it’s not just a platform for success stories, it’s also a mirror that makes us question our own grind.

You’re frustrated.

You open Instagram, just to escape for a while.

And what do you see?

- Beautiful people vacationing in exotic foreign locations.

- Friends partying like every day is a festival.

- Reels that look like movie trailers of perfect lives.

And then there’s you struggling to even get leave to go home for Deepavali or Dussehra. Forget vacations , even family festivals feel like a negotiation.

That’s the paradox of social media:

It shows you the highlight reels of others, while you’re stuck in the bloopers of your own grind.

Ok screw it, let’s hop onto Twitter.

And what do you see?

Some guy doing a podcast with a million+ views.  
Some female earning lakhs just by posting Reels.

And then there’s you.  
Comparing.  
Scrolling.  
Feeling more miserable.  
Wondering if you’re… nothing.

That’s the trap: every platform is a stage for someone else’s highlight reel.

Scroll through your feed and you’ll see it everywhere:

Success measured in money.  
Joy defined by influence.  
Popularity equated with worth.

But here’s the truth, what’s shown as “success” or “joy” is often just a curated illusion.  
A highlight reel designed to impress, not to reveal. Real success isn’t about likes or followers. It’s about growth, meaning, and the quiet victories no one posts about.  
Real joy isn’t staged, it’s lived.

**What if success wasn’t about money, influence, or popularity?**

**What if it was about doing what you love — just for yourself?**

Carpentry, baking, or gardening — not for Reels, but for the quiet satisfaction.  
Reading history or military stories, then writing them out because they fascinate you.  
Hanging out in a tech forum, answering queries, helping strangers without expecting claps.  
Watching a movie you adore, even if no one else has heard of it.  
Browsing a second-hand bookstore, stumbling upon an obscure novel, and losing yourself in its pages.

That’s real joy. Not curated. Not performed. Not measured in likes.Just lived.

Maybe the truest form of success is this, doing what makes your soul feel alive, even if nobody’s watching.

Back then, parents compared you to 3–4 people at most. Someone in your friend or relative circle who always seemed “better.”

Today?  
On social media, you’re comparing yourself to hundreds, even thousands.  
People you don’t even know.  
Their curated lives, their highlight reels, their “success stories.”

And what does it bring?  
Not motivation.  
Not growth.  
Just depression.  
Just a sense of inferiority.

The truth is simple:  
Comparison games are endless.  
And they never let you win.

Not everyone needs to be an entrepreneur.  
It’s absolutely fine to just do a 9–5 job.

Not everything you enjoy has to be “aesthetic” or “artistic.”  
It’s ok to love cringe stuff, to watch or read what makes *you* happy.

Your life doesn’t end if you don’t have a podcast with a million views or a bestselling novel.

What truly matters is this:  
Identify what you love doing.  
Keep at it.  
Don’t fall into comparison games.  
Don’t seek validation from random strangers you’ll never even meet.

Because real success is living a life that feels true to you.
