As a Telugu guy, found some aspects common with Marathis, same New Year( Ugadi/ Gudi Padwa) , use of Rao as a title.

Ugadi and Gudi Padwa, though celebrated with distinct regional flavors, both mark the beginning of the lunisolar calendar year, often tied to the arrival of spring and the symbolic renewal of dharma. The shared reverence for time, auspiciousness, and cosmic cycles speaks to a deeper Indic unity.

In Telugu, Rao often a respectful suffix denoting stature or lineage, while in Marathi, “Rao” (or “Rāv”) has roots in royal and administrative titles, especially during the Maratha and Peshwa eras.

Tilak was an inspiration to many freedom fighters, revolutionaries in Telugu land. His call for “Swaraj is my birthright” resonated deeply in Telugu-speaking regions, especially among early nationalist thinkers and student movements. His assertive nationalism and use of Ganesh Utsav as a public awakening inspired similar cultural mobilizations in coastal Andhra and Telangana.

The naming of children after Tilak, Gokhale, and Shivaji wasn’t just admiration—it was a quiet act of resistance, a way of embedding legacy into everyday life. These names became living symbols of courage, intellect, and dharma.

Shivaji Maharaj had visited Srisailam in 1767 and got the temple renovated there, after it had fallen into neglect. He is said to have renovated the northern gopuram of the temple, now referred to as the Shivaji Gopuram, restoring it after a period of neglect.He spent time in meditation and strategic planning, reportedly building a Darbar Hall and a Dhyana Mandiram—spaces that symbolized both spiritual depth and sovereign vision.

Shivaji Spoorthi Kendram was established in Srisailam, that features a majestic statue of Shivaji Maharaj seated with his Ashtapradhan Mandal, evoking the spirit of Hindavi Swarajya. The walls are adorned with 3D murals depicting key moments from his life—his coronation, fort conquests, naval innovations, and spiritual guidance from Samartha Ramadas.The Dhyana Mandir and Darbar Hall have been restored, offering visitors a visceral experience of Shivaji’s presence in the region.

Bhakti movement saints from Maharashtra like Tukaram, Namdev been as popular here. Their abhangs and teachings were translated, sung, and adapted into Telugu devotional traditions.

The temple near Machilipatnam modeled on Pandharpur is more than architectural homage—it’s a testament to how Vithoba’s image, standing arms akimbo in loving defiance, found a home in Telugu bhakti. His appeal transcends language: the yearning of the devotee, the simplicity of abhangs, the rural mysticis, they all echo in the Telugu psyche, especially among those drawn to Annamacharya’s and Ramadasu’s devotional idioms.

Most of the visitors to Shirdi are Telugu, and many visit Pandharpur too. Nagpur, Solapur, Aurangabad have long hosted Telugu communities—traders, teachers, bureaucrats—who brought with them not just language but festivals, food, and spiritual practices. Over time, they absorbed Marathi influences too, creating hybrid cultural zones.

The larger cities of Mumbai, Pune have pockets of Telugu heritage—cultural associations, temples, and even Telugu-medium schools. The intermingling here is less devotional and more diasporic, but the roots remain.

The founding of Hindupur by Hindu Rao, a Marathi chieftain, is a powerful reminder that Andhra’s soil has long been shaped by cross-regional currents—not just in trade and pilgrimage, but in polity, settlement, and shared memory.

Gooty Fort, with its layered history—from Vijayanagara to Maratha hands—was more than a military outpost. Under Hindu Rao, it became a node of Marathi influence in Rayalaseema.

The Marathi-speaking population in Anantapur isn’t a recent diaspora—it’s a settled community with deep roots, often tracing back to the 18th and 19th centuries.

Many were administrators, soldiers, and traders who stayed on after Maratha campaigns or under the Nizam’s dominion, integrating into Telugu society while retaining linguistic and cultural distinctiveness. Their presence enriched local traditions—bringing in Marathi devotional practices, culinary influences, and even architectural motifs.

During the Nizam’s rule, many Marathi-speaking families migrated as part of the administrative and revenue apparatus. Titles like Kulkarni, Deshpande, and Deshmukh weren’t just surnames—they were designations tied to land revenue, village record-keeping, and governance.

These families often settled permanently, integrating into local society while retaining their linguistic and cultural heritage. Over generations, they became fluent in Telugu, contributing to a bilingual ethos that still persists.

It’s not uncommon to find households where Marathi is spoken at home and Telugu in public life—with devotional songs, folk tales, and even lullabies crossing linguistic boundaries.

Ganesh Chaturthi, Gudi Padwa, and even Vithoba worship have found echoes in Telangana’s devotional landscape, often blending with local traditions like Bonalu and Bathukamma. Many Marathi-origin families became teachers, lawyers, and civil servants—playing key roles in Telangana’s intellectual and civic development.

Swami Ramanand Teertha, born as *Vyenkatesh Bhagvanrao Khedgikar* in Osmanabad, was the principal architect of the Hyderabad Liberation Movement. His transformation from a teacher to a sanyasi and then to a mass mobilizer against the Nizam is a saga of spiritual conviction meeting political courage.

His legacy lives on in Telangana through institutions, memorials, and the Swami Ramanand Teerth Marathwada University, which continues his mission of rural education and empowerment.

Keshav Baliram Hegdewar, the founder of RSS, was born in Nagpur, but his ancestral roots trace back to Kandakurthi village in Nizamabad district. His family migrated to escape persecution under the Nizam, carrying with them a vision of cultural regeneration that would later shape the RSS.

Anurag Kulkarni, born in Kamareddy to a Marathi-speaking family, trained in Hindustani classical music and became a leading voice in Telugu playback singing. His rise reflects the fluid cultural identity of Deccan artists—rooted in one tradition, flourishing in another.

And the classic Panduranga Mahatyam, based on the legend of Pundalik, a man once steeped in worldly indulgence who transforms into a devotee of Vithala.The film dramatizes the moment when Pundalik offers a brick to the Lord—leading to the founding of Pandharpur as a sacred site.

The bond between Telugu and Marathi cultures is not merely a tale of migration or shared festivals—it is a living testament to the Deccan’s soul, where saints walked across linguistic borders, rebels drew courage from each other’s legends, and ordinary families carried dual legacies in their hearts.
