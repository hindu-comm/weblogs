When the annals of modern Indian science are written, the name of Prafulla Chandra Ray will be one of those in golden letters, father of modern Indian chemistry, who discovered **mercurous nitrate** in 1896, a breakthrough in inorganic chemistry and authored *A History of Hindu Chemistry*, mentored legends like **S.N. Bose** and **Meghnad Saha.**

An industrial pioneer who founded **Bengal Chemicals & Pharmaceuticals** in 1901 — India’s **first pharmaceutical company, that laid the foundation for indigenous chemical industries, reducing dependence on colonial imports.**

An educational reformer, who served as **Palit Professor of Chemistry** at Calcutta University, where he remained a lifelong educator.Advocated for **science education in vernacular languages**, making knowledge accessible to all.

Championed **employment generation** through industrial self-reliance. Actively supported the **Swadeshi movement**, aligning science with India’s freedom struggle. Remained a **bachelor by choice**, dedicating his life to public service and intellectual pursuits.

> *While a student at Edinburgh I found to my regret that every
> civilized country including Japan was adding to the world’s stock of
> knowledge but that unhappy India was lagging behind. I dreamt a dream
> that, God willing, a time would come when she too would contribute her
> quota. Half-a-century has since then rolled by. My dream I have now
> the gratification of finding fairly materialized. A new era has
> evidently dawned upon India. Her sons have taken kindly to the zealous
> pursuit of different branches of science. May the torch thus kindled
> burn with greater brilliance from generation to generation.*

The multifacted genius was born on August 2, 1861, in **Raruli-Katipara**, a village in the **Jessore District** of Bengal Presidency (now in Khulna, Bangladesh). His family belonged to the **Kayastha zamindar** class, well-educated and progressive in outlook.

His father, **Harish Chandra Ray**, was not only a landlord but also a man of refined tastes — a **violinist**, a lover of literature, and a believer in modern education. His mother, **Bhubanmohini Devi**, came from a taluqdar family and was equally liberal and intellectually inclined.

Harish Chandra’s progressive views — especially his embrace of Western education and rejection of caste orthodoxy — led to him being labeled a **“Mlechha”** by conservative villagers.

Despite bouts of ill health, young Prafulla immersed himself in his father’s **extensive library**, nurturing a love for science, languages, and literature. His early education was interrupted, but he used that time to read voraciously — a trait that would define his lifelong intellectual hunger.

In **1870**, Harish Chandra Ray moved the family to **Kolkata**, then the intellectual and cultural heart of British India. Young Prafulla enrolled at **Hare School**, one of the city’s oldest institutions, where he began formal education after years of self-study due to illness. Kolkata exposed him to **new ideologies**, **scientific thought**, and **nationalist movements** — a fertile ground for his evolving worldview.

Coming from **Jessore**, a district in **East Bengal**, Prafulla and his brother **Nalinikantha** faced ridicule from classmates in Kolkata. At the time, East Bengal was unfairly stereotyped as **culturally and intellectually backward**, despite its rich heritage.This bias reflected the **urban elitism** of colonial-era Calcutta, where students from rural backgrounds were often marginalized.

The **1860s** were a golden era for Indian thought — the birth years of **Swami Vivekananda (1863)**, **Rabindranath Tagore (1861)**, **Madan Mohan Malviya (1861)**, and **Lala Lajpat Rai (1865)**. Ray’s formative years unfolded in parallel with these towering figures, each shaping India’s destiny through **spirituality**, **literature**, **education**, and **activism**.

Despite recurring health issues, Ray’s **voracious reading** and exposure to Kolkata’s academic circles deepened his love for chemistry. He later joined **Albert School**, Metropolitan( now **Vidyasagar College)**, and supplemented his science education at **Presidency College**, where his brilliance began to shine

With his father’s finances strained, **Prafulla Chandra Ray** and his brother lived in **student lodges**, embracing frugality to pursue education. These humble settings became crucibles of character, where Ray’s resilience and thirst for knowledge deepened.

At **Metropolitan Institution**, Ray came under the influence of **Sir Surendranath Banerjee**, a towering figure in India’s nationalist movement. Banerjee, then an **English lecturer**, was known for his eloquence, reformist zeal, and belief in **self-rule through education and civic engagement**.

In his autobiography *Life and Experiences of a Bengali Chemist*, Ray candidly shares that **low fees** were a practical reason for choosing **Metropolitan Institution** (now **Vidyasagar College**). But the deeper pull was **Surendranath Banerjee**, whose **nationalist ideals**, eloquence, and moral stature made him a **hero to Kolkata’s youth**.

Banerjee’s presence infused the campus with a sense of **purpose and pride**, and Ray was deeply inspired by his vision of **education as a path to self-rule**.

Ray’s passion for chemistry couldn’t be contained within classroom walls. He and his friends **set up a mini laboratory** in their **student lodging**, using whatever materials they could afford or improvise.

These experiments were often **self-directed**, driven by curiosity rather than curriculum — a precursor to his later pioneering work in **inorganic chemistry**.This humble lab became a **sanctuary of science**, where Ray nurtured his love for experimentation and laid the groundwork for his future breakthroughs.

In **1882**, Prafulla Chandra Ray won the prestigious **Gilchrist Scholarship** to study at **Edinburgh University**, becoming **one of only two Indians** to receive it that year. The scholarship required proficiency in **four languages**, a challenge Ray met head-on — despite once being mocked for his poor English, he mastered **English, Latin, French, and Sanskrit**.

His classmates in Kolkata had ridiculed him for his accent and rural background. But Ray’s response wasn’t bitterness — it was brilliance.He turned every slight into fuel, proving that **intellect and grit** could transcend social bias and colonial expectations.

When **Prafulla Chandra Ray** arrived in England in **mid-1882**, he was welcomed by **Jagadish Chandra Bose**, then a student at **Cambridge University**. Though they pursued different disciplines — Ray in **chemistry**, Bose in **physics and biology** — their shared Bengali roots and nationalist ideals laid the foundation for a **lifelong friendship**. This bond would later blossom into mutual admiration and collaboration, with both men becoming **pillars of modern Indian science**.

At the **University of Edinburgh**, Ray studied under **Alexander Crum Brown**, a renowned chemist known for his work on **chemical structure and notation**. Crum Brown’s rigorous approach to **inorganic chemistry** deeply influenced Ray, who would later pioneer research on **mercurous nitrate** and other compounds.

Ray’s doctoral thesis, *Conjugated Sulphates of the Copper-Magnesium Group*, earned him a **D.Sc. in 1887**, and his time under Crum Brown shaped his scientific philosophy — blending **precision with purpose**

In an essay competition announced by the University on India Before and After the Mutiny, Ray criticized the British rule, for which he was not given the prize.

> *The English people has yet to be roused to an adequate sense of
> importance of events which are now taking place in India. Thoughts and
> ideas which pervade the upper strata of society, are now percolating
> through the lower; even the masses are now beginning to be moved and
> influenced. The latter element, it would no longer do to treat as une
> quantite negligeable. England unfortunately now refuses to recognize
> the hard and irrestible logic of facts and does her best to strangle
> and smother the nascent aspiration of a rising nationality .*

Ray distributed copies of his essay to fellow students, the general public and also to John Bright, their finest parliamentarian. Bright acknowledged and also agreed with the points raised by Ray in his essay. In 1885, he got his BSc and later in 1887 his DSc from University of Edinburgh for his work on *” Conjugated (gepaarte) Sulphates of the Copper-magnesium Group: A Study of Isomorphous Mixtures and Molecular Combinations “.*

He also got the Hope Prize Scholarship that would enable him to stay for one more year and was also elected as Vice President of the Chemical Society.Ray returned back to India in 1888, as he wanted to pursue his research and share the knowledge with others too. Science in India was at it’s infancy, and there really were not much career prospects in Chemistry either.

By the **late 19th century**, **Presidency College in Kolkata** (formerly Hindu College) stood virtually alone in offering **structured chemistry education with practical lab work**. Most **private colleges lacked laboratories**, trained faculty, or access to modern equipment — making them ill-equipped to keep pace with the **global scientific revolution** unfolding in Europe and America.

The **Chemistry Department at Presidency** was formally established in **1874**, with **Alexander Pedlar** as its first professor. Pedlar introduced **mandatory practical classes**, imported foreign equipment, and helped build one of India’s earliest chemistry labs — a rare feat at the time. This made Presidency College a **crucible for scientific talent**, nurturing minds like **P.C. Ray**, **J.C. Bose**, and later **S.N. Bose**.

Despite his **exceptional qualifications**, Bose was appointed as a professor at **Presidency College** with a salary of **₹200**, while his British counterparts earned **₹300** for the same role. When offered entry into the **Higher Services**, it came with a humiliating caveat: **he would not be paid according to the official grade**, simply because he was Indian.

In response to growing unrest over **exclusionary policies** and **racial pay disparities**, **Lord Dufferin** established the **Aitchison Commission** in **1886**, led by **Sir Charles Umpherston Aitchison**.

The commission proposed replacing the old **“covenanted” and “uncovenanted”** classifications with a **three-tier system**, **Imperial Service** (reserved for British officers),**Provincial Service** (for Indians, with limited authority and lower pay) and **Subordinate Service** (clerical and support roles).

Even within this new framework, **Indians in Provincial Services** were paid **significantly less** than their British counterparts in Imperial roles — despite often performing similar duties. The **competitive exams** remained skewed, with no simultaneous testing in India and England, further limiting Indian access to top posts.

This patchwork reform did little to quell dissatisfaction. Leaders like **Surendranath Banerjee** and **Dadabhai Naoroji** began to vocally demand **equal opportunities**, **fair pay**, and **Indianization of services**.The **Indian National Congress**, formed in 1885, made **civil service reform** a central demand in its early resolutions.

Armed with a **glowing recommendation** from **Alexander Crum Brown**, his mentor at Edinburgh, and **assurances from Sir Charles Bernard**, a member of the Indian Council, Ray should have been a shoo-in for a prestigious post. Yet, colonial bureaucracy and racial bias meant all he could secure was a **temporary Assistant Professorship** at **Presidency College** in **1889** — with a **modest salary**, far below what his qualifications warranted.

Ray didn’t protest or walk away. He **embraced the opportunity**, seeing it as a platform to **build India’s scientific future**. Over the next **27 years**, he transformed Presidency’s Chemistry Department into a **hub of innovation**, mentoring legends like **S.N. Bose**, **Meghnad Saha**, and **Jnan Chandra Ghosh**.In **1916**, he retired as **Head of the Department**, having laid the foundation for **India’s first modern research school in chemistry**.

In **1912**, visionary Vice Chancellor **Sir Ashutosh Mukherjee** extended an invitation to Ray to join the newly established **University College of Science**, part of the **University of Calcutta**. Ray accepted the offer after retiring from Presidency College in **1916**, becoming the **first University Professor of Chemistry** — a title that reflected both his stature and the institution’s aspirations.

Unlike the modest setups of his earlier years, the University College of Science was now equipped with **state-of-the-art laboratory facilities**, thanks to generous donations from **Sir Taraknath Palit** and **Sir Rashbehari Ghosh**.Located at **92 Acharya Prafulla Chandra Road**, the campus would later be renamed **Rashbehari Siksha Prangan**, honoring its legacy of scientific excellence.

Ray’s tenure here saw the rise of a **legendary cohort**: **Satyendranath Bose**, **Meghnad Saha**, **Jnan Chandra Ghosh**, and **Jnanendra Nath Mukherjee** — all of whom would go on to shape India’s scientific destiny. He published over **120 research papers**, conducted **systematic analyses of Indian minerals**, and continued his groundbreaking work on **nitrites and hyponitrites.**

Ray lived in a **single room on campus**, surrounded only by books — no worldly possessions, just a relentless pursuit of knowledge and service.His presence turned the college into a **sanctuary of science**, where intellect met integrity.

Ray faced **systemic discrimination** throughout his academic and professional journey — from being denied promotions to witnessing British bias in scientific circles.These injustices fueled his **staunch nationalist convictions**, making him a vocal advocate for **Swaraj** and Indian self-reliance.

Though he didn’t march in protests, Ray **actively supported the Indian National Congress**, especially during the **Non-Cooperation Movement (1920–1922)** led by **Mahatma Gandhi**.He offered **moral and logistical support** — including **shelter and resources** to revolutionaries at his chemical factories.

His famous declaration, *“Science can wait, Swaraj cannot,”* became a rallying cry for students and young scientists.

British authorities reportedly described him as a **“Revolutionary in the garb of a Scientist”**, a testament to his quiet but impactful resistance.He was in **regular contact with Gandhi and Gopal Krishna Gokhale**, and even invited Gandhi to Kolkata to inspire students and intellectuals.

Ray published **over 120 research papers** in prestigious international journals, many of them in the *Journal of the Chemical Society (London)* before the *Journal of the Indian Chemical Society* was founded in 1924. His work spanned **inorganic and organic nitrites**, **sulphur compounds**, and **coordination chemistry**, laying the foundation for India’s modern chemical research.

Ray conducted **systematic chemical analyses of rare Indian minerals**, collaborating with geologists like **Sir Thomas Holland** to obtain specimens.His goal was to identify **undiscovered elements** that might fill gaps in **Mendeleev’s Periodic Table** — a bold and visionary pursuit for the time.

In 1896, while preparing mercurous nitrate, Ray noticed a **yellow crystalline deposit** that defied expectations. This led to the **isolation of mercurous nitrite (Hg₂(NO₂)₂)** — a compound previously unknown and chemically unstable, making its discovery all the more remarkable. The paper was published in the *Journal of the Asiatic Society of Bengal*, marking the **first modern chemical research publication by an Indian scientist**.

Ray’s work on mercurous nitrite wasn’t just a discovery — it solved a series of **Kuhnian “normal science” problems**, advancing the understanding of mercury compounds.His method of synthesis and structural insights became a reference point for future inorganic chemistry research.

> *Ray’s real contribution to the development of chemical research in
> India rests not so much on his own personal research publication as on
> his inspiring and initiating a generation of young workers, who,
> dedicating themselves to a scientific career succeeded in building up
> what is now known as the Indian School of Chemistry-Priaydranjan Ray*

In 1902, Ray published the first volume of his celebrated work The History of Hindu Chemistry, and the second volume in 1908.

Ray was inspired by **Marcellin Pierre Eugène Berthelot**, the legendary French chemist and historian of science.Berthelot, curious about India’s contributions to chemistry, encouraged Ray to explore and document them.

In **1898**, Ray sent Berthelot a **short monograph on Indian alchemy**, which sparked the idea for a full-fledged historical study.

Ray spent **over a decade** collecting ancient manuscripts — many moth-eaten, hidden in private collections, or scattered across European museums. He learned **Sanskrit and Pali**, and collaborated with scholars like **Acharya Brajendranath Sil** and **Pandit Nabakanta Kabibhusana** to interpret texts.

The volumes cover **alchemical ideas in the Vedas**, **Ayurvedic and Tantric periods**, and **iatrochemical traditions**, showing India’s deep engagement with acids, alkalis, metals, and alloys.

Berthelot himself wrote a **15-page review** in *Journal des Savants* in 1903, praising Ray’s work. Journals like *Nature* and *Knowledge* hailed it as a **definitive contribution** to the history of science.In **1912**, Durham University conferred an **Honorary D.Sc.** on Ray, citing this work as the cornerstone of his fame.

In **1892**, with just **₹700** and a dream, Ray established **Bengal Chemical and Pharmaceutical Works** in Kolkata. It began as a modest laboratory in his home, but by **1901**, it had grown into a full-fledged company — the **first Indian-owned pharmaceutical enterprise.**

Ray’s goal wasn’t wealth — it was **employment for Indian youth**, **self-reliance**, and **scientific dignity**. He worked **odd hours**, often skipping meals, personally overseeing everything from **formulation to packaging**.

His motto:

*“Let us manufacture our own medicines and chemicals. Let us not depend on foreign imports.”*

Bengal Chemical produced **medicines, disinfectants, and soaps**, including the famous **BoroPlus antiseptic** and **Naphthalene balls**. During epidemics, its products were distributed **free of cost** to the poor — Ray believed science must serve society.

The company became a **symbol of Swadeshi enterprise**, inspiring other ventures like **Calcutta Pottery Works**, **National Tannery**, and **Bengal Enamel Works**. Even **Mahatma Gandhi** praised Ray’s efforts, calling him a **“true servant of the nation”**.

Ray’s goal was clear: **produce affordable medicines in India**, breaking the colonial stranglehold on pharmaceutical imports. He focused on **formulating tonics and essential drugs** using **locally sourced ingredients**, drastically reducing costs for Indian consumers.

Equipped with **modern laboratory tools** and guided by **professional management practices**, Ray ran Bengal Chemical like a precision instrument.He personally oversaw **quality control**, **formulation**, and **distribution**, ensuring that every product met international standards.

By **April 12, 1901**, Bengal Chemical had grown enough to be registered as **Bengal Chemical and Pharmaceutical Works Ltd.**, India’s **first indigenous pharmaceutical company**.Ray reinvested profits and raised capital — including **₹2 lakh**, a massive sum at the time — to scale operations and expand product lines.

Coming from **Raruli-Katipara**, Ray never forgot his rural roots. He regularly visited **village huts**, offering **food, medicine, and moral support** to farmers and laborers during times of hardship. His empathy wasn’t performative — it was **personal**, shaped by his own experiences of poverty and exclusion.

When the **famine struck Bengal** in 1922, the **British administration remained largely indifferent**, offering minimal relief. Ray stepped up, **mobilizing resources**, **appealing to fellow Indians**, and **distributing aid** through his network — including Bengal Chemical and local volunteers.

In **just one month**, P.C. Ray raised **₹3 lakh** — a staggering sum in 1922 — to support famine relief efforts. **Women offered their jewelry**, not out of obligation, but out of reverence for Ray’s integrity and purpose.**Hundreds of youth** responded to his call, volunteering to serve in villages — a grassroots mobilization that rivaled political movements.

*“Had Gandhiji two more such Rays with him, Swaraj would have come faster.”*

This wasn’t just praise — it was a recognition of Ray’s **magnetic moral authority**, his ability to **unite intellect with action**, and his **unwavering patriotism**.

His **“Simple Zoology” (1893)** was a landmark work, praised for its clarity and accessibility — especially for Bengali students navigating science in their mother tongue.Ray didn’t limit himself to chemistry. He wrote on **history, education, economics, and social reform**, often blending scientific insight with cultural critique.

He was a **regular contributor** to influential Bengali periodicals like **Basumati**, **Anandabazar Patrika**, and **Manashi**.Through these platforms, Ray reached **the common reader**, advocating for **scientific temper**, **national self-reliance**, and **ethical living**. His essays often carried a tone of **gentle rebellion** — challenging colonial narratives while uplifting indigenous knowledge.

Despite being a **single individual**, Ray’s output rivaled that of entire institutions. He was a **chemist, historian, educator, reformer, and writer** — all rolled into one.His ability to **bridge disciplines** made him a **cultural force**, not just a scientific one.

He established the **Nagarjuna Prize in Chemistry** with ₹10,000 — a tribute to the ancient Buddhist alchemist who symbolized the fusion of science and philosophy.He instituted the **Ashutosh Mukherjee Prize in Zoology**, honoring the visionary Vice-Chancellor who championed scientific education in Bengal.

Ray donated **₹1.8 lakh** to the **University of Calcutta**, specifically for the **expansion of the Chemistry Department**.His contributions helped transform the department into a **national hub for chemical research**, nurturing generations of scientists.

Though he **founded Bengal Chemicals**, India’s first pharmaceutical company, Ray **refused to take a salary**. He **donated all profits** for the **welfare of workers**, setting a precedent for ethical entrepreneurship.

On **June 16, 1944**, P.C. Ray passed away in his modest single room at the **University College of Science**, Calcutta — the very space that had been his sanctuary for decades.

He was surrounded by **students, admirers, and fellow scientists**, many of whom had been shaped by his teachings and inspired by his ideals. Ray lived with **radical simplicity** — no wealth, no family, no fanfare. Just books, experiments, and a relentless drive to serve.

Though a **pure science enthusiast**, Ray believed that **knowledge must serve humanity** — whether through pharmaceuticals, education, or disaster relief.His work was never about accolades. It was about **utility**, **upliftment**, and **national regeneration**.

*“I have no sense of success on any large scale in things achieved…but have the sense of having worked and having found happiness in doing so.”*

Ray was a true Karma Yogi — one who labors without attachment, who finds joy in the act, not the applause.
