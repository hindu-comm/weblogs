The Akkanna Madanna Caves located at the foot of the Indrakeeladri Hill in Vijayawada, and so named after the two brothers who worked in the court of the Golkonda Sultan Tana Shah, and were maternal uncles of the great saint poet Bhadrachala Ramadasu.

Though the caves were excavated in the 7th century during the Eastern Chalukya reign, they came to be named after these brothers, as it’s believed they renovated or supported the temples here. Also after their tragic execution by Aurangzeb, many monuments were named in their honor.

**Who were Akkanna and Madanna?**

Not much is known about the early life of these brothers, however they became famous during the final days of the 17th century Qutub Shahi dynasty of Golkonda. The brothers had played a role in appointing Abul Hassan Tana Shah as the Sultan, and he in turn appointed them as ministers in his court.

By the **1680s**, the brothers had gained control over **tax collection and the exchequer**, becoming central figures in Golconda’s administration. Their **revenue farming model** replaced the jagir system, separating military power from economic control — a bold move that empowered civilian elites, especially Brahmins. They used this wealth not for personal gain, but for **public welfare**, including **temple restoration**, **trade expansion**, and **building public sarais**.

Their **nephew, Kancharla Gopanna**, was appointed **tahsildar of Bhadrachalam**, likely through their influence. His transformation into **Bhadrachala Ramadasu** is legendary — he used tax revenues to rebuild the **Sita Ramachandraswamy Temple**, leading to his imprisonment and eventual divine redemption.His devotional compositions, especially the **Dasarathi Satakamu** and **Ramadasu Keertanalu**, remain cornerstones of Telugu bhakti literature and Carnatic music.

Golkonda was invaded by Aurangzeb in 1687, during his Deccan campaign, and Tana Shah was taken prisoner, ending the Qutub Shahi dynasty. Akkanna and Madanna too were captured and beheaded by Shah Alam, Aurangzeb’s son. Many monuments in former Golkonda were named after these brothers, that included the Akkanna Madanna Temple in Shah Ali Banda, located in Hyderabad’s Old City and the caves at Vijayawada.

The Akkanna Madanna Caves, like other early Eastern Chalukyan rock-cut temples, consist of a monolithic rock-cut hall, a mandapa and cave sanctums in the innermost part of the rock.

The **Monolithic Rock-Cut Hall was** carved from a single rock face, symbolizing spiritual permanence and artistic endurance, while the **Mandapa a pillared hall,** served as a congregational space, possibly for devotees and rituals, featuring early examples of Chalukyan pillar designs. The Garbagriha cut deep into the innermost part of the rock, these housed Shiva Lingas and may have once contained beautifully carved niches for attendant deities.

The main upper cave was carved out monolithically from a vertical rising rock cliff, which has a front court, and then a pillared mandapa. The adhishtana(plinth) with a flight of six steps in the centre is topped with a damaged *gaja surulyali* (elephant head) balustrade. This is flanked by three square facade pillars and a pilaster on each side, most of which are in ruins now.

![Upper cave, mandapa pillars](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!d1Au!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F77e7a157-de7d-4586-8393-3b6a3dce6b90_800x600.jpeg "Upper cave, mandapa pillars")

Beyond the facade, is a mandapa with rows of six pillars and two pilasters each, which are mainly simple octagonal pillars.The upper cave has three shrines on the back wall, each with projecting fronts, complete with a deep set *prati* and an *adhishthana*, with a shared continuous *pattika*. These are simpler and have no carvings, unlike the lower Akkanna Madanna cave and the nearby Mogalrajapuram caves.

![Upper cave, a sanctum](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!MAe8!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fcfe69034-0a15-4d47-bcde-c891fd406c1a_800x1067.jpeg "Upper cave, a sanctum")

The central shrine has a slightly larger door around 840 cm compared to the two side shrines which are around 760 cm tall. The garba griha to the south covers 1.45 m square area, while the northern one is in a rectangular shape of 1.3 by 2.2 m and the central one is a larger 2.44 m square. The central shrine had a monolithic Shiva Linga which seems to have been destroyed in the invasions.

There is a damaged and eroded platform outside aligned with the central sanctum, which had a Nandi now missing. There is a niche shrine to the south of the Nandi pedestal but the main deity is missing, which would likely have been that of Ganesha.

The upper cave has a long inscription on the southern pillar, containing the phrase “Sri Utpati Pidugu” meaning “Creation of Thunderbolt”. This mark is found in many temples dating to the Eastern Chalukya period. Based on the inscriptions most of the caves were dated to the 7th century.

The lower caves were also cut from the same rocky cliff, and are in three sets of which the northernmost is least preserved, while the southernmost is well preserved. All have a flight of steps and a mahamandapa, with two rows of two pillars, and then a sanctum in the back wall of the cave, flanked by dwarapalas.

![A view of the lost pillars and open mandapa, lower cave](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kG6a!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0e470ca3-066c-468a-9245-2dd1b8293ae4_1024x576.jpeg "A view of the lost pillars and open mandapa, lower cave")

The cells are square in shape, one is 2.36 m, another is 1.9 m, and the ceilings of the lower cave are notable for their artwork. The sanctums had placeholders for the Shiva Linga, nalas for drainage, while southern wall of mahamandapa has a platform for *valampuri* four-armed Ganesha.

![Ganesha, lower cave](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!4nYc!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F46687141-aa23-4ccf-ac19-2757d93379d2_800x1067.jpeg "Ganesha, lower cave")

The lower cave unusually combines cave excavation and parts of a monolithic temple carved out from rock. Also the roof of the sanctums show an effort to carve out a shikara superstructure.

To the north of the lower caves is a small park, which contains inscribed pillars,a restored inscribed granite mandapa and recovered mutilated ruins found from nearby damaged temples. Of these, a notable pillar is the Kiratarjuna pillar with artwork and inscription clearly visible on all four sides.

The Akkanna Madanna caves at the base of the Indrakeeladri is worth a visit, to witness the architectural excellence of the Eastern Chalukyan era, and also a tribute to the two brothers who did immense service to Dharma. When you visit the Kanaka Durga temple, you can also pay a visit here.
