While there were many who fought for freedom in India, the ones in Hyderabad State had to wage a double fight, both against the British and the Nizam who was their stooge. Unlike most regions that primarily fought British colonialism, Hyderabad’s revolutionaries had to confront both imperial power and the entrenched feudal autocracy of the Nizam, who actively collaborated with the British and later resisted integration into independent India.

And one among those was Swami Ramanand Tirtha who truly embodied the spirit of *Karma Yoga*, selfless action rooted in service and sacrifice. His life was a confluence of spiritual discipline, political courage, and educational reform, all directed toward uplifting the marginalized and liberating Hyderabad from both feudal and colonial chains.

He was born Vyenkatesh Bhagvanrao Khedgikar on October 3, 1903, at Sindge in Bijapur dt of Karnataka. His early education was at Ganugapur, a famous Datta Kshetram, then later at Kalyani and Sholapur. He was a graduate of Tilak Vidyapeeth and a post grad with distinction from Pune University.

He began his career as a headmaster at Hipparga National School, where he didn’t just teach, he ignited minds. His blackboard bore lessons in history, civics, and courage. His students weren’t just pupils, they were seeds of a future movement.  

Swamiji infused his teaching with democratic ideals, urging students to question injustice and dream of a liberated Hyderabad. His teaching was radical in its simplicity: *teach truth, live truth, fight for truth.* From Hipparga, he went on to establish institutions like *Rashtriya Shala* and later the *Nanded Education Society*, creating sanctuaries of learning for the rural and the oppressed.

Beyond academics, Swamiji initiated village upliftment programs, cultural awareness drives, and interfaith dialogues. His activism was holistic in nature integrating education, ethics, and empowerment . By the time the Hyderabad State Congress was formed, Swamiji had already become a beloved figure among peasants, students, and reformers. His leadership was earned not through slogans, but through service.

The princely state of Hyderabad, consisting of Telangana, Northern Karnataka and Marathwada, was then governed by the Nizam, Mir Osman Ali Khan. When the British left in 1947, they gave the 562 princely states the option to join **India or Pakistan**, or remain independent. Most chose integration. Hyderabad’s refusal, under the Nizam’s rule, sparked a crisis that culminated in its annexation by India.

Hyderabad had become a burning cauldron, civil liberties were throttled, and the Razakars spread fear. The princely state stood defiant against democratic integration.In this storm, Swamiji stepped forward, not with weapons, but with **wisdom and will**. He called for a **political platform beyond caste, creed, or language**, rooted in the shared dream of liberation.

To build the *Hyderabad State Congress*, he envisioned a federation of regional movements, Maharashtra Parishad, Karnataka Parishad and Andhra Mahasabha which were the pillars of people’s power, each resisting the Nizam in their own way.

Swamiji helped form an ad hoc committee and selected **1,200 members**, issuing a clarion call for responsible governance. The Congress was open to **all communities**, and its mission was clear:

*“Join hands to liberate Hyderabad—not as factions, but as a people.”*

**Haripura Congress Session** was held in **February 1938**, and was presided over by Netaji **Subhash Chandra Bose**, whose radical vision for India’s independence electrified the gathering. It was here, amidst ideological tensions and rising nationalist fervor, that **Swami Ramanand Tirtha** proposed the formation of a **Hyderabad State Congress Unit**, a daring move, given that Hyderabad was still under princely rule and political activity was heavily suppressed.

The Nizam’s regime had banned Congress activities, and any attempt to organize politically was met with arrests and intimidation. Swamiji’s proposal was not just administrative—it was revolutionary, demanding national recognition for a movement that was still underground.

Through persistent lobbying and moral persuasion, Swamiji secured approval. This led to the formation of the Hyderabad State Congress in 1938, a milestone that galvanized resistance across linguistic and cultural lines.

However, unfortunately, the dictatorial Nizam prohibited the Congress State Unit before its formation. In response, Swamiji, led the first formal Satyagraha in Hyderabad State on **October 24, 1938.** Arrested for his convictions, Swamiji’s non-cooperation and civil disobedience became a rallying cry. His actions infused the masses with **new energy and cognizance**, transforming passive subjects into active citizens.

Eventually, Nizam lifted the prohibition on Congress, and Swamiji stepped into history as the first President of the Hyderabad State Congress.

*“The final war for Bharat’s independence, will be fought on this soil.”*

He openly challenged the Nizam and his Razakar commander, **Qasim Razvi**, whose paramilitary terror had gripped the state. Swamiji’s call was not for vengeance, but for **integration, dignity, and democratic awakening**.

As India stood on the brink of independence, Hyderabad remained shackled by the Nizam’s refusal to join the Union. But **Swami Ramanand Tirtha**, armed only with truth and nonviolence, led a movement that would shake the foundations of feudal rule.

Through relentless **Satyagraha campaigns**, the Hyderabad State Congress awakened the masses. Their demand was clear, **Hyderabad must join India**. On **August 7, 1947**, the Congress declared “Join Indian Union” Day. Across Warangal and beyond, citizens hoisted flags, defied bans, and faced brutal crackdowns. The Nizam responded with arrests and repression, but the spirit of unity could not be jailed.

Swamiji was himself jailed for **111 days** by the Nizam but never wavered. His greatest legacy was m**obilizing the soul of Hyderabad**, turning silent suffering into a movement of mass awakening. As the **Razakars** unleashed terror across the state, a war-like atmosphere gripped Hyderabad. The cries of the oppressed reached Delhi and **Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel** answered.

On **13 September 1948**, Bharat launched **Operation Polo** and ithe Nizam’s forces crumbled, as he surrendered to Sardar Patel on **17 September 1948** and the Indian Army, marking the **liberation of Hyderabad** and its integration into the Indian Union.

With the Nizam’s surrender on **September 17, 1948**, the guns fell silent, but the wounds of Razakar terror remained raw. The newly formed **transitional government**, recognizing the trust and moral authority of the freedom fighters, released Swamiji along with Pandita Narendra Ji and **Vinayak Rao Vidyalankar.**

**  ** Together, they were invited to help **restore law and order** in the **twin cities of Hyderabad and Secunderabad**, not as rulers, but as **guardians of peace**. Their presence reassured a fractured society. Their leadership turned fear into hope, and chaos into calm.

As Hyderabad transitioned from princely rule to democratic integration, **Swamiji** looked beyond the immediate victory. He cautioned the Government that beyond force, they must also implement land reforms and address the concerns of the peasants.

After Hyderabad’s liberation, **Swami Ramanand Tirtha** turned his gaze toward the **poor and underprivileged**, dedicating his life to social upliftment and educational reform.In **1952**, he was elected as the **first Member of Parliament from Gulbarga and later s**erved as **MP from Aurangabad.** His campaigns were marked not by pomp, but by **principled simplicity and grassroots connection**.

After a lifetime spent in service, struggle, and spiritual leadership, he passed away on **22nd January 1972**, at the age of **69,** leaving behind not just a legacy, but a living movement. In recognition of his legacy, **Shri P.V. Narasimha Rao**, established the **Swami Ramananda Teerth Memorial** in **Brahmanwadi, Begumpet, Hyderabad**, a sacred resting place for Swamiji’s mortal remains.

PV Narasimha Rao as PM, also established the **Swamy Ramananda Tirtha Rural Institute** in **1995**, with its campus nestled in the historic soil of **Bhoodan Pochampally**, Telangan, a land once touched by Vinoba Bhave’s movement for voluntary land redistribution. Its mission, to **empower rural youth and women**, especially those from underprivileged backgrounds, through vocational training, sustainable technologies, and entrepreneurship.
