One of the famous freedom fighters from Andhra Pradesh, who roused the revolutionary spirt of readers, with his poems was Garimella Satyanarayana. **“Maakodhi Tella Doratanam”( We do not want the white man’s rule)** motivated many Satyagrahis against the British rule, as well as another poem **“Dandalu Dandalu Bharata Maata”( Greetings to you Bharata Mata)** that awakened the masses.He was one of the first to court arrest during British rule, known for his integrity and honesty.

The great soul was born on July 14, 1893 in a small village Gonepadu, near Narasannapeta, Srikakulam district to Venkata Narasimham and Suramma. He did his primary education from Piriya Agraharam, and with financial assistance from Kannepalli Lakshmi Narasimham, he studied further moving to places like Vijayawada, Rajahmundry and Machilipatnam.

On finishing his BA, he joined the Ganjam collectorate where he worked as a clerk for sometime, and later as a teacher in Vizianagaram High School. However due to his restless free spirit, he could not work for long at any place.

The 1920 Kolkata session saw Congress passing a resolution for Non Cooperation movement, and Garimella followed in the footsteps of Mahatma Gandhi for a non violent struggle.

Realizing that the British were using divide and rule, to create differences among Indians and establish themselves, he wrote the poem **“Maakodhi Tella Doratanam”**that proved to be immensely popular with the masses.The then British collector Bracken, called Garimella and asked him to sing the song, who was an equally good singer along with being a writer.

When he sang the song in his deep, booming voice, the Collector was completely taken in by it’s sheer intensity and power. Even though he did not understand Telugu, he realized the power of the song to awaken the revolutionary spirit among masses, and sentenced Garimella to one year rigorous imprisonment. However the song by now had spread widely, as Congress workers began to sing it, marching in the streets, protesting against the British rule.

After release from prison, Garimella once again began to sing the songs of protest amidst the masses, as the British authorities were now worried about his appeal. Realizing he was a danger if left free, the Kakinada magistrate imposed two years RI on him again. His father passed away in 1923, when he was in prison. The British promised to release him, if he sought amnesty, he refused straightaway.

Once again on release from prison, he was felicitated by people everywhere. However he would be beset by a series of troubles, his wife passed away, leaving him with two daughters. He married again,however due to mounting debts, he had to sell his property. He wrote many books, worked for some time as a Library officer, however the books did not sell much, affecting him financially.

Garimella was a scholar and polyglot, fluent in Telugu, Tamil and Kannada. He wrote Swarajya Geetamulu in 1921, Harijanulu Paatalu, songs of Dalits in 1923. He translated the Tirukkural and Nandiyar from Tamil, and Tallikota from Kannada into Telugu. He also translated Bhogaraju Pattabhi Sitaramayy’s book Economic Conquest of India into Telugu, as well as another English poem Heart of the Nation.

He also wrote in various weeklies under the pen name of Dundhubi, Vikari. He moved to Madras in 1933 for a living, and worked as the editor for Gruhalakshmi magazine.Later he joined as sub editor for Acharya NG Ranga’s magazine Vahini. He also worked with Andhra Prabha, Anandavani and later as a freelance journalist.

During the time, when he was financially struggling it was another freedom fighter Kasinadhuni Nageswara Pantulu, founder of Amrutanjan, who helped him out, apart from Vavilala Venkateswara Sastry. However financial difficulties and poor health took a toll on him, he lost an eye in his last years, and was also paralyzed. The man who had aroused the revolutionary spirit of the masses with his songs was fully destitute and bed ridden.

Even independence did nothing to alleviate his condition, as those who got into power completely neglected him. And much like [Pingali Venkayya Garu](https://historyunderyourfeet.wordpress.com/2020/08/01/pingali-venkayya/), who had designed the national flag, the great freedom fighter and writer, passed away on December 18, 1952 in poverty, unmourned, a really sad ending for someone who had roused the masses with his poems.
