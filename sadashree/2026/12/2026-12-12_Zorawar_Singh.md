One of the greatest military generals of India, was Zorawar Singh, who served under the Dogra ruler Gulab Singh of Jammu, as Governor of Kishtwar, extending the territories of the kingdom beyond Ladakh and Baltistan. He also attempted to conquer Western Tibet but was killed in the Battle of To-Yo on December 12, 1842. The conqueror of Ladakh, who strode across the Himalayas like a colossus, from Baltistan to Tibet, from Ladakh to Skardu.

He was born on September 1784 in a Rajput family at Kahluria , in the princely state of Bilaspur located in the mountains of Himachal Pradesh. His family migrated to Jammu, where he would take up service on comming of age under Raja Jaswant Singh of Marmathi, now in Doda dt.He later was hired by the ambitious Raja Gulab Singh of Jammu, and during his service,he bought to note the financial wastage being occured in the administration of the Reasi fort and came up with his own plan to reduce it.

Impressed by his honesty and knowledge, Gulab Singh appointed him as the commandant of Reasi. He worked hard to stop the financial wastage, strengthening the fort, following which he was made commissariat officer of all forts north of Jammu, and later the governor of Kishtwar, given the title of Wazir. He bought in most of the local Rajputs in his army, and captured the Paddar region from Chamba, that gave him control of the sapphire mines there. His greatest feats though would be his expeditions in the Himalayas.

Ladakh was ruled then by the Gyalpo, belonging to the Namgyal dynasty, to whom many petty kingdoms were vassals. One of them the Raja of Timbus sought his assistance against the Gyalpo in 1834, at the same time Zorawar himself was restless, seeking to expand the kingdom of Gulab Singh beyond Jammu, and this was just the right opportunity for him. He crossed the mountain ranges, with an army of 5000, defeating the local tribesmen.

He also subdued most of the local landlords, moving to Kargil, where he however faced a challenge from Tsepal Namgyal, the Gyalpo, who sent his general Banko Kahlon to cut off all communications.However Zorawar doubled back to Kartse, where he sheltered his troops during the harsh winter. And in the spring of 1835, defeated the large Ladakhi army of Banko Kahlon, as he marched towards Leh. The Gyalpo now agreed to pay 50,000 rupees as war-indemnity and 20,000 as an annual tribute.

The Governor of Kashmir, Mehan Singh, alarmed by Zorawar’s advances, incited the Ladakhi chieftains to rebel, but they were suppressed ruthlessly in the valley by Zorawar, as he forced the Raja of Zanskar to pay tribute. Once again Mehan Singh, incited the Gyalpo to revolt in 1836, as Zorawar marched his army once again at a rapid pace and defeated the Ladakhis. He built a fort outside Leh and stationed a garrison there of 300 men under Dalel Singh, while the Gyalpo was deposed to an estate.

Baltistan was the region lying towards the northwest of Ladakh, also called as Little Tibet.Muhammad Shah, the son of the Skardu ruler, Raja Ahmad Shah, had rebelled against his father, and fleeing to Leh, sought the help of the Gyalpo and Zorawar. However some of the Ladakhi nobles, assisted Ahmad Shah, seeking his help to subdue Zorawar’s army. Putting down the Ladakhi rebels, Zorawar invaded Baltistan in the winter of 1839, this time accompanied by a large contingent of Ladakhis too who joined his army.

However an advance party of 5000 under Nidhan Singh, lost their way in the cold and snow, and many died from it, as well as an ambush the local Botis of Skardu. It was Mehta Basti Ram, a prominent Rajput from Kishtwar who established contact with the main army, and on their arrival, the Botis of Skardu were defeated and forced to flee. The Dogras scaled the steep mountian behind Skardu fort and captured it, from where they fired on the forces of the Raja, forcing him to surrender.

Muhammad Shah was placed on the throne, after he had agreed to pay an annual tribute of 7000 Rs, while a Dogra contingent under Wazir Lakhpat advanced west, captured the fort of Astor, and took the Raja Darad as prisoner. Following a complaint by Mehan Singh, Darad was released on the orders of Gulab Singh. By 1840 the Dogra ruler of Jammu was fully in control of Ladakh and Baltistan, subject to the suzerainity of the Sikh Empire.

It also broke the Ladakhi-Kashmiri monopoly over the lucrative trade of the pashmina wool trade from Tibet, as it now began to find it’s way to the British occupied territories, who began to eye the benefits of the trade.This made Zorawar and Gulab Singh turn their eyes towards Tibet, that was under the protectorate of the Qing Dynasty then.

Zorawar Singh led a 4000 strong force into Tibet, consisting of Dogras, Ladakhis, Baltis, Kishtwaris, while the Tibetans themselves had a 6000 strong force, mostly armed with bows, spears, while Zorawar’s army had the latest artillery and cannons. He divided his army into 3 divisions,one via the Rupshu plateau and Hanle monastery under Ghulam Khan, another along the Indus valley towards Tashigang under the Ladakhi prince Nono Sungnam, and the third via the Pangong Lake towards Rudok, led by Zorawar himself.

The three columns swept through Tibet passing Lake Manasarovar, and converging at Gartok, defeating the small Tibetan force.While the Tibetan commander fled to Taklakot, Zorawar stormed and captured that fort in September, 1841. He set up a complete network of communication, supply lines in the harsh terrain of Tibet, through a series of small forts. However the onset of winter, saw all the passes and routes blocked by snow, and the supplies to his army were held up, inspite of all the meticulous preparation.

The intense cold took a toll on his soldiers, many of them losing their fingers to frostbite, and were also unable to use weapons effectively. Many starved to death, while some burnt their muskets to warm themselves. This gave the Tibetans and Chinese enough time to regroup themselves, as they met again on the battlefield at To-Yo on December 12, 1841.

Zorawar was wounded in his right shoulder by a burst of gun fire, and now weary of the constant battles, claimed “Either the Tibetans will take my head, or I will myself”. He hurled himself straight into the Tibetan army, killing many, before a lance hurled against him, tore right through his chest. The great general fell on the ground, unable to reach out for his sword. The Tibetans surrounded him, removing the lance from his chest, and in one swift stroke, he was beheaded, and his hands, ears too were cut off and taken to Lhasa to be shown. It was the end of a great warrior, who died fighting till the end on the battlefield.

With his killing,the Dogra conquest of Tibet came to an end. The samadhi of Zorawar Singh is now located on a 300 feet high hill, on ToYo near Taklakot in Western Tibet, shaped like a Buddhist Chorten. Many Tibetans still visit and pray at the memorial.

Even though he fought against the Tibetans, they still honor him as a great hero. They call his memorial as “Singh ba Chorten” meaning “Cenotaph of the Singh Warrior”. It’s believed Tibetans made their children wear Tabiz with his bones, thinking they would grow up as strong and brave as Zorawar Singh.

Zorawar Singh,a truly great general who expanded the Dogra kingdom of Jammu, all the way up to Ladakh, Baltistan, and into Tibet. He was among those who adopted an Offensive strategy, going right into the enemy territory, and it was due to his vision and wisdom, that the Gulab Singh’s Dogra kingdom, which was part of Maharaja Ranjit Singh’s Sikh Empire, could extend all the way till Western Tibet.
