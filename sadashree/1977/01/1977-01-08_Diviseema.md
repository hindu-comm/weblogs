Cradled in the delta’s embrace, Diviseema is a land of canals, coconut groves, and emerald fields,where the Krishna river bows to the Bay of Bengal at Hamsaladeevi, a confluence as sacred as it is serene.

Unlike its cousin Konaseema, Diviseema rarely finds itself in tourist circuits or cinematic frames. For many, its name evokes the haunting memory of the 1977 cyclone, a wound etched in Andhra’s collective memory.

But Diviseema is more than its grief. It is resilience in green. It is ritual in rhythm. It is the quiet pulse of agrarian life, where every irrigation channel tells a story of survival and renewal.

It is here that *Kuchipudi* was born—in the eponymous village where dance becomes devotion, and every gesture tells a story. One of India’s great classical traditions, it emerged not from courts but from temple courtyards, where Brahmin dancers once offered their art to the divine.

Just a few miles away lies *Movva*, cradle of the poet *Kshetrayya*, whose *padam,* rich in *śṛṅgāra rasa*, still echo in Carnatic concerts and Bharatanatyam recitals. His verses, drenched in longing for Krishna, were not mere lyrics but living bridges between bhakti and beauty.

At the heart of Movva stands the *Venugopala Swamy temple*, where the flute-playing Krishna still listens, perhaps, to the songs once sung in his praise.

In the quiet village of *Srikakulam*, nestled within the Krishna delta, stands the *Andhra Mahavishnu temple*—a site not only sacred, but historic.

It was here that *Sri Krishna Deva Raya*, the legendary emperor of Vijayanagara, composed his Telugu magnum opus *Amuktamalyada*. A poetic vision of devotion, diplomacy, and dharma, it tells the story of Andal’s divine love for Ranganatha, blending bhakti with statecraft.

A statue of the emperor graces the temple grounds, reminding us that even rulers bowed before poetry.

At *Avanigadda*, the mighty Krishna pauses—and parts.

One stream flows eastward to *Hamsaladeevi*, where river meets ocean in a sacred embrace. The other turns southward to *Gullalamoda*, merging quietly with the Bay of Bengal, as if whispering a second farewell.

Spanning the Krishna river with quiet strength, the **Penumudi–Puligadda bridge** is more than concrete and steel—it is connection.

As the **second longest road bridge in Andhra Pradesh**, it links the delta’s heartland to the wider world, offering lifelines of trade, travel, and hope. For the people of Diviseema, it is a daily crossing between past and future, memory and movement.

At the estuary where the Krishna river surrenders to the sea, an *old lighthouse* stands sentinel—weathered, watchful, and wise.

Nearby, the **Krishna Wildlife Sanctuary** flourishes in quiet abundance. Mangroves, migratory birds, and estuarine ecosystems thrive here, reminding us that Diviseema is not only sacred to humans, but also to nature.

Built between **1898–1901** using heavy lime concrete blocks, this structure once guided ships through estuarine mist and colonial ambition. Known during British rule as the **Havelock Point Lighthouse**, it remained in operation until **1940**, after which it faded into silence.

Today, it is approachable only by **motorboat**, a journey through winding waters and wildlife sanctuary.

Stretching across the Krishna river with quiet elegance, the **Campbell Aqueduct cum Road Bridge** is a marvel of colonial engineering, built during the British era and still in use today.

Its dual purpose,carrying both water and wheel, makes it a rare feat of design, blending utility with endurance. For generations, it has served as a vital artery for the people of Diviseema, linking fields, villages, and lives across the delta.

Hamsaladeevi where the Krishna river meets the Bay of Bengal, is worth a visit, not just for the Sagara Sangamam, but also the Venugopalaswamy Temple here, one of the well known Krishna temples in AP.

It’s believed that Ganga after becoming impure having washed away the sins of many, approached Vishnu,who asked her to visit all the Punya Kshetras as a crow, however he blessed her saying the place where she will turn into a swan( Hamsa) will be a holy one.

Ganga in the form of a crow, travelled to all the Punyakshetras, and when she reached the Sagara Sangama of Krishna, and took a dip, she was immediately transformed into a swan. Hence the name Hamsaladeevi. The beach, the meeting point of Krishna river with Bay of Bengal, the Venugopalaswamy temple here, make Hamsaladeevi worth a visit.

Avanigadda was called as Avanijapuram in ancient times, it’s believed that Rama and Seeta spent some time at the Vashistha Ashram here during their exile, the Seetalanka is one of the islands here. It was a port too during Satavahana times.

One of the well known temples in Avanigadda is the Lankamma Ammavaru temple, dedicated to the Grama Devata. The Jatara here is quite well known.

Another well known temple in Avanigadda is the Lakshmi Narayana Swamy temple, known for it’s tall gopuram, built around 1000 years back. However the temple was badly damaged during the 1977 cyclone, and was restored again in 1990.

Movva is home to the Venugopalaswamy Temple, the deity here is believed to have been made from sand, by Maugalya Maharshi, this temple much like the other one at Hamsaladeevi are both child temples of Kanaka Durga temple, Vijayawada.

One of the most famous residents of Movva is Mahakavi Kshetrayya, whose real name was Varadayya, an illiterate cowherd, who became a scholar when a Rishi taught him the Gopala Mantra.

With his enlightenement Varadayya began to tour all the holy Kshetras down South, and writing in praise of the deities there, that would get him the name of Kshetrayya. Known for his Padams on Shri Krishna, and regarded as one of the greats along with Tyagayya and Annamayya.

In the heart of Diviseema stands the **Bhavadevarapalli temple**, a jewel in Andhra’s sacred constellation. It is one of the **Pancha Bhavanarayana temples**, five ancient shrines dedicated to Lord Vishnu in his Bhavanarayana form, the others at Bapatla, Sarpavaram, Ponnur and Chinnaganjam.
