Ahilyabai Holkar, the legendary queen of Malwa and one of the most respected rulers in Indian history, passed away on August 13, 1795. Her reign, marked by administrative brilliance, justice, and religious patronage, had transformed Indore into a hub of prosperity and good governance. But her death triggered a precarious succession crisis.

Ahilyabai had suffered a grievous personal loss decades earlier—her only son, Male Rao Holkar, had died in 1767 at a young age, reportedly afflicted by mental illness. With no direct heir, the future of the Holkar dynasty remained uncertain.

In this turbulent moment, Tukoji Rao Holkar, her loyal commander-in-chief and a close confidant, stepped forward. Tukoji had been the real military backbone of the Holkar state throughout Ahilyabai’s reign. A seasoned warrior and strategist, he had led numerous campaigns against both the internal enemies and external threats, including the Nizam of Hyderabad and Mysore forces.

Tukoji was not just a military man but also a wise statesman who had long advised the queen on matters of administration and diplomacy. His succession to the leadership ensured stability for the short term.

However, Tukoji Rao Holkar’s rule was brief. He passed away just two years later in 1797, plunging the Holkar state once again into a leadership vacuum—this time more volatile, given the rising tensions within the Maratha Confederacy and the external threat from the British East India Company.

His death led to intense power struggles among his sons—Kashi Rao Holkar, Malhar Rao II, and Yashwantrao Holkar—which would eventually culminate in Yashwantrao Holkar emerging as the most capable leader, one who would soon challenge British power on the battlefield.

Following a turbulent period marked by internal power struggles and British interference, Yashwantrao Holkar ascended the throne in January 1799. His rise was not merely a matter of inheritance but of merit — forged in the crucible of conflict, diplomacy, and popular support.

Unlike his elder brother Kashi Rao, whose weak leadership and British leanings alienated both the army and the court, Yashwantrao was charismatic, militarily brilliant, and deeply popular among the Holkar nobles, the Maratha soldiery, and the common people. His leadership offered the promise of revival, strength, and independence for the Holkar state.

With his position firmly consolidated, Yashwantrao turned his attention to expansion and consolidation. Determined to restore Holkar dominance, he led a vigorous northern campaign, aiming to reassert influence over traditional Maratha territories and challenge the growing encroachment of the British and their allies.

Meanwhile, his brother Vithoji Rao Holkar spearheaded a simultaneous southern campaign, creating a two-pronged military thrust that reflected both strategic vision and bold ambition.

This dual offensive was not just territorial but symbolic — a statement that the Holkars, under Yashwantrao’s leadership, would no longer play second fiddle to either the Peshwas or the British.

But it was only the beginning — the coming years would see Yashwantrao Holkar take on the British Empire itself, score one of the most remarkable victories at the Battle of Ujjain, and attempt to forge a united front among Indian rulers against colonial domination — a bold vision that, though ultimately thwarted, would earn him the legacy of a national hero

However, the fragile balance of power within the Maratha Confederacy was shattered when Vithoji Rao Holkar, younger brother of Yashwantrao, openly challenged the authority of Peshwa Baji Rao II. Vithoji believed that Amrut Rao, the adopted son of Raghunath Rao, was a far more capable and deserving leader, and began mobilizing support to replace the Peshwa.

This open defiance alarmed the Peshwa’s court in Pune. Acting swiftly, Balaji Kunjir, a staunch loyalist and minister of the Peshwa, managed to capture Vithoji. Despite numerous appeals and counsel from seasoned advisers—who warned that executing a Holkar prince could spark irreversible consequences—Baji Rao II remained obstinate.

In a shocking and vindictive move, Vithoji was sentenced to death in 1801, and in a particularly gruesome manner: he was executed by being trampled under an elephant in the heart of Pune, a punishment typically reserved for traitors.

This decision proved catastrophic. What was intended as a show of strength instead exposed Baji Rao’s poor judgment and insecurity, and it alienated the powerful Holkar faction permanently. Yashwantrao, deeply enraged and grieving his brother, vowed revenge — not just for personal loss, but for what he saw as a betrayal of the Maratha spirit.

More crucially, this act accelerated the collapse of the already strained Maratha Confederacy. The unity that once held the Maratha sardars together in the face of external threats now unraveled amidst internal mistrust, vendettas, and unchecked ambition.

A furious and grieving Yashwantrao Holkar, burning with the desire to avenge his brother Vithoji’s brutal execution, launched a decisive military campaign in 1801. His first target was Ujjain, the then-capital of the Scindias, who had closely aligned themselves with the Peshwa. In a stunning display of tactical brilliance and sheer force, Yashwantrao routed the Scindia forces, capturing Ujjain — a symbolic strike at the heart of Holkar rivals.

Yet, even in victory, Holkar extended a measured olive branch. He offered the Peshwa a chance to avoid further bloodshed: demanding that Scindia restore the Holkar territories unlawfully seized, and that the Holkars be granted their rightful share of the northern dominions, in accordance with Maratha traditions.

However, Peshwa Baji Rao II, swayed by hubris and ill-advised counsel from the Scindias and British sympathizers, ignored Holkar’s demands. The refusal was a grave miscalculation.

In response, Yashwantrao Holkar marched south towards Pune in May 1802, no longer willing to negotiate through letters — he would now speak through the thunder of cannons. This was not merely a military expedition; it was a campaign of justice, vengeance, and political reckoning. The city of Pune, once the proud nerve centre of the Maratha Empire, was now to face the fury of the very forces it had scorned. As Holkar’s seasoned cavalry swept through the Deccan, it became clear: the most formidable Maratha of his generation was coming not just to punish, but to reshape the balance of power in the subcontinent.

In a prolonged and relentless campaign, Yashwantrao Holkar captured most of Khandesh and the territories surrounding Pune, steadily tightening his grip on the Deccan. His swift and strategic advances set the stage for a decisive confrontation with the combined forces of the Peshwa Baji Rao II and the Scindias.

On October 25, 1802, at Hadapsar, just outside Pune, the two armies clashed in a fierce battle. Though the Holkar forces initially endured a heavy barrage of cannon fire, Yashwantrao’s calm leadership prevailed. He advised his troops to hold their ground, biding their time until the enemy’s firepower waned. When the moment was right, he gave the order to return fire with devastating effect.

The battle turned swiftly in Holkar’s favor, culminating in a resounding victory on Diwali day — a symbolic triumph celebrated with both military prowess and cultural significance. The Peshwa, overwhelmed and facing defeat, fled in haste to Sinhagad Fort, leaving Pune vulnerable to Holkar’s forces.

A couple of days after the Battle of Hadapsar, the Peshwa Baji Rao II fled once more, this time retreating to Raigad Fort, accompanied by a contingent of Scindia soldiers and his loyal associates—Chimnaji, Baloji, and Balaji Kunjir. His position increasingly precarious, the Peshwa made a final desperate move.

On December 1, 1802, Baji Rao II reached Bassein (modern-day Vasai) on the western coast, where he entered into negotiations with the British East India Company. Under intense pressure and seeking to reclaim his throne, he signed the infamous Treaty of Bassein.

This treaty effectively reduced the Maratha Empire to a British client state, handing the Company significant control over Maratha affairs and military decisions. The alliance came at a steep price: the Maratha Confederacy was deeply divided, sovereignty compromised, and the foundations of their once-mighty empire irreversibly weakened.

The British East India Company saw the restoration of Peshwa Baji Rao II on the throne not merely as a diplomatic victory, but as a crucial strategic move. By reinstating the Peshwa under British terms, they aimed to create a buffer against their principal rivals—the French, who still retained pockets of influence in India. Securing a loyal Maratha leader was essential to contain French ambitions and safeguard British interests in the subcontinent.

Moreover, the British viewed the assertive and independent Yashwantrao Holkar as a significant threat to their expanding influence. Holkar’s military prowess and political ambition made him a wild card who could destabilize the region. By empowering the Peshwa and forging an alliance with him, the British sought to counterbalance Holkar’s aggression.

This alliance also served to protect another key British ally, the Nizam of Hyderabad, ensuring a network of friendly states around their territories. In essence, the Treaty of Bassein was a calculated move by the British to divide and weaken the Maratha Confederacy from within, facilitating their eventual dominance over India.

The Treaty of Bassein sounded the death knell for Maratha independence, marking a critical turning point in India’s colonial history and paving the way for the British Empire’s expansion across the subcontinent.

However, the Treaty of Bassein was met with fierce opposition from most of the Maratha sardars, including many of Peshwa Baji Rao II’s own close associates. They viewed the treaty not as a political necessity, but as a shameful surrender to British dominance, a betrayal of Maratha sovereignty and pride.

In defiance, Yashwantrao Holkar seized control of Pune, rejecting British interference and their chosen Peshwa. He boldly appointed Amrut Rao, the adopted son of Raghunath Rao, as the legitimate Peshwa. This move garnered widespread support from the majority of Maratha chieftains and rulers, who saw in Amrut Rao a symbol of true Maratha independence.

The notable exception was the Gaekwads of Baroda, who had already accepted British protection and thus aligned themselves with the new colonial power. This division among the Maratha leadership further fragmented the once-mighty confederacy and set the stage for the turbulent conflicts that would follow.

Yashwantrao Holkar returned to Indore on March 13, 1803, retreating to consolidate his power base after the turbulent events in Pune. However, the British influence continued to grow. By August 14, 1803, under mounting pressure from the British East India Company, Amrut Rao was forced to abdicate the position of Peshwa.

As part of the arrangement, Amrut Rao was granted a generous annual pension of Rs 7 lakh along with a jagir (land grant) in Banda district, effectively removing him from political contention but ensuring his loyalty or at least neutrality.

Meanwhile, Baji Rao II remained the titular Peshwa, but his role was reduced to little more than a ceremonial figurehead. Real power and control had shifted firmly into the hands of the British, marking the effective end of Maratha sovereignty and signaling the growing dominance of colonial rule.

On June 4, 1803, in a critical effort to resist British expansion, Yashwantrao Holkar met with Raghoji Bhonsle and Daulat Rao Scindia at Bodwad to forge a united front against the common enemy. The meeting held great promise, as the Maratha sardars recognized that only through cooperation could they hope to stem the growing power of the British East India Company.

However, Daulat Rao Scindia harbored duplicity. Behind closed doors, he assured Peshwa Baji Rao II that Holkar’s demands and ambitions could be sidelined, and promised that once the British were defeated, they would collectively “dispose of” Yashwantrao Holkar.

When Yashwantrao learned of Scindia’s betrayal and double-dealing, his spirit was crushed. Realizing that internal rivalries and mistrust ran deeper than the threat from the British, a dejected Holkar withdrew from the alliance, leaving the Maratha Confederacy further fractured at its most critical moment.

Meanwhile, Raghoji Bhonsle II, after suffering a decisive defeat at the Battle of Laswari in December 1803, was compelled to sign the Treaty of Deogaon with the British East India Company. This treaty ceded full control of the eastern provinces formerly held by the Marathas to the British, marking a significant territorial and strategic loss for the Confederacy.

At the same time, Daulat Rao Scindia, following a devastating defeat at the Battle of Assaye, was forced to sign the Treaty of Surji-Anjangaon. This treaty stripped Scindia of crucial territories including the entire Ganga-Jamuna Doab region, significant parts of Bundelkhand, and large areas of Gujarat, further weakening Maratha influence and expanding British dominion.

These treaties represented the gradual dismemberment of Maratha power, as internal divisions and military setbacks paved the way for British supremacy in India.

“First country, and then religion. We must rise above caste, religion, and regional loyalties in the interest of our nation. You too must wage war against the British, as I do.”

These words echoed the fierce patriotism and unwavering resolve of Yashwantrao Holkar, who now found himself effectively isolated. With the Peshwa, Scindia, Gaekwad, and Raghoji Bhonsle all having surrendered to the British, Holkar stood alone in defiance.

Undeterred, he reached out to other Indian rulers, calling for a united front to resist the growing colonial threat. Yet, with most already shackled by treaties and British influence, his appeals largely fell on deaf ears.

Despite overwhelming odds and dwindling support, Yashwantrao Holkar remained steadfast — a lone beacon of resistance in an era where political divisions threatened to fracture the very fabric of India’s struggle for sovereignty.

“Although unable to match your artillery on the battlefield, vast countries stretching hundreds of miles will be overrun and plundered. The British shall not have a moment’s respite. Calamities will rain down relentlessly on the people, caught in the ceaseless waves of war unleashed by my army — an unstoppable force crashing like the tides of the sea.”

Though increasingly isolated, Yashwantrao Holkar refused to surrender or bow to the British. When agents of General Perron visited him, he spoke not with words, but with a powerful gesture — pointing first to his spear, then to his horse.

He made it clear: his kingdom rested on the saddle of his horse, and his spear remained as formidable as ever.

With unwavering resolve, he declared,  
“My country and property are upon the saddle of my horse, and, God willing, wherever the reins of my brave warriors’ horses are turned, the entire land in that direction shall fall under my control.”

This vivid declaration was a testament to Holkar’s fierce independence, his mastery of cavalry warfare, and his unyielding commitment to reclaim and defend his homeland against all odds.

Yashwantrao Holkar followed up his defiant stance by defeating the British forces under Colonel Fawcett at Konch near Jalaun, showcasing his tactical brilliance and the resilience of his cavalry. In response, the British dispatched a larger force commanded by Colonel Manson, but Holkar once again routed them decisively near Kota on July 8, 1804.

In a bold move to restore Indian sovereignty, Holkar launched an attack on Delhi on October 8, 1804, aiming to free the Mughal Emperor Shah Alam II from British captivity and rekindle support for his cause. However, the arrival of General Gerard Lake with reinforced British troops forced Holkar to retreat, marking a temporary setback in his campaign.

Meanwhile, the British captured key Maratha strongholds, including Ujjain and Indore, further eroding Holkar’s territorial control. Undeterred, Yashwantrao retreated to Mathura, where he began strategizing his next moves to reclaim his lost dominions and continue resisting British domination.

By now, the British were growing increasingly wary of Yashwantrao Holkar’s strength and tenacity. So formidable had his resistance become that Lord Wellesley wrote urgently to General Lake, warning that *if Holkar was not crushed soon, he would unite the remaining Indian rulers against British rule* — a prospect the Company could not afford.

Undeterred, Holkar moved to Deeg, where he once again proved his military prowess by defeating the British forces under Major Frazer. His arrival at Deeg on November 16, 1804 was met with open arms by Ranjit Singh, the Jat king of Bharatpur, who pledged his support to Holkar’s cause.

In retaliation, Lord Lake launched a direct assault on Deeg on December 13, 1804, but the combined forces of Holkar and the Jats fiercely repelled the British, handing them another humiliating defeat.

Refusing to relent, Lake returned with a much larger force, laying siege to Bharatpur on January 3, 1805. What followed was one of the most remarkable defensive campaigns in Indian history — a three-month-long siege in which Holkar and Ranjit Singh successfully held off the British might, repelling wave after wave of attacks.

Frustrated by repeated failures to defeat Holkar in open battle, the British shifted tactics. Realizing brute force alone would not bring victory, they turned to diplomacy laced with deceit, employing their classic strategy of divide and rule — pitting one Indian ruler against another, weakening alliances, and isolating their enemies.

As part of their shift to divide-and-rule tactics, the British skillfully bought over key allies of Yashwantrao Holkar. The formidable Amir Khan Pindari, once Holkar’s staunch supporter, was lured with promises of power — he was granted the jagir of Tonk in Rajasthan, laying the foundation for the princely state of Tonk. Similarly, Bhawani Shankar Khatrij, another influential figure in Holkar’s circle, was rewarded with a lavish mahal in Delhi, effectively neutralizing his allegiance.

But the greatest blow came when Ranjit Singh of Bharatpur, Holkar’s most crucial ally, signed a separate peace treaty with the British on April 17, 1805. It was a stunning betrayal — for after months of heroic resistance, the tide had finally begun to turn in their favor. Victory had been within reach.

Yet, even in this moment of treachery and political manipulation, Holkar had achieved what few Indian rulers had managed — he had shattered the myth of British invincibility. The failure of the British to capture Bharatpur, despite overwhelming military superiority and months of siege, exposed their vulnerability and inspired future resistance movements across the subcontinent.

Though betrayed and isolated, Holkar’s campaign had proven that the British could be resisted — not just through warfare, but through unity, resolve, and strategic defiance.

Despite his isolation, Yashwantrao Holkar did not give up his dream of forging a pan-Indian alliance to resist British domination. He reached out once again to fellow Indian rulers, attempting to rebuild a united front. However, this time, it was Daulat Rao Scindia who emerged as the spoiler. Unlike his illustrious predecessor Mahadji Scindia, who had valiantly resisted British encroachment, Daulat Rao preferred accommodation and self-preservation, seeking friendly ties with the British.

When Maharaja Man Singh of Marwar attempted to send troops in support of Holkar’s cause, it was Scindia who intervened and blocked their advance, a clear sign of his quiet collaboration with the British. Elsewhere, the rulers of Jaipur, Nagpur, and Satara offered verbal assurances of support, but none committed active assistance, leaving Holkar increasingly isolated.

Yet, in British eyes, Holkar remained the single most dangerous Indian adversary — the only ruler they had failed to defeat in open battle. So acute was the concern that General Lake himself expressed helplessness in containing Holkar, admitting the futility of further military campaigns.

The growing political and military embarrassment led to the recall of Lord Wellesley, and the appointment of Lord Cornwallis as Governor-General in 1805. Recognizing Holkar’s tenacity and the cost of continued conflict, Cornwallis wrote to Lake ordering the restoration of all of Holkar’s territories and instructing him to negotiate peace.

But Holkar, still proud and defiant, refused to submit, unwilling to settle on British terms. The situation changed again with the sudden death of Cornwallis, and the appointment of Sir George Barlow as the new Governor-General. Barlow, shrewd and pragmatic, capitalized on the rivalry between Holkar and Scindia, and successfully weaned Scindia away, further isolating Holkar.

On November 23, 1805, Barlow signed a peace treaty with Daulat Rao Scindia, ending any hope of a united Maratha resistance.

Even as his allies dwindled, Yashwantrao Holkar remained relentless in his quest to build a united Indian front against the British. Turning his attention northwards, he sought to forge alliances with the powerful Sikh rulers of Jind and Kapurthala, but these attempts bore little fruit.

Undeterred, Holkar then reached out to the rising star of northern India — Maharaja Ranjit Singh of Punjab, whose growing power and disciplined Khalsa army could have dramatically altered the balance of power.

However, the British quickly caught wind of Holkar’s overtures. Alarmed by the prospect of an Indo-Sikh alliance, they acted swiftly and decisively. Using diplomatic pressure through Ranjit Singh’s influential uncle, the British persuaded him to drop the idea of joining Holkar’s resistance. To seal the deal, a fresh treaty was signed with Ranjit Singh on December 17, 1805, ensuring his neutrality and effectively closing the door on any northern support for Holkar.

This was a strategic masterstroke by the British — preventing a powerful north-south alliance that could have reignited the flame of resistance and possibly delayed their domination of India by decades.

With no Indian ruler left willing to stand by him, Yashwantrao Holkar found himself completely isolated—the last formidable Indian leader still holding out against the British. Yet even in this moment of solitude, Holkar stood tall. Unlike others who had submitted out of fear or compromise, he had never been defeated in battle.

On December 24, 1805, at a place called Rapur Ghat on the banks of the Beas River, Holkar finally agreed to peace — but on his own terms. Fully aware of his military strength and the cost of further confrontation, the British approached him not with coercion, but with respect. The treaty he signed was radically different from the Subsidiary Alliance agreements imposed on other Indian rulers.

It was an unconditional peace treaty — one that restored all of Holkar’s lost territories, and even recognized his dominion and influence over the Rajput states of Jaipur, Udaipur, Kota, and others. No British garrison was stationed in his territory. There was no permanent British Resident in his court. Holkar retained full sovereignty — a rare distinction in an age when most Indian rulers had effectively become vassals of the Company.

Thus, even in peace, Yashwantrao Holkar emerged as a victor. He returned to Indore in triumph, not as a defeated rebel, but as a proud and independent ruler — one who had stood alone, fought with unmatched courage, and bent the British Empire to negotiate on equal footing.

*The Maratha state had been grasped by foreigners. To resist their aggression, God knows, how during the last two and a half years I sacrificed everything, fighting night and day, without a moment’s rest. I paid a visit to Daulatrao Sindia and explained to him how necessary it was for all of us to join in averting foreign domination. But Daulatrao failed me. It was mutual cooperation and goodwill which enabled our ancestors to build up, the Maratha states. But now we have all become self-seekers. You wrote to me that you were coming for my support, but you did not make your promise good. If you had advanced into Bengal as was planned, we could have paralyzed the British Government. It is no use of now talking of past things. When I found myself abandoned on all sides, I accepted the offer which the British agents brought to me and concluded the war.- Holkar’s letter to Vyankoji Bhonsle of Nagpur.*

Even after signing the peace treaty, Yashwantrao Holkar was far from done. True to his lifelong mission, he made one final, audacious attempt to drive the British out of India. Relocating his base to Bhanpura (in present-day Mandsaur district, Madhya Pradesh), Holkar began preparations for a large-scale military campaign aimed directly at the British heartland in the east — Kolkata, their seat of power.

He established a cannon manufacturing factory, where nearly 200 cannons were produced through round-the-clock efforts — an extraordinary feat of indigenous military production at the time. Alongside this, he mobilized an army of over 1 lakh soldiers, determined to strike a decisive blow and rekindle the flame of Indian independence.

But fate had other plans.

The immense stress of planning such a massive campaign, coupled with personal grief — particularly the deaths of his nephews Khanderao and Kashirao Holkar — began to take a heavy toll on him. His body, which had weathered years of war and resistance, could no longer bear the weight of a nation’s hope.

On October 27, 1811, Yashwantrao Holkar passed away suddenly from a stroke, at just 35 years of age. He was more than a warrior — he was a visionary, a man who dreamed of a free India decades before 1857, and one who refused to submit when others faltered. He fought not just for territory, but for sovereignty, dignity, and unity. Even in death, he left behind a legacy of courage, resilience, and resistance — a legacy that would later inspire countless others in India’s long struggle for freedom.
