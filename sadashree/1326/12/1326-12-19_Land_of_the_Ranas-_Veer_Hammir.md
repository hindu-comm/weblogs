Rana Hammir in a way began the next phase of Rajput rule of Mewar with Chittorgarh as the capital that started in 1326 and ended in 1568, when Rana Udai Singh II, had to flee after Akbar occupied the fort. Hammir’s ancestor was Laksha Singh, a very distant clan relative of Rawal Ratan Singh, who fought during the siege of Chittorgarh by Allauddin Khilji in 1303. When it was inevitable, that Chittorgarh would fall, Laksha decided that one of his 9 sons would live to fight for another day.

But who would it be?

In the face of Alauddin Khilji’s relentless siege, Laksha Singh’s decision to send one son each day to battle was not just tactical,it was symbolic. It was a slow-burning act of defiance, a daily offering to the spirit of Mewar, knowing full well that each son’s fate was sealed. Ari Singh, Hammir’s father, was the first to fall, and with each passing day, the fort’s walls echoed with the loss of another brave soul.

When only Ajay Singh remained, Laksha’s final command wasn’t to fight—but to preserve. He saw in Hammir not just a child, but the seed of resurgence. Kelwara, nestled in the rugged folds of the Aravallis, became their sanctuary. '

Ajay’s departure under the cloak of night wasn’t cowardice,it was courage of a different kind. To walk away from battle, from kin, from honor as defined by the sword, and instead choose the long road of rebuilding,that takes a different kind of valor. His vow to return wasn’t just personal; it was a promise to Mewar itself.

When Alauddin Khilji laid siege to Chittorgarh, the defenders knew they were outmatched. Yet, Rawal Ratan Singh, Laksha Singh, and the remaining Rajput warriors chose death over dishonor. They charged into battle in what is known as a Saka—a final, suicidal sortie meant to die fighting. Meanwhile, the women of the fort, led by Rani Padmini, committed Jauhar, leaping into flames to avoid capture and enslavement. It was a collective act of defiance, sacrifice, and tragic heroism.

Khilji’s forces didn’t just win—they ravaged. Temples were desecrated, homes razed, fields torched, and villages annihilated. It was a calculated reign of terror meant to break the spirit of Mewar. After the devastation, Khilji handed over control to Maldeo of Jhalawar, a collaborator, further deepening the betrayal.

In Rajasthani lore, Chittor ka Saca ka Paap is used as a curse, invoking the karmic weight of betrayal and slaughter. “Saca” refers to battles that end in mass death—especially when defenders know they will die but fight anyway. The phrase loosely translates to:

*“May the sin of the sack of Chittor be upon you.”*

It’s not thrown around lightly. It’s reserved for moments of deep anger or condemnation, often when someone has committed a grave betrayal or injustice. It’s a reminder that some wounds never heal,and some sins echo through generations.

Nestled high in the Aravalli hills, Kelwara became more than a refuge,it was the crucible where the future of Mewar was quietly being shaped. Ajay Singh, wounded and weary, held the torch of survival, while Hammir, just twelve, stood at the edge of boyhood and greatness. As survivors of Chittorgarh’s fall trickled in, Kelwara transformed into a rallying point for scattered Rajput clans.

Munja, a notorious bandit chief—likely Bhil in origin—had turned the valley into his playground of terror. Even Ajay Singh’s own sons couldn’t stop him. But Hammir, driven by a fierce sense of duty and honor, vowed to end the menace. In a dramatic duel, Hammir not only slew Munja but returned with his severed head—a grim but powerful symbol of justice and leadership. That act wasn’t just a victory—it was a coronation by deed.

With Munja’s defeat, Hammir’s claim to leadership was sealed. Ajay Singh’s son, Sajjan Singh, recognizing Hammir’s ascendancy, migrated south to the Deccan. And here’s where history and legend intertwine:  

According to some historical sources, Sajjan Singh is believed to be the ancestor of Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj, the founder of the Maratha Empire. The genealogical link traces Sajjan Singh’s lineage through generations of warrior clans who settled in the Deccan, eventually giving rise to the Bhonsle family. Shivaji’s ancestors, including Bhim Singh Ghorpade and Shubhakrishna Bhonsle, are said to have descended from this Rajput migration.

After securing the hilly terrain of the Aravallis from Munja’s former domain, Hammir transformed Kelwara into a sanctuary and strategic base. It became a magnet for displaced Rajput clans, especially those enraged by Maldeo’s betrayal, who had accepted Khilji’s patronage and ruled Chittorgarh as a vassal. Hammir’s campaign was not just territorial,it was a reclamation of honor.

When Hammir’s initial attempts to retake Chittorgarh faltered, he chose to undertake a pilgrimage to Dwarka, perhaps seeking divine clarity and strength. On this journey, at Khod, he encountered Aai Birwadi, a mystic saint of the Charan community, revered as an incarnation of Hinglaj Mata, the fierce goddess of protection and valor.

According to bardic lore, Aai Birwadi recognized Hammir’s destiny and urged him to return to Mewar and reclaim Chittorgarh. Her words weren’t just encouragement,they were a divine command. Inspired and spiritually fortified, Hammir turned back, now carrying not just the hopes of his people but the sanction of the goddess herself.

She foretold the arrival of Baruji, a wealthy horse trader. True to her word, Baruji met Hammir at Kherwara, near Udaipur, bringing with him,around 500 horses, a formidable cavalry force,a loyal band of clansmen and substantial resources and supplies.

This infusion of strength was more than logistical,it was symbolic. Baruji’s arrival marked the turning point from retreat to resurgence.

In gratitude, Hammir is said to have built the Annapoorna Mata temple within Chittorgarh Fort, dedicated to Aai Birwadi, honoring her as the divine force behind Mewar’s resurgence.

With Baruji’s support, Hammir relaunched his offensive toward Chittorgarh, reclaiming town after town. His growing momentum and swelling ranks sent a clear message to Maldeo, the Chauhan ruler installed by Alauddin Khilji: the tide was turning.

In a bid to avoid conflict and perhaps preserve his own standing, Maldeo offered his daughter Songari in marriage to Hammir. Hammir, recognizing both the political and symbolic value of this gesture, accepted. The marriage was conducted in a modest ceremony, attended by Maldeo and his sons, and marked Hammir’s formal entry into Chittorgarh,not as a conqueror, but as a rightful heir.

When Hammir discovered that Songari, his bride, was a child widow, and that her marriage proposal was a political ploy by Maldeo to retain control over Chittorgarh, his fury was understandable. Yet Hammir’s response was deeply telling of his character.

He refused to punish Songari, recognizing her innocence in her father’s scheme. Instead, he accepted her with dignity, and she, in turn, became a loyal ally, helping Hammir navigate the court’s politics.

With Maldeo away on a military expedition, Hammir seized the moment. He won over the nobles, many of whom were already disillusioned with Maldeo’s rule. Secured the loyalty of the army, which had grown sympathetic to Hammir’s cause. And occupied the throne of Chittorgarh, not through bloodshed, but through strategic brilliance and moral authority.

Upon Maldeo’s return, he was arrested and imprisoned,a poetic justice delivered by his own son-in-law. It wasn’t just a coup; it was a restoration.

With Hammir now firmly in control, exiled clan chieftains who had fled during Khilji’s reign and Maldeo’s betrayal began to return. Chittorgarh once again became the beating heart of Rajput valor and pride.

Hammir declared Eklingji, as the *true and eternal ruler* of Mewar. This wasn’t metaphorical—it was literal. From that moment on, every ruler of Mewar would govern not as king, but as the Diwan (minister) of Eklingji. This sacred tradition, reinforced the idea that power belonged to the divine, not the mortal. Instilled a sense of duty and humility in the rulers and created a spiritual legitimacy that transcended mere conquest.

This practice continues even today, with the current custodians of Mewar still referring to themselves as Diwan of Eklingji, not monarchs.

Hammir adopted the title Rana, which carries deep meaning in Rajput ethos. Unlike “Raja” or “Maharaja,” *Rana* implies, a protector, not a possessor. One who fights for the safety and honor of his people, without seeking personal gain and a warrior-servant, bound by dharma rather than ambition.

This title became synonymous with the Sisodia dynasty, whose rulers,like Rana Kumbha, Rana Sanga, and Maharana Pratap,embodied this ideal.

The dynasty took its name from Sisoda, Hammir’s ancestral village. It was a way of honoring origin, humility, and continuity. The Sisodias would go on to become one of the most storied Rajput lineages, known for their fierce independence and refusal to bow to foreign powers.

After being expelled from Chittorgarh, Maldeo,still nursing his wounded pride,sought refuge in Delhi and convinced Muhammad bin Tughluq, the Sultan of Delhi, to intervene. Tughluq, known for his erratic campaigns and ambitious reach, sent an army to reclaim Mewar. But Hammir routed Maldeo’s forces once again.

Determined to crush Hammir himself, Tughluq led a massive campaign, halting at Singoli, near Bhilwara in 1336 AD, due to torrential rains. The valley, surrounded by rugged terrain and dense forest, became a trap waiting to be sprung.

Hammir, ever the tactician, allied with the Bhils using their knowledge of the terrain, and launched a guerilla-style assault. The Bhils encircled the valley, cutting off escape routes. Hammir’s forces attacked the camp in waves, exploiting the disarray caused by the rains. The Delhi army, unprepared for mountain warfare and ambush tactics, crumbled under pressure.

According to Muhnot Nainsi, the 17th-century chronicler of Rajasthan, the prisoner taken by Hammir was none other than Muhammad bin Tughluq himself. Nainsi’s account claims the Sultan was held captive for three months in Chittorgarh.He was released only after surrendering Ranthambore, Nagaur, Ajmer, and paying an indemnity of 6 lakh rupees.

However, other historical sources dispute this, suggesting that it may have been a high-ranking commander rather than the Sultan himself. Regardless of the exact identity, the symbolic weight of the victory was immense—it marked the first time a Delhi Sultanate force was decisively defeated and humiliated by a Rajput ruler.

In a twist of fate, Maldeo’s own son, Banbir, joined Hammir after his father’s downfall. With Banbir’s support and the consolidation of loyal clans, Hammir expanded Mewar’s boundaries,northward to the Chambal River,eastward into strategic fortresses like Ranthambore and westward securing trade routes and mountain passes

This territorial expansion not only restored Mewar’s sovereignty but elevated it to a regional power, capable of challenging Delhi and influencing Rajput politics across Rajasthan.

Rana Hammir ruled till 1364, and he was succeeded by his son Kshetra Singh, who conquered Mandalgarh and Ajmer in his rule. Kheta was succeeded by his son Lakha who conquered several territories from Delhi and in 1421 was succeeded by his son Mokhal, who in turn was assasinated by his own brothers Chacha and Mera in 1433.

The assassination of Mokhal sparked outrage across Mewar. The people, nobles, and military turned against the usurpers. Chacha and Mera were forced to flee, and the throne passed to Rana Kumbha, Mokhal’s son.

Kumbha’s ascension wasn’t just a dynastic continuation,it was a restoration of dharma. He would go on to become one of the greatest rulers of Mewar.
