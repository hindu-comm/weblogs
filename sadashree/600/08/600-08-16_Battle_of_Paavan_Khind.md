<div class="body markup" dir="auto">

This moment—when Ali Adil Shah II rallied his forces and partnered with the Mughals—was a defining flashpoint in the Deccan’s power dynamics. The Adil Shahis, having tasted repeated defeats at Shivaji Maharaj’s hands in Pratapgarh and Panhala, had little choice but to throw everything into this campaign.

<div class="captioned-image-container">

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!vZ4Z!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0ad6b2e2-cfdd-4df6-8095-06c2cdecc09b_183x276.jpeg)

</div>

Siddi Masud stepping into the role of commander brings its own intrigue. His connection to Siddi Jauhar isn’t just familial—it’s symbolic, almost like a torch being passed from one seasoned warrior to another. Aligning with the Mughals under Shaista Khan meant facing Shivaji on two fronts, a rare tactical escalation that showed how deeply Shivaji had unsettled the status quo.

Shivaji Maharaj’s decision to bide his time while facing a double siege was a masterclass in psychological warfare. He understood not just terrain and military tactics, but also the human limits of an opposing army stretched thin across multiple fronts.

Narayan Orpe’s loyalty adds a layer of emotional resonance—one Maratha holding the fort while others, aligned with Adilshahi, threatened from within. And in the shadows of this strategic waiting game, the figure of Shivaji prepares for a legendary breakout. The looming exhaustion of enemy supplies becomes more than logistics—it’s a countdown.

He reckoned that along with Baji Prabhu Deshpande, and around 600 of his men, he would be able run through the Adil Shahis in the night, to take advantage of their weary, tired out condition. On the night of July 13, 1660, made his escape from Panhala taking advantage of the darkness, accompanied by Baji Prabhu Deshpande, and others.

Shiva Kashid’s brave decoy act of volunteering to impersonate Shivaji Maharaj is the kind of moment that shows the willingness to risk life not just for a leader, but for the ideal that leader represents. That’s loyalty turned legend.

However the Adil Shahis were soon alerted, and a force of 10,000 began to pursue Shivaji and the fleeing Marathas, who by now were hopelessly outnumbered both against the Adil Shahis and the Mughal garrison at Vishalgadh, making the escape even more precarious.

It wasn’t just the Adil Shahis behind—it was danger ahead too. But Shivaji’s gamble was rooted in psychological tempo: exhausting the enemy’s anticipation, throwing decoys, dividing their focus, and cutting through their fatigue with raw willpower.

Shivaji Maharaj then decided that while a smaller force would engage and hold off the Adil Shahis, the remaining would escape with him to Vishalgadh.

<div class="captioned-image-container">

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!9SoL!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffb27a61d-3693-4c36-b0d2-4e7fd8f550c8_680x510.jpeg)

</div>

This small unit of 300 would be led by Baji Prabhu Deshpande,a giant of a personality, who was a long time follower of Shivaji, playing a major role in his campaigns.

The Danda Patta, slicing through the monsoon air, became more than a weapon, an extension of Baji’s soul, honed by years beside Shivaji, shaped by purpose and a bond unbreakable.

Their mission wasn’t victory, it was time. Every second bought with sweat and valor was a step closer to Vishalgadh’s gates for Shivaji Maharaj. And Baji, larger than life, stood at the very center—a wall of resolve as thunder cracked above and enemies pressed in from all sides.

**Ghor Khind**, that rugged ravine, transformed from mere terrain into sacred ground, where strategy met sacrifice. Its narrow passage forced the 10,000-strong Adil Shahi forces to funnel through like water through a bottleneck, exactly where Baji Prabhu Deshpande stood formidable like a rock.

He occupied the pass, blocking the path of the Adil Shahi pursuers, aided by his brother Fulaji Prabhu as well as sardars such as Shambusing Jadhav. Both Fulaji and Shambhu Singh fell after a fierce resistance

Baji’s defense didn’t just repel the enemy, it bought the much needed time and pushed back with the resolve of 300 souls led by a man whose purpose burned brighter than the storm around him.

In spite of severe injuries, Baji Prabhu kept fighting off the Adil Shahi hordes, waiting for the cannon fire signal from Vishalgadh,that wasn’t just a signal, but symbolized relief, legacy, and sorrow.

And when it finally came, Baji fell on the battlefield, a true warrior, with the satisfaction that the mission was fulfilled.Only a handful of Marathas survived—but they did more than survive, they immortalized the ethos of Maratha resilience.

In the meantime Shivaji Maharaj himself had to face fierce resistance from the Mughals at Vishalgadh, and the Maratha sardars who had joined hands with them. Fighting with the Dandapatta in both hands, Shivaji fought back fiercely, and soon Narayan Orpe, sent his man to bring Maharaj safely into the fort.

The **Maratha counterattack**, led by fresh troops, delivered the final blow to the two-pronged enemy force, crushing the Adil Shahis and flipping the encirclement of Vishalgadh into a moment of reclaiming territory and pride. The traitors Surya Rao and Jaswant Rao’s too were defeated, signifying the rejection of betrayal within the fold.

Baji Prabhu Deshpande's sacrifice was on par with Tanaji Malusare's at Singhad, Shivaji Maharaj renamed Ghor Khind to Paavan Khind( Sacred Pass) in honor of the great man's sacrifice and bravery. And honored his family with maanache pahile paan( Court Honor).

**Sri Aurobindo’s English poem “Baji Prabhu”** is a sweeping, spiritually charged narrative that transforms the historic stand at Pavan Khind into a metaphor for divine duty and national awakening. Written around 1904–1909, it portrays Baji not just as a warrior, but as an instrument of Bhavani, the goddess of India’s destiny. One of the most stirring lines reads:

“We but employ Bhavani’s strength, who in an arm of flesh / Is mighty as in the thunder and the storm.”

**Veer Savarkar’s Marathi ballad** on Baji Prabhu was equally potent. It was so powerful in its nationalist fervor that the British colonial government banned it until 1946. His verses, like:

“तोफे आधी न मरे बाजी, सांगा मृत्यूला”  
(“Baji will not die before the cannon sounds—tell Death to wait”)

...were meant to stir the blood and awaken resistance. Savarkar used Baji’s legacy to ignite the spirit of Swarajya in a new generation.

Baji Prabhu Deshpande, led 300 Marathas against 10,000 Adil Shahis at Paavan Khind, fought till the end, helped his master, Shivaji Maharaj to escape to safety. A true hero, whose sacrifice needs to be celebrated and remembered.
