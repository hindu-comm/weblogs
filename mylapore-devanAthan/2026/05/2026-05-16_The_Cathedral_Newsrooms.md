# The Cathedral Newsrooms

### How Tamil Media Became the PR Wing of Secular Piety

There was a time when newspapers reported news.

Now they perform rituals.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!1HZg!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd5a4547f-0fe6-4596-b7b3-2639fc1c4c10_1774x887.heic)

Every morning, I open certain English dailies and Tamil newspapers not to understand Tamil Nadu politics, but to attend what resembles a carefully choreographed secular mass. The incense is editorial prose. The choir is made up of op-ed writers. The sermon is always predictable: Hindu traditions are suspicious, Leftist slogans are enlightened, Christian symbolism is “inclusive,” and any politician wearing a vibuthi streak must immediately undergo ideological customs clearance.

Among the chief priests of this cathedral journalism are newspapers like *The Hindu*, *The New Indian Express*, and several Tamil publications that have perfected the art of selective outrage.

The recent political ascent of actor Vijay and the formation of the TVK government merely exposed this ecosystem more openly than ever before.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## The Saintly Arrival of Joseph Vijay

> “A political alliance in Tamil Nadu was reported like a papal
> coronation.”

**Source Links:**

- <https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/india/new-era-of-real-secular-social-justice-starts-now-tvk-chief-vijays-first-speech-as-tamil-nadu-cm/articleshow/130987572.cms>

- <https://www.editorialwords.com/the-hindu-lead-article-tamil-nadus-political-transition-growth-story-faultlines-may-13-2026/>

The coverage surrounding Vijay’s government formation was fascinating.

Not because Tamil Nadu got a new Chief Minister.

But because sections of the media behaved as though the Second Coming had arrived through Kodambakkam.

Headlines celebrated a “new secular social justice era.” Editorials described “political transition,” “inclusive governance,” and “a fresh grammar of politics.” The vocabulary itself sounded less like political journalism and more like a baptism ceremony for urban liberals.

Relevant references:

- <https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/india/new-era-of-real-secular-social-justice-starts-now-tvk-chief-vijays-first-speech-as-tamil-nadu-cm/articleshow/130987572.cms>

- <https://www.editorialwords.com/the-hindu-lead-article-tamil-nadus-political-transition-growth-story-faultlines-may-13-2026/>

- <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Tamil_Nadu_Legislative_Assembly_election>

What amused me most was not the praise itself.

Every newspaper has preferences.

But the complete absence of skepticism.

When traditional Dravidian parties stitched alliances, newspapers used words like “opportunism,” “horse-trading,” and “power arithmetic.”

But when Vijay’s government was assembled through outside support, last-minute negotiations, ideological flexibility, and sudden political affection from multiple parties, the language suddenly became poetic.

A political deal transformed into “historic coalition building.”

A trust vote became “democratic maturity.”

Defections became “realignment.”

In Tamil Nadu journalism, vocabulary itself has caste reservations.

## The Selective Secularism Department

> “Nuance in Indian journalism is usually reserved for anti-Hindu
> speech.”

One particular incident exposed the double standards beautifully.

When TVK-linked voices made controversial remarks about Sanatana Dharma, newspapers went into spiritual damage-control mode.

Observe how gently the controversy was handled.

References:

- <https://www.newindianexpress.com/states/tamil-nadu/2026/May/15/tvk-mla-mustafas-remarks-on-sanatana-dharma-invite-bjps-fury>

- <https://www.deccanherald.com/india/tamil-nadu/not-against-any-religion-tvk-mla-retracts-remark-backing-udhayanidhi-on-sanatan-dharma-amid-backlash-4002873>

Notice the framing.

“Not against religion.”

“Against inequality.”

“Misunderstood remarks.”

Imagine for one second a Hindu nationalist MLA saying:

“We are in the field to eradicate another religion.”

Would the same newspapers calmly explain the philosophical context?

Would editorial boards patiently discuss “symbolic resistance against structures of oppression”?

Of course not.

There would be emergency debates, primetime moral lectures, candlelight hashtags, and editorials warning about “majoritarian fascism.”

But when Hindu traditions are mocked, diluted, or caricatured, the same institutions suddenly become students of nuance.

Nuance, in Indian journalism, is usually reserved for anti-Hindu speech.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## The Curious Comfort With Christian Symbolism

> “In elite newsrooms, church symbolism is compassion. Temple symbolism
> is politics.”

Let me say this clearly.

Christian & Muslim citizens are equal stakeholders in India.

But media fascination with Christian symbolism becomes obvious when one compares treatment across religions.

Vijay’s Christmas outreach events, Biblical references, and church interactions were repeatedly presented as proof of compassion and inclusiveness.

Reference:

- <https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/city/chennai/tvk-will-not-compromise-on-religious-harmony-says-vijay/articleshow/126127783.cms>

If a politician quoted the Bhagavad Gita in the same emotional tone, the editorial machinery would immediately begin scanning for “majoritarian undertones.”

This asymmetry is not accidental.

It comes from decades of ideological conditioning within elite newsrooms.

In these circles:

- Hindu symbolism is political.

- Christian symbolism is humanitarian.

- Leftist symbolism is intellectual.

- Temple culture is “problematic.”

- Church activism is “civil society.”

I have noticed this pattern for years.

If a mutt conducts social service, the reporting is cautious.

If an activist church group conducts the same activity, the article acquires emotional violin music.

Tamil Nadu media often reports Christianity through the language of compassion and Hinduism through the language of suspicion.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## The Leftist Lens Never Retired

> “Communism did not disappear. It became editorial culture.”

Many assume communism died with the Soviet Union.

No.

It simply became an editorial internship requirement.

The average urban newsroom in India still functions on intellectual templates inherited from 1970s campus politics.

- Capitalism is exploitative.

- Tradition is oppressive.

- Religion is dangerous.

- Nationalism is vulgar.

- Hindu assertion is frightening.

But identity politics wrapped in secular vocabulary? Wonderful.

This is why newspapers become disproportionately enthusiastic whenever a political formation combines:

- anti-BJP rhetoric,

- minority outreach,

- Dravidian vocabulary,

- and performative secularism.

The moment Vijay began using terms like “social justice” and “secular harmony,” sections of the media adopted him emotionally.

One could almost hear newsroom relief:

“Finally! A mass hero who speaks our language.”

Not Tamil.

Ideological Tamil.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## The Disappearing Questions

📌 **Questions rarely asked loudly enough:**

A journalist’s primary responsibility is suspicion.

Yet several uncomfortable questions around the new political developments received remarkably soft treatment.

Questions like:

- What ideological glue actually binds these alliances?

- Are minority vote banks being consolidated through fear narratives?

- Why are anti-Sanatana remarks repeatedly normalized?

- Why is criticism of Hindu institutions culturally acceptable in elite
  spaces?

- Why are Christian and Leftist influence networks rarely examined with
  equal aggression?

These questions do not disappear because they lack relevance.

They disappear because they threaten the moral architecture of the newsroom.

Modern editorial culture increasingly resembles activism wearing spectacles.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Tamil Newspapers and Their Moral Acrobatics

> “Dravidian rhetoric in editorials. Temple advertisements on the next
> page. Tamil journalism is performance art.”

Tamil newspapers deserve special mention.

Many of them function with breathtaking ideological flexibility.

Depending on the political climate, they can become:

- Dravidian revolutionaries before lunch,

- coalition negotiators by evening,

- and spiritual secularists by night.

One day they ridicule temple traditions.

Next day they publish elaborate astrology supplements.

They attack Brahminical structures in editorials while advertising luxury Vedic wedding halls on the adjacent page.

The business model itself is a philosophical thesis.

Even satire writers cannot compete with this level of accidental comedy.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

Udhayanidhi’s “eradicate Sanatana” remark became “social justice” in Tamil media. TVK MLA Mustafa echoed it. Aadhav Arjuna intellectualized it. Speaker J.C.D. Prabhakar recited Bible verses inside the Assembly — quietly normalized. Once, even mild Hindu symbolism invited outrage. The eternal free-loader K.Veeramani opposes a brahmin minister for education. No media outlet had the guts to even chastise him leave alone condemn. Tamil Nadu’s media secularism now looks less neutral and more selectively anti-Hindu.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## The Myth of Neutral Journalism

*The modern reader no longer distrusts media accidentally. Distrust is now a learned survival instinct.*

I no longer believe newspapers are neutral.

And strangely, I am fine with that.

Every institution has preferences.

The real problem begins when ideological preferences masquerade as moral superiority.

That is where newspapers like *The Hindu* particularly irritate many ordinary readers.

Its editorials often carry the tone of a school principal disappointed with civilization.

The reader is not merely informed.

The reader is corrected.

Educated.

Re-engineered.

The newspaper does not simply report India.

It attempts to ‘improve’ India according to a very specific ideological template.

And this template almost always displays:

- softness toward Leftist discourse,

- suspicion toward Hindu civilizational revival,

- comfort with minority political mobilization,

- and deep anxiety about cultural nationalism.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## The Elite Bubble Problem

A major reason for this disconnect is sociological.

Editorial boards increasingly reflect elite urban echo chambers.

Their social circles, academic references, activist networks, and intellectual ecosystems reinforce identical assumptions.

As a result, they often fail to understand how ordinary Hindus perceive bias.

When temple traditions are mocked repeatedly while minority sensitivities receive extraordinary caution, people notice.

When Hindu practices are dissected sociologically but church politics is treated delicately, people notice.

When secularism becomes a one-way traffic signal, simple people like me notice.

Readers are not fools.

They may not write op-eds.

But they possess pattern recognition.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## My Problem Is Not Ideology

> “Readers can tolerate ideology. What they reject is disguised ideology
> pretending to be neutrality.”

Let me end with an uncomfortable confession.

My issue is not that newspapers lean Left.

My issue is dishonesty.

If a newspaper openly declared:

“Yes, we believe in Left-liberal politics and minority-centered secularism,”

I would actually respect the transparency.

But instead, newspapers often present ideological preferences as objective morality.

That is what creates distrust.

And distrust is now visible everywhere.

People increasingly consume news through independent creators, YouTube channels, satire pages, WhatsApp forwards, and alternative media ecosystems because they no longer believe institutional journalism tells the full story.

Traditional newspapers created this crisis themselves.

By preaching neutrality while practicing selective sensitivity.

By romanticizing some identities and pathologizing others.

By treating Hindu assertion as inherently suspicious while decorating every other ideological movement with intellectual sophistication.

In the end, newspapers are free to choose their politics.

Readers are equally free to choose skepticism.

And judging by the growing public cynicism toward mainstream media, that skepticism is no longer a fringe emotion.

It is becoming mainstream.
