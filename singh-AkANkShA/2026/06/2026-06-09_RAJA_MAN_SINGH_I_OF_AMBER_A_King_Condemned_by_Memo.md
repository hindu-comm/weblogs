History is often cruel to those who are too great to be confined within simple narratives. It remembers some men through their achievements and others through the stories later generations choose to tell about them. Few figures illustrate this truth more clearly than Raja Man Singh I of Amber. For over four centuries, his name has been criticized, debated, and condemned in equal measure. Yet remarkably, the man who commanded armies across half the subcontinent, governed vast territories, built magnificent temples, patronized Hindu learning, protected sacred sites, and became one of the most powerful Rajput rulers of his age is today remembered by many for a single battle and a single political association.

In popular imagination, Raja Man Singh has often been reduced to the role of the Rajput general who fought against Maharana Pratap at Haldighati. Everything else—the statesman, the administrator, the conqueror, the builder, the patron of religion, and the protector of Hindu institutions—has frequently been pushed into the background. Generations of nationalist writers, folk traditions, and modern commentators have transformed a complex historical figure into a simplistic symbol. To some, he became the embodiment of compromise; to others, a byword for collaboration. Over time, the accusations grew stronger than the evidence, and the myth became more familiar than the man himself. Yet history is not a courtroom where men can be judged according to the political sentiments of centuries they never lived to see.

*And to understand Raja Man Singh, one must first understand the India into which he was born.*

The middle decades of the sixteenth century were marked by extraordinary political upheaval. By the time Man Singh reached adulthood, Mughal authority was no longer a distant possibility—it was an undeniable reality. The Rajput kingdoms faced a challenge unprecedented in scale. Different rulers chose different paths, each according to the circumstances they faced. None operated within the simplistic categories that modern debates often impose upon them. The Kachhwahas of Amber had already made their choice before Man Singh was born. The alliance forged by Raja Bharmal in 1562 was not an act of surrender by a defeated house but a carefully crafted political settlement. Through diplomacy rather than destruction, Amber secured a position that preserved its ruling family, protected its customs, ensured the continuation of Hindu religious practices, and elevated the Kachhwahas to unprecedented prominence. It was within this political framework that Raja Man Singh rose to prominence.

What followed was a career of remarkable achievement. He emerged as one of the most trusted commanders of his age, leading campaigns across Bengal, Bihar, Orissa, Kabul, and beyond. He defeated Afghan chieftains who had long challenged imperial authority. He secured strategic frontiers, governed some of the empire's most difficult provinces, and demonstrated military and administrative abilities that earned him immense prestige. His influence extended far beyond the battlefield and military success was only one aspect of his legacy. The image of Man Singh presented by his critics often ignores another equally important reality: he remained throughout his life a devoted patron of Hindu religion and culture. The same man accused of betraying Hindu interests sponsored temples, supported Brahmins, patronized Sanskrit scholarship, and invested enormous resources into sacred architecture. Some of the most magnificent Hindu temples associated with the late medieval period bear testimony to his patronage. His contributions to religious and cultural life were neither symbolic nor occasional; they formed a central part of his public identity.

Perhaps the greatest irony in the criticism directed toward Raja Man Singh is that many who condemn him rarely examine the practical consequences of the policies they denounce. They seldom ask what became of Hindu society in territories governed by the Kachhwahas. They rarely study the temples that survived under their patronage, the customs they protected, the religious institutions they funded, or the influence they exercised at the imperial court on behalf of Hindu interests. Instead, a complex historical reality is often reduced to a moral slogan.

*What such criticisms overlook is the brutal world these rulers inhabited. The men of that age had witnessed kingdoms destroyed, cities devastated, temples desecrated, and entire populations displaced in the aftermath of military defeat. They understood, perhaps better than anyone, the consequences of political failure. Their decisions were shaped not by romantic visions of history but by the responsibility of ensuring the survival of their people, their traditions, and their states in a dangerous and unpredictable world.*

**The Life and Career of Raja Man Singh I**

Raja Man Singh I was born approximately in 1550 CE, the son of Kuar Bhagwant Das and the grandson of Raja Bharamal of Amber. His lineage was among the most ancient and prestigious in all of Rajputana. The Kachhwahas traced their ancestry directly to the Raghu dynasty — the solar line from which Lord Rama himself had descended — making them among the most spiritually significant of all Rajput clans. Their capital, Amber, sat on a commanding rocky ridge in what is today the Jaipur district of Rajasthan, its fortifications overlooking a shimmering lake that reflected the grandeur of the Kachhwaha house back at itself.

The ancestry of Man Singh is itself a matter of some scholarly complexity. Contemporary Muslim historians including Khwaja Nizamuddin Ahmad, Shaikh Abdul Qadir Badaoni, and Muhammad Qasim Firishta all record that Man Singh was the son of Raja Bhagwan Das. Abul Fazl, the court historian of Akbar, notes that Man Singh was the son of Raja Bhagwant Das. The Khyats of Banki Das confirm that Man Singh was the son of Bhagwant Das. The Kachhwaha’s Vansawali by Krishna Rai notes that Raja Bhagwant Das had five sons and two daughters, of whom Man Singh was the eldest. The Stone Pillar inscription at Ramgarh, constructed by Man Singh himself and translated by Dr. Gupta, confirms the lineage in the most authoritative terms possible: *the Raja describes himself as a descendant of the Raghu dynasty, the gem among the Kachhwahas, son of the great Bhagwant Das.*

The young Man Singh was brought up in the Mughal court almost from childhood, growing close to Prince Salim (later Emperor Jahangir). He received training both in the arts of war and in the courtly culture of the Mughal empire, under Akbar.

**The Battle of Haldighati (1576) -**

No single episode has done more damage to the reputation of Raja Man Singh than his role as commander of the Mughal forces at the Battle of Haldighati in 1576 and perhaps his greatest misfortune. Popular memory has often reduced this complex historical event to a simplistic tale of heroes and villains, and Man Singh as a traitor who fought against a fellow Hindu on behalf of Akbar. Yet a careful examination of contemporary sources presents a far more nuanced reality.

While Man Singh led the imperial army at Haldighati, his conduct during and after the campaign reveals that he never viewed Maharana Pratap as an enemy deserving humiliation or destruction. His objective was to fulfill his military duty, not to devastate Mewar or erase its ruler.

One of the most overlooked aspects of the campaign is what happened after the battle, Man Singh did not allow the Mughal army to pursue the retreating forces of Maharana Pratap. Contemporary chroniclers such as Badaoni and Abul Fazl attribute the lack of pursuit to the intense heat and exhaustion of the troops. However, several sources suggest a deeper reason. Maharana Pratap continued to command immense respect among the Kachhwaha Rajputs, and Man Singh had no desire to subject him to unnecessary harassment or indignity.

This respect was reflected in his strict orders regarding Mewar. Sources such as Tabaqat-i-Akbari clearly state that Kunwar Man Singh prohibited his soldiers from plundering or devastating the territories of Maharana Pratap. Lakshmi Narain and other historians also note that he expressly forbade looting in the region. This was not a symbolic gesture. The Mughal forces themselves were facing severe shortages of food and supplies, and such restrictions increased the hardships of the army. Yet Man Singh refused to permit the kind of dedestruction His actions came at a personal cost. Contemporary accounts indicate that Akbar was displeased with Man Singh's conduct. The Akbarnama records the emperor's growing suspicion regarding the commitment of his Rajput commanders in dealing with Maharana Pratap. Badaoni further mentions that Man Singh and Asaf Khan were, for a time, denied the privilege of appearing before the emperor. Imperial honors were withheld, and even the standing of Man Singh's father was affected.

*The emperor's dissatisfaction became even more evident when Man Singh was recalled and replaced by Shahbaz Khan. Significantly, after Haldighati, Akbar never again entrusted a Rajput commander with the task of subduing Maharana Pratap. This decision itself reflects the emperor's doubts regarding the willingness of Rajput nobles to pursue the Rana with complete ruthlessness.*

*The common accusation that Raja Man Singh betrayed his people ignores these crucial facts. A traitor does not risk imperial displeasure to protect the lands of the very ruler he is accused of betraying. A traitor does not forbid plunder when his own soldiers are suffering from scarcity. Nor does a traitor preserve the dignity of an adversary at the cost of his standing at court.*

History is rarely as simple as later generations wish it to be. Raja Man Singh and Maharana Pratap stood on opposite sides of a battlefield, but that does not reduce their relationship to one of hatred or betrayal. The historical record suggests something far more complex: a Rajput prince carrying out his obligations while still striving to protect the honor, dignity, and homeland of another Rajput ruler whom he deeply respected.

**The Northwest Frontier: Kabul, Punjab, and the Afghan Tribes (1578–1587) -**Among Raja Man Singh’s most strategically consequential contributions was his decade-long service on the northwest frontier, the territories that today form the eastern regions of Afghanistan and the northwest of Pakistan. This period — from approximately 1578 to 1587 — shaped him into the foremost military commander of the Mughal age and brought him into direct conflict with a series of formidable Afghan tribal confederacies.

The principal adversaries Man Singh confronted and subdued on the northwest frontier were five distinct Afghan power-blocs, each controlling strategic territories in what is today Pakistan and Afghanistan:

First, the Yusufzais: one of the most powerful and warlike of all the Pashtun tribes, the Yusufzais under their leader Jalala controlled the mountain passes and defiled the country between Peshawar and Tirah. In December 1586, as Man Singh was marching through the Khybar region, near guards of the Imperialists led by Takhta Beg were attacked by Jalala, the leader of the Yusufzais. A fierce fight ensued. Kuar Man Singh, who was in the van, sent re-inforcements. The Afghans were defeated and they fled away. Man Singh then pressed through the Khybar Pass with 3,000 horses, defeated the Afridis and collected much booty.

Second, the Afridis: guardians of the Khybar Pass from Peshawar to Tirah, the Afridis were among the most ferocious of all Afghan tribes. On 15th December 1586, an army under Muhammad Quli Beg, a general in Man Singh’s camp, attacked the Afridis, defeated them, and collected much booty. Man Singh personally pressed through their territory despite snowed-up mountain defiles, crossing the Chahar Chobah pass under the most arduous conditions.

Third, the Ghori tribes: encountered during Man Singh’s advance through the Afghan hill country, the Ghori tribes saved themselves by submitting as the Imperialist forces passed through their homelands. The Mahmud and Ghori tribes in conjunction with other Afghans attacked the caravan led by the Kuar when he fell ill at Pesh Bulaq near Jalalabad. Man Singh gradually recovered and made preparations to punish the Afghans, eventually routing them.

Fourth, the Tarikis (Raushanias): a fanatical religious and political movement of the northwest frontier, the Tarikis posed a grave ideological and military challenge to Mughal authority. Man Singh was specifically recalled from Kabul to chastise the Tarikis. Abul Fazl noted that Kuar Man Singh was appointed to chastise the Tarikis. Vigorous attempts were made to extirpate the Tarikis during Man Singh’s governorship. Zain Khan Kokah arrived with his army to assist in this campaign, and the combined Mughal forces eventually suppressed the Tariki movement in the region.

Fifth, the Afghan confederacy of Kabul: upon his arrival at Kabul in 1585 following the death of Mirza Muhammad Hakim, Man Singh found confusion reigning supreme as the nobles of Kabul had brought the administration of the country under their control. The arrival of Kuar Man Singh marked an era of peace and tranquillity in Kabul. Iqbalnamah reports that a large number of Kabulis came to see the imperial commander. The Kuar overpowered the Kabuli nobles, comforted the population, and assured them of imperial protection. De Laet confirms: Mamet Hakim, ruler of Kabul died of disease and Raja Man Singh, a Rajput by race and a commander of 5000 horses, was dispatched to turn that kingdom into a province.

The geographic reach of Man Singh’s northwest campaigns covered all the major strategic positions of the modern Pakistan-Afghanistan border region: Attock (Atak Banaras) on the Indus, Peshawar, the Khybar Pass, Jalalabad, Kabul, the Sind-Sagar Doab, Rawalpindi, and the approaches to Badakshan. By 1587, when he was recalled and transferred to the Eastern Provinces . He had successfully administered Kabul for over two years (August 1585 to December 1587), bringing an end to decades of disorder on the most dangerous frontier of the empire.

**BIHAR AND BENGAL: EIGHTEEN YEARS OF EMPIRE-BUILDING AND DHARMA -**The eighteen years from 1587 to 1605 that Raja Man Singh spent as governor of the Eastern Provinces represent the most consequential period of his career — both as a military commander and as a patron of Hindu civilisation. He arrived in Bihar to find a province in disorder, its revenue systems disrupted, its territory contested by a series of powerful Afghan chiefs who had resisted Mughal authority for decades. He left it transformed: pacified, administered, and marked permanently by his extraordinary programme of temple construction, city-founding, and religious patronage. The task he faced was formidable.

*Abul Fazl summarised it with characteristic precision: When the Raja was sent from the Court to the province of Bihar, he united ability with courage and took care of the country with the wisdom and mildness of a father*.

The principal obstacle was the power of the Afghan chiefs of Bihar and Bengal, of whom the most formidable was Isa Khan of the Baro-Bhuiyans — a confederacy of twelve chieftains. Man Singh systematically dismantled this confederacy through a combination of military force and shrewd diplomacy, capturing forts, defeating armies, and co-opting those chiefs willing to submit on acceptable terms. His governance of Bengal produced equally dramatic results. Man Singh transferred the capital of Bengal from the declining city of Tanda to the newly founded Akbarnagar (later Rajmahal) in 1595, building a palace for himself, erecting a strong rampart with bastions which encircled the city, opening a mint where silver, copper and gold coins were issued, and creating a new administrative centre on the banks of the Ganges.

Yet it is not the military campaigns or the administrative achievements that most distinguish Man Singh’s eastern years. It is the religious and cultural transformation he wrought across two great provinces of India. During his eighteen years as governor, he oversaw the construction or restoration of a large number of temples which are still extant in different subdivisions of the states of Bihar and Bengal — *as Mr. J. D. Beglar, the Archaeological Survey officer who toured the region in 1872–73, documented with astonishment. The individual temple accounts will be given in the section that follows, but the aggregate picture they paint is one of extraordinary religious energy: a Mughal governor who used the revenues of two of the richest provinces in Asia partly to build Hindu temples from the eastern extremity of Bengal to the banks of the Damodar river.*

**Religious Patronage and the Defence of Hindu Dharma -** A crucial question in the historiography of Raja Man Singh concerns his personal religious identity. Raha Man Singh was a follower of Vaishnavism and was an orthodox Hindu, a staunch believer in the conventional forms and rites of Hinduism. The pictures of Radha and Krishna painted on the sandal shutters of his personal apartments at Amber palace, and his construction of the Jagatsiromany temple at Amber installing the images of Radha and Girdhar Gopal — these are expressions of a heartfelt Vaishnava piety. Yet his temple-building programme was far broader than any single sectarian affiliation, encompassing Shaiva, Vaishnava, and Shakta shrines across the length and breadth of India.

What is beyond dispute is his role as a protector of Brahminical institutions. Every major construction project he undertook was supervised by Brahmin priests. The stone inscription in the fort of Rohtas identifies Sridhar, the family priest of Raja Man Singh, as the Daroga of construction works. His fort of Ramgarh was built under the supervision of the Brahmin Purohit Pitamber. This consistent association of Brahmin supervisors with construction work was a deliberate policy and a special feature of his patronage.

The survey tours conducted by Mr. J. D. Beglar, Assistant of the Archaeological Survey Department of India, in the year 1872–73, revealed an astonishing fact: Raja Man Singh was responsible for the construction and repair of a large number of temples which are still extant in different subdivisions of the states of Bihar and Bengal. This finding, combined with the evidence from Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh, and Orissa, establishes Man Singh as perhaps the single most prolific builder of Hindu temples in sixteenth-century India. Below is a systematic account of each temple and religious edifice associated with his patronage.

**THE TEMPLES OF MAN SINGH: STONE PRAYERS ACROSS A CONTINENT -**Raja Man Singh built temples the way other great rulers built armies — with vision, resources, and an almost competitive urgency. The temples he constructed, endowed, and restored span the entire subcontinent from the northwest frontier of modern Pakistan to the deltaic plains of Bengal, from the sacred ghats of Kashi to the jungle temples of Orissa. Each one was an act of devotion; together they constitute one of the most extraordinary programmes of Hindu temple patronage in the entire medieval period.

**Govind Dev Temple, Vrindavan (c. 1577–1590)-** Of all the temples built by Raja Man Singh, none is more celebrated or more architecturally significant than the Govind Dev Temple at Vrindavan (Brindaban), located approximately five miles north of Mathura in the state of Uttar Pradesh. This temple, dedicated to Lord Krishna in the form of Govinddeva or Lord Krishna, was described by the eminent historian Growse as the most impressive religious edifice that Hindu art has ever produced, at least in Upper India. The European architectural historian Fergusson considered it one of the most interesting and eloquent temples in India and the only one from which a European architect might borrow a few hints. The temple was built at a cost of half a million rupees — an almost unimaginable sum for the sixteenth century — funded personally by the Kachhwaha Raja. The construction was apparently begun in 1577, when Man Singh was still a minor mansabdar (holding the rank of Kuar), and was completed around 1590 by which time he had established preponderant influence in the Mughal Court.

The structure is built entirely of red stone and consists of a portico in the form of a cross measuring 117 feet east to west and 105 feet north to south, covered by an arched roof built with radiating arches. The four sides of the portico are roofed by a vault of pointed form, consisting of overlapping brackets. The Govind Dev Temple is an imposing structure and may be considered as one of the major contributions of Raja Man Singh in the field of architecture.

**The Kashi Vishwanath Temple Complex, Varanasi (Banaras) -** Of all the sacred cities of Hinduism, none surpasses Kashi — the eternal city of Lord Shiva, also called Varanasi and Banaras — in sanctity. It is Vishwanathpuri, the city of the Lord of the Universe, and Hindus believe that those who die there go straight to heaven, for the city itself is the embodiment of moksha. That Raja Man Singh chose to build his greatest temple in this city, at enormous personal expense, is the single most powerful statement of his Hindu faith.

Emperor Jahangir recorded in his Memoirs that Man Singh spent eight to ten lacs of rupees from his father Akbar’s treasury on the temple. *A reliable man sent to enquire into the actual expenditure found that the Raja had additionally spent one lac rupees of his own. The Jaipur Vansawali confirms: Raja Man Singh built a big temple at Banaras for the worship of the people. Jahangir himself declared that at this time there is no other bigger temple than this, and so moved was he by its magnificence that he ordered a bigger temple to be constructed near it.*

A building called Man Mandir — the Temple of Man — still stands on the bank of the Ganges, just a few yards to the west of Dasaswamedh Ghat, one of the most sacred bathing ghats in all of Kashi. This is almost certainly the same temple mentioned in the Jahangirnamah. The significance of this temple for Hindu society cannot be overstated. In the sixteenth century, Kashi was under Mughal governance and Hindu temple-building was possible only with imperial sanction.

*Man Singh used the full weight of his imperial standing to put Mughal resources in the service of Hindu worship at the very heart of the dharmic universe. Around 1600, Raja Man Singh spearheaded the renovation of nearly 1,000 temples, helping restore Kashi to its ancient glory as the celebrated "City of Temples.”*

**The Jagatsiromany Temple, Amber -** The Jagatsiromany temple at Amber is described by those who have seen it as one of the brilliant specimens of Raja Man Singh’s architecture and the most beautiful piece of architecture in Jaipur.

The temple is situated on a raised platform about fifteen feet in height and is approached by a long and stiff staircase. There is a big entrance gate called Toran Dwar, which is highly artistic and full of beautiful sculptures. Two big elephants made of marble are placed on either side of the gate. There is a Garura temple in front of the main temple with images of Radha-Krishna, Shankar, Vishnu, Bhagwati, Ganesh and others carved on its gate. The main sanctum contains the images of Radha and Girdhar Gopal and that of Lord Vishnu. The figures of Lord Vishnu and Radha are made of white stone, but the image of Girdhar Gopal is carved out of jet black stone. There is a separate temple of Shitalajee (Mother Goddess) to the east of the main temple, demonstrating the breadth of Man Singh’s religious patronage across Vaishnava, Shaiva, and Shakta traditions.

According to local tradition, the main temple cost Raja Man Singh a sum of 9 lacs and 72 thousand rupees. The Kachhwaha’s Vansawali notes that the Jagatsiromany temple was built by Rani Kanakawati to perpetuate the memory of her son Jagat Singh, confirming that this was a royal family project under Man Singh’s overall direction and patronage.

**The Temples at Baidyanath Dham (Deoghar), Bihar -**Baidyanath Dham, one of the twelve Jyotirlingas of Lord Shiva and an all-India pilgrimage centre of the highest importance, contains a group of temples enclosed within a paved courtyard. Mr. Beglar, during his survey tour of 1872–73, concluded that these temples were either built by Raja Man Singh or belong to the period of his governorship. As Beglar observed: there is a distinct record of a Hindu general Man Singh exercising supreme authority in those parts during Akbar’s reign; I consider it most probable that to Man Singh’s period these temples owe their construction.

The fact that Man Singh, while serving as a Mughal general, personally visited one of the most sacred Shaiva pilgrimage sites in India and ensured that its temples were built or restored, speaks volumes about his priorities as a patron of Hinduism.

**The Gauri Shankar Temple, Baikatpur, Bihar -** At the village of Baikatpur in Bihar, Raja Man Singh built a temple whose chief deity is Gauri Shankar Mahadeva, a single piece of black stone depicting both Lord Shiva (Shankar-Mahadeva) and Mother Gauri (his consort). This unique form gives the temple its name: Gauri Shankar Temple. The architecture of the temple is purely in Hindu style and is unique in some respects. The whole outer wall of the temple is divided into a number of lines in triangular form at mathematically well-planned and measured distances. The petals of the lotus flower, the attractive circular domes, and the beautiful Baradari are indicative of the fact that the builder of the temple — Raja Man Singh — was a man of highly artistic taste.

As is usually the case with Raja Man Singh’s temples, there is an image of Lord Vishnu also in the main sanctum, alongside Gauri Shankar, demonstrating a characteristic integration of Shaiva and Vaishnava worship. A small image of Lord Ganesh is placed in a niche at the top of the main entrance. There is also the image of Shitalajee (Mother Goddess) in a separate temple to the east of the main temple. On all sides of the temple except the north, there are open terraces made of stone and 20 ft. wide. There is a big open pucca courtyard in front of the temple, enclosed by terraces on three sides, with a Yagyasala (sacrificial hall) in the centre. Beautiful pucca ghats still extant on the bank where once flowed the river Ganges testify to the scale of his provision for Hindu pilgrimage.

A parwana (royal decree) in the possession of the chief Priest of the Baikatpur temple clearly indicates that Raja Man Singh had made ample provision for the maintenance of the temple, endowing it with land grants and revenues.

**The Temples at Para and Telkupi, Manbhum District - I**n the village Para in the headquarters subdivision of the formerly existing district of Manbhum in the State of Bihar, Mr. Beglar discovered two temples situated to the east of and just outside the village. One was made of brick and the other of a soft kind of stone — both much weathered and broken but full of interesting architectural and archaeological features. The sculptured figures executed in the plaster coat and the architectural repairs were done during the time of Raja Man Singh, as confirmed by the Manbhum District Gazetteer.

At the village of Telkupi, situated on the south bank of the Damodar river in the district of Manbhum in the State of Bengal, Beglar saw a group of temples within a small space and noticed that extensive repairs and alterations were done in those temples. The ornamentation executed in the plaster coat was consistent with Man Singh’s other temple repairs. This has been accepted by Mr. Coupland, writer of Manbhum District Gazetteer, who affirms that both the temples at Para were repaired during the time of Raja Man Singh, probably by one Sri Purushottam Das of Brindaban.

**Temples in Rohtas Fort, Bihar -** The fort of Rohtas, which Raja Man Singh held as his jagir for approximately twenty years and which served as his administrative headquarters in Bihar, contained a series of temples. The stone inscription in the fort of Rohtas records that Priest Sridhar, Daroga Balbhadra, and architect Ustad Mubarak were associated with the repairs of the fort and its temples. While the idols have been removed by unknown invaders and looters in subsequent centuries, the structural evidence confirms that Man Singh built and maintained temples within this important frontier fortress.

**Temple Complex at Manpur and Other Bengal Locations -**In other temples of Raja Man Singh in Bengal, for example at Baikatpur and Manpur, the chief deities are Siva or Mahavir (Hanuman). These temples were constructed or patronised during the long period of Man Singh’s governorship of Bengal and Bihar (1587–1605), when the Raja systematically used his position to build and endow Hindu worship sites across the eastern provinces. The fact that these temples honoured both Vaishnava (Mahavir/Hanuman) and Shaiva deities confirms that his patronage was broadly Hindu rather than sectarian.

**The Siva Ganga Tank at Baidyanath Dham-** Along the road leading from the northern gate of the great temple of Baidyanath Dham passes along the western edge of a large tank called Siva Ganga, which measures about 900 feet by 600 feet. The western portion of this tank has been cut off by an embankment on top of which runs a road. The Santhal Parganas District Gazetteer notes that the embankment was put up by Raja Man Singh, as the name of the Raja is associated with the western portion which is still called Man Sarovar. While this portion has now silted up greatly, its original dimensions confirm the enormous scale of Man Singh’s contribution to the infrastructure of Hindu pilgrimage.

**The Mangarh Fort and Temples, Manihari, Bihar-** Raja Man Singh built a small fort at Manihari, located north of Godda Subdivision in the district of Santhal Parganas in Bihar, when he encamped there during his conquest of Eastern Bengal from the Afghans. He built a fort called Mangarh (named after him). The village of Manihari contains several large tanks, at present silted up, from the beds of which, as well as from other places in the village, images carved in stone and other architectural remains have been obtained. These are all associated with the memory of Raja Man Singh, confirming that his military encampments invariably gave rise to permanent religious and architectural structures.

**Jagannath Puri and Bengal: Liberation, Pilgrimage, and Devotion -** The Liberation of Jagannath Puri (1590–1592)

The temple of Lord Jagannath at Puri (Orissa) is one of the four sacred dhams of Hinduism — the Char Dham pilgrimage that every devout Hindu aspires to complete before death. It is, along with Badrinath, Dwarka, and Rameswaram, one of the holiest sites in all of India. Yet for decades before Raja Man Singh’s intervention, this supreme centre of Hindu worship had been under the oppressive control of the Afghan chiefs of Bengal and Orissa, who had exercised a cruel and tyrannous sway over the priests and pilgrims of Jagannath.

The background to Man Singh’s liberation campaign lies in the political chaos of Bengal and Orissa in the late sixteenth century. The Afghan chiefs — the most powerful being Qutlu Khan and his successor Nasir — controlled vast territories including the Jagannath zone, defying Mughal authority and imposing their will on the Hindu religious establishment. The priests of the great Jagannath temple lived under perpetual fear; the sanctity of the shrine was hostage to Afghan political power.

Raja Man Singh undertook the expedition for the conquest of Orissa in April, 1590. His attention had been riveted on Orissa because peace and order could not be maintained on a sound footing in Bihar unless order was also ensured in the adjoining province, which had been the abode of the rebellious Afghans. Man Singh had enough experience of the mischievous exploits of the Afghans in the north-west frontier regions of India, and it was with this knowledge of their manners and habits that he undertook the expedition.

Man Singh sent his eldest son, Kuar Jagat Singh, with a strong contingent to subdue the Afghans under Bahadur Kurah near Orissa. However the young Kuar did not appreciate the danger properly. In the meantime, Qutlu Khan, the Afghan leader, sent a large force against the Kuar’s camp. The Mughal forces were badly defeated and a rumour spread that the Kuar was killed. This news made Raja Man Singh very furious. He immediately held a council of war, rejected the cowardly advice of his councillors to retreat, and resolved upon fighting. A few days later Qutlu died of illness in August 1590, leaving the Afghans bewildered and anxious to patch up peace with the Raja’s army.

Khwaja Isa, the wakil of the late Afghan leader, made Nasir the successor to Qutlu Khan and opened negotiations for peace with Raja Man Singh. Most significantly for Hindu religious history, the Afghans also ceded the temple of Jagannath at Puri along with the surrounding districts to the Mughal Emperor. On 15th August 1590, Nasir, the new Afghan king, came to pay homage to Raja Man Singh and presented 150 elephants and other choice articles.

The liberation of Jagannath Puri from Afghan control was a moment of profound religious significance. Man Singh was much pleased at rescuing the holy city of Jagannath from the hands of the turbulent Afghans who had long exercised a cruel and tyrannous sway over the priests. This was not a remote administrative concern for the Raja — it was a deeply personal religious achievement. In June 1592, after the formal submission of Raja Ramchandra of Khurda (the ruler of the Puri region), *Raja Man Singh personally visited the holy city of Jagannathpuri and paid his obeisance to the famous shrine. This pilgrimage visit was the act of a devout Hindu fulfilling a sacred obligation, not merely a military governor inspecting conquered territory.*

*The long-term significance of this liberation for Hindu society was immense. The Jagannath temple at Puri is not merely one shrine among many: it is the organisational centre of a vast pilgrimage network spanning all of India. Its liberation from Afghan oppression restored to hundreds of thousands of Hindu pilgrims the freedom and safety to complete one of the most sacred journeys in their religious lives.*

**Bengal: Temples, Shrines, and the Religious Landscape-**

Man Singh’s nearly two-decade governance of Bengal (1587–1605) was characterised not only by military pacification but by extensive religious construction and patronage across a vast territory. Mr. J. D. Beglar, conducting his landmark survey tour of the Bengal Province in 1872–73, recorded that Raja Man Singh was responsible for the construction and repair of a large number of temples which are still extant in different subdivisions of the states of Bihar and Bengal. The scale of this programme — spanning temples, tanks, ghats, roads, and endowments — was without parallel among the provincial governors of the Mughal period.

In Baidyanath Dham, one of the twelve Jyotirlingas of Lord Shiva and an all-India pilgrimage centre, Beglar discovered a group of temples enclosed within a paved courtyard that he attributed to Man Singh’s period. The road leading from the northern gate of the great temple of Baidyanath Dham passes along the western edge of the large tank called Siva Ganga, measuring approximately 900 by 600 feet. The western portion of this tank was constructed by Raja Man Singh, and it is still known as Man Sarovar — the Lake of Man Singh. This infrastructure of tanks, embankments, and ghats was the physical expression of Man Singh’s commitment to making pilgrimage accessible and dignified for ordinary Hindus.

In the village of Dhad Ki Taur in the district of Manbhum, Beglar found a large temple in an enclosure 120 feet square, surrounded by seven smaller subordinate temples. From the general appearance and mouldings, the learned archaeologist ascribed these temples to the period of Raja Man Singh’s governorship of Bengal. In Patna, Buchanan noted in his survey report of 1811–12 that the Chotti Pattandevi temple was placed in its present situation by Raja Man Singh while that noble was the governor of Bihar. These incidental mentions across multiple District Gazetteers and survey reports confirm the extraordinary geographic spread of Man Singh’s temple patronage across Bengal and Bihar.

**Structures in the Northwest: Attock, Rohtas (Pakistan Region)-** Raja Man Singh’s architectural legacy extends to territories that today fall within the borders of Pakistan. His decade on the northwest frontier left permanent physical marks on the landscape of the Indus region.

Rohtas Fort (in present-day Punjab province of Pakistan) was one of Man Singh’s most important administrative headquarters during his Bihar tenure and held immense strategic value as a frontier garrison. Stone inscriptions confirm that the fort was built and repaired under Man Singh’s supervision, with the family priest Sridhar as Daroga and architect Ustad Mubarak carrying out the works. The dates in the Persian and Sanskrit inscriptions (1005 A.H. or 1596 A.D. and 1654 V.S. or 1597 A.D.) indicate the construction spanned the period from 1587 to 1597. The palace and other structures at Rohtas are rightly described as works of great architectural beauty. Mr. L.S.S.O’ Malley observed: The palace at Rohtas is of unique interest as being the only specimen of Mughal civil architecture in the region. Temples within the fort complex were endowed by Man Singh, though their idols were subsequently removed by unknown looters.

**The Ramgarh Fort and Associated Structures, Rajasthan-**

Raja Man Singh also built the fort of Ramgarh in Rajasthan, laid out a garden within it, and sank a well inside the garden. This was carried out under the supervision of Purohit Pitamber, the son of the reverend Purohit Padmakar. The stone inscription in the fort of Rohtas states that Priest Sridhar, Daroga Balbhadra, and architect Ustad Mubarak were associated with the works, confirming the consistent pattern of Brahmin priests as supervisors of all Man Singh’s construction works.

**Rajasthan (Amber State)**

1\. Jagatsiromany Temple, Amber — magnificent four-storeyed marble Vaishnava temple in Khujuraho style; cost Rs. 9 lacs 72 thousand; images of Radha, Girdhar Gopal, Lord Vishnu, and Shitalajee.

2\. Amber Palace (Diwan-i-Am and personal apartments) — commenced by Man Singh, completed by Jai Singh I; prayer room, Tulsi platform, Radha-Krishna paintings.

3\. Ramgarh Fort with a well and garden — built under Brahmin Purohit Pitamber’s supervision.

4\. Mughal Gateway at Bairat — stucco paintings depicting Sanskrit epic scenes and Mughal court scenes.

5\. Images of Sango Baba (Sanganer), Hanuman (Jaipur), and Sila Devi (Amber) — sacred images brought and installed by Man Singh.

**Uttar Pradesh**

6\. Govind Dev Temple (Govinddeva), Vrindavan, Mathura — the greatest Vaishnava temple of its era; built at a cost of half a million rupees; red stone; dimensions 117 ft. x 105 ft.; architect Govinddas of Delhi; described by Growse as the most impressive religious edifice Hindu art has produced in Upper India.

7\. Temple at Varanasi (Kashi / Banaras) — built at a cost of 8–10 lacs from imperial treasury plus 1 lac personal funds; no other bigger temple at its time; Man Mandir described by a Muslim traveller as so magnificent he wished it were built for Islam.

**Bihar**

8\. Temples at Baidyanath Dham (Deoghar) — group of temples within a paved courtyard at one of the twelve Jyotirlingas; attributed to Man Singh’s governorship by Beglar’s archaeological survey.

9\. Gauri Shankar Temple, Baikatpur — chief deity Gauri Shankar Mahadeva (Shiva-Parvati combined form in black stone); also images of Vishnu, Ganesh, Shitalajee; beautiful domes and Baradari; paved courtyard 150 ft. x 80 ft.; pucca ghats on the Ganges; endowed with land grants and revenue provisions by royal decree (parwana).

10\. Chotti Pattandevi Temple, Patna — placed in its present situation by Raja Man Singh while governor of Bihar (attested by Buchanan’s 1811–12 Patna District survey).

11\. Temples at Rohtas Fort — built and repaired within the fort complex; idols subsequently removed by unknown invaders.

12\. Temples at Para, Manbhum District — two temples (one brick, one stone); repaired during Man Singh’s governorship, probably by Sri Purushottam Das of Brindaban; confirmed by Manbhum District Gazetteer.

13\. Temples at Telkupi, Manbhum District — group of temples on south bank of Damodar river; extensive repairs and alterations in Man Singh’s period; confirmed by Manbhum District Gazetteer.

14\. Temple complex at Dhad Ki Taur, Manbhum — large temple in 120 ft. square enclosure with seven subordinate temples; ascribed to Man Singh’s period by Beglar.

15\. Siva Ganga Tank (Man Sarovar), Baidyanath Dham — 900 ft. x 600 ft. tank; western embankment (Man Sarovar) built by Man Singh; still extant; documented in Santhal Parganas District Gazetteer.

16\. Mangarh Fort and temples, Manihari (Santhal Parganas) — fort built during Bengal campaigns; large tanks; stone-carved images found in the village.

17\. Ambar city / pargana (Santhal Parganas) — named by Raja Man Singh after Amber; Brahmin jagirdar rewarded by Man Singh for support in Sundarbans campaign.

**Bengal**

18\. Temples at Manpur, Central India (Bhopawar Agency) — chief deity Siva or Mahavir; place name Manpur itself derived from Raja Man Singh.

19\. Rajmahal city complex — founded 1595; palace, strong rampart with bastions, mint; Singdalan (Man Singh’s marble hall) still extant.

Orissa

20\. Jagannath Temple complex, Puri — liberated from Afghan tyranny by Man Singh in August 1590; surrounding districts ceded to Mughal (Man Singh’s) protection; Man Singh personally made pilgrimage to Jagannathpuri in June 1592.

**Pakistan Region (then part of Mughal Empire)**

21\. Rohtas Fort structures, Punjab (Pakistan) — palace and temples repaired and built 1587–1597; Sanskrit and Persian inscriptions confirmed; described as the only specimen of Mughal civil architecture in the region.

22\. Attock (Atak Banaras) on the Indus — forward administrative and military base; site of the famous Indus-crossing episode (1585); structural works associated with Man Singh’s decade-long presence.

*What appears in this catalogue is but a small glimpse of Raja Man Singh's architectural and religious legacy.*

**Broader Contributions to Hindu Society, Protection of Brahminical Institutions -** Beyond temple construction, Raja Man Singh was a consistent protector of Brahminical social and religious institutions. The Brahmin purohit (family priest) occupied a position of great prominence and trust in all his major undertakings. His family priest Sridhar was the Daroga of major construction projects. The Brahmin Purohit Pitamber supervised the building of Ramgarh fort.

Raja Man Singh purchased a vast tract of land on the banks of the Ganga to build a Vishnu temple, yet chose instead to donate it to the pujaris of Kashi—a gesture recorded in a surviving sanad and emblematic of his enduring commitment to the sacred city.

**Endowments and Revenue Grants to Temples -**

The parwana (royal decree) in the possession of the chief Priest of the Baikatpur temple demonstrates that Raja Man Singh did not merely build temples but also endowed them with land grants and revenue allocations to ensure their long-term maintenance. This pattern of endowment — assigning jagirs or revenue grants to temple trusts — was the standard mechanism of royal patronage in medieval India, and Man Singh employed it systematically. The Jaipur Vansawali and other Rajasthani manuscripts confirm that the images of Sango Baba of Sanganer, Hanuman of Jaipur, and Sila Devi of Amber were all brought by Raja Man Singh, ensuring that these sacred images were provided with permanent homes and worship.

#### *The central paradox of Raja Man Singh’s legacy is this: the king who has been most consistently condemned by nationalist historiography as a traitor to Hinduism was, in objective historical terms, among the greatest benefactors of Hindu temples, pilgrimage, and religious institutions in the entire sixteenth century. The rehabilitation of Raja Man Singh’s historical reputation is not merely a matter of setting the record straight for one individual. It requires a fundamental re-examination of the categories through which we judge medieval Indian rulers.*
