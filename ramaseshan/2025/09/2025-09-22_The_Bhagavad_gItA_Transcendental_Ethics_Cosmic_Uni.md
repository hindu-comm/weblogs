# The epic context of the Gītā

The epic Mahābhārata is the largest epic poem in the world that was originally composed in Sanskrit. At its core storyline, it narrates the story of two sets of royal cousins - the five **Pāṇḍava** cousins (good guys) and the hundred **Kaurava** cousins (the bad guys) - who compete for a kingdom which eventually climaxes into a huge war fought between them at the battlefield of **Kurukṣetra**. But equally important to the richness and grandeur of the text are the contributions from several embedded nested stories within it that are narrated by the characters of the main story arc in appropriate situations. The Gītā is a small part of the epic and is understood to be the “Hindu Bible” and it does function as the Bible for Hindus in many ways - it is frequently studied and quoted by Hindus, it serves as a text that many Hindus turn towards for moral and spiritual guidance and instruction, and is one of the most widely translated and disseminated texts in many regional languages of India among Hindus. In the West, it was heavily influential and read by transcendentalists like **Henry David Thoreau** (1817 - 1862) and **Ralph Waldo Emerson** (1803 - 1882). Although foreign to the West, if you are a Westerner, you will soon find lots of ideas from this text will strike you as familiar although raised in unfamiliar contexts.

The immediate context of the Gītā occurs in the moments just before the grand war that is about to ensue between the **Pāṇḍavas** and **Kauravas**. **Arjuna**, a Pāṇḍava asks his charioteer **Kṛṣṇa** (who also happens to be the manifestation of Godhead himself!) to take his chariot in the middle of both the armies so that he can have a better look at the field of battle.

> **Note** : This is an unusual and unique situation even in Hindu myths
> and epics as well - that a deity serves as a charioteer of a warrior
> hero.

But upon observing the people at his enemy side, it then suddenly overwhelms Arjuna as it dawns upon him that on the Kaurava side whom he is supposed to kill, are none other than members of his extended family, friends, and teachers. Dejected and overpowered by pity, he feels conflict and doubtful if he should carry on with fighting the battle. He enters into a dilemma as to whether he should discharge his duty as a warrior by fighting the bad people or if he should withdraw from battle in order to respect and uphold his family duties. The bulk of the text consists of the counsel given by Kṛṣṇa to Arjuna who convinces him that fighting the war is the right choice to make in this situation.

In Chapters 2-5, Kṛṣṇa introduces Arjuna to a discipline named **yoga** (a method of unity with the transcendental) and its three types. In Chapters 6-7, Kṛṣṇa tells about the relationship between yoga and judgement / understanding. In Chapters 8-11, Kṛṣṇa explains to Arjuna about divinity and our relationship to the divine aspect of the universe and this leads naturally to Chapters 12-13, where he explains about the nature and importance of devotion in our lives. Chapters 14-16 introduces us to the nature of the world we live in and that of personality and finally, Chapters 17-18 address the nature of faith in the context of all of the things discussed till then. This sermon by Kṛṣṇa covers all the important topics that you would think are important to the meaning of life - honour, family duty, ritual duty (very important in Hindu context), personal destiny, good vs evil, and the order of the cosmos itself.

# The personal context of the Gītā : Making existential choices

The Gītā, through the counsel of Kṛṣṇa, invites us into a deep inquiry on how to lead a life that is detached from narrow narcissistic desires and instead imbibe it with a transcendental meaning beyond our narrow egos and concerns. It is a personal text that speaks for all of us when we encounter personal agony while making hard existential choices in our lives. The story of one Arjuna who has got very difficult choices to make that involve high stakes metaphorically stands for all of our lives in which we are always making poignant existential choices (although we might not be aware that we are making them). This is evident from the very first verse itself when Dhṛtarāṣṭra (the father of the Kauravas) asks the seer **Sañjaya** (who is able to provide live updates on the battle to him by his supernatural powers of television)

Sañjaya, tell me what my sons (the Kauravas) and the sons of Pandu (the Pāṇḍavas) did when they met, wanting to battle on the field of Kuru (Kurukṣetra - the battlefield), on the field of sacred duty (dharmakṣetra)?

The name of the battlefield Kurukṣetra that means the field of Kuru in Sanskrit, is also described by an adjective that is **dharmakṣetra** that means the field of dharma (hereafter translated as “sacred duty”) in Sanskrit. Dharma is a versatile word in Sanskrit that at once stands for the sacred cosmic, social, and ritual order of existence. By qualifying the battlefield as a field of sacred duty (dharma), the text says that it is not merely a battlefield is not just the site of a historical battle but also the site of our battles to establish dharma as we perform it in our lives which demands us to make difficult existential choices. You, I, and most of us, have never been to Kurukṣetra and will never be there at all, but the point the text is making is that every one of us battles everyday in the field of dharma when we make existential choices as we move about in our lives. Arjuna’s moment of his existential choice is poignantly captured in the verses of Gītā that is described as follows :

Arjuna saw them standing there: fathers, grandfathers, teachers, uncles, brothers, sons, grandsons, and friends. He surveyed his elders and companions in both armies, all his kinsmen assembled together. Dejected, filled with strange pity, he said this:

“Kṛṣṇa, I see my kinsmen gathered here, wanting war. My limbs sink, my mouth is parched, my body trembles, the hair bristles on my flesh. The magic bow slips from my hands, my skin burns, I cannot stand still, my mind reels. I see omens of chaos, Kṛṣṇa. I see no good in killing my kinsmen in battle.

They are teachers, fathers, sons, and grandfathers, uncles, grandsons, fathers and brothers of wives and other men of our family. I do not want to kill them even if I am killed, Kṛṣṇa; not for kingship of all three worlds, much less for the earth!

Honor forbids us to kill our cousins, Dhṛtarāṣṭra's sons (the Kauravas); how can we know happiness, if we kill our own kinsmen? … How can we ignore the wisdom of turning from this evil when we see the sin of family destruction, Kṛṣṇa?

How can we ignore the wisdom of turning from this evil when we see the sin of family destruction, Kṛṣṇa? When the family is ruined, the timeless laws of family duty perish; and when duty is lost, chaos overwhelms the family.. I lament the great sin we commit when our greed for kingship and pleasures drives us to kill our kinsmen”.

Saying this in the time of war, Arjuna slumped into the chariot and laid down his bow and arrows, his mind tormented by grief.

What Arjuna struggles with is the conflict between two very important kinds of scared duties of Hinduism - his *family duty* (Sanskrit : ***kula-dharma***) and his own *caste duty* (Sanskrit : ***varṇāśrama-dharma / svadharma***). As a person, Arjuna is supposed to honour his family members but being born into the *warrior caste* (Sanskrit : ***kṣatriya varṇa***), his duty is to fight his unjust enemies and slay them. The conflict arises because the unjust enemies whom he is supposed to slay are his own family members whom he is supposed to honour as per his family duty. At first sight, we might think that it will not be relevant to us in the modern world because most of us do not live in a birth based caste societies but we do have duties peculiar to our professional role and station in life like Arjuna has his caste duty as a warrior. Our difficult choices in life come from not having to choose between choosing what is obviously right and what is obviously wrong, but from choosing between two actions that have strong reasons to be equally right or wrong to do.

None of us are ever going to have to fight for kingdoms or have deities as charioteers. But the following struggles that Arjuna faces in this situation are indeed universal and important for all of us :

- Conflict of duties

- Uncertainty about what is right

- Awareness of our various social roles conflicting

- Uncertainty about our own motivations

- The need to choose and act right now with lots at stake

The text presupposes some basic and natural philosophical assumptions from the outset that are obvious when one reads the text but is best stated at the outset itself here. The assumptions are :

- We are the authors of our own actions - we possess free will to choose

- Actions have consequences

- Leading a meaningful life is hence completely under our control

# Kṛṣṇa’s master flip

Intuitively, it may look like to us that the right thing for Arjuna to do would be to forego the fight and that is how you could expect Kṛṣṇa to advise him as well. That is what Arjuna himself thinks as well. But, to everyone’s surprise, Kṛṣṇa rebukes Arjuna by saying :

Why this cowardice in this time of crisis, Arjuna? The coward is ignoble, shameful, foreign to the ways of heaven. Don’t yield to impotence! It is unnatural in you! Banish this petty weakness from your heart. Rise to the fight, Arjuna!

How surprising is that?! God is recommending Arjuna to massacre his own kinsmen. We have to explore exactly why he is saying here. Even Arjuna is surprised by this! In an emotional moment of his counter to Kṛṣṇa, the text reads :

Kṛṣṇa, how can I fight against Bhīṣma (my own ancestor) and Droṇa (my own teacher) with arrows when they deserve my worship? **It is better in this world to beg for scraps of food than to eat meals smeared with the blood of elders I killed!**

Arjuna thinks that him choosing to fight for the kingdom stems from his personal, this-wordly, narcissistic, temporary, temporal, and immediate concerns stemming from greed whereas him choosing to renounce war with his kinsmen stems from a selfless broader concern about family duties and he hence, intuitively like many of us, initially decides that honouring family members and giving up war with them is the right choice to make, in this situation. But the surprising thing that Kṛṣṇa does in the course of the Gītā is that he flips the tables and convinces Arjuna of exactly the opposite - that him choosing to fight the war is what is his duty that actually stems from the broadest transcendental cosmic concerns and choosing to renounce the war is actually stemming from his personal greed in the context of his immediate, this-wordly, temporal, and narcissistic concerns. # The temporal body vs the permanent ātman

How does Kṛṣṇa argue that choosing to fight is actually the selfless thing to do that arises out of our commitment to the transcendental duties? He does this by re-aligning Arjuna’s perspective by making him realise that **his concern for his friends, family, and enemies are actually temporal because these are all based on their bodily identities, which are temporary whereas Arjuna’s duty to fight by virtue of his svadharma as a warrior is actually in alignment with the eternal order of the cosmos that is actually a transcendental concern** (at least this is what Kṛṣṇa claims) . He begins by saying :

You grieve for those beyond grief, and you speak words of insight; but learned men do not grieve for the dead or the living. Never have I not existed, nor you, nor these kings; and never in the future shall we cease to exist.

Kṛṣṇa thus here is saying that there are long standing eternal issues to worry out; he has to focus on what is eternal, stable, permanent, and cosmic and not on the ephemeral matters of the body and event that happen to temporarily embodied beings. He continues :

Contacts with matter make us feel heat and cold, pleasure and pain. Arjuna, you must learn to endure fleeting things - they come and go!

Kṛṣṇa is saying to Arjuna here that if we have to choose meaningfully, we cannot do it based on our bodily identities and the events that happen to them. What endures are our **ātman**s (souls), and the cosmic structure of values and hence the right choice is when our soul aligns with these transcendental cosmic structures. To put in his own words :

Indestructible is the presence that pervades all this; no one can destroy this unchanging reality. Our bodies are known to end, but the embodied self is enduring, indestructible, and immeasurable; therefore, Arjuna, fight the battle!

**But if everything that endures is just ātman, then what is the motivation to do anything at all, let alone fight a war?** This would seem to lead to an ethic of absolute indifference that can make any act justifiable. In the absence of relevance for any form of differentiating identities in the temporal world, what determines moral judgement? The short answer, according to the Gītā would be svadharma - the sense of transcendental duty. Arjuna should fight because it is fighting that would realise the eternal cosmic values of justice that comes as a part of his svadharma as a result of being born into the warrior caste. The Gītā transcendentalises duty and ethical concerns by embedding it with the metaphysically transcendental reality (more on this in the last section of this article). Dharma is to temporal ethics, the same way the metaphysics of brahman is to the differentiated temporal physical world.

Arjuna, when a man knows the self to be indestructible, enduring, unborn, unchanging, how does he kill or cause anyone to kill? … Death is certain for anyone born, and birth is certain for the dead; since the cycle is inevitable, you have no cause to grieve!

Everybody is going to die to someday. All that is going to happen due to the fighting is that they are going to die a little earlier. Don’t focus on the small things ; focus on your transcendent duty that sustains the order of the cosmos ; focus on what you ought to do ; look at the big picture and act in a manner detached from fleeting things that come and go (like heat and cold, pleasure and pain, etc … ), which enslave you by generating desire and make you focus on your narrow egocentric view based on temporary bodily identities.

But what is the big picture that Kṛṣṇa has been talking about? And what does it mean to act in a detached way with a genuine discipline and recognition of duty? How can we reconcile individual choice with freedom, detachment, and duty? How to align our soul with the cosmos? These will be the focus of the future chapters of the text.

# Yoga, Detached Action, and Svadharma

In Chapters 3-5, we have a transcendental view of duty and an exposition of yoga - a set of disciplines that liberates us from desires and attachments which enables us to realise our unity and alignment with the transcendental order of existence (again, more on this in the last section of this article). Kṛṣṇa expounds on three types of yoga :

1.  The yoga of action (Sanskrit : **karma-yoga**)

2.  The yoga of knowledge (Sanskrit : **jñāna-yoga**)

3.  The yoga of devotion (Sanskrit : **bhakti-yoga**)

Let us begin by quoting from the text where Kṛṣṇa defines precisely what yoga (discipline) is.

Look to your own duty; do not tremble before it; nothing is better for a warrior than a battle of sacred duty (dharma). If you fail to wage this war of sacred duty, you will abandon your own duty and fame only to gain evil.

People will tell of your undying shame, and for a man of honour, shame is worse than death.

**Be intent on action, not on the fruits of action; avoid attraction to the fruits and attachment to inaction! Perform actions, firm in discipline, relinquishing attachment; be impartial to failure and success - this equanimity is called discipline** (**yoga**).

There is a Daoist saying that one can control his shooting of the arrow but not where the arrow ends up after leaving the bow. That does not necessarily imply that a skilled archer should give up on focusing on his target and shoot negligently. It simply means shooting to the best of your abilities and training, while not being perturbed if unaccountable / uncontrollable externalities prevent the arrow from reaching its target and becoming absorbed and fully focused in the action itself rather than the outcome. Similarly, Kṛṣṇa is saying something similar to Arjuna here that one’s obligation is an obligation to act and not to remain attached to the end results or consequences of the action which are not in our control but contingent on other external factors that are beyond our control. Yoga is acting with this kind of detachment. That does not mean negligence in performance of course ; no one acting in accordance with duty can act negligently but detached action simply means acting your role dutifully while realising that it is a necessary but not sufficient part for generating the consequences. This first kind of detachment, that is preached in the Gītā, includes detachment from the consequences of the action but there is another equally important detachment also that is important that Kṛṣṇa now expounds on.

Brooding about sensuous objects makes attachment to them grow; from attachment desire arises, from desire anger is born. From anger comes confusion; from confusion memory lapses; from broken memory understanding is lost; from loss of understanding, he is ruined.

Kṛṣṇa is making a very important argument here and I can say boldly this is the central root teaching of the entire Gītā. **Acting based on the passions induced by sense objects is actually not an exercise of freedom but a loss of freedom**. His central thesis is that when we are not disciplined, **our desires and aversions that drives us to do things are not generated from within freely, but are caused in us from the outside by contact with sensory objects**. So, in order to get freedom, we need to free ourselves from the externally influencing desires so that we can attain the freedom to do things that really matter to us. So, disciplining ourselves to control our reactions to sense desires is not a toil but actually the path to freedom. Hence, in this view, liberation comes through discipline and not from discipline!

The modern Western liberal discourse focuses on a restricted external scope of freedom - which is mostly "freedom FROM something external" - freedom from oppression, freedom from social constraints and so on. But after I am relieved from external constraints, and now that I am free, I am free TO DO WHAT? This is mostly decoupled from the "freedom FROM" concept, in modern Western philosophy. The Gītā sees this entire issue internally and makes the "freedom TO" as an integral part of "freedom FROM" and enable them both to go hand-in-hand, always balanced. Without freedom from passion, one cannot really be in a position to make truly free choices. Without freedom from my passion of over-eating, I cannot become free to become bodily fit. Kṛṣṇa emphasises that yoga (discipline) is what enables us to become free from our passions that cloud us and hence make us see transcendental things that matter freely and objectively for what they are, so that we may make the informed choice. If we allow ourselves to be controlled by feelings of heat and cold, feelings of anger and love, feelings of taste or sound or touch, the world buffets us about and we lose our free will to the temporal world. It is only when we detach ourselves from those ephemeral causes (by engaging in the discipline called yoga) and focus on duty, when we actually gain control of ourselves.

But a man of inner strength whose senses experience objects without attraction or hatred, in self-control, finds serenity.

Without discipline, he has no understanding or inner power; without inner power, he has no peace; and without peace where is joy?

Kṛṣṇa says here that joy comes not from enjoying the ephemeral sensory stuff around us but from shutting them out and paying attention to what is important and transcendent. On the other hand, he says :

If his mind submits to the play of his senses, they drive away insight, as wind drives a ship on water … When he renounces all desires and acts without craving, possessiveness, or individuality, he finds peace.

Here we see that freedom comes discipline that allows us to shut off ourselves to the immediate and the transitory concerns. The next big idea in the idea of the Gītā is the idea of **svadharma** : one’s own scared duty. In classical India, svadharma was about one’s duty based on caste and station (varṇāśrama dharma) which for Arjuna is to fight.

# The three kinds of Yoga

The three kinds of yoga that is presented in the Gītā are normally seen as three different ways but I see of it as three complementary aspects of yoga. The three aspects of yoga presented in the order in the text are : **karma** (action) **jñāna** (knowledge), and **bhakti** (devotion).

### The Yoga of Action (Karma yoga)

But first, Arjuna asks an interesting question. If indeed the bodily identities are ephemeral and do not matter at the transcendental realm, then why should one act and do anything at all? If the entire ephemeral world as we know it ceases to influence us (when we shut our senses which induce passions) and ceases to matter (in comparison to the transcendent), then what difference does it make if one acts or does not act in any situation?

If you think understanding (of the transcendent) is more powerful than action, why, Kṛṣṇa, do you urge me to this horrific act?

Kṛṣṇa immediately grasps this line of thought and replies to this as :

A man cannot escape the force of action by abstaining from actions; he does not attain success just by renunciation. **No one exists for even an instant without performing action; however unwilling, every being is forced to act by the qualities of nature**.

This is a very apt insight for those who think that Arjuna’s choices are between action and inaction. For Arjuna, not to fight, is not choosing inaction, but simply another action - the action of putting his weapons down than picking up and fighting with it. Inaction is never a choice. So if one cannot escape action, one has to choose to act in the right way. But what should motivate someone’s actions in this world in the absence of desires and attachments to the consequences of it? Kṛṣṇa says :

When his senses are controlled but he keeps recalling sense objects with his mind, he is a self-deluded hypocrite. When he control his senses with his mind and engages in the discipline of action with his faculties of action, detachment sets him apart. Perform necessary action; it is more powerful than inaction; without action you even fail to even sustain your own body. **Action imprisons the world unless it is done as sacrifice; freed from attachment, Arjuna, perform actions as sacrifice!** …

Those deluded by the qualities of nature are attached to their actions; a man who knows this should not upset these dull men of partial knowledge. Surrender all actions to me, and fix your reason on your inner self;

So we see that every action that we perform has to be motivated by a sacrificial concern ; that is, it should be an offering of sacrifice to God (which here is also Kṛṣṇa himself). But how does one choose to act the right way?

### The Yoga of Knowledge (**Jñāna** yoga)

It is here where the yoga of knowledge comes in. Kṛṣṇa says :

A man who sees inaction in action and action in inaction has understanding among men, disciplined in all action he performs. The wise say a man is learned when his plans lack constructs of desire, when his actions are burned by the fire of knowledge (of the transcendent).

This is metaphorical. The point of action is to make us deepen our understanding of the world and to give us the insight that will continue to allow us to behave meaningfully. It is knowledge that allows us to transcend the world in which we originally act. Kṛṣṇa further continues :

Just as a flaming fire reduces wood to ashes, Arjuna, so the fire of knowledge reduces all actions to ashes. No purifier equals knowledge, and in time, the man of perfect yoga discovers this in his own spirit. Faithful, intent, his senses subdued, he gains knowledge; gaining knowledge, he soon finds perfect peace.

The way to free ourselves from our passions and contemplate on the transcendent is the cultivation of knowledge that creates a deep understanding. Action, guided by desires, imprisons ; whereas action, guided by knowledge of and reflection on the transcendental, liberates.

Arjuna, actions do not bind a man in possession of himself, who renounces action through discipline and severs doubt with knowledge. So sever the ignorant doubt in your heart with the sword of self-knowledge, Arjuna! Observe your discipline! Arise!

So, to summarise, knowledge liberates us from the senses and enables us to make the right choices after making us understand what matters and what does not, in the transcendental realm.

### The Yoga of Devotion (**Bhakti** yoga) 

The essence of devotion as per the Gītā is grandeur ; it is basically **surrendering our goals to the order of the cosmos.** Devotion requires a kind of mindfulness to realise every moment our place in the cosmos. Mindfulness requires knowledge.

Controlling the body's gates, keeping the mind in the heart, holding his own breath in his head, one is in disciplined concentration … When he constantly remembers me, focusing his reason on me, I am easy to reach, Arjuna, for a man of enduring discipline.

# Transcendental Union and the Cosmic Connection : The Revelation

We now enter the much awaited last section of the article and also pursue a climactic part of Gītā called the ***theophany*** - the revelation of Kṛṣṇa as the cosmos which is called in Sanskrit as **viśvarūpadarśana**. We will now put everything that we saw together and firmly understand the connection between the morality of our individual action in this world and a transcendental cosmic reality. This is the most metaphorical part of the text and hence there are and there have been multiple ways to interpret this part. I will present one way in which I understand this part of the text.

One way to understand this revelation of the god Kṛṣṇa as the entire cosmos is to realise that there is a hidden unified spiritual (divine) dimensions to the universe beneath all the familiar mundane workaday ephemeral entities that we see around which would be accessible to us if only we discipline ourselves through yoga. The universe itself is infused with spirituality and significance. The **Upaniṣads** gave a detailed more theoretical account of this same thing (the all pervading **brahman**) that you can refer later by clicking [here](https://open.substack.com/pub/ramaseshanchandrasekaran/p/the-beginnings-of-metaphysics-in?r=38cmxu&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&showWelcomeOnShare=false), for more details. Kṛṣṇa can be understood as a revelation of the brahman. But let us continue with the text of the Gītā as it becomes absolutely lovely now.

I am woven into all this world; yet my form remains unmanifest. **All beings find their support in me; whereas I do not depend on them at all.** I am the way and the support; the Lord and the witness. I am your dwelling, your refuge, and your heart’s friend. I am the world’s origin and its dissolution, its stability, its treasure, its imperishable seed.

What does the bolded part mean? It means that everything that exists (pigs, cars, chickens, humans, trees, rocks, planets, …) exist and gain importance and context as parts of this cosmic whole. For example, by itself, an individual steer, wheel, glass, gear shift, tire, knob on the radio, seat, etc… does not have any significance but gain so as parts of a whole car. We can’t say that the entire car is present in the tire or the steer ; but the parts gain meaning and importance from the whole car’s identity. Similarly, all things in this cosmos, including us, gain significance when viewed as parts of this all-encompassing spiritual divine identity. Kṛṣṇa continues :

I myself radiate this heat. I withhold the rain and then I release it. I am immortality and mortality both. Arjuna, I am being and non-being both!

What does this mean? Even natural phenomena and all that existed, exists, will exist - all of these gain significance and connection when understood as parts of this whole encompassing divine reality. Hence, if we think transcendentally in cognisance of this connected wholesome reality, how should we act and what should motivate us? Kṛṣṇa says that we should understand all our actions as dedications to play our part in this divine whole. This is made explicitly by Kṛṣṇa himself :

Whatever you do - what you take, what you offer, what you give, what penances you perform - do it as an offering to me (Kṛṣṇa = cosmos =brahman), O Arjuna!

Whenever we act, whatever we do, we have to do it in consciousness of our role in this larger cosmic divine whole that we are a part of. This is the way through which, according to the Gītā, we can make our lives to mean something more than temporary minor distractions in the vast extent and history of the entire cosmos (If you had read my article on the book of Job from the Bible [here](https://open.substack.com/pub/ramaseshanchandrasekaran/p/the-book-of-job-and-the-pale-blue?r=38cmxu&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&showWelcomeOnShare=false), you would recognise that Job indeed presents to us, this latter pessimistic view). Now,

Kṛṣṇa says : Arjuna, look at the forms I take, hundreds of the them and thousands of them. So many divine forms! So many colours and so many shapes! Look at them all ; …

Narrator : Arjuna saw all the universe in its many ways and parts, standing as one in the body of the god of gods.

Thus, the significance of every person, every thing, and every phenomenon derives from this interconnected cosmic whole. We are members of a really big cosmic team and only that grand cosmos matters and hence to derive meaning to our lives, we have to understand where we fit into this transcendental cosmic whole. It is here where **svadharma** enters in; it is our cosmic role that we have been born into (for the case of an ancient caste based society) or have adopted (in the case of a modern capitalist society). An ordinary act of discharge of one’s caste duty or professional duty is elevated by the Gītā to be called as svadharma which then becomes our transcendental role that we are meant to act out and play, in the transcendental unity of the universe itself.

> Another thing to note is that here this cosmic divinity is embedded in
> the wholesomeness of the universe itself. The universe is not
> otherwise meaningless whose meaning requires an eminent personal God
> outside of it like in the Abrahamic monotheisms. We are all, and
> everything in the universe, is a part of the divine. So, I am just a
> piece in this gigantic puzzle, a clog in the gigantic machine, which
> allows me to depersonalise myself and my selfish concerns.

**How can a finite life be meaningful in the infinite universe? It is by union with this infinite divine source that makes it possible for one’s life to be free, non-conflicted, and meaningful. None of this means we have to withdraw from the world and become a monk or that we have to only think and meditate on the that transcendent whole but it can also equally be achieved by engaging fully in detached actions in this world, that have been alloted to us. The duty that we have to act out becomes elevated as transcendental (svadharma) that sustains the cosmic order of the entire universe itself.**

Works of sacrifice, of gift-giving, of austerity, should not be abandoned. In fact, they should be performed. These things purify men of insight. But while such actions should be performed, one should abandon attachment to them as well as their fruit. Arjuna, this is my final judgement on this!

This then, is the message of the Gītā in short.

> **Action is inevitable. Attachment is not so. Attachment arises out of
> our egoistic desires which prevent us from experiencing our
> transcendental unity with the cosmos as a whole.** **Svadharma is our
> part of action that we have (chosen to / been assigned) to engage in
> to sustain this divine order.**

Thank you for reading this far ! May we all realise Kṛṣṇa someday.
