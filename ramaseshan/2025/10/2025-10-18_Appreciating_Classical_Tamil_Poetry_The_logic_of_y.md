In previous articles, we discussed about the prosodical patterns in English poetry and Sanskrit poetry. In this article, we will turn to Classical Tamil, which in prosody, exhibits a uniqueness that has no counterpart in the traditions of any other language - Indian or European. The primary sources for the traditional descriptions of metre are the ***Tolkāppiyam Ceyyuḷiyal***, the ***Yāpparuṅkala Virutti***, and the ***Yāpparuṅkalak Kārikai***.

We saw that English prosody is based on alterations between stressed and unstressed syllable while Sanskrit poetry (and also that of Hindi and many other Indo-European languages) is based on alteration between long and short syllables. Tamil prosody is again based on rhythmic interplay between two types of entities but they are neither between stressed-unstressed syllables nor long-short syllables. It is between two different types of entities that have no counterpart in any well-known language and hence I will call it by what the native Tamil grammarians have called them - **acai**. But before that, let us warm up with learning or recollecting the basic features of the Tamil language.

If you do not know to read Tamil, do not worry too much about the extra lines or dots above letters. The transliteration even without the extra dots and marks capture reasonably well, the approximate pronunciation of the sounds. A line above a vowel means it is long and is held for longer duration. But here are few remarks that have to be emphasised about the phonology of the Tamil language.

1.  Unlike the ए and ओ sounds of Sanskrit, Tamil contrasts between
    shorter and longer forms of the vowels **e/ē** and **o/ō**.

2.  The sounds “**ai**” and “**au**” are considered technically long but
    in poetry, some of them can be scanned as short for metrical
    exigencies. If that is the case, I will denote them by “**aĭ**” and
    “**aŭ**” respectively.

3.  Tamil has a certain discomfort in ending words with hard consonants
    (k, c, ṭ, t, p, ṟ). When a word does end in such a hard consonant,
    there is added an ***over-short*** “u” sound to act as a helper
    vowel to just help with the pronunciation of that final consonant.
    In the script, this final over-short “u” is denoted by the same
    alphabet as that of the normal short “u” but it is often not counted
    as a vowel when counting syllables for poetry. It is called by the
    native grammarians as “***kuṟṟiyalukaram***” (meaning : shortened
    “u”). I will denote this over-short “u” in transliteration as \[u\].
    This vowel at the end of the word completely vanishes when the next
    word is followed by a vowel.   
    E.g. ***aḻak\[u\]*** (beauty) + ***aracaṉ*** (king) =
    ***al̄ak-aracaṉ*** (king of beauty)

> Note : In case if you speak or heard some Telugu (another Dravidian
> language related to Tamil), this final “u” is not over-short but very
> prominent and is easily noticeable by anyone who hears the language!

# Syllables & Syllabification

In case you have read my previous article on Sanskrit poetry (click [here](https://open.substack.com/pub/ramaseshanchandrasekaran/p/appreciating-sanskrit-poetry-introduction?r=38cmxu&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&showWelcomeOnShare=false) to read it) and are already familiar with syllables and syllabification, then you may skip this section and go to the next section straightaway.

A **syllable** is a single vowel that is optionally surrounded by consonants on either side and can be uttered as a single unit of speech.

Examples:

1.  e

2.  ai

3.  te

4.  ar

5.  nām

6.  kupp

7.  pāt

8.  sūt

9.  rac

10. yutt

A syllable can just have a single vowel (like 1,2), or a vowel with consonants only to the left (3), or a vowel with consonants only to the right (4), or a vowel with consonants on both sides (5-10). The defining feature is that it has one and only one (exactly one) vowel. The consonants to either side are optional. And the vowel may be long (like 1,2,3,5,7,8) or short (4,6,9,10).

Now given a word like ‘**pitaram**’, how do we syllabify it? There are several ways to split this word into syllables.

1.  pit - ar - am

2.  pi - ta - ram

3.  pit - a - ram

4.  pi - tar - am

and so on……

Which of the above syllable splitting is correct? The way to split a word into syllables is called **syllabification**. Let us now study the rules for syllabification for Tamil.

### Rule 1 : Number of syllables = Number of vowels

Since each syllable has exactly one vowel, the number of syllables must match the number of vowels. Each syllable has exactly one vowel which may or may not be surrounded by consonants to the left and right. This is however a generic rule and does not tell us which of the above four splittings of the word **pitaram** is correct.

### Rule 2 : Single consonant loves to begin a syllable

This rule says that a single consonant (surrounded by vowels on both sides) always likes to go right and begin a syllable rather than be left and end a syllable. Consider the word **pitā.** It has two vowels and hence two syllables.

We can syllabify two ways as

1.  pi-tā

2.  pit-ā

- The consonant ‘p’ can be associated with only the vowel ‘i’ and can
  only be a part of the first syllable.

- The single consonant ‘t’ that is surrounded by vowels, however has a
  choice. It can go with the first vowel and end the first syllable as
  ‘pit’ or it can go with the second vowel and begin the second syllable
  as ‘tā’.

- This rule tells us that the first of the above options is correct and
  we must make the consonant ‘t’ begin the second syllable on the right,
  rather than make it end the first syllable on the left. Hence, this
  rule renders the first syllabification ‘pi-tā’ as the correct one.

This is also intuitive too. If I ask you how to split the word **“**pitā**”** into two parts, most of you would intuitively go with “pi-tā” rather than “pit-ā”.

### Rule 3 : Two consonants together split between syllables

Now consider the word **pakkam** (meaning : side) in Sanskrit. We see that there are two consonants ‘kk’ together without any intervening vowel between them. A blind application of the previous rule that consonants don’t like to end and prefer to begin might tempt us to throw both the consonants ‘kk’ to the right and begin the second syllable and would suggest a syllabification as ‘*pa-kkam*’. This rule explains why it does not happen and one should be careful when pushing two or more consonants to the right.

Tamil has intrinsic discomfort in pronouncing two consonants together in the same syllable. So, if two consonants occur together (without any intervening vowel), we should split and put them in different syllables. So, the correct splitting of this word would be “pak-kam” and not “pa-kkam” which also is very intuitive - if I ask you how would you split the word “pakkam”, all of you would say “pak + kam” only, and not “~~pa + kkam~~”. A word like “***pantu***” (meaning : ball) splits as “***pan+tu***” and not “***~~pa-ntu~~***” as a consonant cluster “nt” splits between two syllables.

### Rule 4 : Long vs Short Syllables

It turns out that we can classify syllables as “**long**” or “**short**”. In Tamil, a syllable is defined to be **long (guru)** if either

(i) **Long by vowel** : its vowel itself is a long vowel (e.g. pī , cūṭ , mār , tēp , ōm)

(ii) **Long by following consonant** : or the vowel is followed by at least one consonant (the idea being that a consonant following a vowel sort of lengthens it by giving more time to spend on the syllable) (e.g. cel , muk , tal , veṇ).

Pronounce these two types of syllables and you will see that both of them take equal durations to pronounce. A consonant following a vowel delays its duration in the same way as lengthening a vowel increases its duration. Long syllables are denoted by L hereafter in my notation.

A syllable that has a short vowel that is not followed by any consonant and ends a syllable by itself, is called short, and denoted by S. (e.g. pi , ma , ka , ke , pu)

It turns out that a long syllable in Tamil takes twice as much time to be pronounced than a short syllable as lengthening a vowel or appending a consonant to a short vowel both increases its duration of pronunciation (Tamil : **māttirai**) by two times.

# Overview of Tamil Prosody

In this section, we will have a bird’s eye view of the different components of Tamil prosody starting from simple to complex. Since the Tamil prosodic tradition is unique (even other Dravidian languages have their prosodic traditions adapted to Sanskritic models), I give the native Tamil names of these units followed by an approximate descriptive English name. If you already know Tamil, the native names are very easy to remember since they are actually very descriptive of what they describe. The following are the components of Tamil prosodical analysis.

1.  **Acai** - metreme

2.  **Cīr** - metrical word / foot / pada

3.  **Taḷai** - linkage

4.  **Aṭi** - line

Note : Traditional Tamil grammarians also mention another aspect called **Toṭai** (meaning : ornament) but that is about rhyming and alliteration and hence we are not going to cover it in this article as it focuses only on rhythm (prosody). In the following sections, we will see about each of them in detail.

# Acai (metreme)

**Acai** forms the primary brick for Tamil poetry. There are two kinds of these bricks employed in Tamil poetry composition and analysis depending on the number of syllables they contain. An acai can either contain just one syllable or a pair of syllables. They are classified by the native grammarians as :

1.  **Nēr** ***acai*** (Single) - **L (**or **S)** : It consists of just
    one syllable. That syllable has to be always long normally. The very
    name “*nēr*” is actually a mnemonic and serves as an example of such
    a kind of an acai as that syllable “nēr” is single and long.
    However, if a short syllable is lonely and sits at the end without
    nothing to scan further, in this exceptional circumstance, it can be
    scanned long and hence be accepted as a single nēr acai of its own
    (if you remember, even in Sanskrit, the last syllable at the end of
    a pada is always scanned as long irrespective of whether it is
    actually long or short).

2.  **Nirai** ***acai*** (Coupled) - **SS / SL** : It consists of two
    syllables. Both the syllables cannot be long. The first syllable has
    to be short always. So, depending on that, they can be of the form
    SL or SS, where L denotes a long syllable and S denotes a short
    syllable. Again, the native word “*nirai*” is actually a mnemonic
    and serves as an example of such a kind of an acai which is either
    of the form SL or SS, depending on whether the vowel “ai” is scanned
    short or long (ni + rai/raĭ) !! This is why I prefer to stick to
    these native names rather than invent English circumlocutions like
    “single” or “coupled”.

In Sanskrit, short and long syllables (L, S) are themselves the fundamental bricks of poetry with the long syllable L twice as long as the short syllable S. In Tamil however, the distinction is not between short and long syllables but between single (L) nēr acai, and paired syllables (SL, SS) nirai acai, with both types of acai-s taking equal length. How do both the asai have equal length? Let us analyse.

1.  **L** (single) : here the asai is two units long as the syllable is
    long.

2.  **SS** (coupled) : here the asai is two units long as the two short
    syllables have one unit length each.

3.  **SL** (coupled) : this SL pattern gains an equal length because
    according to Tamil prosody, a short syllable is eclipsed when it is
    followed by a dominating long syllable.

### Scanning a poem

Now, how to scan a poem and split it into its constituent acai after syllabifying it? It is actually easy. The following are the ground rules :

1.  An acai cannot have more than two syllables.

2.  An acai cannot normally have just one short syllable (unless it is
    at the fag end of a word or line). A short syllable is desperate for
    a pair to form a nirai.

3.  A long syllable ends an acai so that nothing more can be attached to
    it further.

So, using these two rules, start with the beginning of a word. If the syllable is long, then that is itself an acai and rule 2 says that nothing can come after it. So, scan it as a single nēr acai. If the syllable encountered in the beginning is short, then look for the next syllable to pair it with the next syllable to form a nirai acai of the form SS or SL. Repeat this process again until the end of the world.

# Cīr (word)

One important thing to note is that in Tamil, each word, that is itself a combination of various acai, is a separate poetic unit called a **cīr.** This is in stark contrast with Sanskrit and other Indian and European languages, where the entire line is scanned and syllabified without bothering about word boundaries. But in Tamil, a syllable or an acai cannot run between words.

> **In Tamil, that is why, each word is to be scanned separately and
> analysed ; unlike in Sanskrit, where a single syllable or a foot can
> easily be split between words and run between them.**

But it is important to note that this word as understood in poetry, is not strictly the word as understood in grammar. Two things can complicate this identification.

Firstly, Tamil allows word compounding wherein two or more words combine obeying some euphonic sandhi rules) which make the word boundaries ambiguous. For example, in the Tirkkuṟaḷ verse that you scanned, the two words that I wrote as “ *kāṇpa taṟivu*” are not exactly words. It is actually a sandhi applied to the following words “*kāṇpat\[u\] + aṟivu = kāṇpat aṟivu*” where the \[u\] is the over-short “u” that I mentioned in the section on phonology. So, in this case, the author Tiruvaḷḷuvar has taken the liberty to take the final “t” from the first word “*kāṇpat*” and attach it to the beginning of the second word “*aṟivu*” to give “*taṟivu*” which is not a word in a grammatical sense but a word in a metrical sense. This is also done because in Tamil prosody, it is a rule that no acai can start with a vowel and hence the “a” in “*aṟivu*” would not make an acai. So, it is transferred to be attached to the end the previous word.

Secondly, like many Indian and European languages, when two words combine using sandhi, Tamil also has a provision for making them form compound words from simple words and when this happens, this single compound word is treated as a unity for metrical purposes. For example, the words like “*epporuḷ*” (meaning : whatever matter) and *“yāryārvāy*” (meaning : whosever mouth) are actually compound words as evident when they are translated into English.

> Hence, while a **cīr** might count as a word for metrical purposes, it
> is not always congruent with words as understood in the grammatical or
> lexical sense.

A **cīr** is a unit built out of asai. It consists of either one, two, three, or sometimes four acai. Denoting a nēr acai by the numeral 1 (because it has one syllable) and a nirai acai by the numeral 2 (because it has two syllables), we have the following possible patterns for cīr. The native Tamil grammarians give lots of mnemonic names again for each types of pattern again involving names of flowers, vegetables, fruits, etc; so that those names serve as the examples of the patterns they are supposed to represent. It is not important to remember all those names (I will use the sequence of numerals 1 and 2 to denote a pattern of cīr) but if you are a native Tamil speaker, you can appreciate the creativity behind those names and you would also recollect some of them because you would have recited and mugged them up for your school exams as a part of Tamil grammar!

# Taḷai (linkage)

The word Taḷai in Tamil poetics is used to denote a property of a pair cīr-s that are linked together. A pair of cīr when forming a taḷai is classified into seven types based on two factors

1.  Whether the final acai of the first (preceding) cīr is
    ***agreeing*** (Tamil : **oṉṟiya**) or ***disagreeing*** (Tamil :
    **oṉṟāta**) with the initial acai of the cīr that follows it.

2.  The length ( number of acai) and the nature of the final acai of the
    first (preceding) cīr

Again, native grammarians have given various Tamil names for each of these classes and you can refer to them if you come across them in your future readings but I will always explicitly mention these classes by describing them, instead of using these names, that would sound overwhelming for a beginner or a non-Tamil speaker. # Aṭi (line)

Now we come to the largest grouping in Tamil poetics which is a line that is called by the native grammarians as aṭi. The text ***Tolkāppiyam*** describes aṭi as containing four cīr and classifies it based on the number of letters within a line of four cīr. The later tradition however disregards this completely and classifies aṭi based on the number of cīr (no need to be four anymore) but uses the same name system as that of the Tolkāppiyam, thus creating confusion. > **kaṟṟataṉāl āya payaṉ eṉkol-vāl-aṟivaṉ nal tāḷ toḻāar eṉiṉ**?  
> *Of what avail is learning, if the learned do not adore the good feet
> of Him who is Immaculate Wisdom?*
>
> **malarmicai ēkiṉāṉ māṇ aṭi cērntār nilamicai nīṭu vāḻvār.**  
> *Those who find refuge in the great feet (of Him) who lives in the
> lotus of the heart (of the devotee) live eternally in heaven.*
>
> **vēṇṭutal vēṇṭāmai ilāṉ aṭi cērntārkku yāṇṭum iṭumpai ila.**  
> *Those who have attained the feet of Him who has no likes or dislikes
> will be rid of all troubles.*
>
> **iruḷ cēr iru viṉaiyum cērā, iṟaivaṉ poruḷ cēr pukaḻ
> purintārmāṭṭu**.  
> *Actions, both good and bad that spring from darkness of the mind will
> never touch those who ever chant the glories of the Lord.*
>
> **poṟi vāyil aintu avittāṉ poy tīr oḻukka neṟi niṉṟār nīṭu vāḻvār.**  
> *Those who still the five senses and walk in truth and right will live
> long*.
>
> **taṉakku uvamai illātāṉ tāḷ cērntārkku allāl maṉak kavalai māṟṟal
> aritu.**  
> *Only those who have sought refuge in the feet of the peerless can
> shake off anxiety. Others cannot.*
>
> **aṟa āḻi antaṇaṉ tāḷ cērntārkku allāl piṟa āḻi nīntal aritu.**  
> *Only those who have clung to the feet of the Lord who is the sea of
> righteousness, will be able to sail the other seas. Others cannot.*
>
> **kōḷ il poṟiyil kuṇam ilavē-eṇkuṇattāṉ tāḷai vaṇaṅkāt talai.**  
> *The head that does not bow down before and worship the feet of the
> Lord of the eight attributes, will be as like the impaired senses.*
>
> **piṟavip peruṅ kaṭal nīntuvar; nīntār iṟaivaṉ aṭi cērātār.**  
> *Those who gain the feet of the Lord cross the great ocean of births;
> others cannot.*

The following are the rules (constraints) for this meter :

1.  It can contain two-acai cīr and three-acai cīr of the type XX1.

2.  All the agreeing linkages (taḷai) should be of the type XX1 + 1 …
    only.

3.  All the disagreeing linkages (taḷai) should be either of the type
    X1 + 2 or X2 + 1 only.

4.  Each line has four cīr and the last line has three cīr (since the
    Tirukkuṟaḷ has only two lines, its first line has four cīr while its
    second line has three cīr).

5.  The last cīr of the last line must be either a single acai or a
    single acai with an extra over-short u. Examples of words whose
    patterns are allowed at the last cīr are : nāḷ , malar , kācu ,
    piṟappu

If you read the verses in Tirukkuṟaḷ and scan it, you will see that it satisfies these rules and as you scan more songs in this meter, you will get a feel of the rhythm of this meter.

# The Āciriyappā **Meter**

This meter **Āciriyappā**, also called **Akaval**, which in Tamil literally means ‘calling / address’ is popular and the most dominant meter in Sangam poetry and also the standard meter in Tamil epic poetry. It is relatively free flowing and has no strict rules. For example, the Tamil epic **Cilappatikãram** is written almost entirely in this meter.

This meter is known for its smooth and melodious sound. Each line of the poem typically consists of four *cīr.* It must have a minimum of three lines, and can extend for an unlimited number of lines depending on the poet’s content. It mostly uses two-acai cīr (with an optional extra syllable possible for each acai that contains over-short u). The following table summarises the kinds of two acai combinations possible in each two-acai cīr with green ones being most frequent, blue ones permitted intermittently and red ones going up rarely.

In very exceptional cases there may be three acai cīr also may pop up especially at the end of lines (the third acai should anyway be a grammatical article attached to the word or a case ending or an attached pronoun). Such three-acai cīr cannot have nirai as their second acai typically. Long words that technically take up four cīr are split into two two-acai cīr.

As for the linkages (taḷai) permitted between these two-acai cīr, it is mainly governed by agreeing linkages though disagreeing ones may be used intermittently to signify a break in the narrative.

# Concluding remarks

I’ve come to the black pilled realisation that many Tamils ourselves have absolutely no clue of this more than 2500 year old Tamil prosodical tradition. Popular songs in all circumstances have stopped employing these classical metrical tools (**Ceyyuḷ**), unlike in the early days of Tamil cinema where at least some movies used to have some classical styled songs. I am afraid that this unique system of poetry is dying for which the normalisation and popularisation of the free verse form (**Pāṭṭu**) is to be blamed. The loss of this tradition has already happened once in recorded history about a 1000 years ago inKerala, when this old *Yāppilakkaṇam* system was abandoned there in favour of the free verse (***pāṭṭu)*** and also the emergence and dominance of **Maṇippiravāḷam** (Sanskrit hybrid) systems. Today, this is happening in Tamil with the influence of Anglican modernity right in front of our own eyes. I would say this rigorous system of yāppu has been the anchor of theTamil language, keeping at least its formal literary register firmly rooted to its classical origins, despite constant rapid changes and divergences in the colloquial language. Now that the anchor has been dislodged, the future of the unique poetic tradition of Tamil looks very bleak. This is why I decided to write this article for anyone who is interested. Yāppu ilakanam is a very old system of Tamil prosody, stretching back at least as far as the Sangam age, and possibly older. This article presented is by no means comprehensive of all of Tamil prosody at all, as I exclude many many things that I would love to discuss - like **Toṭai** (rhyming and alliteration), **Aṇi** (ornamentation - similes, metaphors, etc..), **Vaṇṇam** (color - description), etc. But we have to start somewhere, and the best place is from the basics here that the other systems build on or incorporate. In future, I will try to continue this until there are some basic resources for writing at least a poem in the staple **Veṇpā** metre. In a future article, I intend to cover metrical analysis in detail.
