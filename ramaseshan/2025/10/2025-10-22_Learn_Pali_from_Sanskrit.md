# Alphabets & IAST

There is no unique alphabet for writing Pali. The most ancient alphabet that was used for writing Pali is called the **Brāhmī** script that begins to be attested from the time of the Emperor **Aśoka** onwards through his inscriptions. But in today’ Asia, Pali is written in each country or region in its own national alphabet - e.g. Sinhalese, Thai, Burmese, and so on. Since it is infeasible to learn all these writing systems, we adopt the Latin transliteration of the Pali sounds which should be familiar to you if you already know IAST of Sanskrit. If you do not know it, refer to this picture below where the Sanskrit sounds that are absent in Pali are showing in red . Pali texts printed in the West use this system only and hence it is imperative to become familiar with the IAST if you are going to want to read original Pali texts.

# Phonological Developments

## Loss of sounds

As you can see in the IAST table above, there are some Sanskrit sounds that are absent in Pali. The three fricative consonants ś (श्), ष् (ṣ), and स् (s) all convert to just the ‘s’ (स्) sound only. Some examples are:

> **śaraṇa → saraṇa  
> doṣa → dosa**

Also, the Sanskrit diphthongs ‘ai’ and ‘au’ convert to ‘e’ and ‘o’ respectively in Pali. Some examples of this are :

> **maitrī → mettā  
> auṣadha → osadha**

Sanskrit **aya,** **ava,** and **avi** also often reduce to **e** and **o** in Pali. This is apparent in causative verbs or class 10 verbs that have “aya” as a part of their stem. Some examples :

> **dhārayati → dhāreti  
> avatāra → otāra  
> bhavati → hoti  
> sthavira → thera  
> janayati → janeti**

The Sanskrit vowel sound ṛ (ऋ) disappears in Pali and becomes either “a / i / u” depending on the vowel that follows it. But when ṛ is preceded by a consonant that is pronounced by the lips (p, ph, b, bh, m, v), it becomes “u” only. Many examples are given below :

> **kṛta → kata  
> tṛṣṇa → taṇha  
> smṛti → sati  
> ṛṣi → isi  
> dṛṣṭi → diṭṭhi  
> ṛddhi → iddhi  
> ṛju → uju  
> spṛṣṭa → phuṭṭha  
> vṛddha → vuddha**

The visarga at the middle of a word assimilates to the following consonant.

> **duḥkṛta → dukkata  
> duḥkha → dukkha  
> duḥprajña → duppañña  
> niḥkrodha (=niṣkrodha) → nikkodha  
> niḥpakva (=niṣpakva) → nippakka  
> niḥśoka → nissoka  
> niḥsattva → nissatta**

**When a long vowel is followed by two consonants, it is shortened in Pali. This applies even if the vowel is “e” or “o”.** In Sanskrit, the vowels “e” and “o” are always long but in Pali, they shorten before consonant clusters. This is understood even though there are no diacritical marks to distinguish the short and long forms of “e” and “o”. Examples of this vowel shortening are given below :

> **kṣānti → khanti  
> rājya → rajja  
> īśvara → issara  
> tīrṇa → tiṇṇa  
> pūrva → pubba**

When the consonants “ḍ” and “ḍh” come between vowels, they become “ḷ” and “ḷh” in Pali. This happens in Vedic Sanskrit too.

> **cakravāḍa → cakkavāḷa  
> virūḍha → virūḷha  
> dṛḍha → daḷha**

## Assimilation of Consonants

### Middle of a word

Pali hates two or more different consonants coming together (called a consonant cluster technically). When it happens in the middle of a word, the two consonants assimilate and merge to the second consonant normally if both are hard. Only the second consonant can have aspiration in such geminate clusters.

> **vimukti → vimutti  
> dugdha → duddha  
> utpāda → uppāda  
> pudgala → puggala  
> śabda → sadda**

But if one of the consonants is a nasal or fricative or semi-vowel, then the assimilation is to the other hard consonant no matter whether it is first or second.

> **agni → aggi  
> ātman → atta  
> prāpnoti → pappoti  
> śaknoti → sakkoti**

In a sequence of two dissimilar nasals, the first nasal assimilates to the second nasal.

> **unmatta → ummatta  
> pradyumna → pajjunna**

**j** assimilates to a following **ñ** (i.e., **jñ** becomes ññ**)**

Examples:

> **prajñā → paññā  
> jñāti → ñāti**

The Sanskrit liquid consonants **r** and **l** assimilate to a following stop, nasal, sibilant, or **v**

Examples:

> **mārga → magga  
> karma → kamma  
> varṣa → vassa  
> kalpa → kappa  
> sarva →** savva **→ sabba**

**r** assimilates to a following **l**

Examples:

> **durlabha → dullabha  
> nirlopa → nillopa**

**d** sometimes assimilates to a following **v**, producing **vv** → **bb**

Examples:

> **udvigna →** uvvigga **→ ubbigga  
> dvādaśa → bādasa (***besides* **dādasa)**

**t** and **d** may assimilate to a following **s** or **y** when a morpheme boundary intervenes

Examples:

> **ut+sava → ussava  
> ud+yāna → uyyāna**

Also, if both the assimilated consonants turn out to be aspirated, the first one loses its aspiration.

> **udghoṣa → ugghosa  
> adbhuta → abbhuta**

### Word-initial Consonant Clusters

When the consonant cluster occurs at the beginning of the word, one of the consonants is simply omitted (as explained in the brackets below).

> **prāṇa → pāṇa (***not* ppāṇa**)  
> sthavira → thera (***not* tthera**)  
> dhyāna → jhāna (***not* jjhāna**)  
> jñāti → ñāti (***not* ññāti**)**

**m** assimilates to an initial sibilant when in the beginning of a word.

Examples:

> **smarati → sarati  
> smṛti → sati**

### Liquid consonants (Semi-Vowels)

The Sanskrit liquid consonants **r** and **l** assimilate to a preceding stop, nasal, sibilant, or **v,** whether at the beginning of a word or on the middle.

Examples:

> **prāṇa → pāṇa  
> grāma → gāma  
> śrāvaka → sāvaka  
> agra → agga  
> indra → inda  
> aśru → assu  
> pravrajati →** pavvajati **→ pabbajati,**

**y** assimilates to preceding non-dental/retroflex stops or nasals

> **cyavati → cavati  
> jyoti → joti  
> rājya → rajja  
> matsya → macchya → maccha  
> lapsyate → lacchyate → lacchati  
> abhyāgata → abbhāgata  
> ākhyāti → akkhāti  
> saṁkhyā → saṅkhā (***but also* **saṅkhyā)  
> ramya → ramma**

**y** assimilates to preceding non-initial **v**, producing **vv** → **bb**

Examples:

> **divya → divva → dibba  
> veditavya → veditavva → veditabba  
> bhāvya → bhavva → bhabba**

**y** and **v** assimilate to any preceding sibilant, producing **ss**

> **paśyati → passati  
> śyena → sena  
> aśva → assa  
> īśvara → issara  
> kariṣyati → karissati  
> tasya → tassa  
> svāmin → sāmī**

**v** sometimes assimilates to a preceding stop

> **pakva → pakka  
> catvāri → cattāri  
> sattva → satta  
> dhvaja → dhaja**

The sequence **vv** resulting from assimilation changes to **bb.**

> **sarva →** savva **→ sabba  
> pravrajati →** pavvajati **→ pabbajati  
> divya →** divva **→ dibba  
> nirvāṇa →** nivvāṇa **→ nibbāna**

### Sibilants and aspiration

Sanskrit sibilants before a stop assimilate to that stop, and if that stop is not already aspirated, it becomes aspirated; e.g. **śc, st, ṣṭ** and **sp** become **cch, tth, ṭṭh** and **pph**

> **paścāt → pacchā  
> asti → atthi  
> stava → thava  
> śreṣṭha → seṭṭha  
> aṣṭa → aṭṭha  
> sparśa → phassa  
> sthavira → thera**

In sibilant-stop-liquid sequences, the liquid is assimilated to the preceding consonant, and the cluster behaves like sibilant-stop sequences; e.g. **str** and **ṣṭr** become **tth** and **ṭṭh**

Examples:

> **śāstra → śasta → sattha  
> rāṣṭra → raṣṭa → raṭṭha**

A sibilant assimilates to a preceding **k** as an aspirate (i.e., the sequence **kṣ** becomes **kkh**)

Examples:

> **bhikṣu → bhikkhu  
> kṣānti → khanti**

### Palatalisation

Any dental or retroflex stop or nasal followed by **y** converts to the corresponding palatal sound, and the **y** assimilates to this new consonant, i.e. **ty, thy, dy, dhy, ny** become **cc, cch, jj, jjh, ññ**; likewise **ṇy** becomes **ññ**. Nasals preceding a stop that becomes palatal share this change.

Examples:

> **tyajati →** cyajati **→ cajati  
> dhyāna →** jhyāna **→ jhāna  
> satya →** sacya **→ sacca  
> mithyā →** michyā **→ micchā  
> vidyā →** vijyā **→ vijjā  
> madhya →** majhya **→ majjha  
> anya →** añya **→ añña  
> puṇya →** puñya **→ puñña  
> vandhya →** vañjhya **→** vañjjha **→ vañjha  
> udpadyate → uppajjati**

The sequence **mr** becomes **mb**, followed by assimilation to the following stop and subsequent simplification of the resulting geminate.

Examples:

> **āmra →** ambra **→ amba  
> tāmra → tamba**

**t** and **p** become **c** before **s**, and the sibilant assimilates to the preceding sound as an aspirate after palatalisation (i.e., the sequences **ts** and **ps** become **cch**)

Examples:

> **vatsa → vaccha  
> apsaras → accharā  
> martya → macca**

### Three consonant case

When assimilation would produce a sequence of three consonants in the middle of a word, geminates are simplified until there are only two consonants in sequence.

> **uttrāsa → uttāsa (***not* utttāsa**)  
> mantra → manta (***not* mantta**)  
> indra → inda (***not* indda**)  
> vandhya → vañjha (***not* vañjjha**)**

## Epenthesis

When there is a consonant cluster, and both the consonants are not hard and one of them is a nasal or liquid (mostly when both are nasals or liquids), in some cases, Pali prefers to insert a vowel (a / i / u) between them instead of assimilating the consonants. This is called epenthesis. As we saw with the replacement of Sanskrit ṛ, this epenthetic vowel may be **a**, **i**, or **u**, depending on the influence of a neighbouring consonant or of the vowel in the following syllable. **i** is often found near **i**, **y**, or palatal consonants; **u** is found near **u**, **v**, or labial consonants.

Sequences of stop + nasal are sometimes separated by **a** or **u**

Example:

> **ratna → ratana  
> padma → paduma (u** influenced by labial **m)**

The sequence **sn** may become **sin** initially

Examples:

> **snāna → sināna  
> sneha → sineha**

**i** may be inserted between **a** consonant and **l**

Examples:

> **kleśa → kilesa  
> glāna → gilāna  
> mlāyati → milāyati  
> ślāghati → silāghati**

An epenthetic vowel may be inserted between an initial sibilant and **r**

Example:

> **śrī → sirī**

The sequence **ry** generally becomes **riy** (**i** influenced by following **y**), but is still treated as a two-consonant sequence for the purposes of vowel-shortening

Examples:

> **ārya → arya → ariya  
> sūrya → surya → suriya  
> vīrya → virya → viriya**

**a** or **i** is inserted between **r** and **h**

Example:

> **arhati → arahati  
> garhā → garahā  
> barhiṣ → barihisa**

There is sporadic epenthesis between other consonant sequences

Examples:

> **caitya → cetiya (***not* **cecca)  
> vajra → vajira (***not* **vajja)**

### The behaviour of h

Any Sanskrit sibilant before a nasal becomes a sequence of nasal followed by **h**, i.e. **ṣṇ**, **sn** and **sm** become **ṇh**, **nh**, and **mh**

Examples:

> **tṛṣṇa → taṇha  
> uṣṇīṣa → uṇhīsa  
> asmi → amhi**

The sequence **śn** becomes **ñh**, due to assimilation of the **n** to the preceding palatal sibilant

Example:

> **praśna → praśña → pañha**

The sequences **hy** and **hv** undergo metathesis

Examples:

> **jihvā → jivhā  
> gṛhya → gayha  
> guhya → guyha**

**h** undergoes metathesis with a following nasal

Example:

> **gṛhṇāti → gaṇhāti**

Voiced aspirates such as **bh** and **gh,** in rare occasions (especially in frequently used words) become **h**

Examples:

> **bhavati → hoti  
> -ebhiṣ → -ehi  
> laghu → lahu**

### Sporadic Changes

Dental and retroflex sounds sporadically change into one another.

Examples:

> **prati → paṭi (***not* prati**)  
> jñāna → ñāṇa (***not* ñāna**)  
> dahati → ḍahati (***besides* **dahati)  
> nīḍa → nīla (***not* **nīḷa)  
> sthāna → ṭhāna (***not* **thāna)  
> duḥkṛta → dukkaṭa (***besides Pāḷi* **dukkata)  
> vardhate → vaḍḍhati**

There are several notable exceptions to the rules above; many of them are common Prakrit words rather than borrowings from Sanskrit.

> **ārya → ayya (***besides* **ariya)  
> guru → garu (adj.) (***besides* **guru (n.))  
> puruṣa → purisa (***not* purusa**)  
> vṛkṣa → rukṣa → rukkha (***not* vakkha**)**

Sometimes, a geminate consonant shortens and lengthens a preceding short vowel as a compensation.

> **kartavya** **→ kātabba (**also found : **kattabba)**

# Nominal Declension

Having seen the sound changes, let us come to the grammar part. Pali retains the three gender system of Sanskrit nouns and adjectives. But the dual number of Sanskrit nouns and verbs are both lost in Pali which has only singular and plural forms. The gender of a Pali word is mostly the same as that of its Sanskrit counterpart. The declension has some innovations apart from the usual phonological simplifications.

- The ablative (5th case) borrows the instrumental form (3rd case) in
  the plural for all nouns and even in the singular for all nouns except
  the -a stems.

- The dative (4th case) coincides with the genitive (6th case) in both
  singular and plural.

The most frequently occurring declension in Sanskrit is that of masculine-neuter stems that end in “a”. It is the same in Pali too. The corresponding Sanskrit and Pali forms are placed side by side in the table below.

There are several things to note apart from the usual and expected sound changes that we saw in the phonology :

- The Sanskrit visarga forms “***aḥ***” and “***āḥ***” become “**o**”
  and “**ā**” in Pali respectively but this is not surprising because
  due to sandhi, even in Sanskrit, they often become so. Pali just
  generalises the Sanskrit visarga sandhi then.

- The form with the “āya” ending is lost and instead the “asya **→
  assa”** ending is adopted, creating a merger between Sanskrit
  “dharmāya” and “dharmasya”.

- The ending “ehi” actually comes from the Vedic Sanskrit “ebhiḥ” ending
  instead of the classical Sanskrit “aiḥ” ending. Sometimes, the full
  ending form like “***dhammebhiḥ***” is also seen.

- As noted, Sanskrit paradigm merges the “from” and the “for” plurals to
  “ebhyaḥ” ending but this is lost in Pali. Instead, for the “for” from,
  the Pali adopts the “ānāṃ” ending (the same way its singular adopts
  the “assa” ending) and the “from” form adopts the “by” ending which is
  “ehi”.

- Vowel shortening happens as expected by the rule that a long vowel
  cannot end a syllable

Many times, the pronominal suffixes spill over into the declension of other nouns as well to create alternative forms of words. For example :

>  Ablative (5th case) ***dhammasmā*** (apart from the usual
> ***dhammā***) is seen more often and it also helps in disambiguating
> ablative singular from nominative-accusative plural
>
> Locative (7th case) ***dhammasmiṃ*** and ***dhammamhi*** (apart from
> the usual ***dhamme*** are seen more often.

The demonstrative main pronouns (*so, sā, taṃ*) and the interrogative pronouns (*ko, kā, kiṃ*) too follow almost the same above pattern as well in all their three genders. The patterns from nouns too can spill over into pronouns to create alternative forms of pronominal words. For example :

> Masculine Genitive & Dative (6th & 4th case) ***tesānaṃ*** (apart from
> the usual ***tesaṃ***)
>
> Masculine Genitive & Dative (6th & 4th case) ***tāsānaṃ*** (apart from
> the usual ***tāsaṃ***)
>
> Feminine Locative (7th case) ***tāyaṃ*** (apart from the usual
> ***tassaṃ***)

Sometimes, a geminate consonant might shorten and lengthen the vowel to create new forms of pronouns. And “a” can change to “i” after this change as well. For example :

> Feminine locative (7th case) ***tāsaṃ*** (apart from the usual
> ***tassaṃ***)
>
> Feminine locative (7th case) ***tissaṃ*** (apart from the usual
> ***tassaṃ***)

For the complete list of all such irregular forms, refer [here](https://ancient-buddhist-texts.net/Textual-Studies/Grammar/Guide-to-Pali-Grammar.htm#Nouns).

# Verbs in Pali

## Overview & The Regular Paradigm

Pali verbs on the whole retain their Sanskritic paradigm with the phonological changes that was discussed in the first section. Some simplifications enter the picture which are :

1.  The *ātmanepada* pattern of endings falls out of usage drastically
    and is used less frequently in only rare forms and most verbs take
    *parasmaipada* endings.

2.  The second person plural ending for all tenses and moods becomes
    “*tha*” that is taken from the present tense.

3.  Most of the verbs form their future tense by adding -***issa*** to
    the present stem and then using the present tense endings, even
    those verbs that originally took only -*sya* in Sanskrit.

4.  The historical past tense (liṭ) falls out of usage and is preserved
    only in some fossilised forms. One important pair among that is
    ***āha*** (he/she/it said) and ***āhu*** (they said).

5.  The aorist tense (luṅ) becomes the dominant way of forming the past
    tense instead of the prefixed imperfect tense (laṅ). Many classical
    aorist forms are lost and are regularised based on the ***-iṣ***
    form which is much simplified compared to the original seven-method
    way for forming that tense. The initial augment “a” is also removed
    from the aorist tense and also optionally removed in the imperfect
    past tense,

6.  The optative mood (vidhirliṅ) undergoes a lot of truncation as Pali
    does not allow a final consonant. The following Sanskrit optative
    forms *pacet*, *paceḥ*, and *paceyam* all give *pace* in Pali, which
    leads to confusion. Hence, this necessitates the addition of the
    endings of the present tense to a strengthened stem to create new
    forms to give *paceyyāsi* and *paceyyāmi*.

7.  The injunctive mood (āśīrliṅ) mood completely falls out of usage.

8.  The causative (nijanta) is formed using “e” consistent with the rule
    “aya**→e”.**

9.  Other secondary conjugations like the intensive (yaṅanta) and
    desiderative (sananta) also vanish except for rare instances of
    fossilised remnants.

Passive verbs are formed from active verbs by attaching the infix
-***ya***- either to the root in its weak grade, directly or by means of adding a connecting vowel ***-i-/-ī-*** to the present stem. A passive verb often seen in the suttas is ***paññāyati***, “is known, is discerned” (Sanskrit : *prajñāyate*). The verb is the passive of ***pajānāti***, “understands, knows,” built on the root ñā with doubling of ñ- to represent the lost j- of the Sanskrit. An example with insertion of the vowel is ***pahīyati*** (Sanskrit : *prahīyate*). When ***-ya-*** is inserted to form a passive verb, the ***-y-*** may be assimilated to the preceding consonant or both consonants may be modified by sandhi, often through palatalisation. In Sanskrit, ***-ya-*** would be preserved, but in Pāli assimilation or mutation is the rule. We thus find the passive verbs ***vuccati*** “is said,” (Sanskrit : *ucyate* ), ***bhijjati***, “is broken” (Sanskrit : *bhidyate*), ***vimuccati*** “is liberated” (Sanskrit : *vimucyate*), and ***dissati*** “is seen” (Sanskrit : *dṛśyate*)

# Sample Reading

In this section, we will get practice of reading Pali by a sample reading. Note that my Sanskrit renditions rectify not just the phonological changes but also the grammar changes (for example, if Pali uses the parasmaipada ending for a Sanskrit ātmanepada verb, I restore the original Sanskritic ātmanepada ending too. Same for case endings of nouns and adjectives.

The most recurring phrase in the Pali canon of sūtras especially as a saying of Buddha is recounted by his disciple **Ānanda** is this

> *evaṃ mayā sutam* (Sanskrit : evaṃ mayā śrutam)  
> Thus, have I heard

> *buddhaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi  
> dhammaṃ saraṇam gacchāmi  
> saṃghaṃ saraṇam gacchāmi  
> dutiyaṃ pi buddhaṃ saraṇam gacchāmi  
> dutiyaṃ pi dhammaṃ saraṇam gacchāmi  
> dutiyaṃ pi saṃghaṃ saraṇam gacchāmi  
> tatiyaṃ pi buddhaṃ saraṇam gacchāmi  
> tatiyaṃ pi dhammaṃ saraṇam gacchāmi  
> tatiyaṃ pi saṃghaṃ saraṇam gacchāmi*  
>
> Glossary :  
> **saraṇa** - śaraṇa (refuge)  
> **dhamma** - dharma  
> **dutiya** - dvitīya (second)  
> **tatiya** - tṛtīya (third)

Now let us go to the next reading. The word “*bhikkhave*” is a frequent word that is vocative that means “O monks” as the Buddha gives sermons. This particle of address prefers to come at the second word of a sermon rather than as the first word. For example, in English, we nowadays prefer to say : “O monks, this is the cause of that”. But the Buddha prefers to say : “This, O monks, is the cause of that”. So, ignore “bhikkhave” and continue translating.

> *...cittam, bhikkhave, adantam mahato anatthāya samvattatīti  
> ...cittam, bhikkhave, dantam mahato atthāya samvattatīti.  
> ...cittam, bhikkhave, aguttam mahato anatthāya samvattatīti.  
> ...cittam, bhikkhave, guttam mahato atthāya samvattatīti  
> ...cittam, bhikkhave, arakkhitam mahato anatthāya samvattatīti.  
> ...cittam, bhikkhave, rakkhitam mahato atthāya samvattatīti.  
> ...cittam, bhikkhave, asaṃvutam mahato anatthāya samvattatīti  
> ...cittam, bhikkhave, saṃvutam mahato atthāya samvattatīti.  
>   
> nāham, bhikkhave, aññam ekadhammaṃ pi samanupassāmi yaṃ evaṃ adantaṃ,
> aguttaṃ, arakkhitaṃ, asaṃvutaṃ, mahato anatthāya samvattatīti
> yathayidam.*
>
> ...*micchādiṭṭhikassa, bhikkhave, anuppannā c’ eva akusalā dhammā
> uppajjanti. uppannā ca akusalā dhammā vepullāya saṃvattantīti.*
>
> *samma̅diṭṭhikassa, bhikkhave, anuppannā c’ eva akusalā dhammā n’
> uppajjanti. uppannā ca akusala dhammā parihāyant̄iti.*
>
> *… anuppannānaṃ pāpakānāṃ akusalānāṃ dhammānāṃ anuppādāya chandaṃ
> janeti: vāyamati : viriyaṃ ārabhati; cittaṃ paggaṇhāti ; padahati;*
>
> *.... uppannãnam pāpakānāṃ kusalānāṃ dhammānāṃ pahānāya chandaṃ
> janeti: vāyamati: viriyaṃ ārabhati; cittaṃ paggaṇhāti ; padahati...*
>
> Glossary :  
> **danta** - dānta (tamed)  
> **gutta** - gupta (guarded)  
> **attha** - artha (advantage, benefit)  
> **rakkhita** - rakṣita (protected)  
> **saṃvuta** - saṃvṛta (shut / covered)  
> **saṃvattati** - saṃvartate (leads to - takes dative as object)  
> **añña** - anya (another)  
> **samanupassāmi** - samanupaśyāmi (sees correctly)  
> **yathayidam** - yathā + idam (i.e., that is, namely)  
> **micchā** - mithyā (falsity)  
> **diṭṭhi** - dṛṣṭi (view, sight)  
> **samādiṭṭhika** - samādṛṣṭika (one who has right views)  
> **upapanna** (having come into being)  
> **kusala** - kuśala (bad, inefficient, sinful)  
> **vepulla** - vaipulya (fullness)  
> **uppajjanti** - utpadyate (arises, comes into existence)  
> **parihāyati** - parihāyate (decreases, deteriorates)  
> **paggaṇhāti** - pragṛhṇāti (takes up, makes ready, holds out)  
> **janeti** - janayati (produces, gives birth to)  
> **padahati** - pradadhāti (exerts, strives, confronts)  
> **ārabhati** - ārabhate (begins)

So you see how closely Pali follows Sanskrit and with the familiarity of Sanskrit and some sound changes, you can read easily a Pali text.
