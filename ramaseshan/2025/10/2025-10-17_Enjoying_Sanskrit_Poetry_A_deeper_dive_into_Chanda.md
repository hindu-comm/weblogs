# Chandas in Sanskrit culture

**Ācārya Durga** writes in his commentary on ***Nirukta*** “*No speech takes place without chandas*”. The same is echoed by **Bharata Muni** in his famous treatise on dance ***Nāṭyaśāstra***. **Bhartṛhari**, the famous philosopher of language and grammarian says that this universe itself came forth from chandas only. These must tell the important place that chandas (prosody or meter) has in Sanskrit literature most of which is poetry only.

The first systematic treatise on this topic called **Chandaśāstra** was written by **Piṅgala** but other texts also deal with this topic paritally. Among later dedicated works, we have the ***Śrutabodha*** of **Kālidāsa**, ***Suvṛttatilaka*** of **Kṣemendra**, ***Chandomanjarī*** of **Gangadāsa** (very popular, systematic, and comprehensive of Classical Sanskrit), and ***Vṛttaratnākara*** of **Kedarabhaṭṭa**.

In this article, assuming that you already know how to syllabify a verse and identify long syllables (represented as “DUM”) and short syllables (represented as “da”), we examine eighteen popular meters in Classical Sanskrit and demonstrate them by sampling popular works that employ them.

# 1. Anuṣtubh (8 syllables)

We saw this meter already in our previous article and it is also called as ***śloka*** in popular usage. The majority of the epics and purāṇas are written in this meter and it has the least constraints out of all meters. It has eight syllables per pada. If **DUM** represents a long syllable and **da** represents a short syllable, then the pattern of this chandas, if you recall, is :

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!FRIZ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3ff2a1ea-8f77-4297-ba76-53384c189cf3_772x336.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!FRIZ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3ff2a1ea-8f77-4297-ba76-53384c189cf3_772x336.heic)

The additional constraint is that the second and third syllable both cannot be short. This is the default meter that it is almost equivalent to prose (**gadya**) in Sanskrit. Some popular examples are given below and you can feel the meter for yourself.

> **lokānām tvaṃ paro dharmaḥ  
> puruṣaḥ purṣottamaḥ  
> śaraṇyaṃ śaraṇaṃ ca tvām  
> āhurdivyā maharṣayaḥ**  
>
> *You are the embodiment of the highest dharma in all the worlds. You
> are the puruṣa, and best among men. The holy sages proclaim you as the
> greatest refuge and the saviour.*   
>  - Rāmāyaṇa, V.CXIX.14

# 11 syllabled meters

In this section, we will see some popular meters that have 11 syllables per foot (pada).

## 2. Triṣtubh

In the last article, we introduced to this meter, that is most popular in Vedic literature and has 11 syllables per pada. It also survives in Classical Sanskrit and is employed especially to convey an archaic feel. We introduced to its two important subtypes - **indravajrā** and **upendravajrā** and said that a mix of both falls under the general category of **upajāti**. Recollecting the **indravajrā** and **upendravajrā** patterns, we have :

> Indravajrā (I) :  
> **DUM DUM da / DUM DUM da / da DUM da / DUM DUM /**
>
> Upendravajrā (U) :  
> **da DUM da / DUM DUM da / da DUM da / DUM DUM /**

There is traditionally a pause after the fifth syllable of the pada as eleven syllables is too long to be uttered in a single breath.

### **Indravajrā**

For the example of a verse written completely in Indravajrā, we have as mentioned in the previous article, Bhagavad Gītā 15 : 15.

Other famous examples from this style are shown below.

> kāruṇyarūpaṃ karuṇākaraṃ taṃ

>
> *I surrender to Lord Rama, the prettiest in this world, brave in the

> enchanted (by you), O Devi, you joyful, verily are the cause of
> salvation (to everyone) on the earth.*  
> - Devīmāhātmyam 11.4

Another example of verses completely written in Upendravajrā from Śrīmadbhāgavatam is :

> **na nākapṛṣṭhaṃ na ca sārvabhaumaṃ   
> na pārameṣṭhyaṃ na rasādhipatyam   
> na yogasiddhīrapunarbhavaṃ vā   
> samañjasa tvā virahayya kāṅkṣe**  
>   
> *O excellent one, I desire not heaven, kingship, nor the position of

> freedom from birth and death, (if it is) after separation from you.*
>   
> - Śrīmadbhāgavatam 6.11.25

When both the types of Indravajrā and Upendravajrā come mixed, they are collectively referred to as **Upajāti**. Depending on the combination, they are of 14 types.

## 3. Śālinī

This meter **Śālinī** too has eleven syllables per pada like the Triṣṭubh. Here, the sixth and ninth syllable of each pada are short and all the others are long. So, this meter is dominated by long syllables with just two short syllables in the middle. The rhythm is hence

> Śalinī pattern :   
> **DUM DUM DUM / DUM DUM da / DUM DUM da / DUM DUM**

A verse from the Skandapurāṇa (4.1.10.129) that is written in this meter is scanned below for reference.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!86aP!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Faa130ea8-d5c0-4892-ba39-568bd5324e46_1342x1494.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!86aP!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Faa130ea8-d5c0-4892-ba39-568bd5324e46_1342x1494.heic)

Another example is given below from the first verse of ***Ātmapañcakam*** composed by **Śaṅkarācārya**.

> **nāhaṃ jāto janmamṛtyū kuto me   
> nāhaṃ prāṇaḥ kṣutpipāse kuto me   
> nāhaṃ cittaṃ śokamohau kuto me   
> nāhaṃ kartā bandhamokṣau kuto me**   
>   
> *I am not born, (hence) wherefore (is this) birth and death for me?  
> I am not prāṇaḥ, (hence) wherefore (is this) hunger and thirst for
> me?  
> I am not the mind, (hence) wherefore (is this) misery and delusion for
> me?  
> I am not the doer, (hence) wherefore (is this) bondage and freedom for
> me?*

## 4. Ratoddhatā

This **Ratoddhatā** is also a 11-syllable meter but unlike the previously seen Śālinī, has relatively evenly distributed short and long syllables. Here, all odd numbered syllables except the fifth are all long while the fifth and all even numbered syllables are short. Hence, the rhythm of this meter would be :

> Ratoddhatā pattern :   
> **DUM da DUM / da da da / DUM da DUM / da DUM**

Here too, there is traditionally a pause after the fourth syllable of the pada. A famous verse to illustrate the phalaśruti of Rāmāyaṇa is set in this verse and is scanned below.

# 12 syllabled meters

In this section, we will see three meters that have 12 syllables per pada.

## 5. Vaṃśasthavilam

This Vaṃśasthavilam meter has six long and six short syllables that are distributed as follows :

> Vaṃśasthavilam pattern :   
> **da DUM da / DUM DUM da / da DUM da / DUM da DUM /**

## 6. Bhujaṅgaprayātam

This meter **Bhujaṅgaprayātam** is dominated by long syllables with the following pattern.

> Bhujaṅgaprayātam pattern :   
> **da DUM DUM / da DUM DUM / da DUM DUM / da DUM DUM /**

Note that the first syllable in the start of each triplet is short while all the others are long. Traditonally, there is a pause after six syllables in this meter.

The famous opening stanza of the ***Bhavānī Aṣṭakam*** composed by **Śaṅkarācārya** that is fully composed in this meter is scanned and shown below :

Lots of other works of Śaṅkarācārya are composed in this meter like the **Nirvāṇaṣaṭkam** (very famous composition), **Gurvaṣṭakam**, **Vedasāraśivastotram**, etc.

Some Sanskrit verses from the **Rāmacaritamānasa** of ***Tulasidāsa*** is also in this meter. An example from 7.1 is shown below.

> *namāmīśamīśānanirvāṇarūpaṃ   
> vibhuṃ vyāpakaṃ brahma vedasvarūpam   
> ajaṃ nirguṇaṃ nirvikalpaṃ nirīhaṃ   
> cidākāśamākāśavāsaṃ bhaje ‘ham*
>
> *I salute the all pervading great Lord, who is liberation itself, who
> is the embodiment of vedas, who is unborn, who is without attributes,
> who is unconditioned, who is desireless, who is pure consciousness
> robed with the garment of the sky.*

The opening verse from the famous **Nirvāṇaṣaṭkam** of Śaṅkarācārya is also given to get a feel for this meter if you are more familiar with that song. It is said that when Ādi Śaṅkara was a young boy of eight and wandering near River Narmadā, seeking to find his guru, he encountered the seer **Govinda** **Bhagavatpāda** (who would be his guru) who asked him, “Who are you?” The boy Śaṅkara is said to have answered spontaneously with these stanzas, upon hearing which Bhagavatpāda got immediately impressed and accepted Śaṅkara as his disciple. The verses are said to be valued to progress in contemplation practices that lead to Self-Realization.

> **mano-buddhy-ahaṅkāra-cittāni nāhaṃ  
> na ca śrotra-jihve na ca ghrāṇa-netre  
> na ca vyoma bhūmir na tejo na vāyuś  
> cid-ānanda-rūpaś śivo ‘haṃ śivo ‘haṃ**
>
> *Neither am I the Mind, nor the Intelligence or Ego,   
> Neither am I the organs of Hearing (Ears), nor that of Tasting
> (Tongue), Smelling (Nose) or Seeing (Eyes),  
> Neither am I the Sky, nor the Earth, Neither the Fire nor the Air,  
> I am the Ever Pure Blissful Consciousness; I am auspicious,I am
> auspicious*

So, this Bhujaṅgaprayātam meter seems to be a recurring favourite of Ādi Śaṅkara.

## 7. Toṭakam

This meter **Toṭakam** is dominated by short syllables. Here, the third syllable of every triplet is long while the first two syllables are short. The pattern is :

> Toṭakam pattern :   
> da da DUM / da da DUM / da da DUM / da da DUM

# 15 syllabled meters

## 10. Mālinī

This meter with fifteen syllables called **Mālinī** is dominated by short syllables. The first two triplets (first six syllables) are all short. Among the rest, all syllables except the 10th and 13th syllables are short with the rest of them long.

> Mālinī meter pattern :  
> **da da da / da da da / DUM DUM DUM / da DUM DUM / da DUM DUM /**

It is also another favourite of **Ādi Śaṅkara** and employed in his works like ***Dakṣiṇāmūrtistotram*** and ***Śrīgaṅgāṣṭakam***. The second verse of the former is scanned below in detail for reference below.

## 15. Mandakrāntā

**Mandākrāntā** in Sanskrit literally means “*slow-stepping*”. It is said to have been invented by **Kālidāsa**, who used it in his well-known poem ***Meghadūta*** (the Cloud-Messenger). The metre characterises the longing of lovers who are separated from each other, expressed in the Sanskrit word viraha (separation of lovers). It is often associated with themes of longing and distance. Its pattern is :

> Mandākrāntā pattern :
>
> **DUM DUM DUM / DUM da da / da da da / DUM DUM da / DUM DUM da / DUM
> DUM**
