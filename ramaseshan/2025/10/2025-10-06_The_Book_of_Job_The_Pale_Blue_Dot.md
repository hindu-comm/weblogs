# Mysterious Origins

The book of Job is a part of the Jewish and Christian Bible and originally written in Hebrew. As with many other Biblical texts, we cannot tell when exactly it was written and who wrote it and/or edited it. The core central part of the text that is full of conversations that Job has with his friends and later with God himself is written in a much archaic and difficult poetic form of the Biblical Hebrew language compared to the first two chapters (prologue) and the latter part of the last chapter (epilogue) which are in workaday prose and standard Biblical Hebrew. This tells us that the prologue and the epilogue were added much later to the core poetic text whose archaic language tells us that it must have been in circulation as an oral poem for much longer. There are also Mesopotamian texts like the Babylonian book of theodicy which deals with a similar story and theme as Job does and hence the Biblical book was also definitely drawing from the wider frameworks of near Eastern thought. The book reached its final form after the 6th century BCE when the city of Jerusalem was burnt and the Israelites were captured by the Babylonian empire and exiled to Babylon which is an existential crisis and a defining moment for the Jewish people. This text has exerted a great deal of influence in Jewish and Christian and indirectly Islamic theology as well. Let us get going into the text.

# Introducing Job and satan

The text first introduces us to Job and repeatedly emphasises that he is a very very righteous and good man. He is also described as extremely rich and prosperous. The book begins as :

There was a man in the land of Uz, whose name *was* **Job**; and that man was **blameless and upright**, and one who **feared God and shunned evil**. And seven sons and three daughters were born to him. **** Also, his possessions were 7000 sheep, 3000 camels, 500 yoke of oxen, 500 female donkeys, and a very large household, so that this man was **the greatest of all the people of the East.**

So, Job is outstanding in both riches and virtue. He is many times described as good and righteous over and over, by both the narrator and God. Then, what happens? Enters a character who is called in Hebrew as **ha-satan**.

> NOTE : This word “***ha-satan***” in Hebrew is the source of the later
> Satan who becomes the Devil in Christianity. But note that in Hebrew,
> the phrase “*ha-satan*” is not a proper name but a title that means
> “*the prosecutor*” or “*the advocate*” ; some lawyer like figure who
> argues his case to God. The phrase “the *Devil’s advocate*” in English
> comes from this text. But, there is no Devil figure in Judaism. The
> snake that tempted Eve in the garden of Eden, this prosecutor in the
> book of Job, and many other bad figures in the Old Testament were
> combined later by the Early Church that identified all of them with
> the single figure of the **Devil** (the opponent of God) in the New
> Testament and later Christian theology. But in the text itself and in
> the original Judaic context, this satan in the book of Job is not the
> Devil of the New Testament. So, I will not be capitalising satan in
> this article while I discuss the book of Job.

God boasts to satan that how faithful and pious his servant Job is. Now, satan now raises an issue with God that Job is very virtuous and pious only because he is rich and prosperous. He claims that Job’s piety and virtue is simply a quid pro quo with God for his blessings and then claims to God that if Job loses everything he has, he will curse God to his face. He says :

Have You not made a hedge around him, around his household, and around all that he has on every side? You have blessed the work of his hands, and his possessions have increased in the land. But now, stretch out Your hand and touch all that he has, and he will surely curse You to Your face!

What God does next is very surprising. He makes a cruel bet with satan. He says to satan that he can do anything to Job except hurting his body. God says to satan :

Behold, all that he has, *is* in your power; only do not lay a hand on his *person.*

Then, a whole slew of disasters successively come in Job’s way. Job loses his sheep, camel, oxen, asses, and all of his possessions. Even all his sons and daughters are killed successively along with all their children. But, what does Job do? He does lame and mourn but never blamed God. The text says :

Then Job arose, tore his robe, and shaved his head; and he fell to the ground and worshiped. And he said:

“Naked I came from my mother’s womb,  
And naked shall I return there.  
The Lord gave, and the Lord has taken away;  
Blessed be the name of the Lord.”

In all this Job did not sin nor charge God with wrong.

So, it looks like God is winning the bet so far. The first chapter ends here. But satan provokes God by further saying that if Job himself is physically hurt, he would curse God. Satan says :

Skin for skin! Yes, all that a man has he will give for his life. **** But stretch out Your hand now, and touch his bone and his flesh, and he will surely curse You to Your face

To further surprise, God accepts this bet too, and tells that satan can do anything to Job himself now except killing him. So, satan makes Job really sick (*smote Job with sore boils from the sole of his foot unto his crown*), really disgusting, and really horrible. Job’s wife then comes up to him and surprisingly suggests :

Do you still hold fast to your integrity? Curse God and die!

But Job is very mature in his answer when he replies :

You speak as one of the foolish women speaks. Shall we indeed accept good from God, and shall we not accept adversity?

Then, we are told in the text that three of Job’s friends **Eliphaz** the Temanite, **Bildad** the Shuhite, and **Zophar** the Naamathite, came to visit him. They sit and mourn with Job silently for seven days and seven nights until Job himself begins to speak. What does he speak now? We find that Job is now indeed lamenting his fate. He does not curse God but does curse himself. The second chapter ends and the text now shifts to poetry as though, to signal this shift.

May the day perish on which I was born, … Why did I not die at birth?…

# Rejecting the Deuteronomistic theology

We get that Job indeed is unhappy despite being steadfast in his faith. After hearing this, one of the friends Eliphaz replies to him that bad things do not happen to good people and since God is just, hence Job definitely must have sinned. So, he exhorts Job to find, confess, and repent for his sins. Job then angrily retorts back by asking what kind of a friend Eliphaz is (friends console, don’t rebuke), and that how could he presumably blame Job for sins despite not knowing anything about what actual sins he committed. Job continues the long monologue that implies an important point :

> **Misfortune does not need to be our fault.**

This is a strong statement both theologically and literarily. All the remaining books of the Bible (especially the Torah and the Prophets) drive home seriously the point that flourishing is a result of obedience to the covenant with God and suffering is a result of disobedience to the convenant with God. This is called as deuteronomistic theology as it is explicitly articulated and explained in the book of Deuteronomy. In fact, the entire section of the prophetic books of the Bible is to explicitly demonstrate this theology and explain that Israel as a nation prospered when her people were obedient to the covenant with God and suffered when her people were disobedient to the covenant with God. But, here we have Job who is now saying that misfortune does not necessarily follow sin and can befall on anyone even if he has been faultless. What does that mean? Does this imply that life is meaningless and chaotic? That is what Job worries about now and asks a series of questions from Chapter 7 onwards.

*Is there* not a time of hard service for man on earth? *Are not* his days also like the days of a hired man? Like a servant who earnestly desires the shade, and like a hired man who eagerly looks for his wages.

Job says that life is really hard and we work for it and we do not always get our reward. He rants that life is short and brief.

My days are swifter than a weaver’s shuttle, And are spent without hope. Oh, remember that my life *is* a breath! My eye will never again see good. **** The eye of him who sees me will see me no *more; While* your eyes *are* upon me, I shall no longer *be. As* the cloud disappears and vanishes away, So he who goes down to the grave does not come up. He shall never return to his house, Nor shall his place know him anymore.

How can such a short and hard life have any meaning, and more importantly and how can there be any sense of justice? He basically asks God why can’t He leave him alone and what he had done to deserve this terrible fate.

What *is* man, that You should exalt him, *That* You should set Your heart on him, That You should visit him every morning, *And* test him every moment? How long? Will You not look away from me, And let me alone till I swallow my saliva? Have I sinned?  
What have I done to You, O watcher of men? Why have You set me as Your target, So that I am a burden to myself? Why then do You not pardon my transgression, And take away my iniquity? For now I will lie down in the dust, And You will seek me diligently, But I *will* no longer *be.*

Then, the second friend Bildad comes in and says that Eliphaz was right and Job has blasphemed by charging God with injustice and creating life bereft of meaning. Bildad reiterates that God is indeed just and Job still has it under control and all that Job needs to do is repent for his sins after which everything to be okay. He says :

How long will you speak these *things,* and the words of your mouth *be like* a strong wind? Does God subvert judgment? Or does the Almighty pervert justice? If your sons have sinned against Him, He has cast them away for their transgression. If you would earnestly seek God and make your supplication to the Almighty, If you *were* pure and upright, Surely now He would awake for you, And prosper your rightful dwelling place. Though your beginning was small, Yet your latter end would increase abundantly.

Job is no fooled and will have absolutely none of this. He makes another subtle argument. He says that justice is the wrong metaphor for thinking about himself and God as both the two parties are unequal. God made the entire cosmos and knows everything - who is Job in front of Him? Justice only makes sense if there is a fair judge between two parties with a dispute. But who can be a judge in front of God? He says that God is really not just and it makes no sense to talk about divine justice. He says that the world is obviously unjust - wicked people prosper and good people suffer all the time - and hence God is an unjust guy by any standards.

Truly I know *it is* so, but how can a man be righteous before God? If one wished to contend with Him, He could not answer Him one time out of a thousand. *God is* wise in heart and mighty in strength…He commands the sun, and it does not rise; He seals off the stars; He alone spreads out the heavens, and treads on the waves of the sea; … If He (God) goes by me, I do not see *Him;* If He moves past, I do not perceive Him; **** If He takes away, who can hinder Him? Who can say to Him, ‘What are You doing?’…

…I am blameless, yet I do not know myself; I despise my life. It *is* all one *thing;*  
Therefore I say, ‘He destroys the blameless and the wicked.’ If the scourge slays suddenly, He laughs at the plight of the innocent. The earth is given into the hand of the wicked. He covers the faces of its judges. If it is not *He,* who else could it be?

# Job’s central question

At this point, the third friend Zophar comes in and offers another critique of Job. He argues that Job being a tiny puny human being does not know anything about the almighty omniscient God and hence asks him to trust that God’s scheme is just and repent of his sins. Zophar justifies the traditional Deuternonomistic theology and convinces Job that since God knows more than Job, he should just trust His scheme and repent and move on. Job gives a generic rebuttal to Zophar by asking him who is he to talk and his views are superficial but he is talking as if he were God’s own advocate. Job tells his friends that none of what they said makes sense to him in front of the misery and calamity that has befallen him. **Job says that the real problem he is facing is trying to make sense of his life in the light of the fact that he is going to die, and that his suffering is all about him, while he does not deserve any of it, despite suffering itself possibly being inevitable. That is the question that the book of Job wrestles with.** He says :

Man *who is* born of woman is of few days and full of trouble. He comes forth like a flower and fades away; He flees like a shadow and does not continue… For there is hope for a tree, if it is cut down, that it will sprout again, … But man dies and is laid away; Indeed he breathes his last and where *is* he? *As* water disappears from the sea,  
And a river becomes parched and dries up. So, man lies down and does not rise..

Job says that the human condition is worse than even that of trees. The universe is not making sense. What is then, the meaning of all of this? That is what is addressed in the rest of the book.

# Another round of conversations

Job now thinks that the idea of divine justice does not make sense and is just a kiddish interpolation of our limited experience with human justice. Job himself now has shown that there is nothing particular about his situation and asks the larger question that why do good people suffer and why bad people prosper. We now are in the 15th chapter of the book where the book has a turning point. So far, the friends explain that what is happening to Job is still comprehensible and due to a sin that he had done. But now, the friends now turn the dialectic to discuss the theme of the incomprehensibility of the universe and God. Eliphaz tells him :

Should a wise man answer with empty (windy) knowledge?… Should he reason with unprofitable talk?…For your guilt teaches your mouth, and you choose the tongue of the crafty…Your own mouth condemns you, and not I; Yes, your own lips testify against you…

Eliphaz is saying that everything that Job has said now is the problem ; the elaborate theories spun by Job and ranting about the incomprehensibility of divine justice - Eliphaz says that this is what Job is punished for. As for Job’s accusation to Eliphaz that he was arguing as if he was in the counsel of God, Eliphaz turns it around by shooting the question back to him :

Have you heard the counsel of God? Do you limit wisdom to yourself? What do you know that we do not know? *What* do you understand that *is* not in us?

Job then retorts by saying that it is really easy for Eliphaz to say these stuff as he is not suffering as much as Job is and he is not as pious as Job is. Hence, he tells Eliphaz that his job is not to justify but console, and that is what Job would have done, if Eliphaz were in his place.

I have heard many such things; miserable comforters *are* you all! Shall words of wind have an end? Or what provokes you that you answer? I also could speak as you *do, i*f your soul were in my soul’s place. I could heap up words against you, and shake my head at you;

His rant continues on and on when Bildad again replies to defend Eliphaz. Bildad asks Job what was so special about him that he is behaving as though he is the only one suffering despite himself acknowledging that everyone suffers.

How long *till* you put an end to words? Gain understanding, and afterward we will speak. Why are we counted as beasts, a*nd* regarded as stupid in your sight? You who tear yourself in anger, shall the earth be forsaken for you? Or shall the rock be removed from its place \[for you\]?

Bildad continues to argue that Job’s wickedness (blasphemy) was just not the one he had initially committed but his refusal to accept the justice of God because of which his punishment is continuing. Job dismisses it pretty quickly by defending that no reasonable person would ever think that Job deserved in any way, the suffering he has been enduring. The third friend Zophar then again attempts at consolation. He consoles him by saying that eventually justice of God wins out in the end and that this is just a temporary phase. Job again does not want any of this and again points out that good does not triumph over evil eventually at all times. The book of Job creates lot of heat in this moment by having lots of back and forth exchanges between Job and his friends (you should read the text yourself to get the full details) and again reaches another turning point. Bildad argues again that we are tiny, worthless, insignificant specks (in the text *: maggots and worms*) in the universe and that God is so great, majestic, and transcendent that we can’t see the justice around us and we deserve everything that God does for us. Job reacts by arguing that this view was indeed blasphemy because if we were actually maggots and worms, then it means that God who is just, indeed justly created a bunch of maggots to sadistically torture them and would render life meaningless. Job kind of argues that if my relationship to God is such that God thinks me to be utter trash that deserves to be trampled under His feet, then the relationship with Him is not meaningful and I can still blame Him for creating me to be like this in the first place. There would be no sense of **theodicy** (justice from God). So, this is a blasphemy as it makes God look pretty bad and makes our lives look meaningless.

# Elihu : Suffering is a message from God

At this point, we are in Chapter 32 of the text when a new character enters. It is a young man named **Elihu**, whom some scholar think to be a later interpolation into the text, who enters as another interlocutor, and provides a fresh argument, by claiming that all the three previous friends missed something crucial in their arguments. Elihu prompts Job by conveying to him that if Job looked around more attentively and would be quiet, God is actually trying to tell something to him through all of this, and that Job would understand it.

And if *they are* bound in fetters, held in the cords of affliction, then, He tells them their work and their transgressions — That they have acted defiantly. He also opens their ear to instruction, and commands that they turn from iniquity. If they obey and serve *Him,* They shall spend their days in prosperity, and their years in pleasures. But if they do not obey, they shall perish by the sword, and they shall die without knowledge.

Job thinks that this makes him feel even worse. He does not understand what is the message behind killing all of one’s descendants, destroying all his wealth, and giving him all sorts of physical afflictions? Why couldn’t God communicate more transparently?

There are two interesting deep points made here by Elihu and Job. One possibility is that things are actually meaningless and unfair and we are smart to see it - Job argues like this. Or there is another possibility that things are actually meaningful and convey messages but we are too dumb to see it - Elihu argues like this. So, Elihu says that Job is blaming God instead of his inability to understand what is happening. It is like a guy complaining to his teacher that the textbook that the teacher assigned to him is stupid and meaningless when the fact is that it appears so only because the student has not understood it while the teacher does. This point is so deep and profound that Job stops to respond. The book now reaches a climax when God himself responds out of the whirlwind in Chapter 38 to break the silence!

# God’s rebuke to Job

Instead of answering his questions, God responds to Job by asking a slew of questions of his own. He starts by asking a series of questions that take up four chapters, some extracts from which are given below :

Who *is* this who darkens counsel by words without knowledge? Now, prepare yourself like a man; I will question you, and you shall answer Me.

Where were you when I laid the foundations of the earth? Tell *Me,* if you have understanding. Who determined its measurements? Surely you know! Or who stretched the line upon it? To what were its foundations fastened? Or who laid its cornerstone, when the morning stars sang together, and all the sons of God shouted for joy?….

Have you commanded the morning since your days *began, and* caused the dawn to know its place…?

Have you entered the springs of the sea? Or have you walked in search of the depths? Have the gates of death been revealed to you? Or have you seen the doors of the shadow of death? Have you comprehended the breadth of the earth? Tell *Me,* if you know all this….

Who has divided a channel for the overflowing *water,* or a path for the thunderbolt, to cause it to rain on a land *where there is* no one, a wilderness in which *there is* no man; …

Can you bind the cluster of the Pleiades, or loose the belt of Orion? Can you bring out Mazzaroth in its season? Or can you guide the Great Bear with its cubs? Do you know the ordinances of the heavens? Can you set their dominion over the earth?…

Can you lift up your voice to the clouds, that an abundance of water may cover you? Can you send out lightnings, that they may go,…

Who has put wisdom in the mind? Or who has given understanding to the heart? Who can number the clouds by wisdom?…

Can you hunt the prey for the lion, or satisfy the appetite of the young lions..? Who provides food for the raven, when its young ones cry…? …

…

God is telling Job that He knows and accomplishes at a level much much greater and vaster than Job that he could never comprehend Him. God is echoing here with some points made in the text - there is no judge that arbiters justice in the relationship between man and God (point made by Job) - and that Job has no business challenging God at all (point made by Elihu). God is telling that Job is contradicting himself by saying on the one hand that there is no sense of justice between man and God while on the other hand also saying that his suffering is unjust and undeserved.

> God admits that there is no justice and He does not owe us any hidden
> purpose or justification or caring about us at all. Indeed, God says
> that we don’t deserve the suffering we endure because **God is making
> the ultimate point that the universe is not organised around our
> narrow selves to reward us for our good deeds and be comprehensible to
> us so that we can lead a meaningful life. In fact, thinking that God
> organises the universe around us is the greatest sin of pride and
> hoping that by working hard enough, we can figure out why and how
> everything is all about us, so that God can think of every moment
> “What can I do for men?” is a terrible and self-centred view-point to
> possess.** We can’t necessarily make complete sense of our lives and
> God in all its vastness.

Job finally is finally thoroughly chastised and agrees to everything that God has said :

I know that You can do everything, and that no purpose *of Yours* can be withheld from You. *You asked,* ‘Who *is* this who hides counsel without knowledge?’ Therefore I have uttered what I did not understand, things too wonderful for me, which I did not know.  
Listen, please, and let me speak; *You said,* ‘I will question you, and you shall answer Me.’ **** “**I have heard of You by the hearing of the ear, but now my eye sees You. Therefore I abhor** ***myself,*** **and repent in dust and ashes”**.

The same Job who argued on and on with his friends self-righteously about his innocence has done a complete turnaround. He has been humbled in his encounter with the divine. God seems to have answered Job’s question of “Why am I suffering?” by asking something like : “You are not asking the right questions, Spend time with me and you will see your situation in an entirely different light in the brodest scale of vision, away from your narrow self-centered fantastic viewpoint that the universe is centred around you and to cater to you”.

# Epilogue : The Pale Blue Dot

After this, the text suddenly shifts back to prose and gives us a happy ending; that Job gets cured of all illnesses and receives back everything he had lost from God and also gets new children and descendants. But this epilogue, as we saw, was added later to the text as prose and hence was not a part of the original story. I think the epilogue was added because the editor who put it together could not digest that Job simply suffered unjustly and hence put it to give a comfortable feel that everything turned out okay at the end. But the entire point of the text so far is to challenge the very notion that God owes us anything. I think the book of Job is meant to stimulate us and have us get deeply thinking about theodicy. It is a terrible mistake to think that the blessings given to Job at the end in the epilogue is a reward for Job’s righteousness in the face of extreme suffering. The entire point of the book has been to challenge that theology. The book is clear that Job has always been righteous and the answer it has given to the problem of righteous people suffering is that righteousness is beside the point when it comes to suffering. In fact, removing the later added artificial epilogue, Job does not offer closure of any kind. Instead, it challenges the very act of asking questions like why do righteous people suffer. It forces us to look away from simplistic explanations of our suffering (punishment from sin etc..) to a embrace a wider galactic focus on God and the grand scale of the universe that he has made that hardly is for our sake.

But there is also another message that the book of Job can be interpreted to offer. It is maybe that suffering and this tough shift of focus, brings us closer to God. Only a handful of people in the Bible are granted intimate contact with God, and Job gets the greatest privilege of being among them due to his experiences.

It is also imperative that we have some basic understanding of the historical context in which this text was compiled to its final form. The book was written to the Jewish community after they had experienced one of their worst national crises - the Babylonian exile. At that time, the Jews would have felt the most deserted from their God especially given the promise that God had made to him that the land of Israel was promised to them as a nation. The Babylonian conquest and exile made the Jews feel that God had gone back on his promise to His chosen people. They would have wanted an explanation so badly. The prophetic books of the Bible give an explanation that the friends of Job give - that the exile was a result of the sins of the Jewish nation (the sin that the prophets thought was responsible for this was idolatry). But the book of Job provides an alternative more philosophical explanation of this suffering and crises. In this historical context, the character Job represents the Jewish community as a whole and his suffering stands for the despair and the suffering that Jews felt from the hand of their Babylonian conquerors.

When we suffer, the answer that the book of Job provides is that we are looking at the universe and God in a selfish self-centred way and that we have to turn the gaze around from ourselves to God who is much more greater than anything we can ever imagine and does not run the universe to cater to our whims. Is this a convincing answer? Of course, nothing can be a convincing answer to a person at his moment of suffering. But, after everything relaxes, and we become mature to turn the tables around away from ourselves to the big picture, this might be an answer. The answer that we are nothing compared to the vastness of the universe and God and that the universe is not centred around us is not such a foreign notion for us in the modern world.

***Pale Blue Dot*** is a photograph of our earth taken on February 14, 1990, by the *Voyager 1* space probe from an unprecedented distance of over 6 billion kilometers (3.7 billion miles, 40.5 AU), as part of that day's *Family Portrait* series of images of the solar system. In the above photograph, Earth's apparent size is less than a pixel; the planet appears as a tiny dot against the vastness of space, among bands of sunlight reflected by the camera. Commissioned by NASA and resulting from the advocacy of astronomer and author **Carl Sagan**, the photograph was interpreted in Sagan's 1994 book, *Pale Blue Dot*, as representing humanity's minuscule and ephemeral place amidst the cosmos. Carl Sagan says this about this photograph :

From this distant vantage point, the Earth might not seem of any particular interest. But for us, it's different. Consider again that dot. That's here. That's home. That's us. On it everyone you love, everyone you know, everyone you ever heard of, every human being who ever was, lived out their lives. The aggregate of our joy and suffering, thousands of confident religions, ideologies, and economic doctrines, every hunter and forager, every hero and coward, every creator and destroyer of civilisation, every king and peasant, every young couple in love, every mother and father, hopeful child, inventor and explorer, every teacher of morals, every corrupt politician, every "superstar", every "supreme leader", every saint and sinner in the history of our species lived there – on a mote of dust suspended in a sunbeam.

The Earth is a very small stage in a vast cosmic arena. Think of the rivers of blood spilled by all those generals and emperors so that, in glory and triumph, they could become the momentary masters of a fraction of a dot. Think of the endless cruelties visited by the inhabitants of one corner of this pixel on the scarcely distinguishable inhabitants of some other corner, how frequent their misunderstandings, how eager they are to kill one another, how fervent their hatreds.

Our posturings, our imagined self-importance, the delusion that we have some privileged position in the Universe, are challenged by this point of pale light. Our planet is a lonely speck in the great enveloping cosmic dark.

**I personally think that the book of Job provides us with this galactic shift in perspective that Carl Sagan does. It provides us with a disturbing vision of a vast cosmos (created and governed by a deity) in which we are very small players. We might think reflectively or instinctively that the universe is there to cater to us but the book hammers down to us that maybe we are not at all that important. Suffering is indeed rampant but our entire lives are all ephemeral in a pale blue dot. There could be a divinity to the world but whatever/whoever that divinity is, it/he/she is not about us and our justice. The fundamental nature of the universe and of our lives may be beyond our understanding. Meaning in life is found in a relationship to a very mysterious divine (or to a mysterious cosmos for Carl Sagan). This may be a pessimistic view but it does directly speaks to us because we do live in a world that in many ways is deeply mysterious and incomprehensible in many ways.**

Prof. **Kathryn McClymond,** in her lecture on the book of Job says this about the text **:**

Books like Job reframe our day-to-day existence and concerns by re-situating the human experience by pulling back from a close-up camera shot of the universe that is focused on “me, me, me” and zooming back for a panoramic shot of the entire cosmos (from a divine point of view). With this cosmic perspective in mind, it challenges us to rethink how we imagine our place in the cosmos and the questions we ask in life.

This is a very uncomfortable book, I know, and gets under my nerves every time I read it. I have read it several times in both the English and in Hebrew with a running dictionary but the range of chaotic emotions that I feel while reading it, does not diminish at all. I think the book was intended to be such an experience and this is also the reason why it has spoken to us over the centuries. It is not merely a religious text that speaks to certain people in certain times but is a universal text speaking to the human condition, not because it answers our questions but it dares us to ask new ones!
