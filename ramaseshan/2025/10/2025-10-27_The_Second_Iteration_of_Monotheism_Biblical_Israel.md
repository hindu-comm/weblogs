The development of monotheism in Biblical Israel that eventually would develop into Judaism is the most important development in history in terms of the lasting impact it had on subsequent world history through its two successor world religions : Christianity and Islam. But this foundational processes that were behind this development is not that clear because for many of the crucial events in the history of Judaism in the Biblical period, the only literary evidence that we have are from the Hebrew Bible and archaeology too is not always clear to give some picture as artefacts cannot speak. But nevertheless, it is easy to get an overall idea of the process and that is what will be the focus of this article.

# Israel before Judaism : Welcome to Canaan

The land that later came to be known after the character **Israel** (also popularly known as *Jacob*) from the Bible is called **Canaan** in the Bible itself. It is a narrow strip of land that lies between Asia and Africa in the Eastern end of the Mediterranean Sea. To the left of this land, lies the hostile sea, personified by the god **Yam**. To its right, lies the desert, personified by **Mot**, the god of death. Between the hostile sea and the hostile desert, lies this narrow strip of promised land. There are also no useful river systems in Canaan to sustain agriculture - the Dead Sea is useless. In this precarious situation, the only hope for life is the storm that brings rain every year. Hence, the god of storms and rain **Baal** is the most important Canaanite god and the king of all the gods in the Canaanite pantheon.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!guSc!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc78f32a9-3b40-490c-90b5-056b10ccbba2_980x980.heic)

Every year, the Canaanites saw clouds coming from the storms of the sea giving rain, then vanishing into the underworld via the desert again, only to be renewed and return in the next season. They would have conceived it as victory of the god **Baal** over **Yam** (sea), then to be defeated by **Mot** (death - desert) & to be renewed again to return next year. Baal’s sister **Anat**, the goddess of war, defeats Mot and renews Baal. This cosmic cycle is technically called the **Baal cycle**. The Canaanites saw the fierce storm emerging from the sea again as a periodically recurring cosmic battle.

> *Note* : The word **Baal** in the Canaanite (and other West Semitic)
> languages simply means “*Lord*”. The word sometimes becomes “**Bel**”
> in other related East Semitic languages. Different Semitic cultures
> add epithets to either of these names to narrow down and refer to the
> storm god in particular like the Canaanite **Baal Hadad** (the rider
> of the clouds). But let us just use the word **Baal** to refer to the
> storm god unless otherwise specified which the Bible does as well.
>
> As an example, the national god of ***Carthage*** (a Canaanite
> settlement in North Africa that eventually became the famous rival to
> Rome) is ***Baal-Hamon*** which is not the storm god *Baal Hadad*.

There is another important divine character in the Canaanite pantheon. It is the god **El**. He is described as the father of all gods in Canaanite mythology. Note that El is not just the father of useful gods like Baal and Anat, but also the hostile deities Mot and Yam. He tries to sell out the elevated god Baal to the other gods in the conflict as he is in a dilemma because of his children fighting. He definitely does not have good PR despite being called by the titles like “***El**, the kind and compassionate*”. It is important to note that in the Bible, the god of the Jews is called as “**Eloh\[im\]**” which has “**El**” as a part of it.

If El is the not so popular deity despite being the father of all gods, his consort **Asherah** (also called **Elat**) was not such a case. She was an important supreme mother goddess in Canaanite religion. Particularly important for the Bible are an important kind of objects associated with her. It is called the **Asherah pole** that is a sacred (living) tree or wooden pole in the shape of Asherah that stood near Canaanite religious locations to honour her.

Another set of objects mentioned in the Bible and attested archaeologically are idols or images for household veneration which are called in Hebrew as **teraphim** in the plural. The teraphim of Asherah were very popular with Canaanites.

What happened to the Canaanites? Around the time of 1200 BCE, one sees the collapse of Canaanite city states. This is not just a local event but a global catastrophe across the Mediterranean with lots of cities burning and lots of migrations going on. This period is called the **Late Bronze Age Collapse** in history and marks the end of the Bronze Age. The big empires like Egypt, Hittite, and Assyria too collapse or weaken during this period. It also marks the end of the **Mycenaean Greek** city states and writing is forgotten in Greece (until they again adopt the Phoenician alphabet and start to write again). Look at the picture below that summarises the burning of cities and migrations of new peoples. It is after the collapse that the Israelites emerge as a people in Canaan.

# What the Bible tells us

What does the Bible tell us about all this? Ignoring obviously mythic events like the creation of the world, the Bible may provide some historical clue. It tells us of a person named **Abraham** from the city of **Ur** in Mesopotamia who is called upon by a deity named **Yahweh** whom he had never seen. Yahweh asks Abraham to migrate to Canaan and promises him that his descendants would be multitude and would inherit the land of Canaan that was between the *Jordan river* and the *Mediterranean Sea*. Is this episode in the Bible a remembrance of an actual pre-historical migrations of Semitic peoples from Mesopotamia to Canaan? We can’t say for sure. But let us continue reading further in the Bible.

Abraham’s son grandson **Jacob** is renamed as **Israel,** and the twelve sons of Jacob become the ancestors to the twelve tribes of Israel later. Due to interesting circumstances and eventually a famine, the sons of Jacob end up in Egypt but eventually as centuries pass, the descendants of Abraham become slaves in Egypt. Then, Yahweh uses the prophet Moses to rescue the enslaved Israelites from Egypt. This event is the celebrated **exodus** in the Bible. Is the migration of the family of Abraham to Egypt a remembrance of the migration of some of the Semites in Canaan to Egypt? Is the exodus of Hebrews from Egypt, too, an actual historical event? Again, we can’t say for sure but we know for sure that the episodes in Egypt in the Bible were definitely written by someone who was in Egypt and knew the culture from the inside out by looking at the extraneous cultural details in the Bible and comparing it to what actually is attested about Egyptian culture from archaeology and hieroglyphic sources. But let us continue reading further in the Bible.

After wandering for forty years in the desert, we read that the Israelites, led by the general **Joshua**, enter the land of Canaan that was promised by their god Yahweh to their ancestor Abraham. But there are already Canaanites settled in Canaan. What does Yahweh tell the Israelites to do to the various peoples of Canaan to do?

> **Genocide**.

The reason?

> **Because Canaanites worship other gods**.

The giant **Goliath** whom David fights is a Philistine.

How credible are these events that are narrated in the Bible? It turns out that there is archaeological proof of the destruction of Canaanite city states. There is also archaeological proof of the divided monarchies of Israel and Judah but we still have not managed to find any evidence for a united Israelite monarchy or even Solomon’s temple in Jerusalem. The current temple mount in Jerusalem we have only dates back to the second temple that was supposedly rebuilt after the return from Babylonian exile. We also do not find any evidence for the exodus of the Hebrew people from Egypt.

# What Actually Happened to Canaan?

Now comes the next question - what actually happened? Are the Israelites really responsible for the destruction of Canaanite city states that we observe in the Late Bronze Age Collapse? Note that in Greek epic, one hears of the Trojan War that too is set in the Late Bronze Age Collapse. But despite the command to genocide, even in the Bible, we see Canaanite peoples and eventually Canaanite kingdoms surrounding Israel. Also, despite the deep seated enmity, the Israelites are Canaanites in every other way you can think of. Their language Hebrew is a dialect of Canaanite and their Paleo-Hebrew script based on the Phoenician alphabet as well is the same for many Canaanites. Culturally too, the Israelites are all same as the Canaanites. The Biblical scholar Mark Smith writes :

Despite the long-regnant model that the Canaanites and Israelites were people of fundamentally different cultures, archaeological data now casts doubt on this view. The material culture of the region exhibits numerous common points between Israelites and Canaanites in the Iron I period (c.1200–1000 BCE). The record would suggest that the Israelite culture largely overlapped with and derived from Canaanite culture. \[...\] In short, Israelite culture was largely Canaanite in nature. Given the information available, one cannot maintain a radical cultural separation between Canaanites and Israelites during the Iron I period.

But what about the Egypt story - how did the exodus get the cultural context right? The Israelites could have emerged as a nation from a mixture of the pre-existing Canaanite cultures and another group of people coming from Egypt (whose ancestors had migrated from Canaan). The author of the Egyptian stories of the Bible would have belonged to one or some of the Israelite tribes who came from Egypt. How can the memory of just one group of people, that is the Egyptian origins, become a part of the entire collective memory? It has happened even in recent times. The US celebrates Thanksgiving as its national festival to commemorate the anniversary of those pilgrims of English Puritan Christians who landed on the shores of America from the ship Mayflower after fleeing from Europe in search of a land to freely practise the Puritan version of Christianity, which was not gaining favour in England during the sixteenth century. But, obviously, not everyone who is a citizen of the US traces their ancestry back to those 102 pilgrims who came from England aboard the Mayflower in 1620. Most Americans came gradually over time from different parts of Europe, but this Mayflower memory has been etched into the consciousness of all Americans who celebrate Thanksgiving.

> **Yahweh could have been one tribal deity of the clan or group of
> clans of Israelites escaping Egypt who ended up becoming the national
> deity of the unified Israelite tribes.**

The theme of a god rescuing his chosen people would have etched deeply into the consciousness of all the Israelite tribes which elevated the status of the deity Yahweh among all the Israelite tribes and led to monolatry and eventually monotheism as we will see in the next section. The idea that Yahweh is the national god of Israelites on whose name they go when they fight in wars is seen clearly for example in **The Song of Deborah** that occurs in Judges Chapter 5.

# The Israelites are Reluctant Monotheists

In the Bible, even the Israelites themselves are frequently seen falling into sin by worshipping Canaanite gods (especially Baal and Asherah). Yahweh has to rebuke them and punish them to make the Israelites mend their ways and come away from these other gods. The Israelites are shown as reluctant monotheists in the Bible from the very beginning; despite many commandments and warnings from Yahweh himself and the prophets. Just after the exodus, when Moses goes up the hill to get the tablet of ten commandments from Yahweh, we read in the Bible that the Israelites fashion a golden calf and begin worshipping it as they are not sure when Moses would come back. Yahweh gets furious and wants to destroy them all and it is Moses has to broker peace on behalf of the Israelites.

The book of Judges in the Bible is basically this algorithm.

1.  The people of Israel fall into Canaanite idolatry

2.  Yahweh sends them into defeat when they fight with their enemies

3.  The Israelites realise and repent to Yahweh and pray to rescue them

4.  Yahweh is moved and sends a charismatic leader (called a judge) to
    rescue them from their enemies

5.  The Israelites are happy and some generations pass

6.  Repeat step 1

Enter the books of Samuel and Kings. Why did the united monarchy under King Solomon collapse? The Bible gives a theological justification as well for this. Solomon married lot of foreign wives from neighbouring kingdoms to secure his diplomatic ties and ended up paying respects to the deities of his spouse and that is why Yahweh himself tells in the Bible that he will cause the united monarchy to fall after his death. In the book of II Kings, we see the prophet **Elijah** vehemently touring Israel and fighting and challenging the priests of Baal. In one dramatic episode, he asks the priests of Baal to offer food that he also does for Yahweh and tells that if Baal was real, then he should be able to come and accept the food. Yahweh comes down and burns the food offering of Elijah and accepts while the offerings to Baal remain unacknowledged, thus signalling the victory of Yahweh over Baal.

Even as late as the times of King Josiah of Judah, we see that the Jews are worshipping Baal and Asherah right at Yahweh’s temple in Jerusalem.

**⁴** The king (Josiah) ordered Hilkiah the high priest, the priests next in rank and the doorkeepers **to remove from the temple of the Lord all the articles made for Baal and Asherah and all the starry hosts**. He burned them outside Jerusalem in the fields of the Kidron Valley and took the ashes to Bethel. **⁵ He did away with the idolatrous priests** appointed by the kings of Judah to burn incense on the high places of the towns of Judah and on those around Jerusalem—**those who burned incense to Baal, to the sun and moon, to the constellations and to all the starry hosts.** **⁶ He took the Asherah pole from the temple of the Lord** to the Kidron Valley outside Jerusalem and burned it there. He ground it to powder and scattered the dust over the graves of the common people. **⁷ He also tore down** the quarters of the male shrine prostitutes that were in the temple of the Lord, **the quarters where women did weaving for Asherah** (II Kings 23)

> We can hence conclude that **the exclusive worship of Yahweh during
> the Biblical period was the wish of just the priestly class, the
> ruling class (like King Josiah), and the upper echelons of Israelite
> society, who wanted to strengthen the unique national identity of the
> kingdom of Judah by elevating Yahweh and centralising his worship in
> Jerusalem. It is this class whose voice and prescriptions ended up
> being written and preserved in the Bible. But even then, we can see
> that, both in the Biblical text and in the archaeological record, what
> was happening on the ground amongst the common people and even inside
> Jerusalem, was polytheism, for a very long time.** **Even
> archaeologically, one finds cultic objects of worship in the houses of
> the Israelites in the Biblical period.**

Also, there are many verses in the Bible that do not make sense if one assumes that Yahweh is the only god. Even the commands that Yahweh gives implies that there are other gods to whom other people can turn up to; just that the Israelites are to worship only Yahweh (this is called **monolatry** where you acknowledge the existence of other gods for other peoples despite you worshipping only one God).

(Yahweh says) : “On that same night I will pass through Egypt and strike down every firstbornof both people and animals, and **I will bring judgment on all the gods of Egypt.** I am the Lord”. (Exodus 12 : 12)

For the above sentence to make sense, it implies that the gods of Egypt must exist. Yahweh is referred to by the epithet “**God Most High**” (El alyon) which again implies that other lower divinities must exist. Even archaeologically, we see teraphim and other cultic objects of worship in ancient Israelite houses.

So, we see that the Israelite religion emerged in a polytheistic Canaanite context with Yahweh then being elevated as the national deity of Israel and then as the supreme deity (henotheism) and then as the only one to be worshipped (monolatry). In the entire Torah (first five books of the Bible), Yahweh does not deny that other gods exist and says that other gods are allotted for other people. The Israelites continue to worship Canaanite divinities well up to the time of King Josiah right at the heart of the Jerusalem temple to Yahweh. Even the ***shema*** prayer (evening prayer) of Judaism that is derived from Deuteronomy 6 : 4 and widely seen as the creed for Judaism reads as :

Hear, O Israel: The LORD **our** God, the LORD alone

This says “our God” and not “the God”.

So, we see that despite a prescriptive monolatry just for Israel, and that the Israelites were reluctant monolaters and kept falling back to Canaanite gods. What eventually made the Jews come in line with their priests and prophets by becoming monolatrous and eventually monotheistic?

# Canaanite Culture in Israel

One sees lots of elements of Canaanite religion surviving in Judaism in the Biblical period. One obvious element is the reference to Yahweh as “**Elohim**”, after “**El**”, the father of gods in the Canaanite pantheon. The stormy imagery of Baal too survives in the Psalms of the Bible. For example, consider Psalm 29 below :

This imagery would be more appropriate to a storm god like Baal. Also, when we analyse the sounds of this psalm in the original Hebrew, some phenomenal insight is gained. First, I present to you this psalm in the transliterated Hebrew with the word for God “Yahweh” presented as “yhwh” and highlighted in yellow. Read this first and develop an ear for this psalm.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ydhb!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8e32f14f-81f9-4bba-ab18-157bd2800f39_1210x658.heic)

Now, I replace the word “yhwh” by the word baʿal (which is the name of Baal). The ʿ is a guttural consonant like “h” in Hebrew. I also do a colour coding.

- I highlight in in red, consonants that are produced by the joining of
  the lips like **B**, **M**, and **W.**

- I highlight in blue, the consonants that are pronounced by the tip of
  the tongue touching the front teeth like **L, T, D, Z, R, S, Ś**, and
  **Ṣ**.

- I highlight in green, the consonants that are produced close to the
  throat like **H, K, Q, G, ʿ**, and **Ḥ**.

- I highlight in black, the consonants that are produced by the palate
  of the mouth like **Y** and **S̆** (English “sh”).

- All the vowels are lightened to grey and unbolded to clarify the
  distinctions in consonants.

With this in mind, observe the rhythmic alternation in these four types of consonants by observing the colour alternation between red, green, blue, and black. One sees that replacing yhwh by baʿal improves and fixes the rhythm in consonantal alliteration of this poem (look at just the alternating Bs and Ls in phrases like HA**B**U **L**A**B**AʿA**L** and QO**L** **B**AʿA**L** and so on.

So, now do you see what has happened? Replacing ‘yhwh’ by ‘baʿal’ fixes the rhyming of the poem. Baal is a storm god and this poem has storm imagery. What can this mean? Definitely, this psalm must have been a Canaanite song dedicated to Baal which the Israelites later adopted and adapted to Yahweh! In replacing Baal by Yahweh, they messed up the rhyme a little but fixed up their theology!

In the 1920s, archaeologists dug up to find the entire ancient Canaanite city of **Ugarit** (located today in the coast of Northern Syria) that had many cuneiform inscriptions and among them was discovered lots of songs inscribed to Baal, and phrases from the Biblical psalms that I showed you also appear!! The Ugaritic texts portray El as a benevolent patriarch and Baal as a dynamic warrior. In the Hebrew Bible, Yahweh absorbs attributes of both, combining El’s sovereignty with Baal’s martial prowess. This synthesis allowed Yahweh to encompass the roles of other deities, reducing the need for a pantheon. The Ugaritic texts describe a divine assembly led by El, with Baal as a prominent subordinate deity. This structure mirrors references in the Hebrew Bible to a “divine council” (e.g., Psalm 82, Job 1–2), where Yahweh presides over other divine beings. We will see in the next section about another divine council led by Yahweh where the gods of other nations get demoted to being mere mortals, thereby asserting the supremacy of Yahweh as the only god.

> So, the Israelites adopted and adapted the entire Canaanite religion
> and culture to serve their monolatrous deity Yahweh.

# The Babylonian Exile & Prophetic Monotheism

The greatest changes in our lives happen at the most troubled moments. That holds true for nations, religions and cultures too. It is crisis that can redefine and reorient one drastically. We saw that during the collapse of the Bronze Age, large empires had fallen or weakened. But as time passed, they grew stronger and hungry for conquests. The **Assyrian Empire** rose again to conquer the northern kingdom of Israel around 722 BCE and its successor **Babylonian Empire** conquered the kingdom of Judah at around 586 BCE. The elites of Judah were exiled to Babylon, the royal family was killed, and the city of Jerusalem including the temple to Yahweh was burnt to the ground. The Bible recounts this event briefly in the book of II Kings Chapter 25 as follows :

**⁸** On the seventh day of the fifth month, in the nineteenth year of Nebuchadnezzar, king of Babylon, Nebuzaradan commander of the imperial guard, an official of the king of Babylon, came to Jerusalem. **⁹** He set fire to the temple of the Lord, the royal palace and all the houses of Jerusalem. Every important building he burned down. **¹⁰** The whole Babylonian army under the commander of the imperial guard broke down the walls around Jerusalem.**¹¹** Nebuzaradan the commander of the guard carried into exile the people who remained in the city, along with the rest of the populace and those who had deserted to the king of Babylon. **¹²** But the commander left behind some of the poorest people of the land to work the vineyards and fields.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rP29!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb3a7c473-09a1-4ece-8907-1e4a49b881f8_1129x773.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rP29!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb3a7c473-09a1-4ece-8907-1e4a49b881f8_1129x773.png)

Throughout their history, Judeans debated how to understand the relationship between them and their national deity, Yahweh. The Judeans definitely faced a crisis during the exile. They had a patron deity Yahweh who was closely associated with their nation and the temple in Jerusalem. But now, the temple was gone, and they were not even in their homeland where Yahweh lived. So, going by the inseparable association between geography and religion in the ancient world, the exiled Judeans—who could no longer visit Jerusalem—could not worship their patron god in any meaningful way. They were now away from their nation and hence from the home of their national god as well. The tension of worshipping their God away from their homeland was felt by the Judeans, and this is recorded in the Bible. For example, consider this famous poem from the Book of Psalms (137).

**¹** By the rivers of Babylon we sat and wept when we remembered Zion.  
**²** There on the poplars, we hung our harps, **³** for there our captors asked us for songs, our tormentors demanded songs of joy; they said, “Sing us one of the songs of Zion!” **⁴ How can we sing the songs of the Lord while in a foreign land?** **⁵** If I forget you, Jerusalem, may my right hand forget its skill. **⁶** May my tongue cling to the roof of my mouth, if I do not remember you, if I do not consider Jerusalem my highest joy.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!0ofZ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F651373a1-90f7-4819-9b96-ad14f9d375a1_1132x766.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!0ofZ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F651373a1-90f7-4819-9b96-ad14f9d375a1_1132x766.png)

So, in this psalm, we see the exiled Judeans lamenting about how they could sing the songs of their lord in a foreign land. This was a norm in the ancient world. Every deity of a nation or a city could be worshipped meaningfully only at that place. The Judeans could give up the worship of the other Canaanite gods now that they were away from Canaan, but they could not give up the worship of their patron god, Yahweh, who was special to them and was the ‘God Most High’. What happened next?

**It was also at this time that the Jews began to reflect on why they had failed as a nation**. According to their theology, their god Yahweh had promised to them the land of Canaan as an eternal inheritance and that he would look after them, Why did not their patron god Yahweh protect them from the Babylonians? Had Yahweh deserted them during the times of crisis?

The Judeans thereby had to invoke something else to explain why their patron deity would make his nation suffer an exile. According to standard belief, Yahweh was either weak or he had failed his own people. This could not be digested. It is during this time that the prophets came and told them that the exile was not because Yahweh had broken his promise but because the Judeans had broken their promise by worshipping other gods. So, the prophets convinced the Jews in exile that exclusive worship of Yahweh would placate him and then he would deliver them from exile. The prophets at this time came to believe that when the Babylonian king **Nebuchadnezzar** went to attack Jerusalem and her temple, Yahweh had already departed from the temple. This meant god had left of his own will, and therefore, technically not defeated. In other words, Yahweh had not been defeated but had forsaken his people. He himself caused the defeat of Judah by Babylon. This is found for example, in the Book of Ezekiel Chapter 9. Yahweh had forsaken them because they had strayed from him to worship other gods. Quoting the scholar **Robert Goldenberg** here :

A Babylonian army destroyed the Kingdom of Judah and the holy city of Jerusalem during the lives of two great prophets, Jeremiah and Ezekiel. Facing this calamity, the nation’s leaders had to consider the usual explanation for such events: that the Babylonians’ gods had been stronger than their own and had overcome Yahweh’s attempts to protect them. At an earlier time, an Assyrian general had made precisely such a mocking claim while besieging Jerusalem. That siege had failed, but the idea haunted later generations: could any other god be stronger than Israel’s? Could Yahweh’s protection fail them at a critical moment? The prophets’ answer was to deny this possibility. If Yahweh’s people have suffered disaster, it must be because their own God has inflicted this punishment upon them for their sins against him. The other so-called gods are not Yahweh’s rivals: if anything, they are his agents and servants \[for Israel’s punishment\]. They are surely no more than that. Now people sometimes treated a king’s ministers as if they were actually the king himself. This could be a useful habit. The king himself was often so far away as to be invisible, a mere name, which other, more local officials invoked to justify their own power. It could not hurt to treat such officials with deference (respect): they were the ones with real power over ordinary people and their daily lives. But if this were true of the human agents of a human king, could not the same be said for the heavenly agents—sun, wind, and so on—who were messengers and creatures of the one true God? The forces of nature have great power over our lives, and for centuries it seemed only prudent to treat those forces with the same deference that one offered to human servants of high rank. This kind of thinking lay behind Israel’s adoption of the religious veneration of nature that was so important throughout the ancient Near East.

The Judeans saw **Nebuchadnezzar** not as the king who defeated their nation and their God but as a servant of their own God who was sent to punish the Judeans for their apostasy by worshipping other gods. For example, Jeremiah 25 : 9-11 reads :

**“I \[God\] will summon** **all the peoples of the north and my servant Nebuchadnezzar,** **king of Babylon”**, declares the Lord, “and I will bring them against this land and its inhabitants and against all the surrounding nations. I will completely destroy them \[ Judeans\] and make them an object of horror and scorn, and an everlasting ruin. I will banish from them the sounds of joy and gladness, the voices of bride and bridegroom, the sound of millstones and the light of the lamp. This whole country will become a desolate wasteland, and these nations will serve the king of Babylon for seventy years”.

From here on, there are only two steps in the jump to monotheism. If the gods of other nations and natural forces were indeed weaker than Yahweh and subordinate to him, then why did these other gods even deserve to be worthy of the title ‘god’? So, there is a demotion of the other gods from godly status. This demotion is also seen in the Bible in Psalm 82.

God presides in the great assembly; he renders judgment among the “gods”: **²** “How long will you defend the unjust and show partiality to the wicked? **³** Defend the weak and the fatherless; uphold the cause of the poor and the oppressed.**⁴** Rescue the weak and the needy; deliver them from the hand of the wicked. **⁵** “The ‘gods’ know nothing, they understand nothing. They walk about in darkness; all the foundations of the earth are shaken. **⁶** “I said, ‘You are “gods”; you are all sons of the Most High.’ **⁷** But you will die like mere mortals; you will fall like every other ruler.” **⁸** Rise up, O God, judge the earth, for all the nations are your inheritance.

Here, the gods of other nations become demoted to mere mortals and all nations become the inheritance of Yahweh. So, the other gods do not deserve to be called as gods. Yahweh is the only divinity now. Another example can be seen from the Book of Daniel where the gods of other nations are called princes and guardian angels who are each protecting their own nations but under the control of the supreme Yahweh.

If theology is linked to nationalism, then xenophobia and national supremacism leads to monotheism. To again quote Robert Goldenberg :

Monotheism thus became a by-product of patriotism \[or xenophobia\], of the wish to strengthen Israel’s identity by purging its life of elements that were felt to be of foreign origin \[this is important to maintain one’s national identity when in exile\].

Eventually, in the prophetic books, we arrive at the notion that the other gods of other nations are not just powerless mortals but figments of imagination by other peoples. For example, Jeremiah 10 :

They \[people who worship idols\] are all senseless and foolish; they are taught by worthless wooden idols. Hammered silver is brought from Tarshish and gold from Uphaz. What the craftsman and goldsmith have made is then dressed in blue and purple—all made by skilled workers. But the Lord (Yahweh) is the true God; he is the living God, the eternal King. When he is angry, the earth trembles; the nations cannot endure his wrath.

Thus, we see that ***Jewish monotheism was a cope to the trauma faced by the community during the Babylonian captivity. It was a way for them to maintain their distinct identity as a distinct people with a distinct origins and project a superior identity when under exile in a hostile foreign land. What the prescription of the priests and kings could not achieve was finally achieved by the rhetoric of the prophets during the time of the exile.***

Also note that it was the non-poor class of Judeans who were exiled by Babylon. These people had already strong monolatrous tendencies. So, it was also easier for monotheism to take hold and solidify during their exile as these upper class people constituted a bulk of the people who were exiled. When these monotheistic Jews would return to Judah after the exile ended (thanks to the conquest of Babylonian Empire by the Persian Empire), they would dominate the land again and build a second temple under the permission of the Persian emperor Cyrus. It is this second temple one of whose walls stands today after it was destroyed by the Romans again at about 70 CE.

> **As Israel sought to maintain its identity in exile, monotheism
> became a marker of distinctiveness. By rejecting the gods of their
> neighbours, the Israelites held on to their distinct identity (despite
> being away from their homeland) by emphasising their unique covenant
> with Yahweh.**

# Why not proselytise?

Now, if Jews are, indeed, to believe that Yahweh is the only god and that no other gods exist, then the next logical step would be to proselytize other peoples and turn them away from their false gods and make them worship the one true god Yahweh. Yet, this missionary step was not pursued by Judaism but would be taken up later by Christianity.

From this, it is only one more small step to a universal monotheistic proselytizing cult. It would be to say that since none of these other gods even exist and are all illusions of other peoples, it must be the duty of the Judeans to make them turn away from their false idols to the one true god, Yahweh. This step would be taken much later by Christianity—the first religion with a missionary zeal that wanted all other nations to abandon all their false gods and worship the one true god Yahweh. Christians would claim that Yahweh is the only god worthy of worship for all nations.

But why did not Judaism itself pursue this next step? Is it because the Jews never had any real political power? Over time, particularly from the Rabbinic tradition to the present, Judaism came to prohibit proselytisation—Jews are not allowed to convert others to their faith and are expected to discourage conversion.

Why? We will see one exception to this in the chapter on Hellenistic Judaism when Jews convert some of their enemy peoples. But still, we need to explain the general case that Judaism is not systematically proselytising and has the impulse to convert all nations of the world, even though it believes only its god exists.

The reason is that the Jews still saw themselves as their God’s chosen people. Recall the verse in which God asks Abraham to circumcise himself and all his male descendants who would become a great nation due to the covenant that God made with Abraham. God did not choose other peoples—so Jews would have nothing to demand from these people. For Jews, Yahweh was still only their God—because God chose only Israel—even if they could say that the other gods don’t exist. Their Yahweh had chosen Israel; hence they would demand nothing from other peoples. Remember the pharaoh **Akhenaten** who postulated that Egyptians are not any special in front of his only true god Aten? That was not the case for the Jews who always saw themselves as a community chosen to be in covenant with God. So, despite being godless, they would not have the systematic urge to convert all nations.
