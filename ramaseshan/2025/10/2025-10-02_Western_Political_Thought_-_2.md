# Machiavelli and the Dawn of a New Order

The Florentine philosopher **Machiavelli** (1469 - 1527) created a new paradigm by breaking away from his classical and medieval predecessors. All of the philosophers that we have seen so far are prescriptive to varying extents and emphasizing that states should be moral in some way. But Machiavelli is the first philosopher who is a pragmatic realist and focuses on describing how states actually function and stay in power and even goes on so far to dare to ask if a state could be moral in the first place. Although he is most famous for his amoral work "**The Prince**", Machiavelli also wrote the "**Discourses**", which supported republicanism which he admired too.

Machiavelli lived at a time when the various city states of Italy were competing with each other in trade and jostling for power and when the Pope was a very political figure (when has Italian politics not been turbulent?!). Also, these city states of Italy were facing threats of invasions from the large kingdoms of France, Spain, and the Holy Roman Empire because of how much the Italian city states had become rich due to the trade with the East. Machiavelli signals the beginning of the eclipse of the ancient and medieval political philosophical view that only moral virtue can form a good or just polity. From Plato through the medieval period, the idea that a just society could be constructed without virtuous citizens or virtuous rulers would sound insane to any philosopher. But Machiavelli finds that political society often has to get along without moral virtue and find a way to be just and stable without it.

### Reputation as a cynic

Machiavelli has gained a reputation for being a cold hearted cynic. It is not entirely unjust when we read some of his suggestions in “The Prince”. For example, when an unpopular task must be done, Machiavelli suggests the ruler to appoint a special advisor to make the needed changes; if the people hate those changes, the ruler can claim ignorance of the advisor’s activities and then have him executed. Another cold hearted aphorism that he makes is that people with small grievances are more dangerous than people with large grievances because the more badly a person is hurt by you, the more unable he becomes to secure revenge against you. Another famous saying of him is that it is better for a ruler to be feared than loved, if only one is possible. This is because people’s minds are fickle and they will forget the love bestowed on them very easily when the chips are down.

### Justifying wickedness

But Machiavelli does not prefer wickedness for its own sake. He argues that the end justifies the means and the end of every ruler should be to keep his control over the state stable. If cruelty is the way by which stability is maintained in certain situations, so be it. Machiavelli tells us that a prince needs to learn to be wicked in order to deal with the weakness of human nature and the inevitable wickedness of others. An ideal ruler should be able to act like a lion or a fox depending on the situation. But Machiavelli also says that even cruelty does not guarantee success and there is always an element of fortune involved but also recognises that fortune tends to favor the brave.

"***The Prince***" is undeniably the most famous classic and represents the first instance of the advocacy of realism in ruling a state. But Machiaevelli says that although his suggestion is new and unprecedented, leaders have always acted and still act in this way. Just that the philosophers had not advocated or justified it. Machiavelli explicitly states that he is talking about real politics about what rulers actually do to stay in power in the real world instead of ideal politics about what rulers should do. Stable rule, not justice, is what matters at the end. He calls this approach of his as a “new order” of political thought. Machiavelli gives lots of examples from Western history to justify his political thought.

Republics are fragile, but when they are possible, they are morally superior to monarchies but the masses of the populace are fickle and prone to unlawful behavior. Hence, he argues that both republicanism and monarchy are good in their own ways. What the populace is good at is not so much knowing the right or the best policy to take but they are good at listening to various arguments for opposing policies and picking the more trustworthy argument. Thus, “government by the populace is better than government by princes,” but it usually takes a prince to establish or reform a republic; that, the people cannot. Based on the Discourses, it seems that Machiavelli does not throw out ideals and morals exactly; he simply asks about the real costs and effects of acting according to ideals. In politics, what matters is the security, order, and the kind of state that is created and maintained. But whatever must be done to create such a state is legitimate. Still, the world being what it is, all will decline.

### Exploiting division

One argument of Machiavelli that attracted my attention a lot is when he says that the best political system is not the one that reduces antagonism between the upper and lower classes but that which recognises it and takes advantage of it. How is this possible? Let us analyze it. He says in Discourses 1.4:

*They do not consider that in every republic, there are two different kind of humors - that of the \[common / poor \] people (*Italian*: **popolo**) and that of the great (*Italian*: **grandi**); and that all laws made in favour of liberty are born from their disunion, as we easily see to have happened in Rome.*

This is a very unconventional idea. Every ancient and medieval philosopher agreed that unity and amity between social groups is healthy, and enmity between them is unhealthy. Machiavelli here specifically talks of conflicts between two classes of people - the common people and the rich people. In his own day, the common people in a Renaissance Italian city state would be represented by the merchants and their guilds while the rich would be the aristocratic elite. In the Roman times, it would have been the plebeians versus the patricians. Machiavelli states that it is a universal tendency for the rich and the elite to rule and have as much control over the poor and for the poor to want as much freedom from being ruled by the elites. We all intuitively get that if nobles are given a completely free hand, then they will compete and one of them will take full control over the common people leading to tyranny. But Machiavelli argues that doing the exact opposite by granting excessive freedom to the common populace (where they are allowed to run things without constraints) also would lead to tyranny. Because in such a state of freedom, confusion and chaos will come about and spiral into instability and disorderly and unstructured mob justice. The people can thus be swayed by persuasive leaders or demagogues who exploit their grievances and promise a stable polity while seizing control and establishing a tyranny under the guise of popular will. Unconstrained power in the hands of the mob thus leads to chaos, paving the way for a strongman to restore order, resulting in tyranny. People often defer to capable leaders in times of crisis. If their power is unchecked, they may cede authority to a single figure who promises to fulfill their demands, inadvertently enabling a dictator. Thus, the government should be balanced and hence distribute power between the common people and the elite so that they can be at each other’s throats and keep the polity stable without one dominating the other which would enable a dictator to arise. Machiavelli thought that the Roman Republic was great because its multiple institutions allowed it to balance power, thus enabling it to encourage virtue and public spiritedness in its leaders; and it maintained its military vitality for a long time.

Machiavelli also openly criticises the effect of Christianity and believed that The Roman Church led to the downfall of the Roman Empire and was also harming Italy. Christianity, according to Machiavelli, reduced “vigor” and martial virtue.

# Thomas Hobbes: Natural Law and the Social Contract

We now move to the English philosopher **Thomas Hobbes** (1588 - 1679)
who was a member of the 17th century scientific revolution and the first to attempt a modern theory of society. The historical context is very different. When the Protestant Reformation emerged in Germany in 1517 and spread to other countries, it soon plunged Europe into bitter wars of religion pitting Catholic kingdoms against Protestant ones which ended only in 1648 with the signing of the peace of Westphalia. Europeans, after having drowned in blood for a century finally realized that it was best to live and let live rather than kill each other over religion and began to accept religious diversity as an inevitable reality and world order for posterity. The idea of a united Western Christendom under a single church and under a single Holy Roman Emperor had finally been repudiated. The freedom of kings and princes to choose their denomination (Catholic or Protestant) was recognised.

### The English Civil War

England was the only European nation to stay out of this messy war but only because it had its own bloody civil war that it had to face in the 17th century. The King of England **Henry VIII** in 1534 embraced the Reformation not out of theological conviction but because he wanted a divorce (that the Pope refused to grant) and the prospect of owning church land sounded attractive. But in doing so, he did one thing that would ultimately lead to the collapse of the power of the English monarchy. He convened the Parliament to vote for the motion to break away from the Roman Church. Traditionally, the Parliament in England had the power to only vote on taxes. But even a monarch as powerful as Henry VIII was too scared to break away from the almost thousand year relationship with the Roman Church, alone. So, he sought the approval of the Parliament. But in doing so, he had created a precedent. This was the first time the Parliament voted on a matter of foreign policy (breaking from Rome), rather than on taxation, which was the only jurisdiction of the parliament so far. Each of Henry’s successors also went through Parliament to get their religious settlements done: Edward VI to establish Protestantism, Mary to re-establish Catholicism, and Elizabeth I in 1559 to establish the Anglican compromise - **Anglicanism** (a mild form of Protestantism with Protestant in doctrine but Catholic in ritual, hierarchy, theology, and practice) would be the official religion of the Church of England with toleration for others. ***Empowering the Parliament in such a way would embolden one to ask a question in England that would never be raised in an absolute monarchy like France or Spain: Who was truly sovereign, the King or the Parliament?***

Since Queen Elizabeth died as a virgin queen in 1603, she was succeeded by James VI, who was also the King of Scotland. James was now a king of three countries - an Anglican England, a Catholic Ireland, and a Presbyterian Scotland. In England, a group of people called **Puritans** thought that the Reformation had not gone far enough in England and the Church of England should be fully cleansed of Catholic rituals, images, and hierarchy. But James being a pacifist king managed to rule over by not seeking to enforce uniformity and willing to let things stay their way as far as possible. But his successor **Charles I** (r.1625 - 1649) was more rigid and expected instant conformity and obedience. He kept on suspending the Parliament regularly and tried to dissolve it once for all in 1629 and legislated dubiously high taxes. In religion too, Charles was a High Church Anglican but also an inflexible man who wanted every one of his subjects to worship as he did and persecuted Puritans for failing to conform to Anglican ritual.

### The Parliament executes the king

By 1642, the English Parliament came to believe that it, and not the King, represented the interests of the nation and that royal policy had to be stopped. This led to the break out of the **English Civil War of 1642–1646** where the King was defeated. When King Charles would not negotiate in good faith, the Parliament tried and convicted him on a charge of high treason in 1649. When the king questioned the court’s legitimacy at trial, Parliament responded that it represented the interests of the people. The king was publicly executed by beheading on 30 January 1649! For the first time in English history, the English people had judicially and publicly murdered their king whom they so far believed to be the God's representative on earth. They had now abolished a thousand year old monarchy. What was next?

### Chaotic times

A period of political and religious chaos followed in England after the execution of the king, especially with religious freedom and free press proclaimed by the Parliament. Many religious sects arose in this period. The **Baptists** or “**Dippers**,” believed that baptism, and the choice of faith it implied, should be delayed until adulthood, when one could make a rational choice. The **Seekers** actually went from congregation to congregation seeking a permanent home. The **Diggers**, who were proto-communists, believed that Christ wanted to abolish private property and wanted everything to be shared in common. The **Ranters** believed that nothing was a sin unless one conceived it to be a sin! The **Quakers** believed that all people contained God’s inner light in equal measure, implying that a peasant was as good as and equal in status to a lord, a woman as good as a man. The **Muggletonians** believed that Muggleton (1609–1698) was the last prophet mentioned in the book of Revelation. Most disturbing of all though were the **Fifth Monarchy Men**. They believed that the Bible had foretold five great monarchies on Earth. Four had already fallen: Babylon, Persia, Greece, and Rome. Given that the fifth was to be the kingdom of King Jesus, the Commonwealth was only an interim arrangement, soon to be brought into conformity with Mosaic Law! In the end, the Parliament that ruled the Commonwealth was too radical for conservatives and too conservative for the radicals. Finally, the English ruling elite in 1660 decided to restore the monarchy by inviting the exiled Charles I’s son, **Charles II**, to the throne of his father!! The same would happen in France too - the Bourbon monarchy that was overthrown by the French Revolution of 1789 would be restored after the chaotic events that culminated in the death of Napoleon in 1814. The Puritan Revolution in England left a bad aftertaste; it seemed to convince the English that revolution and religious zealotry should not be allowed to go too far.

### Ingredients of a Contract Theory of Politics 

It is in this context that Thomas Hobbes operated. Hobbes’s work "**Leviathan**" (1651) is the first attempt at an account of society that could be compared to the new sciences of nature. It presents the first version of what came to be known as the **social contract theory**, which we will also see in later thinkers like Locke and Rousseau. Hobbes is a part of the Enlightenment that elevated material science as the benchmark for knowledge and hence he conceived of the human being as a machine, like all living things. The difference between humans and animals is that humans are rational machines that are capable of making rational decisions related to their passions, which are the sole source of their actions. Thus, ***for Hobbes, the justification of political society must be rooted in objective human nature, disregarding any appeal to history or tradition like a typical Enlightenment thinker***. The basic template for a contract theory of politics consists of four things:

1.  **State of Nature** - a description of how humans are before any
    political association

2.  **Creation of a contract** - the formation of a contract between
    individuals who decide to leave the state of nature and give up some
    rights

3.  **Community** - the community that is formed by the establishment of
    the contract

4.  **Government** - what kind of government do people establish through
    the contract

### Pessimistic view on the state of nature

Hobbes argues that all human actions are motivated by passions. Reason just adjudicates between passions. We are pulled by our pleasures and pushed by our vices. Hobbes thinks that humans before any political association when they are in the state of nature compete with each other for the same resources and can do anything against anyone to attain what they want. It is a state where everyone is afraid of everyone else and a state of complete distrust. As a result, the state of nature is a horrible place—*a war of all against all* — where there can be no progress because the overriding concern of each person is merely to live through the night. He says: “*no knowledge of the face of the earth; no account of time; no arts; no letters; no society; and which is worst of all, continual fear and danger of violent death; and the life of man, solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short*”. There is no objective right and wrong according to Hobbes. What is right in this state of nature is relative to a person and is the same as whatever feels good for that person. Similarly, what is wrong is person dependent and is simply whatever feels bad for the person.

### Formation of contract

For purely rational reasons, such people in a primitive state of nature would come together to form a contract to leave this condition “*so that a man be willing, when others are so too, as far forth as for peace and defense of himself he shall think it necessary, **to lay down this right to all things, and be contented with so much liberty against other men**, as he would allow other men against himself*”. Here, Hobbes says that since human beings are rational, they would come together to form a contract wherein every man decides to give up his rights to do something on to others that he would not like to be done to him by others. For example, a man would not like to be killed by another. So, he gives up his rights to kill others under this contract that he enjoyed in the state of nature. Thus, since this state would be beneficial to all, these primeval men agree to sign a contract.

But any contract has no validity without a power to enforce it. So, this is where the government or the sovereign comes in. The government gains those rights that the contracting citizens have given up so that it can enforce the contract with impunity by punishing anyone for violating it. We each give up our right to everything to the sovereign. Without a government, there is no legal political community at all; the existence of government makes things right and wrong, relative to the government. Now, the standard of good and evil is relative to what the sovereign of the government decides, not the passions of individual citizens. The sovereign government can be: **monarchy**, **aristocracy**, or **democracy**. Hobbes clearly prefers a monarchy, because the smaller the number of people with power, the greater the power and freedom of action of the government.

### *Absolute sovereign better than anarchy*

How much power does the sovereign enjoy? Notice that there is no covenant between the sovereign and the people. The sovereign cannot be bound by the law because he is the legitimation of the law. The sovereign cannot unjustly injure a subject; whatever the sovereign does is just. Freedoms in this state are whatever the sovereign does not prohibit. In fact, there is no greater crime than to presume to judge the sovereign. The only constraint of the sovereign is that he should rule by laws that apply uniformly on everyone. The law can be anything.

Hobbes’ theory might sound harsh to us. The overly pessimistic state of nature. The absolute power of the sovereign. How is this good? The answer is the historical context when he is writing - a horrible civil war and breakdown of society. For Hobbes, anyways fear we must always. The question is: Whom do we want to fear - the one sovereign power, who acts predictably by law, or all of your neighbors? The sovereign alone, with maximum power, without checks and balances, can stop the development of factions and ambitious men that would throw us into civil war. Only absolute power justifies its own legitimacy according to Hobbes. There is no “right” to protest or revolt in this framework, but if a revolt were to succeed and establish a new sovereign power that prevented further revolt, then it would be the legitimate sovereign. The former sovereign was illegitimate because he was weak.

In Hobbes, we see the clearest example of a design for a just political society that is not based on the virtue of citizens or rulers but on the pure rational self-interest of the citizens and a structure to express and limit that. Justice comes from the structure that selfish citizens freely impose on themselves. We have completely left behind classical and medieval notions of virtue. This doesn’t imply that politics is inherently immoral but only that certain kinds of virtues have been abandoned as baseless..

# John Locke: Limited Governance and Toleration

### The Glorious Revolution of England 

We now move to the next English philosopher **John Locke** (1632 - 1704) who also proposed a contract theory of politics that was different from Thomas Hobbes. If Hobbes justified his pessimistic state of nature and justified absolute control by the sovereign from his experiences with the English Civil War, John Locke was the opposite and his political theory justifies the historical context of his own day which is the **Glorious Revolution** in 1688. Let us catch up where we left with English history. **Charles II** was reinstalled in the English throne in 1660 but again problems began to arise when his brother **James II** succeeded him in 1685 because James II was a Roman Catholic. Catholics were always seen as traitors in the country whose loyalties lay with Rome rather than England especially after the disastrous events of the previous century which was the war of the Spanish Armada and the Catholic conspiracy to assassinate Queen Elizabeth. Charles II was attracted to Catholicism during his exile in France but was smart enough to not show it in public but James II openly worshiped as a Catholic. New political parties emerged: The **Tories** supported James and Charles, but the **Whigs** wanted James excluded from possible ascension. When James took the throne in 1685, the result was a tense situation in predominantly Protestant England and Scotland. But there were two reasons as to why no one revolted yet. One was because nobody wanted a rerun of another horrible civil war and the chaos that ensued. Another reason why people put up with him was because he was old and they assumed that when James died, he would be succeeded by his Protestant daughter Mary, married to the Dutch prince **William of Orange**, a Protestant. So, they thought they had rather put up with a short Catholic reign rather than a civil war. But in 1688, James’s second wife, Mary Beatrice, gave birth to a son, who would supersede the female heir apparent Mary, and continue his father’s policies. It is at this time the Glorious Revolution happened where the Parliament (with the Tories and the Whigs united) invited the Dutch prince William of Orange to invade England and assume the throne as King **William III**. This again was unprecedented because the Parliament was inviting a foreign king to invade their country and overthrow their king. John Locke’s political theory justifies this Glorious Revolution in England. William III agreed to rule as King accepting the supremacy of the Parliament.

### Optimistic view on the state of nature 

In the first section of his work "**Second Treatise on Government**", Locke gives us the main point of the entire book. Locke denies any traditional notions of political rulers as father, husband, Lord, or religious leader. Locke subscribes to the contract framework of Hobbes but differs on the details. First of all, Locke’s assessment of the state of nature is much more optimistic than Hobbes. Locke proposes that even in the state of nature, most people would just silently pursue their self-interests without harming anyone. Because Locke says that there is a natural moral law that any rational being can deduce: “*No one ought to harm another in his life, liberty, or possessions*”. But due to passions, some people do violate this law in the state of nature at which point, other members of the community punish them and force them to confirm.

In this state of nature, individuals pursue a lifestyle of pursuing self-interest without interference. But now and then, someone violates natural law, “*seek\[ing\] absolute power over another*.” When that happens, a state of war exists. Thus, Locke admits that the state of nature is insecure. It is then when rational people decide to join and form a contract. They transfer justice from the individuals to the community as a whole. Although people give up a right when they leave the state of nature (the right to justice on a wronged person), they participate in making the law that they obey and the justice that they want is still enforced by the community; thus, people are merely obeying themselves and have a freedom exactly equal to what they enjoyed before even after the contract. For Locke, the only goal of this commonwealth should be the preservation of property, life, and liberty. He believes that preservation of property and the public good are one and the same. This explains why Locke is also thought of as a proto-capitalist and enemy by some socialists.

### Minimal governance 

Locke says that whatever the government decided by the citizens (monarchy, oligarchy, democracy), the government is legitimate only as long as it acts on the behalf of the community. The power of the government is exactly the rights given up by the citizens and transferred on to the community when they signed the contract - the right to enforce justice based on the natural law (protection of life, liberty, property). Nothing more or nothing less. Any government that fails to do so can be legitimately overthrown by citizens by a revolution and political power reverts to the community until it decides who its next government should be. Protection of individual liberty is the same thing as the community good in this theory. Locke feared government power as the greatest threat to individual liberty and property. The basis for individual liberties is that the government has no power to take away our liberty or property, except with our consent.

### Toleration

Another of Locke’s important political works is his “**Letter Concerning Toleration**” that talks about religious tolerance and separation of church and state. It presents a compelling case for separating civil authority from religious objectives. Locke argues that civil government should focus exclusively on protecting life, liberty, and property, while religion addresses matters of truth, virtue, and salvation. This distinction is pivotal, as it suggests that the state is not responsible for fostering all human virtues. Locke emphasizes that religious faith is an internal matter, inherently subjective and impossible to judge accurately, rendering the enforcement of religious decrees not just unfair but also impractical - it is not pragmatic to enforce private belief. Furthermore, he notes that church membership is voluntary, unlike citizenship in civil society, which is involuntary by birth. Locke contends that determining the "correct" church is an irresolvable question, as reason—used in civil affairs—cannot adjudicate matters of faith. Consequently, he asserts that a "Christian commonwealth" is a contradiction in terms, as a commonwealth, by definition, remains nonsectarian and distinct from religious identity. Even if all members share a single religion, the political structure of the commonwealth remains independent of that faith. However, Locke acknowledges limits to toleration, recognizing that certain beliefs and practices may justifiably be restricted.

# Rousseau: Critique of the Enlightenment & Republican Community

### The Enlightenment Philosophes

**The Enlightenment** in the 18th century followed the **Scientific Revolution** that led to an explosion of knowledge. The modern notion that the combination of science, technology, education, and political freedom would bring social progress is distinctively a product of the Enlightenment. You can never find this view articulated in any previous era. Throughout history, human civilizations have not accepted the idea that the future is always going to be better than the present or the past. They would have in fact laughed at that notion; the future is supposed to repeat the present and the past in long term under a framework of a cyclical time and in short term, most human cultures, placed their greatest or advanced or golden age in the past when their heroic ancestors spoke directly to the gods and performed superhuman feats. The current age was merely supposed to imitate in their limited capacity, whatever their glorious ancestors did. The idea of a sort of endless improvement and progress in the conditions of life, that change is good—that was completely lacking until the 18th century. The Enlightenment in the 18th century invented this idea of perpetual progress. Also the idea that freedom, progress, and knowledge go together is an Enlightenment one. There is a saying that one should know his enemy to know oneself. What were the enemies for the Enlightenment? It saw the following as its enemies - ***tradition, superstition, aristocratic and church authority, and cultural constraints and inertia***. The Enlightenment thinkers—in French they were called "**les philosophes**"—thought of themselves as a dawn of a new modern tradition where people dared to think of themselves as releasing humanity from its chains of the past

We now move to 18th century France to meet an extremely influential philosopher **Jean-Jacques Rousseau** (1712–1778) who also proposed a contract theory of politics. But while Hobbes portrays the state of nature as a brutal war of all against all situation and admires the post-contract polity as orderly, Rousseau turns the tables over and does the exact opposite by romanticising the state of nature as a lovely place filled with virtue and well being while portraying political civilisation as one filled with greed, competition, and immorality.

### Romantic view on the state of nature 

In his essay "**Discourse on the Sciences and Arts**", Rousseau argued that progress in the arts and sciences does not lead to progress in morals. This was his first critic of the Enlightenment: Modernity is endowing us with more things to enjoy and more knowledge than ever but doesn’t make us better or happier. He argued that cosmopolitan learning does not improve the soul of a person and does not make one morally better but is merely training them to ape adult behavior without maturing and cultivating their inner dispositions. But this essay was just a pin compared to his next essay "**Discourse on the Origin of Inequality among Men**" which was a bomb that he threw at the very heart of Enlightenment.

He opened the essay with his famous sentence “*Man is born free, but everywhere he is in chains*”. Rousseau argued that the state of nature which was scary and savage for Hobbes, was in fact morally superior to the state of modernity in all ways one can imagine. Rousseau literally supported the superiority of primitive man and the state of nature, where people lived the healthiest, most independent lives, foraging alone, owning nothing but the simplest tools, unable to develop vices. He is thinking about primitive hunter-gatherer societies which engage in minimum subsistence farming without any political organization beyond the local. The first evil or the original sin in the human race historically according to Rousseau (which would be later taken up by **Karl Marx**)—was ownership of private property; which began inequality. Also another evil of civilized society that came about after the ownership of private property was dependence. The economist Adam Smith celebrated division of labor where everyone would do what they did best and then exchange their surpluses with each other as trade so that we all would produce and enjoy more of everything. Rousseau sees this as a terrible weakness wherein we are forced to depend on other people for our survival that goes hand in hand with inequality. Once this dependence is created, it leads to jealousy and status consciousness. For Rousseau, it is the post-contract modernity and civilization that have made us evil and savage, not the state of nature.

### Types of love

People in the modern world, according to Rousseau, have become concerned with status of what he called in French "***amour propre***" (*the love of self as it is seen by others*), instead of manifesting the simple and healthy "***amour de soi***" (*love of oneself*) that existed in primitive societies in the state of nature. All humans rightly possess amour de soi, love of oneself. The primitive man loves his own self, and so feeds and cares for it and wants to go on living. But amour propre, the desire for me to get other people to think highly of me, is different and bad when it becomes more important than anything else. This is what happens in modern, cosmopolitan, educated circles—where people are dependent on each other for their survival—hence great envy and inequality, the artificial desire to have more than others to enhance your amour propre which is the root of all evil in politically organized and economically sophisticated societies.

### Peasants under the oak tree

So what did Rousseau route for? Should we all abandon civilization and go back to the primitive state of nature? **Voltairre** responded to him saying “*One longs, in reading your book, to walk on all fours. But as I have lost that habit for more than sixty years, I feel unhappy about the impossibility of resuming it*.” Rousseau was literally ridiculed by the other great thinkers of the time. But even Rousseau acknowledged that returning to the primitive state of nature was impossible. But we could instead transform our society into something that was the next best thing - a compromise that would not turn the clock backwards but still would make us enjoy as many virtues that we did in the primitive state of nature. In his other book “**On the Social Contract**”, he addressed this problem in detail: *How can humans create society yet remain as free as before*? Rousseau says that this happens when the entire community of equals establishes a “***common moi***” (French: *common me*) and surrenders to the “***volonté générale***” (French: *general will*). According to Rousseau, by default, the will of any person in a community of equals coincides with the “general will” that aims for the best for the entire community as a whole. It is only when he is under the control of his greedy passions that his will is selfish and deviates from the general will and instead aims for his own benefit at the expense of his community. Such a man with his own selfish private will is not in his true self but is under the control of his passions and his will is hence not truly free. When a man is truly free and the society is a communal and equal society, a man’s will is always free and coincides with the general will that wants the best for the entire community. In such a society where everyone or most people are equal and free, the will of all or majority of the community coincides with the general will that benefits the entire community. So, the best we can do to approximate the ancient tradition but also avoid the evils of contemporary society is to adopt a democratic equality in our society. Rousseau romantically described it by the phrase “***the peasants around the oak tree***”——sitting together and passing their laws with simple common sense. This is a direct democracy in which all equal citizens participate in the law making processes. They are not smart enough to be duped, meaning their wants are so simple that you can’t bribe them and trick them into adopting policies that aren’t good for them because they are simple and free. It is crucial to remember that the government for Rousseau is solely executive and judiciary; the legislation or making of the law directly comes from the people. The government just executes the law made directly by the people. Inevitably, when a government degenerates over time to concentrate its power and eventually usurps the power of the people, a revolution is needed which returns executive power to the people until they establish another government.

Rousseau is also concerned with the virtue and culture of society, not just the political and government structures. Virtue and the right culture is needed to encourage citizens to make them free thinkers so that they prioritize the general will over their private wills driven by passions. He proposed additional governmental institutions and a civic religion to reinforce independence and virtue and keep the will of all as close as possible to the general will. But this is the paradox. Since a person who is willing out of selfish interests is not truly free and under the control of his passions according to Rousseau, such an individual who does not obey the general will should be “*forced to become free*”. It is not coercing them but making them free because freedom is obedience to the general will.

Here we find a great paradox: *Rousseau, the most anti-modern writer of the 18th century, the one most averse to progress, ended up inspiring one of the most radical political changes in Western Civilisation at the end of the 18th century more than almost anyone - the French Revolution of 1789*. Intellectual history is made out of such paradoxes. Rousseau is also a major inspiration for anarchist socialism that values equality over individual liberty. The greatest threat to the justice of society for Rousseau is selfishness, *amour propre*, the urge of one citizen to have more and think better of himself or herself than others. Socioeconomic inequality is incompatible with political equality.
