# Thomas Paine & the Left Wing: History Conflicts with Reason

If the conservative reaction to the French Revolution is well represented by Edmund Burke, **Thomas Paine** (1737 - 1809) well represents the progressive reaction to the revolution. Being liberal, egalitarian, and anti-royalist, he avidly supports the French Revolution and criticises Burke in his work “**Rights of Man**” written in 1791. Paine advocated for a republican form of government, as outlined in the French Constitution of 1791, which he viewed as a model of rational governance. He contrasted this with the British monarchy, which he criticized as corrupt and outdated. Paine believed the French Revolution’s aim was to establish a republic aligned with the Enlightenment ideal that sovereignty resides in the people, not in kings or nobles.

Paine argues that history is irrelevant in either understanding nature or society. In fact, he believes that history and reason are in conflict. He considers Burke to be an irrational man arguing based on emotion, sentiment, and a nostalgic attachment to tradition rather than reason. He emphasizes that each generation is free and chooses according to their own contingencies rather than being bound to the past. The past is dead and should not weigh in when we build modern society. He views historical continuity as an antithesis to freedom. No generation should possess the power “*of binding or controlling posterity*”. Only God and nature are relevant in deciding how to build a new society. Hereditary monarchy cannot guarantee to put authority in the hands of the most merited, virtuous, or talented. He holds the view that governments must be judged by their ability to secure the rights and happiness of the people, not by their age or tradition. He accused Burke of prioritizing the interests of the elite over the common good.

Paine was initially optimistic about the French Revolution’s potential to inspire global democratic movements. He believed it could spark a wave of revolutions across Europe, replacing feudal systems with governments grounded in reason and justice. His enthusiasm is evident in his assertion that the revolution marked the dawn of a new era of human rights and universal liberty. Paine’s commitment to the revolution was not merely intellectual. He traveled to France in 1792, was elected to the National Convention despite limited French proficiency, and became associated with the moderate ***Girondin*** faction. He supported the abolition of the monarchy and the establishment of the First French Republic but opposed the execution of King **Louis XVI**, advocating instead for exile, which reflected his belief in humane governance. As the revolution radicalized during the Reign of Terror (1793–1794), Paine’s optimism waned. His opposition to the execution of Louis XVI and his association with the Girondins put him at odds with the more radical **Jacobins**. In December 1793, he was arrested and imprisoned in Paris under suspicion of being a foreign agitator. He narrowly escaped execution and was released in 1794 after American diplomatic intervention. This experience tempered his views, and he later criticized the revolution’s descent into violence and factionalism, though he never abandoned his belief in its core principles. Paine saw the French Revolution as a necessary uprising against monarchical tyranny and aristocratic privilege. He believed it embodied the universal principles of natural rights—liberty, equality, and fraternity. For Paine, the revolution was a step toward dismantling hereditary rule and establishing a government based on reason and the consent of the governed. He argued that the French were justified in overthrowing an oppressive monarchy that denied citizens their inherent rights.

# The Anarchists

While Paine represents a softer non-radical side of the pro-revolutionary left, the far left too had its own voices that called for anarchy. **Pierre-Joseph Proudhon** (1809–1865) was the first to call himself an anarchist. In his work “**What is Property**” he says that the mere notion of property is incompatible with equal rights of man (the motto of the revolution) and it is nothing but theft and homicide. Proudhon distinguishes property from mere *possession*, meaning the act of occupying a piece of land or using tools to produce what satisfies needs of one’s family. He thus recognizes the rights of living persons to live and work and to have possessions. However, the alleged notion of property—understood as a right to do whatever one wishes with a possession—is wrong and unjust and makes for an irrational, unequal system of society. Claiming absolute dominion over anything is wrong, irrational, and unjust. There is no right to property nor can there be. Further, no right can go beyond death, which means that passing on a property via inheritance makes no sense. The entire system of property and the rights that go with it are intrinsically unjust. Anarchists in general tried to work out a vision of society that was as small and decentralized as possible with each such decentralized unit being communal; they particularly criticized things like rent, interest, wages, and profits, all of which they believed created large differences in wealth and made one man dependent on another. While anarchism did not survive for long, another left wing reaction to the French Revolution and Industrialization was destined to eclipse it and have a bright global future which is **socialism**.

# Socialism : From Utopia to Revolution

> All socialists like anarchists are against private ownership of
> property but socialism is about state ownership of property. While
> anarchists would have fantasized about small decentralized communities
> living communally with common ownership of everything without much
> interference from outside, socialists were thinking farther and bigger
> at the level of the state.

In fact, the early socialists dreamt so big that they are called the **Utopian socialists**. At least initially, these socialists did not want to abolish the private ownership of all property. They did not want to snatch the house that you live in or the car that you drive or the ice cream that you just purchased. Since they were thinking big, they just wanted to take away only the big things from private hands - factories, mines, power plants, forests, agricultural land and so on. Utopian Socialists hoped to persuade the upper and middle classes to relinquish ownership of these resources and means of production voluntarily or through government action.

# Christian Origins of Socialism

## Pre-modern Christian precedents

Karl Marx wasn't the first to conceive of socialism. He had precedents from whom he was drawing on, who were all Utopian socialists of some kind. **Count Henri de Saint-Simon** (1760 - 1825) of France is credited with originating Socialism in its mature form. But even before him, there were lots of Christian thinkers who saw socialist ideas as a logical outgrowth of Christianity. For e.g, as early as the year 1380, **Norman Cohn**, argued that people lived in a distant past in a golden age without any distinctions based on status and without possessing any property, and believed that this would be possible to recreate in the immediate future. Much of the inspiration of such Christian socialists came from the belief that a primitive socialist communal life was practised by Christ’s apostles as mentioned in the New Testament’s book of Acts.

*All the believers were one in heart and mind. No one claimed that any of their possessions was their own, but they shared everything they had. With great power the apostles continued to testify to the resurrection of the Lord Jesus. And God’s grace was so powerfully at work in them all that there were no needy persons among them. For from time to time those who owned land or houses sold them, brought the money from the sales and put it at the apostles’ feet, and it was distributed to anyone who had need.*

—Acts 4:32–35

## The failed Taborite Experiment

This was the underpinning of the beliefs of fourteenth-century English churchmen **John Wycliffe** and **John Ball**. Inspired by this was a Czech theologian in early 15th century Bohemia named **Jan Hus**, who was burnt as a heretic for arguing against Church hierarchy. Outrage at Hus’s execution saw the rise of a **Hussite** movement which in one of its more extreme offshoots, known as the **Taborites**, practised a form of communism in expectation of the imminent Second Coming of Christ. Thousands of peasants in this movement gave up their belongings to be held in common, but since they had given no thought to the need to produce as well as to share, their ideals soon collided with economic reality. Such communal Taborite societies failed due to a fundamental inability to produce the necessary goods to survive, combined with internal strife from doctrinal divergences and messianic figures, and external pressures from warfare and persecution by both Catholics and other Hussite factions. Their idealistic vision of an anarcho-communist, classless society clashed with the practical realities of sustaining a community, leading to reliance on plunder and ultimately, their splintering and military defeat.

## More’s Utopia : The birth of a new literary genre

**Sir Thomas More,** another devout Christian, published his novel **Utopia** in 1516 and paved the way for an entirely new genre of literature called the utopian literature for the next five or six centuries. More’s Utopia takes the form of a dialogue in which arguments both for and against common ownership are made. The ‘traveller’ who has been to an island in the New World named Utopia where ‘*recognition of merit is combined with equal prosperity for all*’ argues that ‘*you’ll never get a fair distribution of goods, or a satisfactory organization of human life, until you abolish private property altogether*’. Otherwise, laws will only treat the symptoms of injustice, while ‘*wealth will tend to vary in inverse proportion to merit*’. However, ‘*there is no hope of a cure so long as private property continues. If you try to treat an outbreak in one part of the body politic, you merely exacerbate the symptoms elsewhere*’.

More poses his own objections to this: there would be shortages and nobody would work hard enough, for people would rely on everyone else to do the work for them. He clearly had his doubts about the viability of the society whose virtues he was expounding, yet he tilted the balance of the argument in favour of the society of his imagination and against that in which he lived. More concludes his reflections on what the ‘traveller’ has told him by remarking that ‘there are many features of the Utopian Republic which I should like—though I hardly expect—to see adopted in Europe’.

Another notable utopia was that elaborated by the Italian Dominican Christian monk, **Tomasso Campanella**, at the beginning of the seventeenth century. His work “***La Città del sole***” \[Italian : ***The City of the Sun***\] saw the family as the main obstacle to the creation of a state based on communist principles. Therefore, he argued, the state must assume responsibility for children’s education. Working hours, however, would be reduced to four a day, and much of the rest of the time would be devoted to joyful learning.

## Cabet and Travels in Icaria

Another, more utopian, French Christian socialist, **Étienne Cabet**, is credited by the Oxford English Dictionary as being the first person to use the term ‘*communism*’ (French : *communisme*) in 1840 and founded the **Icarian movement**. Cabet published the work “*Voyage en Icarie”* in French in 1839 (and in English in 1840 as “*Travels in Icaria”*), in which he proposed replacing capitalist production with workers' cooperatives. In this romantic novel, he describes a communistic utopia, whose terms he had dreamed out; and he began at once to try to realize his dream. He framed a constitution for an actual Icaria. Étienne Cabet, then led his Icarian followers to America where they established a group of egalitarian communes during the period from 1848 through 1898. Karl Marx mentions his work in an 1843 letter to Arnold Ruge, contrasting the "communist utopia" of the book with the real and pragmatic conditions necessary for building socialism in the Germany of his time. But, ultimately, Cabet rejected violent revolution which was one of the major areas in which Marx would later disagree with him.

## Saint-Simon and the New Christianity

The Christian thinker who systematically laid out the framework and arguments for socialist thought was **Count Henri de Saint-Simon** from whom Marx himself directly drew upon a lot. He is credited to be the father of socialism. His book advocating socialism was titled "**The New Christianity** \[French : ***Nouveau Christianisme***\]" (1825), where Saint-Simon urged the state to organize common exploitation of resources as directed by scientists and intellectuals. In fact, he is the one who coined the now famous Marxist phrase:

"*From each according to his ability; to each according to his needs*".

Let us then now explore the thought of Saint-Simon in more detail.

### Return to Christ’s Core Teachings

Saint-Simon begins "*The New Christianity*" by asserting that traditional Christianity has strayed from its original mission like any Protestant Reformer. He argues that the essence of Christ’s teachings—love, brotherhood, and care for the poor—has been obscured by centuries of dogma, ritual, and institutional corruption. Saint-Simon proposes a radical reimagining of Christianity, stripping it down to its core moral precepts and aligning it with the needs of an industrializing society. He envisions a "new" Christianity that serves as a moral and spiritual guide for a socialist reorganization of society, one that prioritizes the welfare of the poorest classes and harnesses science and industry for the collective good.

Saint-Simon emphasizes that the fundamental message of Jesus is the principle of universal love: “*Men should treat each other as brothers.*” He argues that this sublime principle is inherently socialist because it demands the elimination of social hierarchies that perpetuate inequality. For Saint-Simon, true Christianity is not about theological disputes or rituals but about creating a society where the material and spiritual needs of all people, especially the poorest, are met.

### Religion’s Role in Socialism

Saint-Simon declares that the primary goal of religion should be “improving as quickly as possible the conditions of the poorest class.” He sees this as a direct application of Christ’s teachings, particularly the Sermon on the Mount, which prioritizes the meek, the poor, and the marginalized. In his view, traditional Christianity has failed to fulfill this mission, becoming complicit in maintaining feudal and capitalist systems that exploit the working class. By contrast, his New Christianity would serve as a moral framework for a socialist society, where the state and industrial leaders work together to ensure equitable distribution of resources and opportunities. He calls for a return to a simplified, practical Christianity that aligns with the needs of an industrial society, where science and industry can be harnessed to fulfill Christ’s mandate of brotherly love.

### Socialism as a Christian Imperative

Saint-Simon’s socialism is grounded in the idea that the state should own and administer the means of production for the benefit of all, a concept he sees as a natural extension of Christian ethics. He argues that the greed and competition inherent in capitalism are antithetical to Christ’s teachings, which he interprets as a call for cooperation and communal ownership. In The New Christianity, he envisions a society where industrial leaders—scientists, engineers, and managers—replace the old aristocracy and clergy, guiding society toward the elimination of poverty through planned production and distribution.

### Universal Religion and Social Unity

Saint-Simon predicts that the world has reached a crisis—foretold, he claims, by the Old Testament—that will culminate in the establishment of a “truly universal religion.” This new religion, his New Christianity, would unite all nations under a “pacific social organization” focused on the “speedy betterment of the condition of the poor.” He sees this as a fulfillment of biblical prophecy, where the messianic promise of a just society is realized not through divine intervention but through human effort guided by Christian principles. This universal religion would replace the divisive doctrines of existing churches, fostering a global brotherhood that transcends national and class boundaries.

### Spiritual and Material Synthesis

Saint-Simon sees Christianity as an essential component of his socialist vision. He argues that a reformed Christianity can provide the spiritual “glue” needed to bind society together, ensuring that material progress (through science and industry) is accompanied by moral progress. In this sense, his socialism is not atheist but deeply Christian, aiming to reconcile the material needs of the industrial age with the ethical demands of Christ’s teachings. He justifies socialism by the New Testament, where the apostles share all they had and lived a communal life after Christ's death, as we saw in the beginning of this section.

## Owenite Communities

**Robert Owen** (1771 - 1858) was another early utopian socialist who wanted to reorganize society, but he wanted factory owners themselves to do this arguing that it was in their own interest. As a self-made man, Owen purchased the cotton mills at New Lanark, Scotland, in 1799. Appalled at the conditions of the workers, he himself provided them decent working hours andconditions, housing, schools, hospitals, and churches. In 1813, Owen published “***A New View of Society***”, urging the establishment of similar self-sufficient factory communities. Like Saint-Simon, Owen believed in class co-operation and in the development of a growing rationality. In his “***Address to the Working Classes***” of 1819, Owen concluded that

‘the past ages of the world present the history of human irrationality only’ but now the world was ‘advancing towards the dawn of reason, and to the period when the mind of man shall be born again’

Owen was one of those rare socialists who actually did something to put his ideas into action and attempt to alleviate the conditions of the poor. The older he became, the more utopian were Owen’s enterprises. He made more than one attempt to set up a co-operative commune. The best-known was “**New Harmony**” in the American state of Indiana. It aspired to complete equality of income, with all the residents - right from the owner to the worker - having also similar food and clothing as well as education. Several other Owenite communities were established after the establishment of the New Harmony. But soon, most such communities failed as a result of internal mismanagement, strife, fall of production, corruption, and the hostility of the outside world.

Thus, eventually, it became clear to the socialiasts that the Utopian dream that factory owners could be persuaded to share their wealth with their workers was misguided. They had to be coerced by the workers themselves or by the state. There comes the next brand of socialists who are the **Revolutionary Socialists**.

# Karl Marx and Historical Materialism

The **Revolutionary socialists** thought that factory owners cannot be persuaded to relinquish their wealth themselves and hence the workers had to brand together and revolt to seize the means of production themselves. There were many brands of revolutionary socialists, but very soon they were overshadowed by **Karl Marx** (1818–1883) and **Friedrich** (Frederick) **Engels** (1820–1895) who systematized this call for revolutionary action, rooting it in a theory of history. The factory owners have many advantages in fighting it (wealth, political power, and so on), and because the factory owners will never voluntarily relinquish the resources and means of production, the only hope for workers is found in revolution. Marx propounded a new theory of history called **historical materialism**, in which he claimed that all of history could be explained through a single parameter - economics. Marx gives some arguments to justify this but to give a simple example, he would ask you questions like “When have you ever voted for a political party that acts contrary to your economic interests” or “When have you ever done something that will make you economically less well off?”. If the answer is a no for most people, Marx says that it is a vindication of this statement.

### Proletariat vs Bourgeoisie: History as a Series of Class Struggles

The first step in historical materialism is that Marx divides all of humanity across all times and nations into two—and only two—classes: the **working class (*****proletariat*****)**, who work, and the **wealthy class (*****bourgeoisie*****)**, who own the means of production and for whom the working class work in exchange for money. A Marxist has to define these two classes no matter which society he studies, at whatever point in time. All of history is simply a series of class struggles for Marx. To put in the words of Engels:

‘**the whole history of mankind** *(since the dissolution of primitive tribal society, holding land in common ownership)* **has been a history of class struggles, contests between exploiting and exploited, ruling and oppressed classes**’

Let me explain this with an example. In each period in every society, one class is victorious and owns the most important means of production; another labors for them, with other classes playing marginal roles. The victorious owning class sets up government, laws, cultures, and religions that serve and promote its interests. Then, when a new technology comes into the picture that serves as a superior means of production compared to the current one, this new technology-bearing class cannot advance because of the political-legal-cultural structures that favor the old owning class and hence they revolt against the existing system and win. After winning, this new technology-owning class begins a new historical period, now dominated by this new means of production with them the people who own this technology as the victorious class. This continues until another technology comes about. For example, in medieval Europe, the dominant production was from agriculture and hence agricultural land would be the dominant means of production; the ruling class in the Marxist parlance would be the aristocratic owners of that land; and the working class would be comprised of the peasants who work that land for their owners. Over time, as the Middle Ages declined, the commercial merchant class of the towns improved its means of production, gaining wealth and power through trade and investment which was emerging as the dominant means of production. But their ascent was blocked by the monarchical, traditional, and legal structures the land-owning aristocracy had set up to serve its own ends. Eventually, the merchant class gained enough power to stage revolutions against the landed aristocracy to set up a capitalist driven economy. Marx sees his communist revolution as the final revolution that will usher in an utopian paradise of equality. Marxism is explicitly an internationalist theory: Nations do not matter; only classes do. Capitalism will destroy itself for purely economic reasons, leading to a bloody revolution of the proletariat, which as the largest class in history will end all class differences in a communist utopia.

### Alienation

Another central notion in Marx’s historical materialism is that of **alienation** (German: ***entfremdung***). This may remind you in Christianity of the alienation or separation between believers and disbelievers, and the messianic oppression that the disbeliever inflicts upon the believer. Let us now see how this is secularised in historical materialism. While a standard capitalist sees a worker earning money as a good thing that benefits both parties (the worker needs money and the wealthy man needs labour, so they exchange what they have for what they need), Marx sees this as a form of alienation or oppression by the wealthy. Marx views productive work as the creative essence of what makes someone human. In selling his labor power to the bourgeoisie for money, the worker is selling the essence of his self. Then, at the end of the production process, the bourgeoisie owns the product of the worker’s labor, sells it at a profit, thus owning the worker’s own human essence for a wage. Consider a worker employed in a bottle manufacturing plant owned by a wealthy person. The worker helps produce bottles and receives wages in return. Marx views this transaction as oppressive. He argues that the bottles the worker helps to produce are, in a sense, his—created by his effort—but the factory owner, the oppressor, snatches them away and merely offers money as a consolation. Since the output of the worker does not belong to him, his work life remains in a state of dissatisfaction: what he produces is not his, but has been taken away. So, the worker is working solely for wages and gains no satisfaction, since he is not working for something he has a personal attachment to (in this case, the bottle).

### Use value & Exchange value

Marx further dissects the distinction between **use value** and **exchange value** of an item or labor, which were distinguished by Adam Smith himself. Take the water bottle example again. The use value of a water bottle for someone is determined just by how useful it is to a person and how badly he needs it. The use value of a water bottle to a person is a measure of how badly the person needs it. But money (price worth) in capitalism captures not the use value of the water bottle but what is called the exchange value that depends on the supply and demand of the water bottle. The price of the water bottle in capitalism is not determined just by how useful it is to a person but also on how many water bottles are in supply and how many other people want the water bottle. When the supply is high and the demand is low, the price of the same water bottle is low compared to the high demand and low supply scenario where the price is high. So capitalism substitutes the exchange value of a commodity for its use value. Similarly, Marx argues that what a worker is paid as wages captures the exchange value of his labor and not the use value. The wage of a laborer in a capitalist economy is not decided based on how useful the labor is to the owner but based on the supply and demand of that labor. If there are lots of people willing to work and only few employers who want that work, the wages of the labor is low. Similarly, when a given labor is in high demand but there are only a few people who can perform that labor (because it requires special skills), the wage for that labor is high. If a factory worker really paid the laborer for the use value of its labor, then he would not be paying him very low wages and make him work under horrible working conditions. He does that simply because he knows that he has lots of other workers who will come even if this worker quits. Thus, capitalism is irrational because it ends up allocating resources to people based on the exchange value of labor and commodities and not based on the use value of labor and commodities.

### Economic dependence and loss of freedom

In a capitalist society, everyone is dependent on many others for their livelihood. Money and private property connect us to countless others through trade (imagine for a moment where the food you eat, the chair you sit on, the water you drink, and the shoes you wear come from). But Marx sees this dependence as a loss of freedom like Rousseau—since it creates interdependence between the working and owning classes—and believes capitalism leads to a superficial, alienated society full of meaningless relationships dictated solely by money. What capitalists like Adam Smith view as a self-regulating free market governed by self-interest, Marx sees it differently as a loss of liberty and a key factor that prevents worker revolution. Quoting his own words:

*The bourgeoisie (working class), wherever it has got the upper hand, has put an end to all feudal, patriarchal, idyllic relations. It has pitilessly torn asunder the motley feudal ties that bound man to his ‘natural superiors’, and has left remaining no other nexus between man and man than naked self-interest, than callous ‘cash payment’. It has drowned the most heavenly ecstasies of religious fervour, of chivalrous enthusiasm, of philistine sentimentalism, in the icy water of egotistical calculation. It has resolved personal worth into exchange value, and in place of the numberless indefeasible chartered freedoms, has set up that single, unconscionable freedom—Free Trade. In one word, for exploitation, veiled by religious and political illusions, it has substituted naked, shameless, direct, brutal exploitation. The bourgeoisie has stripped of its halo every occupation hitherto honoured and looked up to with reverent awe. It has converted the physician, the lawyer, the priest, the poet, the man of science, into its paid wage labourers. The bourgeoisie has torn away from the family its sentimental veil, and has reduced the family relation to a mere money relation…All fixed, fast-frozen relations, with their train of ancient and venerable prejudices and opinions, are swept away, all new-formed ones become antiquated before they can ossify. All that is solid melts into air, all that is holy is profaned, and man is at last compelled to face with sober senses, his real conditions of life, and his relations with his kind.*

From: ‘The Communist Manifesto’ (Chapter 1)

He argues that, in the past, the wealthy bourgeoisie used other pretences to exploit the working class. For example, the mediaeval aristocratic lord said to the peasant: “*I am well-born; I am a noble. You are born a peasant. You will remain uneducated. Therefore, you need me. You will work for me or starve. I am superior in every way*.” But the modern capitalist doesn’t say this. Instead, he says: “*You, the worker, are going to do what I tell you. Not because I’m morally good, better educated, or well-born—but simply because I have the money, and you need it*. That’s why”. In a famous passage from “**The Communist Manifesto**”, Marx says that capitalism coarsens life, showing the power relations among men to be what they really are: naked exploitation which Marx views as honest, direct, brutal exploitation—without the pretext of moral, religious, or caste superiority. Marx predicts that, as capitalism progresses, the working class will be increasingly exploited by the wealthy class (whose only interest is profit), and inequality between the classes will widen. Eventually, Marx foresees a revolution: the working class will rise against the owning class and reverse the existing power structures. After this revolution, the wealthy will be forced to relinquish their property, and the working class will establish a utopian communist society where everyone lives as equals, without private property or money, and all means of production are owned collectively by the state.

### Marxism as secularized messianism

If you have understood messianism even slightly, this must remind you of the Day of Judgement—when the messiah arrives (Marx sees himself as a kind of messiah who proclaims the coming revolution), and God descends to overthrow the forces of evil, reward the righteous oppressed, and establish a kingdom of God where everyone lives happily ever after. This is the Marxist version of the same idea. A deeper elaboration of this parallel is given in the work of W. S. Goldstein.

*Messianism and Marxism share in common a parallel structure in their philosophy of history. In the Bible, history begins with paradise from which there is a fall. According to Marx and Engels, the earliest form of society is primitive communal society in which there is no private property, no classes and no state. The beginning of class society can be seen as a fall from paradise. In Judeo-Christian Messianism, the Messiah only enters during periods of disaster (Unheil) and brings about salvation (Heil). In Marxism, revolutions occur during crises and bring about social transformation. Messianism hopes in the coming of the Messiah while Marxism hopes in the coming of the revolution. In both Messianism and Marxism, at the end of history there is a return to the beginning of history. In Messianism, there is a return to paradise. In Marxism, there is the reestablishment of communism (however, this time it is industrialized).*

### Religion as an opium of the masses

Marx sees abstractions like nationalism and religion as illusions cast upon the working class by the owning class so that the workers do not unite and rise against them. According to Marx, a rich factory owner in Germany has more in common with the rich factory owner in France than with his own workers in Germany. German or French nationalism hence is an illusion cast upon the working class masses to make the German working class somehow feel a patriotic connection to their German owning class than with their fellow working class compatriots in France. So, this creates an obstacle for the working class of various nations to unite against the owning class. So while the rich wealthy elite owning class travel around a lot between nations and enjoy together and share similar tastes, the working class is kept divided on the basis of nationalism. Think of a war between nations; only the poor soldiers fight and the rich stay away from it and are least affected. Religion too is viewed in the same manner by Marx as a set of beliefs and institutions to coerce the working class and disable them from rising against their owning class in the name of morality and assurance of a good afterlife. So, both religion and nationalism are obstacles for creating a class consciousness among the proletariat.

### After the Revolution

After the revolution, according to Marx, the utopian socialist dream of a classless society would ensue. All would work according to their abilities and be provided according to their needs. Because all would have the necessary material conditions of life, all would be content and class struggle would cease. Because everyone would now be on their best behavior, after a brief interval to set things up, the state would wither away. The result would be a workers’ paradise—indeed, a paradise for all. Marx says that a communist utopia will have the following features:

- It is classless and also without any other distinction like ethnicity
  or race or religion or caste.

- The engine of history is class struggle. With no classes, the engine
  stops. Thus, communism will be the final period of human history.

- All humans will engage equally in practical and creative activities.
  The division of labor will cease.

- The distributive principle will be: “From each according to his
  abilities, to each according to his needs.”

- There can be no government because government exists only as a tool of
  the ruling class. In communism, the government is replaced by
  “administration.” The implication is that politics must also disappear
  because humans will no longer be divided into groups with distinct
  political needs. Man himself will be human for the first time.

# Western Europe rejects the Marxist Revolution

Yet Marx and Engels mostly failed to achieve their worker’s revolution. The revolutions of 1848—in Germany, the Austro-Hungarian Empire, and Italy—were to the contrary, about liberalism and nationalism, which Marx loathed!. There were no worker revolutions in England or Belgium either, which were the cradles of industrialization. Only in France did the economic slump of 1846 to 1848 produce a revolution against Louis-Philippe—uniting workers, middle-class intellectuals, and other individuals. During the June Days in Paris, workers formed communes and fought street battles, demanding universal suffrage. In July 1848, a national assembly proclaimed France a republic, but the new voters elected Louis Napoleon, the nephew of the late emperor, as president. The Chamber of Representatives was to be elected by universal male suffrage—far more liberal than any other major country in Europe. But, there was no seizing of factories and no communal ownership of natural resources or of the means of production. In 1852, Louis Napoleon Bonaparte (Napoleon III) declared himself emperor (1852–1870). The 1870 revolt brought the abdication of Louis-Napoleon and the Paris workers’ Commune of 1871, where a red socialist flag briefly flew only for two months. After extensive fighting in the working-class areas of Paris, the Commune was brutally put down. Eventually, the Third French Republic was installed, which lasted until the Nazi occupation of 1940.

Why did revolutionary socialism not succeed in Western Europe? This was because quite a bit of aspects of socialism for which the revolutionary socialists were fighting for, were eventually brought within the framework of democracy and also its aspects were gradually incorporated within the other systems of political thought in Western Europe (especially liberalism) and the evolution of such socialist and liberal political parties that represented workers, enabled them to efficiently and satisfactorily address their concerns within the political system through socialist political parties that represented them rather than attempt a revolution to destroy the entire system itself. We will see in detail about this in future articles. By the end of the 19th century, urged on by the German Socialist **Eduard Bernstein** (1850–1932), workers began to support their own Socialist political parties, dedicated to their own interests: building on predecessor organizations, the **Social Democratic Party of Germany** was legalized in 1890, the **British Labour Party** was founded in 1892, and the **United Socialist Party (Parti Socialiste Unifié)** came together in France during 1905–1906.

It is only in 20th century Russia that Marx would have his dream put to practice followed by far off non European lands like China and Korea, about which too, we will see in later articles in the series.
