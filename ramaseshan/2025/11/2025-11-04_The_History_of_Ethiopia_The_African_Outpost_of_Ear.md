Much of Ethiopia is a high mountainous country, associated with the Great Rift. The fertile soils of the country has enabled it to sustain quite high population density and maybe one of the highest in sub-Saharan Africa. Ethiopia also has one of the longest histories of agriculture in the world.

Around approximately 1000 BCE, the people of modern northern **Ethiopia** and **Eritrea** began establishing commercial and political contacts with the peoples of Egypt. By about 600 BCE, the **Sabaean** speaking peoples from the southern part of Arabia crossed the Red Sea into Ethiopia in search of ivory for trade and intermingled with the local population. The Sabaean language that they spoke eventually spread to Ethiopia and evolved into **Ge’ez**, that belongs to the Semitic family of languages that include other famous Middle Eastern languages like Arabic, Hebrew, Aramaic, etc. Its modern descendant is **Amharic**, a language that is spoken in modern day Eritrea and Ethiopia while the ancient Ge’ez language remains the liturgical language of the Ethiopian Orthodox Church and the Eritrean Orthodox Church. The oral poetic tradition of **Qene** employs the classical Ge’ez language wherein theological poems are composed and used in liturgy and remains the means of initiation into learning the classical language. But apart from Amharic, there are also 80+ other indigenous languages spoken in Ethiopia.

Before Christianity, Ethiopia practiced a mix of religions, including traditional Semitic polytheism, forms of Judaism, and other indigenous beliefs like **Waaqeffanna** among the **Oromo** pastoral people. Paganism, with the worship of gods like **Mahram** and **Astar**, was prevalent, particularly and it existed alongside Jewish and other traditional faiths.

# The Ancient Kingdom of Aksum

*Figure* : The extent of the kingdom of Aksum

Around the first century CE, a major kingdom developed centred around the northern Ethiopian region called the Kingdom of **Aksum** (named after its capital city), which was very wealthy not just due to agriculture but also trade through the Red Sea port of **Adulis**, largely ivory. The mid first century CE Greek shipping manual “***The Periplus of Erythraean Sea***” reported the Aksum’s Red Sea port of **Adulis** as the most important ivory market in the whole of Northeastern Africa. The Aksums enabled import of silver, gold, olive-oil and wine in exchange for their export products.

They built tall stone monoliths known as **stellae** to mark the tombs of rulers, some of which still stand today.

*Figure :* A stella in Ethiopia

The people’s language was Ge’ez, the basis of modern Amharic, and they had already developed their own, quite unique literary script. Christian missionaries (chiefly from Alexandria in Egypt) entered the kingdom of Axum and translated the Bible into Ge’ez for the native people of Ethiopia and were successful in converting King **Ezana** (r. 320 CE - 350 CE). Two centuries later, at about the time Nubia in the Arabian peninsula was undergoing conversion to Christianity, Monophysite monks from Syria also reached Aksum and contributed to the development of a monastic tradition. At present, the oldest surviving Ge’ez manuscript of the Bible is the richly illustrated set of **Garima Gospels** (now housed in the **Abba Garima** monastery) that date from the fifth or sixth centuries CE and constitutes the oldest surviving Christian manuscripts in the world. Monastic tradition ascribes the gospel books to **Saint Abba Garima,** said to have arrived in Ethiopia in 494 CE.

Tradition holds that this translation was undertaken by Syrian monks “***The Nine Saints***” and contains 81 books. This canon has several extra books in both the Old Testament and the New Testament and some of these extra deutero-canonical books of the Bible such as **1 Enoch** survive in full now only in their Ge’ez translation!

For a while, in the 6th century CE, Aksum was powerful enough to expand across the Red Sea to annex the region of Saba (in modern day Yemen) from which their ancestors had come from, but soon they were expelled from the Arabian peninsula by their Persian rivals. By the 8th century CE, the rulers of Aksum were buffeted by Arab Muslim and Persian Muslim pressure and hence the dynasty declined as the centre of trade shifted away from the Red Sea to the Persian Gulf. Hence, the dynasty retreated south into the central highlands of modern Ethiopia and subsequently developed in isolation, thus leading to a unique Christian monastic tradition. Thus, Ethiopia transformed itself into an isolated outpost of Christianity with a large agricultural economy managed by a powerful landed aristocracy.

# Zagwe Dynasty & Mountain Carved Churches

From about 1000 CE onwards, Ethiopia began to recover as the Red Sea trade gained ascent again. In 1150, the founders of what would later become known as **Zagwe** dynasty seized power from the Aksum dynasty and led an aggressive Christian expansion in the southern part of Ethiopia. By the early 13th century, especially during the reign of **Lalibela** (r. 1185 - 1225 CE), Christian monks began building churches carved out of single solid mountain rock and these monuments remain among the most astonishing architectural wonders of sub-Saharan Africa that stand as a testament to the strength of faith of Ethiopian Christians. Many of these churches are named after places in Jerusalem as Ethiopian Christians took regular pilgrimages to that city and hence this was their attempt at creating a Jerusalem in the mountains of Ethiopia.

# The Solomonid Dynasty

In 1270, the **Solomonid** dynasty (named after the Old Testament character of King Solomon) seized the Ethiopian throne after overthrowing the Zagwe dynasty. At this time, the myth began to develop that this dynasty descended from King Solomon of the ancient kingdom of Israel, for the kings to bolster their legitimacy in the eyes of the Church. Their tradition has it that the Queen Sheba who visits Solomon in the Bible (because he was reputed for his wisdom) had a child with Solomon named **Menelik**. It came to be believed that Menelik came to Ethiopia and brought with him, the famous ***Ark of the Covenant*** (the tablets containing commandments that God gave to Moses in Exodus of the Bible) around 1250 BCE and that it is housed in one of the Ethiopian Churches. No one is allowed to see this ark though and replicas of it are there in all of the 20,000+ churches in Ethiopia. This story is extensively chronicled in the epic composed in the 14th century called the ***Kebre Negast*** that serves as the national epic of Ethiopia.

The Solomonid rulers came from the central highland region of **Amhara** where Ge’ez speakers had mingled with the local Cushitic speaking peoples to produce the **Amharic** language that ever since began to dominate Ethiopia after displacing Ge’ez and is still the official language of the modern nation of Ethiopia. This dynasty also ceased to build rectangular churches in stone as before and instead moved to a plainer circular style of architecture without stone.

By the 13th century, the dynasty stopped building in stone and shifted the capital frequently due to shortage of resources that resulted from years of environmental exploitation. Following a period of civil war in the 1290s, future problems of dynastic rivalry were resolved by an unusual practice. As soon as a new king had succeeded to the throne, all possible royal rivals were imprisoned in the mountain fortress of **Gishen**. This novel device was so successful that Gishen remained a royal prison for the best part of two-and-a-half centuries, until the Muslim conquests of the sixteenth century.

The Solomonid dynasty expanded their empire further south and enabled Christianisation of their conquered populations. This eventually brought them into conflict with the Muslim sultanates of the Awash valley. Over the next several centuries, Ethiopia came under pressure from Muslim merchant/military penetration from the east and then from the **Oromo** pastoralists in the south. In fact, substantial sections of the area comprising the nation of modern Ethiopia have ever since been occupied by these parties forming various sultanates, and the Ethiopian nation was almost destroyed in the 1530s.

*Figure* : The Solomonid empire in the 15th century CE

Many regions fell under the sway of local nobility and the Church, both great landholders, with the central monarchy becoming more of a figurehead. It is for good reason that Ethiopia has often been described as feudal, resembling the situation in medieval Europe.

# Expansion of Islam

During the 10th and 11th centuries, Arab Muslim merchants began penetrating the **Awash Valley** and established trading settlements and began to exercise control of trade through the coast. In the early 12th century, some Arabs formed the **Sultanate of Shoa** and soon similar other Muslim states followed suit and by the 13th century, another one of these named **Sultanate of** **Ifat** began to dominate. As the Arabs raided the interior of Ethiopia for goods and slaves, this brought them into conflict with the Zagwe dynasty in Ethiopia.

Ethiopia was at a distinct advantage when it came to military conflict. The Zagwe kings ruled over a relatively united extensive highland kingdom and hence could command a large and effective standing army. The small Muslim sultans, on the other hand, were generally disunited and could not muster an efficient fighting force. Their armies, such as they were, were more accustomed to raiding for captives than fighting in defence of territory. Thus, between 1320 and 1340, Ifat and the other Muslim states of the highland plateau were defeated and brought within the expanding empire of Christian Ethiopia.

These defeated sultans of Ifat withdrew east to the plateau of **Harar** and founded the **Sultanate of Adal** in 1415. The Walaswa dynasty there emerged which set about rebuilding Muslim power and uniting the Muslims of the interior to form a united standing army. As they also converted the pastoral nomads of the neighbouring **Somalia**, they proved to be a useful source of manpower too. In the early decades of the 15th century, for a brief period, the Christian Ethiopians were successful in occupying the Harar Plateau but soon by 1450, Adal recovered. A near perpetual state of war ensued throughout the fifteenth century between the Muslims in the east and Christians in the west that disrupted trade and development in the entire region.

In 1526, a Muslim general Ahmad ibn Ibrahim declared jihad on the Christian Ethiopic kingdom after invoking a fervour of Muslim unity. By this time, the Muslims were more united than the Ethiopian Christians (also Christianity had still not taken a hold in the southern regions) and also were better armed with the supply of up-to-date firearms from Ottoman Turks who had recently expanded into Egypt. This led to the defeat of the Christian army at the Battle of Shimbra Kure in 1529 and soon the Muslim army overran southern Ethiopia, burning or looting churches, replacing Christian governors by Muslims. In desperation, the Solomonid king Lebna Dengel appealed to Christian Europe (especially the Portuguese who had been in regular contact with Ethiopia) whose help enabled to give a sharp defeat to the Muslim army in 1543. Following the death of Ahmad, the Ethiopians under **Galadewos** recovered much of the Amharic and the Shoan plateau over the next ten years. But the weakening of the kingdom due to war enabled the invasion from the **Oromo** pastoralists (who lived in the northeast of Lake **Turkana**) toward the upper Awash valley.

Although the Muslims were defeated, the sultanate of Adal did regroup again as a smaller kingdom and the effect of Muslim rule did leave a permanent impact on the religious demographics of Ethiopia which still continues to be predominantly Muslim in the east to this day.

# The migration of the Oromo Pastoralists

The Oromo were Cushitic speaking pastoralists who were originally from modern day northern Kenya and southwestern Ethiopia. From 1530 - 1565, they occupied the southern third of Ethiopia and were pushing up further north in the valley of Awash and the plateau of Harar. But the Oromo were a bunch of tribes and hence the pressure due to their expansion was not centralised or coordinated and was simply in search of newer pastures. Due to the fall in population density in southern Ethiopia from war with the Muslims, the Oromo were able to move in very easily. Also, due to their lack of unity, they could not be brought together in battle and defeated.

The Oromo who came into contact with Islamic Adal converted to Islam while others converted to Christianity and gave up pastoralism for settled agriculture. By the early 17th century, the Oromo had become the dominant population of the southern half of Ethiopia and dominated the Harar plateau demographically. The Christian kings of Ethiopia did not give much attention to this Oromo incursions (1526 - 1597) and instead concentrated their hold on the northern half of Ethiopia and opened trade connections with the Ottoman Empire. Later int the 17th century, King **Susenyos** (r. 1607 - 1632) gave official recognition to the Oromo in the central and southern regions and hired them as mercenaries for the Ethiopian army and this only merely legitimised what the Oromo were already doing. During the 18th century however, the Solomonid kings of Ethiopia lost all effective power (except around their capital at **Gondar**) and the state become more and more feudal.

# European Attempts at Colonisation

In 1855, a provincial governor, after modernising his local army grabbed the monarchy and took the title **Tewodros** (from the Greek name Theodore) **II**, and wanted to bridge the monarchy with the aristocracy. Tewodros succeeded to a considerable degree in establishing a more effective central government and took control of the throne. His efforts were crucial in unifying Ethiopia and reintroducing central government there. But, this also earned him the enmity of some people in the Church and the landed aristocracy. In 1868, the arrest of a British consul led to a brief but forceful British invasion to free him. With the encouragement of the Church, and the growing unpopularity of Tewodros, many of his own soldiers deserted him in the war, which led to his suicide. **Dej Kassa of Tigray,** who had assisted the British declared himself the successor of Tewodros and took the title **Johannes IV** in Axum’s Church of Mary. He returned regional power back in the hands of the Church and the landed nobility, thereby regaining the trust that Tewodros lost.

The British later withdrew, but this is an era where the emerging European powers were colonising and dividing various parts of Africa, with this period popularly known as the “**scramble**”. Another general **Menelik II**, a rival prince from Shoa, succeeded Johannes to the throne in 1889 who expanded the empire south and defeated the Muslim state entered at Harar once and for all. Non-Christian areas in Oromo, Sidama, and Somali too were brought under the domination of the Ethiopian empire. In 1880s and 1890s, the newly formed nation of Italy which entered this game of colonisation too late tried his hand at Ethiopia. The Italians did indeed succeed in colonising Italy by eventually defeating the newly emerged emperor **Menelik** **II** and signing the **Treaty of Wuchale** during which Italy imposed a protectorate over Ethiopia. But this phase this did not last long and in 1896, the skilled ruler **Menelik** came back to inflict a resounding defeat to the Italians at **Adwa**, finally leading to the **Treaty of Addis Ababa** that led to the recognition of an Italian colony in that part of Ethiopia now known as **Eritrea** while the rest of Ethiopia (the modern day nation of Ethiopia) remaining independent under his rule. It is to be noted that Eritrea had already been under **Ottoman** rule (since the 16th century) and later its tributary **Khedivate** **of Egypt** until it came under the control of Italy.

The significance of Menelik’s repulsion of the Italians cannot be overstated. Aside from Liberia (founded by freed African-American slaves), **Ethiopia was the only place in Africa not colonised by Europeans in this period**.

Independent Ethiopia was an inspiration to Africans in Africa and in the diaspora. Indeed, the Salomonid emperor through much of the 20th century, it was **Ras Tafari** or then later known popularly as **Haile Selassie**, who was seen by many as a symbol of African pride.

But the Italians then came back with a vengeance under **Benito** **Mussolini** (founder of fascism) who invaded Ethiopia in 1935 and captured most of the country. The emperor Haile Selassie went into exile further south and issued a dramatic plea to the League of Nations against Italian aggression. Their failure to do so was viewed by many as a seminal moment, leading to World War II and demonstrating contempt for Africa.

But soon, World War II began and the Allied forces succeeded in expelling the Italians in 1941 and reluctantly recognised Ethiopia’s independence, merged the former Italian colony of Eritrea to Ethiopia, and also restored Haile Selassie on the throne. Though both Ethiopia and Haile Selassie symbolised African pride to many Africans throughout the globe, the reality in Ethiopia was not quite so happy.

# The Communist Revolution, Civil Wars & Secession

Haile Selassie met his own reckoning in 1974, when Marxist military officers overthrew his government and ended the Salomonid dynasty, after centuries. On 12 September 1974, Haile Selassie and his government were overthrown by the **Derg**, a committee of low-ranking officers and enlisted men in the Ethiopian Army who became the ruling military. On 21 March 1975, the Derg abolished the monarchy and adopted Marxism–Leninism as their official ideology, establishing themselves as a provisional government for the process of building a socialist state in Ethiopia. The Crown Prince went into exile in London, where several other members of the House of Solomon lived, while other members who were in Ethiopia at the time of the revolution were imprisoned. Haile Selassie, his daughter by his first marriage Princess Ijigayehu, his sister Princess Tenagnework, and many of his nephews, nieces, close relatives, and in-laws were among those detained. On 27 August 1975, Haile Selassie died under mysterious circumstances in detention at the National Palace in Addis Ababa. Industries and private urban real estate holdings were nationalised by the Derg regime. The assets of the former royal family were all seized and were nationalized in a program designed to implement the state ideology of socialism.

But as is usual in revolutions like the French Revolution or the Russian Revolution, the brutality of the new communist regime led to a reign of terror (called the **Red Terror**) and the cruelties that were committed in the name of revolution, spawned its own seeds of destruction and was itself overthrown in the late 1980s and led to a bitter and bloody civil war that lasted from 1974 to 1991. After that, the new rulers’ problems were armed secession movements in **Tigray** and **Eritrea**. Eritrea indeed broke away and established its own independence in the 1990s.

The Ethiopian Civil War left at least 1.4 million people dead, with 1 million related to famine and the remainder from violence and conflicts, which was one third of the population. It also had impacts on land and agriculture: the former feudal system under which peasants would lose up to 75% of their production to landlords was reversed by the implementation of nationalised reforms. A great many have noted the artificiality of Africa’s borders, drawn by colonialists, but this is the only instance in postcolonial Africa of an actual, legal, formal change.
