I have been learning Classical Chinese for a while and I must admit that it is not the easiest writing system in the world. Everyone knows that there is something distinctive about the Chinese writing system, but there is considerable ignorance and confusion about how that writing system works. What sets apart the Chinese writing system is that it is not an alphabet in the strictest sense that its characters do not directly represent sounds of Chinese words in the sense that by knowing the pronunciation of a word, one cannot say much about the character representing it. Then what is it?

> Chinese writing is a **record of ideas** **with phonetic clues**,
> instead of being a record of spoken sounds.

Let us proceed slowly. The simplest of the Chinese characters have nothing to do with sounds. In the language of technical linguistics, these simple characters are ideograms. To give an initial idea, it is kind of like how we use emojis. By seeing the emoijis 👍☀️ , an English speaker is able to understand it as “*good day*”. But in this emoji system, the emojis don’t spell out the words for “good” and “day” respectively but convey the ideas directly. A French speaker could use this writing system without any trouble by reading the sequence 👍☀️ as “*Bonjour*”. So, the fact that an English speaker associates the emoji 👍 with the word “good” and the emoji ☀️ with “sun” or “day” is just a matter of convention of direct association between words and the emojis and is not a consequence of an association of the sounds of words with the emoji symbols.

Roughly speaking, the simple Chinese characters also work this way. For example, the Chinese character 日 directly stands for “sun” or “day”. The fact that the Mandarin word for this is **rì** is just a convention and has got nothing to do with the shape of the character and no part of the 日 encode the sounds of the word **rì**. While the emoji ☀️ is transparent and obvious in representing the idea of the sun, the Chinese 日 is not. But turns out that originally when writing was first invented in China, they were pictorial. For example, the historical versions of the character that eventually became 日 are shown below.

# Types of Chinese Characters

Chinese writing is based on a system of pictograms and ideograms created 3,200 years ago, which evolved in form and function over the centuries to meet the cultural and communicative needs of the Chinese people. Chinese characters are the oldest writing system still in use, as they have been continuously utilised, with variations, since around 1200 BCE to the present day. The earliest recorded Chinese writing are from the **“oracle bones”**. Oracle bones are animal bones (mostly ox scapulae) and turtle plastrons (undershells) used during the late **Shang** dynasty (c. 1200–1050 BCE) for divination. The Shang kings and their diviners would inscribe questions on the bones—about weather, warfare, harvests, illnesses, dreams, or sacrifices—then apply heat until the bone cracked. The pattern of cracks was interpreted as the ancestors’ or gods’ answers. These inscriptions on oracle bones are the oldest substantial body of Chinese writing discovered so far. They predate the next oldest inscriptions (on bronze vessels) by centuries. Many of the roughly 4,500 different characters found on oracle bones are recognizably the same as modern characters (often in more pictographic form). You can trace the evolution of individual characters from oracle-bone script → bronze script → seal script → modern forms almost without break. Thus, the oracle bones mark the beginning of China’s continuous written record that extends unbroken for over 3,200 years—longer than any other literary tradition on earth.

The standardization of Chinese script occurred during the **Qin Dynasty (221–206 BCE)** under China’s first emperor, **Qin Shi Huang**, and his chancellor **Li Si**. Qin Shi Huang ordered the unification and standardization of the writing system to impose central control. Li Si led the creation of the **Small Seal Script** as the official standard script. All regional variations (the “large seal” and other ancient scripts of the Warring States period) were abolished. The Qin published official dictionaries (e.g., the *Cangjie pian*) to enforce the new standardised forms. This **Qin Small Seal Script** became the direct ancestor of the later **clerical script** and all modern Chinese characters. It marks the moment when the Chinese writing system became essentially uniform across the empire—the form that has been preserved (with stylistic evolution) ever since - for more than two thousand years.

Chinese characters are divided into five broad categories based on their complexity and their way of conveying meaning. The first two types are simple characters (radicals) and the next three types are compound characters.

## SIMPLE CHARACTERS

### Pictographic characters

**Pictographic characters** are those whose form is related to the object they represent (kind of like emoji). But sometimes, the relation can be difficult to determine as in the case of 日 which does not readily look like the sun. ### Ideographic characters

**Ideographic characters** are also similar to pictograms whose form is related to the object they represent (kind of like emoji) but the meaning is little abstract. For example, the emoji 👍 is an ideogram because its meaning is not a finger or thumb but actually means “good” because in the Western world, thumbs up is used to denote the abstract meaning behind the concept of goodness. Similarly, the Chinese characters 上 (**Shàng**) and 下 (**Xià**) mean “up” and “down” respectively. They are indicative but not directly pictographic as concepts like “up” and “down” are abstract to be conveyed by pictures.

Most people, if they have any preconceptions about Chinese characters, seem to think that all of Chinese writing works like pictograms or ideograms and that they have to learn thousands and thousands of distinct pictograms or ideograms. In fact, only a small percentage of Chinese characters are either pictograms or ideograms. Almost all Chinese characters (97%) belong to the fifth group of characters: semantic-phonetic compounds. The pictographic and ideographic single characters together are known as **radicals**. Chinese writing system contains only 214 such radicals which are simple characters that serve as either pictographic or ideographic characters. But together, these radicals can then only represent 214 ideas. Chinese expresses the meanings of so many different words by combing these 214 simple radicals. Characters that use the combination of two or more radicals are called **compound characters**. The remaining four categories of Chinese characters are those of compound characters.

## COMPOUND CHARACTERS

### Associative Characters

Here, two or more radicals combine to create a new character whose meaning is related to them. For example, the radicals for sun and moon combine to form an associative character that means “bright”.

## Borrowed Characters / Loangraphs

The phenomenon of existing characters being adapted to write other words with similar pronunciations was necessary in the initial development of Chinese writing, and has continued throughout its history. Some borrowed characters are introduced to represent words previously lacking another written form—this is often the case with abstract grammatical particles. As another example, the character 來 (***lái***) was originally a pictograph of a wheat plant, with the meaning ‘wheat’. As this was pronounced similar to the Old Chinese word meaning ‘to come’, 來 was loaned to write this verb. Eventually, with the passage of time, this new usage overshadowed eclipsed the old usage so much that ‘to come’ became established as the default reading of this character, so that a new picto-phonetic character 麥 (***mài***) using this character was devised for ‘wheat’. When a character is used as a rebus this way, it is called a borrowed character, or a ‘**rebus character**’. Other popular examples are shown below :

Loangraphs are also used to write words borrowed from other languages, such as the various Buddhist terminology in Sanskrit introduced to China in antiquity, as well as contemporary non-Chinese words and names. For example, each character in the name 加拿大(***Jiānádà***; meaning : ‘Canada’) is often used as a loangraph for its respective syllable. However, the barrier between a character’s pronunciation and meaning is never total: when transcribing into Chinese, loangraphs are often chosen deliberately as to create certain connotations. This is regularly done with corporate brand names: for example, Coca-Cola‘s Chinese name is 可口可乐; 可口可樂**(*****Kěkǒu Kělè*****;** meaning : ‘delicious enjoyable’).

We saw two important things : the conventionality of the meaning of Chinese characters and their strong connection with the spoken language. I have been doing this in order to warn you against what is sometimes called “the ideographic myth,” which is the mistaken belief that Chinese characters somehow directly represent ideas or meanings, without conventions or connections to the spoken language. One extreme illustration of the ideographic myth is provided by the 1960 science fiction film “12 to the Moon”. In this film, an international crew of astronauts receives a video transmission from space aliens that is written in what the astronauts describe as “hieroglyphs.” The Japanese crewmember helps out by sight-translating the alien script.7 The “logic” here is apparently that kanji (the Chinese characters used in written Japanese) are hieroglyphs, and both are pictures, and as such they have intrinsic meaning that can be understood by anyone familiar with any picture-language. I hope that, even before reading this book, you would roll your eyes at this scene, but you are guilty of a similar misconception if you think that Chinese characters are all pictures, or have no connection with spoken words. So remember:

> *Chinese characters usually provide some phonetic information, and
> even if you know exactly what the structure of a Chinese character is,
> you will not necessarily know what it means. Like a word in any
> language, written or spoken, to know the meaning of a character you
> must know how it is used.*

# Number & Simplification

How many characters are there? It is like asking how many words are there in the English language? It is not an easy question to answer. The answer depends on many things like :

-  Do we count variant forms of the same character (some of them
  extremely obscure)? For example, is the British English word
  “***colour***” a different word from the American “***color***”?

- Do we count characters that are now completely obsolete. For example,
  is the word “***fardels***” used by Shakespeare, still an English
  word?

The larger Chinese dictionaries that aim at being comprehensive and record all these variants separately and also include archaic characters across the complete history of the language have about sixty thousand characters or more. But don’t despair: the three thousand most common characters include 99 percent of all characters in use in contemporary Chinese documents. In addition, the eight thousand characters in “Kroll’s Student’s Dictionary of Classical and Medieval Chinese” include almost every character you are likely to run across in the most commonly read premodern documents.

There have been various proposals for reforming or simplifying the Chinese written language. In the 1950s, the government of the People’s Republic of China (PRC) introduced a set of simplified characters wherein some characters were simplified so as to enable their writing easier. For example, the traditional Chinese character for “horse” mă is 馬, while the simplified form used in PRC is 马 which we saw previously. These simplified characters were not ad hoc but are often based on the handwritten cursive style of characters that have already been in use for centuries when writing informally. So, for example, the traditional character 習, (meaning : “to practice”) was simplified to 习, while the traditional character 門(meaning : “gate”) was simplified to 门. Not all characters have a simplified form; in those cases, people still use the “long” or “traditional” form. But these simplified forms were already in use while writing in cursive informally in China. These simplified Chinese characters are now used in PRC and Singapore while Taiwan and Hong Kong still continue the usage of traditional Chinese characters.

Most contemporary Chinese language programs in the United States teach the simplified forms. However, the traditional forms are often used by Chinese while reading ancient texts and outside the PRC, like in Taiwan, only traditional characters are used completely. In addition, well-educated people in the PRC also recognise the long-form traditional characters. People sometimes have very passionate views about the choice to use simplified or long forms. There have been people who get yelled at in conferences in mainland China for including some traditional characters in the printed version of their talks. The political tussle between PRC and Taiwan transform into character battles!

# Compounding in Chinese

So I have learnt all the Chinese characters, their pronunciations, and meanings. Am I now on a fast track to fluency? Turns out it is just the beginning!

Chinese syllables are very short (you might have noticed it already!!). A syllable can start with just one consonant, followed by a vowel, and then end with nothing or “ng” or “n”. Every basic sound in Chinese is just one short syllable (for example: mā, māo, mǎ, mà, bēi, huǒ, tiān, rén, etc.). Even with the four tones (plus the neutral tone), there are only about 1,300–1,600 possible distinct sounds. That’s still far too few for a modern language that needs tens of thousands of words.So how does Chinese solve this problem? It sticks simple syllables together like Lego bricks to make almost every word longer than one syllable. These “stuck-together” words are called compound words. In English, compound words exist (black + board = blackboard, blue + bird = bluebird, butter + fly = butterfly), but they feel special. Note that compound words have meanings that are richer and more specific than their parts. For example, a bluebird is not any bird that is blue, and an ice cream is not any cream that has ice in it.

Most everyday English words are not compounds: table, run, happy, thing — these are single units that are not made of two meaningful parts. In Chinese, the opposite is true: compound words are the normal, everyday way to create new vocabulary. More than 80–90 % of Chinese words have two or more syllables, and almost all of them are made by combining two (or more) shorter words that have their own meaning. Sometimes, the meaning of the compound is related to the meanings of the individual words but sometimes it can be haphazard. Even in English, the meaning of the compound verb “understand” has got nothing to do with the meanings of “under” and “stand”. The same is true for Chinese as well and most words are compounds. “Left-wing” and “Right-wing” do not mean wings on the left or right !! This is because progressives who supported the French Revolution sat in the left side of the legislative assembly while conservatives who opposed it sat on the right and hence the compound words began to take political meanings. Now, no one remembers that historical context but just uses the compounds in a different way in appropriate contexts. The thing is Chinese has lots and lots of such compounds!

Some examples are given below :

**Meaning** : Thing / stuff

**Mandarin compound** : 东西 dōng xi

**Literal meaning** : “east + west”

Why?!! In old markets, one side sold goods from the east, the other side sold goods from the west, so “east-west” came to mean “all kinds of stuff.”

**Meaning** : To introduce someone

**Mandarin compound** : 介绍 jiè shào

**Literal meaning :** “connect/between + continue”

Why?!! You put one person “in between” and let the conversation “continue.” The literal meaning is strange today, but everyone just uses it as one chunk meaning “introduce.”

**Meaning** : Horse

**Archaic Mandarin compound** : 马匹 mǎ pǐ

**Literal meaning** : “horse + cloth.

Why?! Long ago, horses were counted with the same counter word used for bolts of cloth (匹 pǐ). It stuck, so even today we say “three horses” as 三匹马 sān pǐ mǎ.

**Meaning** : Girlfriend

**Mandarin compound** : 女朋友 nǚ péng you = “female + friend + friend”

**Literal meaning :** “female friend friend.”

Chinese often repeats the word “friend” to make it more intimate, so a “girl friend friend” in Chinese is some girl who is more than a friend!!

> **Hence, you can’t learn to read Chinese simply by memorizing what all
> of the symbols mean. Learners also have to know what they mean when
> combined.**

# Is Chinese a single language?

Imagine that in two languages M and C, the word for “introduce” are :

> Language M : **jieshao** (ignoring tones)
>
> Language C : **gaaisiuh** (ignoring tones)

Definitely, these languages are as different as Spanish and French. But typically, they are called as “dialects” of Chinese. Because, M is Mandarin and C is Cantonese.

We often hear that there are different “dialects” of Chinese. Mandarin is the standard dialect with Cantonese being the dialect of Hong Kong. It turns out that Chinese has lots of dialects and all these dialects are technically different languages as different as French, Italian, Spanish and Portuguese. A person who can speak only Mandarin will find it next to impossible to have a conversation with a person who speaks only Cantonese. It is writing that has produced the illusion that Chinese is a single language. Since the Chinese script is the same throughout the nation and the characters do not directly represent sounds, every dialect pronounces the characters in their dialect to vocalise and understand them. Imagine that speakers of Italian, Spanish, and French all use an emoji based system and upon seeing 👍☀️ , they understand it as “bongiourno” , “buenos dias” and “bonjour” respectively and pretend that they are all speaking the dialects of the same language. This is the situation in Chinese as well. Fortunately, everyone in China who has graduated from high school can speak what we call the Mandarin dialect, even though they may have been raised speaking another dialect and use that in their homes. **Mandarin** was the court dialect of the Chinese kings for long time and derived from the Sanskrit word for court minister “***mantrin***”.

The so-called Chinese dialects are different languages, just as the Indo-Aryan languages like Hindi, Bengali, Marathi, Sindhi, and Nepali are. These Chinese dialects are all the descendants of the Old Chinese language that existed 3,000 years ago, just as the Indo-Aryan languages descended from Sanskrit. But, they have diverged over time and are now very different languages that are not mutually intelligible in any way. The terms language and dialect are often used arbitrarily, in reference to culture and writing as well as speech. China is a classic case.

**Mandarin** is spoken by about 1 billion people (including second language speakers) in China, Taiwan and Singapore — that is, about every seventh person in the world. Seventy percent of Chinese speakers speak it. Cantonese is the next most prominent language, but only in America. That is because most Chinese immigrants to the US before 1965 spoke **Cantonese**. But inside China, Cantonese is just one of the many dialects there. **Taiwanese** is one dialect of a dialect grouping called **Min**, spoken by more people than Cantonese. **Shanghainese** is an entire separate language, as different from Mandarin as Dutch is from German. **Hakka**, **Gan** and **Xiang** are less known outside China, but are all distinct as well. These languages all exist in various dialects, many mutually unintelligible. There are actually dozens of Chinese languages even if one is a lumper. The writing system, however, encourages a sense that they are all one unit. Additionally, education and policy inculcate Mandarin, such that it permeates the other dialects to an extent, giving the illusion that there is a single “real Chinese.”

# How to look up characters in a dictionary?

By this point you might be wondering how to look up a character in a Chinese-English dictionary. Some contemporary dictionaries are organized phonetically and alphabetized according to Pinyin romanization based on the Mandarin pronunciation. But what if you don’t already know how to pronounce a character? Most Chinese dictionaries have a finding list that follows the famous *Kang Xi Dictionary* (康熙字典 **Kāng Xī Zìdiǎn**, published 1716 CE) in organising characters according to the 214 radicals. As we saw, every Chinese character has at least one radical in it, or the character is only a radical. There is an order for these 214 radicals that you have to memorise as you have to memorise the alphabetical order of the 26 English letters to use an English dictionary.

So if you encounter a character that you do not recognise, you first take a guess about what its important radical is. This is called the **main** **radical**. In picto-phonetic characters (that are most Chinese characters), the main radical is the pictorial part that tells the meaning. Usually the radical is fairly easy to spot, but sometimes there is more than one radical, and other times the radical may be obscure. Next, count the number of “strokes” in the character in addition to the radical, where a stroke is defined by when you would lift up the brush or pen in order to draw the next line. With this information, go to the part of the dictionary that lists all the characters with that radical plus that many additional strokes. There will typically be a number of characters fitting this description, so you go down the list until you find the character you are looking for. A traditional dictionary whose main entries are organized by radicals will usually also have an index that allows you to find a character by its pronunciation, and another index organized by the total number of strokes in the character. This last system works well for short lists of characters, but finding a character by its strokes in a full dictionary is a last resort, as there are (for example) about two hundred characters with six strokes in even a basic dictionary.

# Japanese Writing System : Hiragana, Katakana, & Kanji

My friend asked me: “*I hear you’re learning Chinese. I’m going to Tokyo next week. Any advice?*” I explained, as gently as I could, that Chinese and Japanese are very different languages. People often fail to realise just how different the Chinese and Japanese languages are. In fact, spoken Chinese and Japanese are not merely distinct languages but they are in completely different language families. This means that spoken English is more closely related historically and structurally to let’s say Hindi (both are in the Indo-European language family), than Japanese is to Chinese!!

One important matter of difference is worth mentioning here. Most students who learn Chinese are bogged down by the complex writing system, compounding, and the tones of vowels. But after getting past all that, people find Chinese to be extremely easy grammatically, much easier than European languages in fact. The important thing is that :

> **Chinese words do not change form (like taking case endings or
> prefixes or changing the internal vowel) depending on grammatical
> parameters and hence is an isolating language with no inflections and
> endings.**

For example, English nouns change depending on whether they are singular or plural. Either an extra suffix like “s” or “es” is added to make a noun plural (e.g. book =\> books, church =\> churches) or the internal vowel of the singular word is changed to make it plural (e.g. mouse =\> mice , goose =\> geese). Chinese nouns do not do this thing. Chinese nouns retain the same form whether they are singular or plural. If you want to emphasise plural, you can add an extra word that serves as a plural marker but typically, when it is understood from context (like when there are numbers or adjectives before the noun), it is left out. Also, in case you have learnt languages like Sanskrit or Latin or Ancient Greek or German, where nouns take endings depending on their role in the sentence (whether they are subject or object or possessives, etc..), Chinese nouns do not do that as well. The nouns and adjectives remain the same whatever number they are and whatever role they are playing. Only context and position of words in a phrase tell these information.

Similarly, Chinese verbs also do not change depending on factors like voice, tense, mood or agreement. A typical English verb has five distinct forms - for example :

- begin, began, begun, begins, beginning

- sing, sang, sung, sings, singing

- bite, bit, bitten, bites, biting

If you have learnt a decent European language like Spanish or Latin or Sanskrit or Ancient Greek, you must have memorised boat loads of case endings for a given verb. Verbs are also divided into conjugation classes and nouns are divided into declensions and adjectives are gendered and each class dictates what kinds of endings they take. But Chinese has none of this. The same verb is found whether one is using it in active or passive voice, present tense or past tense, first person or second person, imperative or indicative, and so on. Some separate extra helping words indicate these information but mostly context and other syntax rules will decide the exact role of the word in a sentence.

> **This is why characters work for Chinese very well because since
> Chinese words do not change forms, a single abstract character symbol
> is enough to capture them.**

But Japanese is on the polar opposite end of the spectrum. Japanese is a highly inflected language, where endings on verbs and nouns change to reflect nor just tense, person, mood, etc (your usual) but also factors like politeness level. Chinese is uninflected; it does by context or grammatical particles what languages like Japanese and English do with inflections. This is why it is so utterly amazing when more than a thousand years ago, the Japanese adopted the Chinese writing system and borrowed their characters. Chinese characters that are borrowed into Japanese are called **kanji**. Japanese did not do the PRC style reform and hence the kanji in Japanese are all traditional characters instead of the simplified characters. Also, the meaning of a kanji is not always the same in Chinese and Japanese ; they would have been same at the time of borrowing, but over the course of millennium, the meanings of Chinese characters and Japanese kanji have drifted apart (sometimes mildly but sometimes wildly).

However, kanji characters alone do not work well for representing Japanese verbs, because they do not give you a way to express the changing inflections.

Traditional Japanese writers solved this problem by developing a two-track writing system. For centuries, high literature and official documents in Japanese were written in Chinese characters with Chinese grammar. Anyone who can read Literary Chinese can read these works written by Japanese authors. The same thing happened in Korea and Vietnam, which also have a rich, native literatures written in Literary Chinese.

But, in addition to writing in Chinese, Japanese authors also developed multiple phonetic syllabic scripts, derived from stylised forms of certain characters but used to represent syllables in Japanese. These latter scripts were used to transcribe diaries, novels, or other works using Japanese words and syntax. Eventually, a hybrid script developed that combined Chinese characters (called kanji in Japanese) to represent the stems of common nouns and verbs, along with two phonetic syllabic scripts (called kana) to represent verb endings, distinctive Japanese grammatical particles, and loanwords from Western languages. The additional level of complexity is that the hiragana and katakana are not alphabets but syllables. Unlike Indian alphabets, the symbols for “ka” and “ki” have nothing in common with each other! So, a native Japanese uses three writing systems in parallel !!
