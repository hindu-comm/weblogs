2.  Portable

3.  Icecream

4.  Breakfast

5.  Common

All native speakers of English stress and destress the vowels in the same way at the same places. Here are some ground rules regarding stress assignment in English.

# Rules for Stress in English Words

### One word = One stress

Every word has utmost one stressed vowel. If you hear two stresses, it comes from two different words. Two or more stresses cannot be in the same word. A compound word like “ice-cream” or “breakfast” counts as one word as they convey a single idea by being together.

### Nouns & Adjectives : First of the two syllables stressed

If the word is a noun/adjective and has only one vowel sound and hence only one syllable, then obviously that syllable carries the stress. If it has two vowels, the first one carries the stress. ### Polysyllabic Nouns & Adjectives

If a word has more than two vowels (and hence more than two syllables) and is not a compound word, either the second-to-last vowel (**penultimate** vowel) is stressed or the third-to-last vowel is stressed (**anti penultimate** vowel). The stress cannot go farther left than that. For a given word, this is done consistently by all speakers. Normally, words with more than two vowels end in some suffixes and there are predictable rules for stress depending on the suffixes.

When a word ends in *“ic,” “sion” or “tion,”* the stress is usually on the second-to-last vowel. You count syllables backwards and put a stress on the second one from the end. (hereafter, unstressed vowels are not coloured and only stressed vowels are bolded and given in red)

Of course these are just the general rules and there might be exceptions but the important thing is that the exceptions too are the same for all native speakers. Everyone puts the stress at the same place in a given word whether they follow the above rules or not.

So, we have now discovered (surprised that you maybe knew it already without any high school lessons) that English words carry regular stress patterns in their vowels and rhythm in English poetry is achieved by a regular alternation of stressed and unstressed vowels that convey the mood of the poem!!

# Types of Feet & Meter in English

Having seen how to stress syllables in words, let us see how English poetry works in creating rhythm. English uses five kinds of bricks as ingredients in poetry - each such brick is called a **foot** (plural : feet). The following are the seven simple types of feet in English poetry and their stress pattern with S denoting stressed syllable and U denoting unstressed vowel :

One syllabic feet :

Type 1 - Monosyllabic foot : **S**

Two syllabic feet :

Type 2 - ***Iamb*** : **US**

Type 3 - ***Trochee*** : **SU**

Type 4 - ***Spondee*** : **SS**

Type 5 - ***Pyrrhic*** : UU

Three syllabic feet :

Type 6 - ***Anapest*** : **UUS**

Type 7 - ***Dactyl*** : **SUU**

Based on the number of feet per line, poems are classified by Greek suffixes as being **Monometer**,

**Dimeter,**

**Trimeter**,

**Tetrameter**,

**Pentameter** (most common in English),

**Hexameter,**

**Heptameter**,

**Octameter**,

and so on.

So a line in an ***iambic trimeter*** would consist of three iambs in it. A line consisting of six dactyls would be called as a ***dactylic hexameter***.

# Iambic Pentameter

Iambic pentameter is the most ubiquitous and staple meter in all of English literature. We find it in the plays of Shakespeare, in the sonnet, and in the blank-verse poems (that is, poems written in unrhymed iambic pentameter) of Wordsworth, Tennyson, and many others. As you read the poems below, feel the following rhythm in each line

da DUM da DUM da DUM da DUM da DUM

where *da* denotes unstressed syllable and *DUM* denotes stressed syllable.

Here’s an example of iambic pentameter: **Christina Rossetti’**s sonnet, ‘*In an Artist’s Studio*’, written in the 1850s.

One face looks out from all his canvases,  
One selfsame figure sits or walks or leans:  
We found her hidden just behind those screens,  
That mirror gave back all her loveliness.  
A queen in opal or in ruby dress,  
A nameless girl in freshest summer-greens,  
A saint, an angel — every canvas means  
The same one meaning, neither more or less

Here’s a masterly example, from the opening of **John Milton’**s *Paradise Lost* (1667):

Of Man’s first disobedience, and the fruit  
Of that forbidden tree whose mortal taste  
Brought death into the World, and all our woe,  
With loss of Eden, till one greater Man  
Restore us, and regain the blissful seat,  
Sing, Heavenly Muse, that, on the secret top  
Of Oreb, or of Sinai, didst inspire  
That shepherd who first taught the chosen seed  
In the beginning how the heavens and earth  
Rose out of Chaos …

Read here **Alexander Pope**’s eighteenth-century didactic work, *An Essay on Man*:

The bliss of man (could pride that blessing find)  
Is not to act or think beyond mankind;  
No pow’rs of body or of soul to share,  
But what his nature and his state can bear.  
Why has not man a microscopic eye?  
For this plain reason, man is not a fly.

Iambic pentameter is so popular because it comes close to the rhythms of ordinary English speech. But iambic tetrameter – which, as the name implies, is a foot shorter than iambic pentameter – comes pretty close, too. Here is an example from **Tennyson**’s long Victorian elegy for his friend, *In Memoriam A. H. H.* (1850):

Old Yew, which graspest at the stones  
That name the under-lying dead,  
Thy fibres net the dreamless head,  
Thy roots are wrapt about the bones.

The seasons bring the flower again,  
And bring the firstling to the flock;  
And in the dusk of thee, the clock  
Beats out the little lives of men.
