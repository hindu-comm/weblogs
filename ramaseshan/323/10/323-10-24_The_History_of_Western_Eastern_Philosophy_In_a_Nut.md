In this series of articles, I intend to chart the history and evolution of philosophy in the three great traditions of world philosophy - **Western**, **Indian**, and **Chinese**. All articles in this series can be read individually as well. The numbering of parts are given for those who would like a more systematic treatment. I focus on metaphysics and epistemology here because I write about political philosophy in another series.

Comparative philosophy has always been close to my heart. Even though all civilizations have had a history of rich intellectual thought, it is only in Europe and Asia where we find rich and continuous written or oral records of intellectual thought spanning over millennia.

# Western Philosophy : Personal Metaphor

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!uxCT!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F012bb4bf-ce67-44bc-9a5b-1f79ff36461e_1264x710.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!uxCT!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F012bb4bf-ce67-44bc-9a5b-1f79ff36461e_1264x710.heic)

Although the notion of the “West” is difficult to define and pin down, the notion of “***Western intellectual tradition***” is much easier to pin down. Imagine the entirety of Western philosophy as a person. I saw this metaphor in the lectures on Western philosophy by Prof. Philip Carry from The Great Courses. According to this metaphor, the Western tradition stands on two legs:  

The first leg is **Ancient Greece**. The beginnings of the Western intellectual tradition goes all the way to the ancient Greek world in about the sixth century BCE with a bunch of philosophers called the **pre-Socratics** pondering about what the world is made of. Ancient Greek philosophy reaches a cultural high point in Athens during the middle of the fifth century BCE starting from **Socrates** and then proceeds on to his student **Plato** and his student **Aristotle** who lived in the fourth century BCE. After that, Greece comes under the domination of King **Philip II** of **Macedon** with his son **Alexander** the Great conquering the huge Persian Empire and spreading Greek culture and philosophy in a very large area from Egypt in the West to Persia in the East. After the death of Alexander in 323 BCE, his huge empire split into various Hellenistic kingdoms until they were all conquered by the Romans in 30 BCE. The **Roman Empire** is the largest political unit that the West has ever seen until the advent of modern colonialism. The Roman Empire lasted in the West until 476 CE when the last Roman Emperor in the West was deposed. The fall of the Roman Empire in the West traditionally marks the end of the ancient period in Western history and the dawn of the medieval era.

The second leg of the Western tradition is **Biblical Israel** whose history is the subject of the Jewish Hebrew Bible. This Hebrew Bible constitutes the first 80% of the Christian Bible, which is called by the Christians as the “Old Testament”. In the Old Testament, we see how the God who created the universe revealed himself to Abraham and chose his descendants as his own people who eventually became to be known as the “**Israelites**” or the “**Hebrews**”. The text then chronicles the history of the Israelites through a theological lens that demonstrates the hand of God marching the history of his chosen people. After rescuing the Israelites from slavery in Egypt, God makes a **covenant** with Israelites through the prophet Moses wherein he gives a set of laws that should be kept by the Israelites in exchange for his protection. The first part of the Bible that narrates the history of Israel shows us that the Israelites prosper if they keep those commandments and suffer at the hands of their enemies if they turn away from the covenant. After they conquer the promised land of **Canaan** in the Levant, the Israelites are split into twelve tribes and charismatic leaders come in time and again to unite them as they face problems from their enemies. Finally, at about 1020 BCE, they transitioned into a monarchy under King **Saul**, followed by King **David** in 1000 BCE (who conquers Jerusalem and makes it the capital of his kingdom) and then King Solomon in about 960 BCE where the kingdom reached a high point. After the death of King **Solomon**, the kingdom split. The ten northern tribes retain the name of Israel and splinter as a separate kingdom and then the two southern tribes of Judah and Benjamin form the Kingdom of **Judah** retaining the capital at Jerusalem. After that, the **Assyrian** Empire conquers the northern kingdom in around 720 BCE and exiles its elite inhabitants where the Israelites disappear as a distinct people. The southern kingdom of Judah was conquered by the **Babylonians** in 586 BCE and its elite inhabitants too were exiled. But this time, the **Persian** Empire led by King **Cyrus** soon conquered the Babylonian Empire in 539 BCE and allowed the people exiled by the Babylonians to return to their original lands and re-establish themselves. The exiled Judeans who eventually came to be known as Jews were constantly conquered by foreign powers and many continued to live outside their ancestral homeland. This made Judaism transition to a book based religion as sacred texts could unite people from diverse parts of the world.

Christianity began as a Jewish reform movement based in Roman ruled **Judaea** in the first century centered around the death and resurrection of **Jesus Christ**, whom his followers believed to be the **messiah** foretold in the Jewish Bible. But Christianity could never make inroads as a Jewish sect and was rejected as a heresy and even persecuted as a blasphemy in the wider Jewish world as Jesus never fulfilled any traditional Jewish expectations of the messiah. Its real success would come about when it was taken out of its Jewish context in Israel and transplanted onto the wider ***gentile*** (non-Jewish) Greco-Roman world. The idea that non-Jews could worship the Jewish God and believe in the Jewish messiah without following the Jewish law codes and rituals (like circumcision for males and kosher dietary regulations) was a revolution that would transform it from a Jewish heresy to a world religion. The fiercest defender of this view was a Jewish Pharisee turned convert named **Paul** who called himself as “***the apostle to the gentiles***” and undertook extensive missionary journeys across various cities of the Roman Empire to convert people and establish house churches and whose letters ended up being a part of the New Testament of the Christian Bible when the collection was canonized.

Emperor **Constantine** in 312 CE was the first Roman Emperor to convert to Christianity and Emperor **Theodosius I** made it the only officially tolerated religion in 380 CE. Soon, paganism died in the Roman Empire and it became entirely Christian. Then, Christianity slowly but firmly spread to and established itself in the rest of Europe through a combination of missions and conquests. In the medieval period, Christian philosophers focused on harmonizing their classical Greco-Roman philosophical inheritance with the Christian faith. The philosopher **Augustine** in the fifth century CE combined Plato with Christianity to create a **Christian Platonism** that would dominate the theology of the Western Roman Church for centuries. In the thirteenth century, **Thomas Aquinas** combined Aristotelianism with Christianity to create a comprehensive theological framework called **scholasticism**. Scholasticism would constitute the standard education for all Europeans and constitutes the background in which the **Renaissance**, the **Reformation**, and the **Scientific Revolution** would take place. This part of Western philosophy in the Middle Ages is represented by the torso of the human that arises when the two legs meet.

After the fall of the Middle Ages, two movements arose in the West to go back and connect to the original foundations which are the two legs. The **Renaissance** which emphasized **humanism** and a revival of Greco-Roman wisdom and culture and the **Reformation** which sought to return to the original Biblical Christianity. The two hands go hand in hand with the two legs. It is the hands that do stuff. Reformation theologies and Renaissance humanism influenced and inspired much of what Europe did in modernity, especially colonialism and their interaction with other civilizations.

The next is the head which is the most important part of the body that does all the thinking and in Western philosophy, it is represented by modern philosophy. **Modernity** in the West informs all its current actions and motives. But as all parts of the body are interconnected, so are all periods of Western philosophy that have always informed their successors. The discovery of America, the Protestant Reformation, and the Black Death created ripe conditions for the fall of medieval feudalism and also the decline in scholasticism. The **Scientific Revolution** of the 16th and 17th century would render scholasticism outdated and philosophers who emerged in this period would focus on harmonizing philosophy with modern science rather than Christianity or Classical Greek thought. The 18th century in the West called the **Enlightenment** would see a period in philosophy where the optimism of modern science and technology made philosophers believe that the scientific method could apply to all aspects of human experience and study. **Romanticism** arose in the 19th century to counter the over-optimism of the Enlightenment and this movement wanted to restore feeling and subjective human experience once again as the focus of philosophy. The impacts of other important movements like the capitalist free market economy, the French Revolution, and nationalism can be clearly seen in modern philosophy.

In the 20th century, Western philosophy would experience a split into three major themes. Unlike other splits where the various schools would act as rivals and debate with each other, this 20th century split into three schools would stop talking to each other as they never could find any common theme or framework to agree on to even interact with each other. Thinkers within each of the three traditions would fiercely argue with one another in various schools but interaction between any of these three traditions would cease. The first theme dominant in the United Kingdom and scientific academia would be **analytic philosophy** that concerned itself with the detailed analysis of language and logic to demystify philosophical problems. The second theme dominant in continental Europe would be **existentialism**. There is tremendous diversity within this label that thinkers within this tradition often even disagree if they all deserve a common label. But they do share one goal in common which is the exploration of human life and existence and its authenticity. The third theme dominant in the United States would be **pragmatism** which most call as a distinctively American school which is focused on reducing philosophy to practical concerns rather than arm chair based abstract theoretical analysis of philosophical problems.

# Is there an Eastern Intellectual Tradition?

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!5KB8!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fba72f2fa-de5f-4f7a-9db1-68ad3e752929_1278x724.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!5KB8!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fba72f2fa-de5f-4f7a-9db1-68ad3e752929_1278x724.heic)

I have read so many books for the layman about “Western Philosophy” as a whole that cover in detail the history and works of all the important philosophers in the Western tradition starting from **Socrates** to **Sartre**. Then, I wondered if there was anything like that covering the entire Eastern philosophical tradition as a whole - covering India, China, Japan, Tibet, Korea, etc. I could only discover a few and even those were very surface level and written for a Western audience. Hence, I decided to write this.

I will be introducing to several hundreds of Eastern philosophers and texts in this book who have had tremendous influence within their own culture. Construct a list of the most famous thinkers from the Western tradition - **Moses**, **Socrates**, **Plato**, **Aristotle**, **Cicero**, **Jesus**, **Plotinus**, **Augustine**, **Boethius**, **Aquinas**, **Machiavelli**, **Descartes**, **Leibniz**, **Spinoza**, **Berkeley**, **Hume**, **Adam** **Smith**, **Newton**, **Kant**, **Hegel**, **Marx**, **Freud**, **Einstein**, etc, who have had the greatest impact on the trajectory of Western thought. Here, I will be introducing to you analogous figures about whom anyone growing up in India, China, Japan, or Tibet would have heard about. From the perspective of this articles and philosophical academia, Islam belongs to the Western tradition due to its Abrahamic monotheism and its engagement with only Western philosophical works - especially Plato and Aristotle. Islamic intellectual thought in India, if at all existent, rarely engaged with the ongoing Indian traditions.

Get ready for lots of lists - the **four noble truths**, the **eightfold way**, etc. Eastern thinkers use lots of lists partly because they were working in a predominantly oral tradition which demanded such concise listings to remember the main concepts and serve as a road map. A lot of wisdom is captured in these lists. There will also be lots of names. **Sanskrit** and **Pali** names will be presented in the standard IAST Roman diacritics in their stem forms. Chinese names will be presented in the now standard **Pinyin** style of Romanization followed by their traditional Chinese character. Also note that in China, the family name comes first and then the personal name.

Now, to the main question - is there an “Eastern” intellectual tradition? The short answer would be “sort of”. Why? What is “***tradition***”? The word “tradition” comes from a Latin word that literally means “*something that is handed over*”. The Western intellectual tradition is a single tradition because all of its thinkers (at least all of its important thinkers) and their thoughts can be arranged chronologically with each thinker being educated in the thoughts of all those who preceded him in the tradition and also influencing all those who succeeded him in the tradition. The famous philosopher **Whitehead** said:

Western philosophy is a series of footnotes to Plato

Although it is an exaggeration, it has a grain of truth in it - the Western philosophical tradition is a linear tradition beginning with Plato. Even though most lay people in the West have never read all of Western thought, each intellectual in the West was familiar with all those who came before him and impacted all his contemporary intellectuals and those after him. So, in some sense, an intellectual tradition is one “***great conversation***” that has been handed down continuously from one generation to another and is continuing into our time. So, the Western intellectual tradition is a single great conversation initiated by Socrates and Plato and carried on by all intellectuals who came after him. So, the history of Western thought is linear, compact, and handleable easily.

> There is historical integrity to the Western philosophical tradition.
> In this context, historical integrity means that later thinkers
> possessed the awareness of all the works of earlier ones.

Of course, historically this need not have been so. If the ancient Greek tradition had not been rediscovered again in the High Middle Ages, after the chaotic period that followed the collapse of the Roman Empire, there would be no such combination of Judaeo-Christian and Ancient Greek thought that we now call “Western philosophy”. Also, historical integrity does not mean that all the thinkers share common ideas or even common goals and frameworks. A modern analytic philosopher from Britain can barely talk to an existentialist from the continent. But still, they all are familiar with the same figures from Plato. It is the common mutual awareness (not common ideas and goals) that leads to the historical integrity of the Western philosophical tradition even though its geographical borders and cultures of the people who engaged with it are diverse and diffuse.

But when we come to the East, we have a problem. There is no such linear sequence for the entire Eastern intellectual output as a whole. There are huge mutual ignorances at least between India and East Asia. The **Buddha** was as unknown to **Confucius** as **Plato** was. The very idea that there is a single Eastern intellectual tradition comes about mainly due to Western lumping of civilizations that are not familiar to it and the common influence of Buddhism. Eastern intellectual thought is not linear but more diffuse, one sided, fragmented, and coupled in a complex manner. As we will see in the course of this book, Buddhism did not make India, China, and Japan talk to each other in the complete sense. Even though Buddhism made Indian concepts flow to China, Tibet, and the rest of East Asia it never went the other way. Indian thought (even Indian Buddhist thought) was never influenced by what was happening in China. Also, Chinese Buddhism soon took on a distinctively Chinese framework and addressed Chinese concerns. Similarly, Chinese thought had a huge influence on Japan and Korea right from their formative stages but was not in turn influenced by them. Efforts to link and connect these intellectual traditions have always focused on comparing Asian thoughts to the West.

To give an easy way for you to keep track of the complex web of interactions of Eastern intellectual thought that will happen, let me give a metaphor. Imagine three major conferences going on at four different hotels in three different countries.

The earliest of these conferences begins in a hotel named the **Darśana** hotel that represents India. This conference opens with the plenary session on the **Veda**s, which are a collection of ancient hymns followed by reflections on the fundamental nature of reality and the self. The key ideas discussed after the opening songs of this plenary session are those of **saṃsāra** (reincarnation), **dharma** (an integrated sense of cosmic, social, and moral order), **karma** (law of cosmic morality), and **mokṣa** (liberation from rebirth). The Vedas introduce the notions of **ātman** (individual self) and **brahman** (cosmic self) as objects of focus to effect this liberation.

After this plenary session, there are two sessions by two dissenting speakers **Buddha** and **Mahāvīra** who agree with the key ideas discussed in the plenary session of the Vedas but disagree on how the Vedas engaged with them - especially the notions of .**ātman** and **brahman**. There are some members of those who listened to Buddha and Mahāvīra who end up agreeing with them. Those who want to stick to the Vedas become called as **Hindu**. There is also a minority splinter group called the **Cārvāka**s. They think that all the conference sessions are pointless and convince everyone to withdraw from all sessions and just party in the bar. Next, all these groups split into several subgroups who all present their papers to everyone. There are six subgroups in the Hindu group (**Sāṇkhya**, **Yoga**, **Vais̀eṣika**, **Nyāya**, **Mīmāṃsā**, **Vedanta**), five in the Buddhist group (**Theravāda**, **Sarvāstivāda**, **Sautrāntika**, **Mādhyamaka**, **Yogācāra**), and two in the Jain group (**Śvetāmbara**, **Digāmbara**). One Buddhist subgroup (**Theravāda**) soon stops engaging with all others, goes south, and eventually isolates itself into a separate hall ( an island called **Śrī Laṅkā** at the bottom of India) within which it will henceforth be confined. But the other four Buddhist subgroups present are very interactive amongst each other and defend their positions and criticize their rivals from all other groups, and are then criticized by them, only to come back again with a better defense and offense. Occasionally, a few curious Zoroastrian and Muslim visitors from abroad will come into the hotel to listen but rarely participate in these conversations themselves.

After the conference has started at the Darśana hotel, another conference begins at another hotel called the **Dào** 道 hotel that represents China. The Chinese word Dào 道 actually means a path or a road or a way. Because all of the events at this conference will teach about this **Dào** 道. The plenary session consists of not one but a hundred speakers. This conference was initiated because there was intense war and breakdown of social order in the country and hence the discussions and debates in this conference began not about abstract concepts and ideas but about urgent this-worldly concerns on how to organize and maintain society. Some famous among the plenary speakers include **Kǒngzǐ** (**Confucius**) 孔子, **Lǎozǐ** 老子, **Mòzǐ** 墨子, etc (Note that the Chinese word **zǐ** 子 that occurs in all of these names is a title that means Master). All these speakers are presenting on the **Dào** 道 - the path that people should follow to create a good character and form a good society. Some of these hundred groups attract followers and various groups are formed - **Confucians**, **Daoists**, **Mohists**, **Legalists**, **Naturalists**, **Logicians**, etc.

The third conference represents Japan and starts much later and takes place in a hotel named the **Dō** 道 hotel . Since they are starting much later, they send people over to the Dào 道 hotel to go there and see what is happening there and how things are arranged there and then go back to their Dō 道 hotel and talk about them. So, the participants of this third conference despite having their own political and cultural concerns, borrow from the ideas in the Dào 道 hotel. This conference in the Dō 道 hotel is smaller and always has people who go to the Dào 道 hotel and bring back the information on what is happening there although the attendees of the Dào 道 hotel do not pay attention to these people from the Dō 道 hotel.

There is a particularly active group at the Darśana hotel in India - the four Buddhist subgroups - some of whom travel and make their way to the Dào 道 hotel in China, listen to the Daoists and initiate conversations with them in their own language but focusing on Buddhist concerns. They manage to attract enough speakers to their side of the auditorium that soon, the Daoists and Confucians begin adapting their pitch to address the concerns of these Buddhists. Over time, the folks at the Dào 道 hotel who sided with the Buddhists end up making their way to the Dō 道 hotel in Japan as well where they become extremely popular. These Buddhists also inaugurate a fourth conference at a hotel named the **Snyan Srin** hotel in **Tibet** and thus enabling their thought to also serve as the foundations for the subsequent events at that hotel too.

Meanwhile, due to an unusual turn of events, there are no more Buddhists left in the Darśana hotel from where they originally came from, except for the isolated Theravāda group in Laṅkā. As these Buddhists are dwindling at the Darśana hotel, the **Vedānta** group in Hinduism somehow manages to unite all the other five Hindu groups and also incorporate Buddhist thoughts into its framework and manages to become hugely popular across the entire Hindu attendees. But later, the Vedanta itself splits into rival subgroups - three major among them being **Advaita**, **Viśiṣṭādavita**, and **Dvaita**.

As India suffers from Islamic conquests, its native philosophical traditions suffered a halt in evolution and also the contact and philosphical exchange between India and East Asia too came to a stop. With British Colonialism, Western and Indian philosophy began to influence each other.
