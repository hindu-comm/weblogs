# Origins in Mesopotamia

The Yazidis are located in the land broadly known as Mesopotamia and are the only surviving living bridge from the ancient past to the present day. They currently are spread in modern northern Iraq, southeastern Turkey, and north-eastern Syria. The **Sinjar** mountain range that you see highlighted in the picture below is an important landmark in their history because this mountain has served as both a mountain of refuge as well as a prison when the turbulent deluge of persecution hit them. The heartland of Yazidis is around this mountain range.

The exact question of Yazidi origins is hard to determine because they have not that ancient written texts and not left much archaeological remains as well and their traditions were orally transmitted for millennia. Ethnically, the Yazidis identify with the Kurds and speak **Kurmanji**, which is a dialect of **Kurdish**, (an Indo-Iranian language related to Persian) that also serves as their liturgical language. The sacred hymns of the Yazidis, known as the **qewls**, are in an archaic versions of Kurmanji, and record some words that may contain the last traces of words from Sumerian, Akkadian, and the other languages once spoken in Mesopotamia.

Broadly, it is believed that Yazidis are simply those Kurdish tribal groups that maintained their pre-Christian and pre-Islamic religious traditions but their religion also has incorporated influences from other ancient Middle Eastern religions. There is some similarity with Zoroastrianism as the Yazidis also revere fire, have prohibitions against blowing on flames, and their cosmology emphasises the duality between light and darkness. But unlike Zoroastrianism, Yazidis reject any evil deity (like Ahriman) opposing God. There are other links of the Yazidi religion to the ancient Mesopotamian religions which is reflected in their calendar, their worship of the sun, and certain iconography that resembles ancient Sumerian, Akkadian, and Babylonian religious practices. Links have also been suggested with the cult of Mithras, a mystery religion popular in the Roman Empire in the first centuries CE among soldiers that itself drew from Persian sources. There are also similarities with Hinduism like anointing of forehead, pyramid temple architecture, the belief in reincarnation of souls until they achieve perfection, and also in their iconography, about which we will see in detail in the course of this article. There are also similarities to the Abrahamic religions about also which we will see in the appropriate places.

The Yazidis themselves maintain in their tradition that they are the original people created separately from the rest of humanity and trace their lineage back to only Adam (unlike others whom they believe to be descended from both Adam and Eve). This explains their strict endogamy and their religion has hence always been confined to their ethnic boundaries.

# God, the Seven Angels & Yazidi Theology

## God & the Seven Angels

Yazidis believe in one supreme creator God whom they call **Xwedê** (cognate to the Persian word ***Khudā***) or **Êzî** (that gives the community their name). This God they believe to be transcendent and disinterested in worldly affairs and that after having created the universe, he appointed seven divine beings (sometimes referred to as angels) to govern the creation. The most important among these seven is **Melek Taus**, also referred to as the **Peacock Angel**, to whom God entrusted the governance of the world. The countless “eyes” of this peacock (as its feathers) stands for divine omniscience.

## Creation

This Yazidi myth must sound familiar to anyone from the Christian or Islamic tradition. When God created the seven divine beings (angels), he commanded them to bow to Adam, the first human. Six of them obeyed but Melek Taus refused because he wanted to keep up the previous commandment from God that he would worship none but Him. God recognised his true devotion and hence elevated him among all angels and made him God’s representative on earth. In Yazidi understanding, this story demonstrates the primacy of the first commandment and the importance of understanding the spirit of divine law rather than blindly following its letter. Melek Taus becomes the model of perfect devotion, the being who loved God so purely that he would risk everything rather than compromise his worship of the one true God. This monotheistic aspect and also the story of a rebellious angel makes it similar to the Abrahamic traditions but is also distinct that he does not come Satan and there is no figure of the Devil and there is no eternal damnation in Yazidi religion. But due to the resemblance between the fallen angel Melek Taus and the Satan, the Muslims surrounding them and the Christians who studied them have called them Devil worshippers.

The Yazidi creation story tells us that God existed first as a pearl and then inside that, he created a white pearl that encoded the forms of the universe that would ensue. Then God created the bird Anfar which broke the pearl, thereby releasing the components of the universe using which He fashioned the universe which included the creation of the seven divine angels on seven successive days (this aspect of it must remind you of the Gensesis story in the Bible). After creating the universe, God created Adam from whom directly descend the Yazidis while the rest of humanity descends later from Adam and Eve through a different process. One of the most important qewls (sacred oral hymns) describes Melek Taus, his attributes, his roles, and his governance. These hymns speak of his beauty, his power, and his devotion to God. They describe how God entrusted the world to Melek Taus, how he watches over humanity, and how he will ultimately gather all souls to himself. The imagery is beautiful and sometimes cryptic, reflecting the mystical dimension of the Yazidi theology.

## Reincarnation & Cosmic Justice

Instead of linear time and final judgement, the Yazidis believe in reincarnation and that souls are purified through successive lives until they attain spiritual perfection and unite with God. The journey may take many lifetimes but they believe that ultimately it is destined to succeed. But unlike in Hinduism or Buddhism where the soul can be technically be reborn as an animal or plant, Yazidis believe that the soul can be reborn only as a human. Heaven and hell are not places of eternal paradise or torment but only as temporary states between incarnations. The soul keeps reincarnating until it overcomes attachments, errors, and impurities and attains a state of spiritual perfection upon which it unites with the divine. Bodies are typically buried quickly after death. Some Yazidis claim to remember their past lives that reinforce this belief. Also, there is a theory of cosmic justice like the karma in Eastern religions where in the quality of the next life is determined by the moral conduct in this life. Suffering is hence not a result of the devil like figure but as the result of choices that we make that violate the cosmic order and justice ultimately prevails by operating across reincarnations. Yazidi ethics emphasise personal behaviour, honesty, and ritual piety. There are usual taboos against lying, miserliness, disrespecting elders, ritual impurity, adultery, premarital sex, and so on.

## Divinities

The seven divine beings (including Melek Taus) are associated with the aspects of the natural world and also are identified with the seven days of the week and the seven colours of the rainbow. The exact names and attributes of these divine beings vary within the Yazidi tribes due to the oral nature of transmission but Melek Taus is considered supreme among them. Among the other six divine angels, the most important one is **Sheikh Shams**, associated with the sun, heat, and light and represents illumination and the life giving power of the sunlight. Yazidis pray towards the sun at specific times of the day. Yazidi theology also recognises various other spiritual beings pervading various parts of the cosmos and associated with particular local places, activities, or times and there are lots of local shrines to venerate them which are sites of pilgrimage. Water particularly holds significance and the spring at Lalish (also called **Zemzem** after the sacred spring in Mecca) is believed to be sacred and healing and hence is used for baptism and consumption.

## Taboos

Yazidis avoid consumption of certain foods although the reason for some of the taboos is not very clear in the sense that whether it is a religious prohibition or a cultural practice that has become religiously sanctioned. Some of the prohibited foods for Yazidis are lettuce and pork. They also avoid wearing blue colour in religious occasions and do not spit or blow on the fire (as they consider it as a sacred element).

## Attitude towards other religions

In principle, Yazidis hold that all other faiths represent different paths to God and do not condemn others as damned or wrong but due to the centuries of persecution at the hands of Muslims, and also the hands of Christian powers in enabling it and proselytising to them, they harbour a deep distrust and hatred towards those two religions.

## Reformer Figure

An important figure in Yazidi religious history is **Sheikh Adi bin Musafir** (about whom we will see in detail in the next section) who was a 12th century saint who reorganised and systematised the religion and gave it its classical form and is venerated as a saint. His presence in the **Lalish Valley** (in modern Iraq) transformed the Valley into the most holy site of the religion.

## Calendar, Festivals & Pilgrimages

The Yazidi calendar is dotted with fasts, festivals, and holidays that commemorate both natural cycles and sacred events in their mythic tradition. At the spring equinox, **New Year (Çarşema Sor)** is celebrated which coincides with for example the Persian Nowruz. This day also marks the beginning of creation in Yazidi faith and on this day, the Peacock Angel descended to the earth. Thus, this new year represents renewal of creation and a particular thing to note is their decoration with coloured eggs on this day that symbolises this creative process and also the emergence of universe from its cosmic pearl.

Another important Yazidi festival that marks the winter solstice is the **Festival of Ezi** that involves three days of fasting followed by feasting and celebration. Yazidis light lamps and fire during this festival to get through the winter darkness.

The most significant annual pilgrimage and festival is the Feast of the Assembly, called **Cejna Cemaiya**, held at Lalish in early October. Yazidis from throughout the region traditionally used to make a pilgrimage to Lalish for this week-long gathering, which combines religious observance with celebration. The festival includes ritual bathing in the sacred spring, circumambulation of shrines, lighting of lamps, and the performance of qewls. This gathering renews Yazidi identity, bringing together members of the dispersed community in their holiest place. During this pilgrimage, religious leaders perform special ceremonies including the washing of sacred **sanjak**s, which are brass peacock figures representing Melek Taus. They are traditionally carried throughout Yazidi regions by traveling religious figures who collect offerings and perform blessings. Earth from Lalish and water from its springs are considered sacred and Yazidis traditionally brought handfuls of this earth and water home from pilgrimage. The sacred water was used for **mor kirinê** (baptism) that initiated children into the fold of the community. They also placed the sacred earth of Lalish in the graves of their dead. The sanjak ceremony involves ritual cleansing and anointing of these sacred objects, renewing their sanctity for another year. The sanjaks are tangible symbols of the Peacock Angel’s presence.

## Hereditary Caste System

Like in traditional Hinduism, religious knowledge and authority are passed down hereditarily along specific castes who claim descent from Sheikh Adi’s companions. A figure called the baba sheikh is the supreme religious authority of the community and traditionally he negotiated with the ruling powers on the behalf of the entire community. Below the baba sheikh are various classes of specialists :

1.  **Sheikh**s - perform rituals, bless marriages, mediate disputes,
    guide

2.  **Pir**s - slightly different ceremonial rules

3.  **Qewwal**s - sing the sacred hymns and are religious educators

4.  **Kochek**s - assist the above three and maintain religious shrines

Ordinary Yazidis called **Murid**s rely on the above four religious classes for religious guidance and ceremonial services. There is no equivalent sacred written scripture in Yazidi religion and their sacred hymns (qewls) are preserved and transmitted orally. Each murid family is linked to a specific sheikh or pir family for their religious needs, thus creating bonds of obligation and patronage.

# Sheikh Adi & the Yazidi Synthesis

The Yazidi religion does not have any founder and is a continuous tradition stretching all the way back to recorded history but there is a personality who sort of crystallised the religion and gave it its current classical form. This figure of **Sheikh Adi bin Musafir** is a weird figure to serve as a focal point of the tightly ethnic Yazidi religion because he was a Levantine Muslim in the initial years of his life ! Sheikh Adi who was a Lebanese Muslim born around 1073 CE claimed descent from an Umayyad Caliph and was a pious and intelligent Muslim who memorised the Quran and the Haidths and became an expert on Islamic jurisprudence in the Shafi’i school. Later in his life when he was in Baghdad, he was attracted to mysticism and became associated (or founded?) a Sufi order named the **Adawiyya** and gained recognition as a spiritual teacher attracting students and emphasised asceticism and **dhikr** (remembrance of Allah). Around the time he approached 40, Adi who was now desiring a greater solitude, travelled and found his way to the mountains of Kurdistan where Yazidi communities lived.

Tradition has it that when Adi arrived in the valley of Lalish, he experienced a remarkable spiritual power and settled there, subsequently attracting both Muslim and Yazidi disciples. It is clear that the interaction between Adi’s teachings and the indigenous Yazidi religion created the synthesis that defines Yazidism in its current form but the exact nature of this syncretism is unclear : Did Yazidis adopt Adi as a saint while maintaining their indigenous beliefs? Did Adi adopt Yazidism and create a new religion with some sufism into the mix? Did Adi convert Yazidis to a heterodox form of Islam that became the current form of the religion? It is unclear what exactly about the Yazidi religion is indigenous and what is a contribution from Adi. But what is clear is that Adi created a framework that systematised Yazidi religion and also gave it some amount of protection by claiming a connection to an Islamic Sheikh, despite the accusations of paganism directed against them.

In Yazidi tradition, Sheikh Adi eventually transformed into far more than a historical Sufi teacher. Legends accumulated around him, portraying him as a miracle worker who could control nature, heal the sick, defeat demons, and manifest supernatural powers. Some traditions identify him as a manifestation or companion of Melek Taus himself. His tomb in Lalish became the holiest site in Yazidi religion, further more powerfully consecrating the site that was already considered holy. It is shown below.

The nephews and other relatives of Adi would eventually become the ancestors of the Yazidi religious aristocracy - the sheikh and pir families who would claim descent from Adi’s relatives and companions - and thereby possess hereditary religious authority. This establishment of a hereditary religious elite, despite looking unequal, whatever its origins, has proved crucial for Yazidi survival. It created a structure for preserving and transmitting religious knowledge across generations even without written texts, and it provided leadership for the community during times of persecution. Adi’s birthday and death-day are important Yazidi festivals.

The veneration of Sheikh Adi created theological tensions within Yazidism. If Melek Taus was the supreme among the seven divine beings governing creation, where did a 12th-century human being fit in the cosmic hierarchy? Yazidi theology addressed this by distinguishing between Sheikh Adi as a historical person and as a spiritual reality. The physical man who lived in Lalish was a great saint and teacher. But the spiritual reality of Sheikh Adi transcended his historical existence, making him a manifestation of divine wisdom, perhaps an avatar of Melek Taus himself in human form (there are stories where he transforms into a peacock to reveal his connection to Melek Taus). This theological move parallels developments in other religious traditions where founding figures are gradually divinized. Just as Christians transformed Jesus from a Jewish preacher into God incarnate, and Buddhists elevated Buddha from a human teacher to a cosmic Bodhisattva principle, Yazidis transformed Sheikh Adi similarly to accommodate his central importance in their religion. The only difference is that this evolution happened gradually, through oral tradition rather than being mediated by theological councils.

The influence of Sheikh Adi extended beyond theology and also affects Yazidi material culture and daily practice. The distinctive clothing worn by religious figures reflects both Islamic and indigenous influences. The architecture of Yazidi shrines contained both Islamic and pre-islamic elements. Even dietary practices and daily rituals show traces of both ancient Yazidi tradition and modifications introduced subsequently during the period of Sheikh Adi’s influence.

Thus, the formation of modern Yazidism through Sheikh Adi’s influence represents one of the most interesting examples of religious change in history. A Sunni Muslim Sufi sheikh became central to a non-Islamic religion. Islamic concepts and practices were subsumed into an ancient tradition rather than the other way around which is what happens most of the times.

# Persecutions & Genocides

The Yazidi collective civilisational memory remembers around 72 firmans that demanded their annihilation and led to organised campaigns for their extermination that involved murder, enslavement, forced conversion, and cultural destruction. As various Islamic empires arose culminating in the Ottoman Empire, the Yazidis gained more visibility and faced persecutions. The connection to Islam through Sheikh Adi proved a double edged sword because this made them appear as heretics or apostates who had shifted away from orthodox Islam (hence deserving death or forced conversion) rather than non-Muslims (like Jews and Christians) who despite their complaints, at least enjoyed legal recognition as distinct religious communities. The relationship with the Ottoman Empire oscillated between forced conversions and military campaigns. The failure of the Ottoman Empire to stamp out the Yazidi religion completely was more due to the geography and difficult access to mountainous regions where the Yazidis would retreat to, rather than benevolence. Throughout the Ottoman period, attacks on Yazidis happened periodically and targeted their most sacred site at Lalish. But after ever time the shrine at Lalish was destroyed, the resilient Yazidis would return, rebuild, and resume their pilgrimage. The most recent 2014 genocide by ISIS was just a continuation of an already well established phenomenon. Persecution and genocide of the Yazidis occurred from the medieval period onwards although records become very clear from the times of the Ottoman Empire.

One of the most virulent persecutions occurred in 1832 under Ali Rida Pasha, the governor of Baghdad who attacked and burnt Yazidi villages killing thousands of them and enslaving many more. The survivors scattered into the mountains. Another major wave of persecution hit in 1892 under another Ottoman governor Omar Wehbu Pasha who not just killed and enslaved but also destroyed the shrines at Lalish and temporarily converted the shrine to Sheikh Adi into a madrasa and also forced the religious leadership to convert leading many to flee into the mountains. Many died there exposed to winter and starvation and some survived by claiming them to be Muslim while practising their faith secretly until danger passed. This has always been the pattern - attacks would come, survivors would flee and retreat to the mountains and gradually then rebuild after things became manageable again. Their own fellow Kurdish Muslim tribes themselves have organised attacks on Yazidis sometimes at the behest of Ottoman authorities. Kurdish agas (tribal elders) saw Yazidis as a legit target of raids and slaves despite sharing the same language and culture.

After the collapse of the Ottoman Empire, the rise of various nationalist movements in the Middle East vying control destabilised the region and the Yazidis found themselves caught in the tussle between Arab, Kurdish, and Turkish nationalists. The British Mandate period (1920 - 1932) brought some stability but when Iraq gained independence in 1932, Yazidis again became vulnerable to violence for even as frivolous reasons as local disputes over water, land, or livestock. Throughout the 1930s and 1940s, the Yazidis suffered from sporadic persecution and continuous systemic discrimination and abductions of their women.

One particularly painful episode happened in 1969, when the newly installed **Ba’ath Party** launched a campaign to arabize regions of northern Iraq which resulted in Yazidi families being forcibly relocated from their ancestral villages to collective settlements in Arab areas. Their lands were confiscated and given to Arab settlers. This campaign was part of the effort to consolidate government control over oil-rich regions and minority areas, but for Yazidis, it represented yet another attack on their community and religion. The situation in Syria was relatively okay although they remained second class citizens, The anti-religious Soviet period in Armenia and Georgia too saw suppression of religious practise of Yazidis but they were recognised as a distinct nationality and were protected and provided education in their own language.

What made these centuries of persecution even more traumatising for the Yazidi community was that they were unpredictable yet totally catastrophic. The community might live in relative peace for years or even decades, developing normal relationships with neighbours, building homes, and planting orchards. Then, suddenly without warning, a firman would be declared, and everything would be destroyed. Men would be killed, women and children enslaved, and survivors would be forced to start over with nothing.

This made the Yazidis see the outside world with deep hostility and reinforcing the need for strict endogamy for the preservation of boundaries of their community. When faced between the choices of conversion and death, most Yazidis always chose death. Although some who converted under pressure were accepted back into the community after some purification ritual, a Yazidi who willingly converted was considered lost to the community forever.

In the dawn of the new millennium with the twenty first century, there was hope that modernity, democracy, international organisations, consciousness of human rights, and rational world order would lead to protection of all minorities including the Yazidis. But the summer of 2014 marked one of the darkest chapters in Yazidi history where they were mercilessly betrayed by their own ethnic Kurdish **Peshmerga** forces to the ISIS who mercilessly tortured and massacred them. Village by village, ISIS forces separated families and organised people by age and gender. Men and older boys were typically taken to the outskirts of villages or to secret places where they were executed en masse. The fate of Yazidi women and girls was sexual slavery that the ISIS justified through Islamic texts regarding the treatment of non-Muslim captives. Yazidi females, particularly girls and young women, who were considered spoils of war, were transported to facilities in ISIS-controlled cities and were literally sold like commodities to their men with price tags hanging from them. Those who refused were punished or killed. A number of Yazidi women took their own lives to escape the horror of their captivity, jumping from buildings or finding other means to end their suffering. Captured Yazidi boys were indoctrinated and used as human shields or sent to suicide missions.

Sheer devastation only remained when everything settled down after the ISIS was curbed. In a remarkable development, the Yazidi spiritual leadership issued a decree in 2019 accepting back into the community all women and children who had been held by ISIS, regardless of what had happened to them during captivity. However, this decree did not extend to children born of rape during captivity, creating heartbreaking situations where mothers had to choose between their communities and their children.

The Yazidis have thus survived everything history has thrown at them. They have maintained their distinct identity through empire after empire, through countless attempts at their forced conversion or elimination. They have preserved sacred knowledge across centuries when it could not be written down, passing it from generation to generation through memory and ritual. They have rebuilt after each destruction, mourned their dead, and continued to practice their faith. I only hope that they will get through this trauma as well.
