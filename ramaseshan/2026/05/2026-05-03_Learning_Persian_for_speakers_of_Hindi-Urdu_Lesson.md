# The Past Participle : The final á Suffix

In this article, we will continue to explore more tenses and moods. Before we begin, it is important that we learn a construction called the past participle.

Imagine you’re reading a story in English, and you come across phrases like “*the **fallen** leaf*”, “*the **risen** sun*”, “*the **defeated** warrior*”, or “*the **sunken** treasure*”. These phrases paint a picture, don’t they? The words in bold—“*fallen*,” “*risen*,” “*defeated*,” “*sunken*”—are doing something special. They describe a noun (like “*leaf*” or “*warrior*”) by telling us about an action that happened to it in the past, and that action was done to the noun (the noun is the suffering object of the verb), not by the noun (the noun is not the doer subject of the verb). For example, the “defeated” warrior didn’t defeat someone else; the warrior was defeated. That’s what makes these words passive. And since the action (defeating, falling, sinking) happened before the moment we’re talking about, they’re also past. This combination—past and passive—gives us the name **past passive participle** or simply the past participle **(PP)** here (because unlike other languages, Persian does not have any other kind of past participle).

In Persian, the PPP works in a similar way. It’s a word derived from a verb but behaves like an adjective (a describing word). It attaches itself to a noun to tell us something about what happened to it. How to form the PPP in Persian? It is very simple actually. It is based on the infinitive/past tense stem. Take the “n” out of the infinitive ending “an” and you get this participle !! What remains is only a final long “a” and denoted by ه in the Arabic script. To help you distinguish this participle final “a”, I denote it in this article alone by a special accent á. It is illustrated below :

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!eucA!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2da3062f-c225-4e55-ac93-28c3c0175c27_1486x796.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!eucA!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2da3062f-c225-4e55-ac93-28c3c0175c27_1486x796.heic)

These participles now can be used like any other adjectives following the noun like to say phrases like “*the work done*” or “*the welcome guest*” or “*the seen sign*”. The following are some examples using past participles as adjectives.

Below are **10 examples** designed exactly for your audience (Hindi-Urdu speakers).  
I deliberately chose **verbs and nouns familiar from Persian loanwords** in Hindi-Urdu so that learners feel immediate recognition. Before we look at examples however, it is worth noticing a small but important subtlety. Earlier we said that the **e-z̤āfat** usually corresponds to the English word **“of.”** That is still true here. However, when a **past participle** is involved, the meaning of the phrase may appear in English as if the ezāfat meant **“by.”** This can be confusing at first. The reason is that the past participle itself already carries a **passive meaning**. For example, the word *“written”* describes something that has **been written**, not something that performs the action of writing. When we say an e-**z̤āfat** connected phrase in Persian that literally reads like “letter-e written-e friend-e me” (literally: *letter - written of friend of me*), the grammar of Persian is still simply linking words together with **“of.”** But because *written* is passive, the “friend” then also naturally becomes the **agent who performed the action**. English therefore translates the phrase as **“the letter written by my friend.”** In other words, the sense of **“by”** comes from the **passive participle**, not from the **e-z̤āfat** itself.

# Present Active Participle : The “indá” Suffix

Before we proceed to more tenses, it is useful to introduce another construction derived from verbs: the **present participle**. To understand what this means, think of English expressions like *“the **rising** sun,” “the **running** water,” “the **approaching** army,”* or *“the **shining** star.”* The bold words—***rising, running, approaching, shining***—describe a noun by referring to an action that the noun itself is **doing**. The sun is rising, the army is approaching, the star is shining. In other words, unlike the past participle we saw earlier (where the noun *suffers* an action), the present participle describes a noun that is **performing the action itself**. Because the action is seen as ongoing or characteristic, these forms are called **present active participles** or simply **present participles**. Persian also has a present participle that behaves like an adjective in exactly the same way.

For simple verbs, the present participle is formed very simply from the **present stem of the verb** by adding the ending **-indá** (ـنده). Again, to help you distinguish this participle final “a”, I denote it in this article alone by a special accent á. For example, the verb **dīdan** (to see) has the present stem **bīn-**, so the present participle becomes **bīnindá** (seeing). Likewise **kardan** (to do) gives **kunindá** (doing), and **raftan** (to go) gives **rawindá** (going). These participles can then attach to nouns through the **e-z̤āfat** construction just like ordinary adjectives, allowing us to say phrases such as *“the shining sun,” “the approaching army,”* or *“the speaking man.”* In the examples that follow, we will see how this form is used in actual sentences.

Before we look at examples, notice another subtle point about how **e-z̤āfat** works with participles. Earlier we saw that with the **past participle**, ezāfat could connect the participle to the **agent** of its action: for example, *“the letter written by my friend”* could be rendered in Persian as “*letter-e written-e friend-e me*”. With the **present participle**, something slightly different happens. Because the present participle describes a noun that is **performing an action**, the noun following the ezāfat often corresponds to the **object of that action**. For instance, in a phrase like *“mard-e nawīsindá-ye nāma”* (literally: *man of writing of letter*), the participle *nawīsindá* means “writing,” and the word *nāma* is the thing being written—the **object** of the action. Persian grammar is still simply linking words together with **e-z̤āfat**, but the meaning of the participle naturally creates the relationship that English expresses as **“writing a letter.”** In other words, the ezāfat itself still functions as a linker (“of”), while the participle determines whether the following noun is interpreted as the **agent** (with past participles) or the **object** (with present participles).

> ديدن — dīdan — “to see”  
> مردِ بينندَهٔ اين نشانه بسيار دانا است  
> *mard-e **bīnindá**-ye īn nishāna bisyār dānā ast*  
> The man **seeing** this sign is very wise.

> The **burning** fire of this war is very harsh.

This is for simple verbs. How do compound verbs form their present participles? For compound verbs (which make up most Persian verbs), the formation of the present participle works in a slightly different way. As usual, the non-verbal element in front remains unchanged. The participial ending **-indá (ـنده)** is then added to the **light verb**, because that is the part that actually carries the verbal meaning. Thus from *suḥbat kardan* (“to converse”) we obtain *suḥbat-kunindá* (“conversing / one who converses”), and from *tamīr kardan* (“to repair”) we obtain *tamīr-kunindá* (“repairing / one who repairs”). This is the same old method we saw in the previous section. This participle, like any other adjective, can be used as a noun as well and can refer to a person doing that action.

# Compound Verbs & Agent Participles

But, in compounds, however, Persian also contains forms where the **present stem itself attaches directly to the preceding non-verbal part of the compound verb**, producing words such as **dānishjū** (“seeker of knowledge,” hence “student”) from *dānish jūstan* (seek knowledge). These forms mostly tend to become lexicalised nouns and they beautifully illustrate how Persian can combine a noun and a verbal stem into a single descriptive word and form new vocabularies for new concepts as required. Note however that these kind of formations differ from the usually formed participles in the sense that they **can only be used as agent nouns to refer to someone or something doing that action and cannot be used adjectivally like all other participles**.

# Some more Participles

### Participles of Manner

There is one more present participle suffix attached to the present tense stem that is used to convey things in English like “while doing X, …..”. The suffix is **(y)ān**. This tendency is seen more in compound verbs and in many simple verbs. Some examples are given below.

> خندیدن — khandīdan —“to laugh” (regular verb)  
> **خندان** رفت  
> ***khandān** raft*   
> (While) laughing, he left
>
> اُفتادَن (افت) — uftādan (uft) — to fall  
> **افتان** از شرابخانه آمدند***  
> uftān** az sharābkhāna āmadand  
> **Falling**, they came from the bar*
>
> *“yā rabb” **gōyān** bā marg rō bah rō shudand  *
> یا رب” **گویان** با مرگ روبه رو شدند  
> (While) **saying** “O Lord”, they came face-to-face with death (faced
> death).
>
> زدن (زن) — zadan (zan) —“to hit, to strike, to hit string (play an
> instrument)”  
> **نعره** **زنان** حمله کردند***  
> na’ra zanān** hamla kardand*  
> (While) **shouting**, they attacked

# Infinitives and their uses

In Hindi, the nā ending which denotes the infinitive has two uses :

1.  **Purpose** : It is used in place of the English “to” form to denote
    purpose. Eg.   
    मैं **खेलना** चाहता हूँ   
    *maiṃ **khelnā** chāhtā hūṃ*   
    “I want **to play**”

2.  **Verbal Noun** : It is also used as a verbal noun like the -ing
    form in English to describe the action. E.g.   
    **खेलना** स्वास्थ्य के लिए अच्छा है   
    **khelnā** svasth ke liye acchāh hai   
    “**Playing** is good for health”

In Persian however, the infinitive ending -**dan/-tan** that is the dictionary form of the entry of the verb, has only the second of the above two uses which is to serve as a **verbal noun**. The first function which is to denote purpose is taken care in Persian by a special verb form called the **subjunctive mood** verb, about which we will see in the next article (which is why I have striked it out here so that you may skip it freely).

1.  **~~Subjunctive for purpose clauses~~** ~~:  
    من می خواهم بازی کنم  
    man mēkhwāham bāzi kunam  
    “I want to play” (literally : I want \[that\] I might play) → more
    details about this in the next article~~

2.  **Infinitive as verbal noun** :   
    **بازی کردن** برای سلامتی مفید است  
    **bāzi kardan** barāy-e salāmati mufīd ast   
    “**Playing** is good for health”

In Persian, when the suffix “i” added to the infinitive, it functions as a verbal adjective rather than a verbal noun. In this case, if the infinitive comes from a verb X, then its infinitival adjective means something like “***that which must be X-ed***” or “***that which is about to be X-ed***” or “***that which can be X-ed***” or “***X-able***”.

3.  **Infinitival adjective** :   
    در آنجآ چیزی **دیدنی** نیست  
    *dar ānjā chīzi **dīdanī** nēst*  
    There is nothing worth seeing there

# All Active Indicative Tenses

Now, it is time to pick up gears again. We are now ready to learn almost all the tenses of the active voice in Persian verbs. There are eighteen total tenses and moods for Persian verbs in the active voice (active voice means subject is the doer of the verb). Seventeen of these active modes of formation and possible translations are summarised below, out of which we have seen three so far. Do not get scared after seeing them ; they are only for your reference and we will take them slowly one at a time. All of these are based on either the past stem or the present stem. ### Present & Present Continuous Tense

We saw how to form the simple present tense in Persian. Take the present stem, add the mē prefix to the left, and personal endings to the right. In the Classical and Poetic language, turns out that this same form can mean both the simple present and the present continuous tense. Even in the modern language, it can sometimes refer to a continuous action if context demands that. Thus, we have

> ***mēkunam*** **میکنم**  
> **Classical** : I do, I am doing (both translations allowed)  
> **Modern** : I do (only simple present), I am doing (sometimes)

The only other thing to note is that in Classical Persian, a longer version of the ***mē*** prefix which is ***hamē*** is used to emphasise this continuity and repetition (the -ing translation of English). Also, this ***hamē*** is not attached to the verb and is a separate word; and hence, it can sometimes coms after the verb too!!

> **Only Classical :   
> همی کنم** ‌ or **کنم همی**  
> ***hamē kunam*** (or) ***kunam hamē  ***
> Translation : I am doing (mostly) / I do (in a repetitive way - again
> and again)

An example for when this prefix comes after the verb is given below :

>  بوی جوی مولیان **آیاد همی** ؛ یاد یار مهربان **آید همی**   
> *bū-ye jū-ye Mūlyān **āyad hamē** ; yād-e yār-e mehrbān **āyad
> hamē***  
> The scent of the river of Mulyan **is coming** (continually); the
> memory of the beloved friend **is coming** (continuously).  
> - Rudaki

But, modern Persian has another way to form this emphatic continuous tense. It uses the conjugated form of the present tense of the verb of having (which is **dāshtan \[dār\[** ) and places it before the simple present form and thus we have :

> **Only Modern Iranian :  
> دارم میکنم   **
> ***dāram mēkunam***  
> Translation : I am doing (emphatic and right now)

### Past Tenses

#### Unprefixed Past Tense Forms

Now, we will see about types of some past tenses. The simplest un-prefixed simple past tense that we saw so far which involves attachment of personal endings directly to the past tense is actually the starting point.

> Unprefixed Past :   
> **کردم** ***kardam***

In the Classical language, this is the most ambiguous and least informative and can be translated as a simple past (I did) or a continuous past (I was doing). Some examples from the Shāhnāma are given below.

> چون دریا و چون کوه و چون دشت و راغ ؛ زمین شد به کردار روشن چراغ  
> *chū daryā o chūn kōh o chūn dasht o rāgh ;   
> zamīn **shud** bah kirdār-e rōshan chirāgh*   
> Like the sea and like the mountain and the plain and the meadow ;   
> the earth **became/were becoming** illuminated (chirāgh shud) with the
> deed of the luminous one.

#### Gnomic Usage of Past Tense (Universal Tense)

In Classical Persian, this unprefixed past tense is also used to convey universal truths as found a lot in proverbs, for instance. English uses the simple present tense for conveying universal truths like “*Time and tide wait for no man*” but Persian uses this unprefixed past tense to convey the same. This kind of usage is also called technically as the “*gnomic past*” ; no need to remember this term though unless you want to impress scholars! Some examples of the gnomic usages in proverbs are given below :

> Verbs used :   
> **بر خاستن (خیز)** ***bar-khāstan (khēz)*** = disappear (lit : to rise
> up)   
> **نبشتن (نبیس)** ***nabishtan (nabis)*** = write → (archaic of)
> ***nawishtan (nawīs)*** **نوشتن (نویس)**  
> **پیش آمدن (آ)** ***pēsh āmadan*** = to come forth (lit : to come
> front)  
>   
> فرق شاهی و بندگی **برخاست** چون قضای نبشته آمد پیش  
> *farq-e shāhi o bandagi **bar-khāst** chūn qaz̤ā-ye na(b/v)ishtá āmad
> pēsh*   
> The difference between kingship and slavery **disappears** when
> written fate (*qaz̤ā-ye nabishtá*) \[death\] comes forth (Note:
> “disappear” is in the unprefixed past tense form)  
> - Sa’di

Let us now read another proverb. Before reading it, it is wise to note how the Persian word for “wind” which is “**bād**” sounds similar to the Sanskrit “**vāta**” which points to their shared Indo-Iranian inheritance. Note the word for desert “**ṣahrā**” (which is actually Arabic) reminds you of the Sahara desert because that word simply means desert in Arabic. The main verb used in the below proverb is **گذشتن** (**گذر**) **guẓashtan (guẓar)** which means “cross / pass away”. In the proverb, it is used along with the preposition **به** ***bah*** which does not affect the meaning much in the this case. Notice how this verb is conjugated in the unprefixed past tense form in the proverb but translated as a simple present in English (because English expresses universal truth in that tense).

> دوران بقا چو باد صحرا **بگذشت** تلخی و خوشی و زشت و زیبا **بگذشت**   
> *dawrān-e baqā chū bād-e ṣaḥrā **beg(u)ẓasht** ; talkhi o khushi o
> zisht o zēbā **beg(u)żasht***   
> The duration of survival \[life\] **passes away** like a desert wind ;
> bitterness, sweetness, ugly and beautiful \[they all\] **pass away  **
> - Sa’di

Another proverb in a similar spirit is expressed by Khayyam which we will see below. The new verb used here is یاد گشتن (گرد) ***yād*** ***gashtan*** (***gard***) which literally means “turn memories” (actual meaning : *concern oneself with*) as the simple verbal part ***gashtan*** (***gard***) means “turn” which we saw already. This verb here in this proverb below is used in the un-prefixed past tense because the poet says he does this always.

> این یک دو سه روز نوبت عمر گذشت ؛ چون آب به جویبار و چون باد به دشت ؛
>   
> هرگز غم دو روز مرا **یاد** **نگشت** ؛ روز کی نیامدست و روز کی گذشت   
> *īn yek-dō-se rōz-e nawbat-e ‘umr g(u)ẓasht ;   
> chūn āb bah jōybār o chūn bād bah dasht ;  
> hargiz gham-e do rōz ma(n) rā **yād** na-**gasht** ;   
> rōz-e ki na(y)-āmadast o rōz-e ki guẓasht*  
> This one, two, \[or\] three-day period of life passes away like water
> in a stream and like wind on the plain.   
> Always, \[as\] for me, **I don’t concern** \[myself\] **with** the
> worry of two days ; \[of\] the day that has not \[yet\] come and
> \]of\] the day that has passed.   
> - Khayyam

#### Past Habitual Tense

To convey a past action that was habitual and used to occur often over a period of time, English uses the phrasing “*used to*” as in sentences like “*He used to do this*”. In Persian, this habitual past tense was conveyed by attaching a vowel “ï” to this un-prefixed simple past tense form. This is called the past habitual tense. To distinguish this habitual marker from other final “i” markers, I will denote this by ï.

> پرستنده ای ک ش به بر داشتی ؛ زمین را به پی هیچ نگذاشتی   
> *parastindá-ī k(i)-ash bah bar dāshtī ; zamīn rā bah pēy hēch
> na-g(u)żāshtï*  
> A servant who always had him \[direct object\] in \[his\] embrace ; he
> **used to** never **allow** the ground \[to be touched\] by \[his\]
> feet
>
> Verb : **گذاشتن (گذار)** ***guẓāshtan (guẓār)*** “to allow, to permit”
> (think of the Hindi-Urdu word *guzārish*)

Note a possible source of confusion here. The word ***g(u)żāshtï*** translates as “used to allow” when the final *ï* is understood as the habitual ending attached to the third person singular unsuffixed past tense form ***g(u)żāsht***. But, when the final “i” is understood as the second person singular ending “ī” attached to the past stem ***g(u)żāsht***, then it means “you allowed”. Again, context and practice will help you get comfortable in recognising the correct intended meaning. The forms of this archaic past habitual tense are given below.

#### Archaic Simple Past

We saw that the un-prefixed past tense form is ambiguous and least informative of the past tense. Now, we will see what kinds of prefixes can be added to it that give more information. To emphasise that the past tense is simple and not continuous ( did ~~was doing~~), Classical Persian uses the prefix “**bi**” to be added to the past tense which tells us that the past action happened only once and is not continuous. An example of this was seen just now in the above passage which is repeated below.

> Verb : **شکستن (شکن)** ***shikastan (shikan)*** “to smash” →
> “*Butshikan*” (idol smasher!!)  
> کسان که در رمضان چنگ میشکستندی ؛ نسیم گل **بشنیدند** و توبه
> **بشکستند**   
> *kisān ki dar ramaz̤ān chang mēshikastandï ;   
> nasīm-e gul **bishunīdand**-o tawbi* **bish(i)kastand**   
> People who, during Ramzan, would have been smashing harps, suddenly
> **heard** \[idiomatically : smelled\] (for once) the breeze of rose
> (rose-scented breeze) and **broke** (for once) their vow.   
> - Sa’di

Here, the “bi” prefix tells us that while the action of breaking harps was a repetitive process (fanatics used to break harps repeatedly every Ramzan out of habit), the hearing of \[or smelling of\] a rose-scented breeze happened only once and the breaking of their vows also happened once and for all. Other examples of this usage are given below.

> این **بگفت** و **برفت**  
> *īn **biguft** o **biraft***  
> He **said** this (once) and **left** (once and for all).
>
> Verb : سوختن (سوز) ***sōkhtan (sōz)*** “to burn”   
> دلم **بسوخت**  
> *dilam **bisōkht***  
> My heart burned (once)
>
> پدر **بخندید** و ارکان دولت **بپسندیدند** و برادران به جان
> **برنجیدند**   
> *pidar **bikhandīd** o arkān-e dawlat **bipasandīdand** o birādarān
> bah jān **biranjīdand*****  **
> The father **laughed** (once) and the minsters of government
> **approved of it** (once) and the brothers **got** mortally
> **offended** (idiom for literally : got offended with life)  
> - Sa’di in “Gulistan”
>
> به یزدان **بنالید** ک(ی) : ای کردگار ؛ بدین کار این بنده را پاس
> دار**  **
> *bah yazdān **binālīd** k(i) “ay kirdgār ; bah (d)īn kār īn bandá rā
> pās dār”***  **
> To God, he **cried out** (once) that : O maker, protect (literally :
> have near \[you\]) this servant in this endeavour
>
> آن پیر لاشه را که سپردند زیر گل ؛ خاکش چنان بخرد ک(ی) از او استخوان
> نماند   
> *ān pīr lāsha rā ki s(i)purdand zēr-e gil ;   
> khāk-ash chunān **bikhward** ki az ō ustukhwān namānd*   
> That old corpse which they entrusted to the bottom of the soil,
> **was** so **eaten up** (once) that, from him, no bone remained.   
>  - Sa’di in “Gulistan”

#### Past Continuous Tense using “mē”

Classical Persian uses the un-prefixed past tense form ambiguously and uses the prefix “bi” to convey explicitly the simple un-repetitive action of the action. Modern Persian does not do have the “bi” prefix and reserves the un-prefixed past tense form for the simple past tense meaning exclusively. To explicitly convey continuous and repetitive action, both Classical and Modern Persian uses the already familiar prefix “***(ha)mē***” (but this time attached to the past tense form) where the longer form of the prefix “***hamē***” is found exclusively in the classical language while the modern language uses only “***mē***”.

> همی بود بوس و کنار و نبید   
> ***hamē** **būd** bōs o kinār o nabīd*  
> There **was** (**continual**) kissing, embrace, and wine.   
> - Firdausi
>
> چشمانش همچنان در چشمخانه همی گردید  
> *chashm-ān-ash hamchunān dar chashm-khāna **hamē** **gardīd***   
> (His) eyes **were** still **turning** (continually) in their sockets.
>   
> - Sa’di
>
> از او اندر همی پرورش (بود) ؛ که پوشیدنی نو بد (بود) و نو خورش  
> *az ō andar **hamē** parvarsh (**būd**) ; ki pōshīdani naw **būd** o
> naw khwarish*   
> From him inside, there was (**continuously**) nourishment ; so that
> clothing was new and food was new   
> - Shahnama

Thus, we have now seen the unprefixed, bi-prefixed, (ha)mē-prefixed, and ï-suffixed past tense forms that are summarised below.

> Unprefixed → Ambiguous   
> bi-prefixed → Simple / One-time occurrence  
> (ha)mē-prefixed → Continuous   
> ï-suffixed → Habitual

### Perfect Tenses from Past Participles

Now we will see tenses that are formed using the past participle as the base. If you have by now forgotten what past participles are and how they are used, I urge you to scroll up and revise them once more. The tenses formed using this past participle are called “perfect” tenses in general. Let us explore their formation one by one. Note that as the past participles are also formed from the past stem, these perfect tenses are also formed from the past tense stem of the verb like all other past tenses.

#### Present Perfect Tense

The present perfect tense is formed by adding the suffix forms of the verb “ast” to the past participles. They are conjugated for the verb “raftam” (to go) below. They are translated by the English “has/have”.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!1QuG!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1a09a939-8df7-4dbb-bb7d-80bea3f9a026_1498x460.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!1QuG!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1a09a939-8df7-4dbb-bb7d-80bea3f9a026_1498x460.heic)

So, if you translate a present perfect tense form like “*raftá-**ast”*** literally (***raftá*** = gone, ***ast*** = is) , you would get “*he is gone*” and the correct English translation is not that far and is actually “*he has gone*”. English grammar books say the present perfect tense is used for a past action that has been completed but is of still relevance to the present. This thumb rule works most of the time but English is not consistent in following this rule. If the action happened really long time ago, English does not use the present perfect tense even if the relevance to the present is emphasised. For example, if I am talking about a king named Shah Abbas who lived centuries ago, and I want to emphasise how important he is to all of us right now, I would still say in English “Shah Abbas was a great king” (past tense) and not “~~Shah Abbas has been a great king~~”. But Persian is very consistent regarding following this rule and would use the present perfect tense and say “شاه عباس پادشاه بزرگی **بوده است**” “*Shāh Abbās pādshāh-e buzorg-ī **būdá ast***”. If I am telling to the modern people in the city of Ghazna that Al-Beruni died there, English would still use the simple past tense (“Al-Biruni died in Ghazna”) even though I am explicitly emphasising the relevance to the people of Ghazna that it is a relevant thing now and a pride for them. But again, Persian being consistent, would put this in the present perfect and say “بیرونی در غازنه **مرده است**” “*Bērūni dar ghazna **murdá ast***”. English is shy to follow the rule of using present perfect for currently relevant past events when the past is very distant, but Persian does not, and follows it consistently. So, while it is good to learn the present perfect in Persian as equivalent to English present perfect (has/had), it does not always correspond one-to-one and for distant past events, you might want to translate the present perfect in Persian by a simple past tense in English, despite being relevant now.

#### Present Perfect Continuous Tense

Consistent with the same rule, the present perfect continuous tense is used for past completed events that were continuous or repetitive but which are relevant to the present. One simply adds the continuous prefix ***(ha)mē*** to the present perfect tense to get the present perfect continuous tense. For example :

> ابن سنا در اصفهان زندگی میکرده است   
> ibn Sinnā dar isfahān **zindagī mēkardá ast**   
> Avicenna **used to live** in Isfahan (and it is relevant now).

This is not the same as the English’s present perfect continuous tense which is used for action that started in the past and continue into the present (e.g. I have been doing this).

#### Past Perfect Tense

The past perfect tense in Persian generally corresponds to the past perfect tense in English. This is also called the pluperfect tense and is also called ‘past of past’ tense by student learning grammar. When there is a main narrative that is going on in some past time frame, the past perfect is used to refer to some event that happened before this narrative past time frame. This is how the English past perfect / pluperfect is used as well, with the auxiliary “had”. When you want to refer to a more remote past action that has already been completed relative to the main narrative past tense, one uses the pluperfect or past perfect tense. English uses the word “already” sometimes to emphasise this in sentences like “By the time they arrived, he had (already) left”. Note that even without the word “already” , it is understood that the leaving happened before the arrival which is only emphasised by the word “already”. Persian also functions the same way with this tense.

How to form this past perfect tense? It is simple. Take the past participle and add after it, the conjugated simple past of “būdan”. It is illustrated below.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Eqxn!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F24b3e729-96a4-4cc6-86d2-94a4b0924715_1068x522.heic)

If past perfect tense of other verbs are formed using the help of ***būdan***, what is the past perfect of ***būdan*** itself? Turns out that the verb that assists all other verbs in forming their past perfect, does not have a past perfect for itself! So, seeing only a conjugated form of ***būdan***, you can either translate it as “was” or “had been” in English. Some examples to illustrate this are given below :

> قبل از آن او را **دیده بودیم**   
> *qabl az ān, ō rā **dīdá būdēm***   
> Before that, **we had already seen** him
>
> پیش از آن وقت **برگشته بودند**  
> pēsh az ān vaqt, **bargashtá būdand**  
> Before that time, **they had already returned**
>
> دیروز شما او را دیدید ولی ما پریروز هم او را **دیده بودیم**  
> *dīrōz shumā ō rā dīdēd, vali mā parīrōz ham ō rā **dīdá būdēm***  
> Yesterday you saw her, but **we had** already **seen** her the day
> before
>
> شما به آمریکا برگشته بودید که من به ایرام رفتم  
> *shumā bah amrīkā **bar-gashtá būdēd** ki man bah irān raftam*  
> You **had (already) returned** to America when I went to Iran

#### Past Perfect Narrative

If pluperfect or past perfect is the past tense of a past tense (past relative to another past), then past perfect narrative is one step further and is used for extremely remote actions, relative to the main past tense. This past tense is not encountered often in Persian (both modern and classic) and hence you may feel free to skip it on first reading if you wish to.   
  
It is formed by the past participle of the verb followed by the present perfect of “***būdan***”. Since it is not that frequent, I give only one example.

> در آن وقت به سی سالگی نرسیده بوده است   
> dar ān vaqt, bah sa sī sālagī na **rasīdá būdá ast**  
> At that time, **he had** not **reached** the age of thirty.

#### Archaic Past Narrative

This is the last tense that is formed from the past participle. It is formed by adding the conjugation of “***ast***” to the past participle after it is merged to the word “-ast”. An optional habitual suffix ï is also possible here. These tense forms do not occur in Modern Persian but are seen with some frequency in poetry. In the process of assimilation with “***ast***”, the ***á*** disappears. The forms of this tense for the verb افتادن (افت) ***uftādan (uft)*** “to fall” is illustrated below. This has no exact English translation but should just be learned as another type of past tense for the main narrative.

> کاشکی آن شب و آن روز که ترسیدم از آن ؛ نفتادستی و شادی نشدستی تیمار   
> *kāsh ki ān shab o ān rōz ki tarsīdam az ān ; **na(u)ftādastï** o

> Would that the night and day I feared had not **befallen** and joy had
> not **become** grief!

With this, we have completed all the fourteen tenses (past, present, and future) that tells us something factual that happened, happens, or is expected to happen. Such forms are called **indicative** tenses. But, how to convey hypothetical scenarios (e.g. it would have happened) or commands (do it!!) or conditionals (if it happens / as long as it happens) or hopes (may it happen)?? For that, we have the four remaining Persian verb forms that we will learn in the next article !

After all, in your mother tongue or in English, the same tense forms have entered your instinct (think of forms like and you know, use them casualty in your every day speech (for e.g, think of the myriads of Hindi forms like *kiyā hai, kiyā thā, kiyā hogā, kartā hai, karnevālā hai, karegā, kar diyā, kar liyā, kartā rahtā hai, karnā, karnā hogā, kar rahā hai, kar rahā thā, kartā thā*, etc..!). You are comfortable in instinctively understanding and using all of them! The same thing soon will hap in your Persian expression as well as you practice your Persian more.
