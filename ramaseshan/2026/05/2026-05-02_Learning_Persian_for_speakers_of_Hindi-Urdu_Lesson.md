# The Paradigm of Regular Verbs

Before going deep into Persian verbs, let us start with conjugating a simple verb that means “to understand” in both the present and past tense.

What do you observe? First, we see that we see some small endings that are shaded blue. All of these endings, except for the third person singular, are the same in both tables and coincide with the endings of the conjugation of “**ast**”. This conjugation is recollected below for your convenience.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!YB01!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5a38f7a4-447a-4d07-b597-21d3843922a0_1498x710.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!YB01!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5a38f7a4-447a-4d07-b597-21d3843922a0_1498x710.heic)

So, in the present tense, one sees the core part of the verb in yellow. A prefix “mē” shaded in red is added to the left of the stem of the verb. And then, to the right, one adds personal endings that are shaded in blue. Except for the third person singular, these blue personal endings coincide with the endings of “ast”.

What happens in the past tense? The prefix “mē” its gone but now there is an extra green-shaded part containing the “īd” added after the core yellow part of the verb. But after that, the same personal endings of the present tense continue as final suffixes. The personal endings of the past tense are same as that of the present tense except for the third person singular where the past tense does not have any ending and the present tense has “ad” ending. This is the paradigm for any regular Persian verb.

What happens in the “to ..” form called the infinitive? We just add an ending “an” to the past tense stem, instead of the other blue personal endings.

# Stem Changes from Present to Past

Life would be nice if all verbs were regular and followed easy rules, right? But alas, that is not the case in any language. Even in English, we see that most normal verbs form their past tenses by adding an extra “**d**” or “**ed**” or “**t**” to the present stem (e.g. sleep → slep**t**, move → move**d**, log → logg**ed**). But, there are very frequently occurring irregular verbs that change their internal structure while going from the present to past tense (e.g. do → did, go → went, stand → stood). The same thing happens in Persian. But, note that, for all verbs, the following rules do hold :

- The personal endings (shaded in blue) stays the same for all verbs,
  however irregular they may be otherwise.

- The orange prefix in the present tense “mē” also stays the same in the
  present tense of all verbs, however irregular they may be otherwise.

- There is an extra “d” or “īd” or “t” in the past tense stem (shaded
  green). While regular verbs only have extra “īd”, irregular verbs may
  have either “d” or “t”. Note that the possibilities of the past tense
  marker in Persian “d” or “īd” or “t” neatly somewhat parallel the “d”,
  “ed” and “t” for English past tense suffixes ; a happy coincidence !

- The only place where irregularity strikes hard is that there is an
  additional internal change in the stem of the verb (yellow part)
  between the present and past tense. In such cases, one has to learn
  the past tense and present stem stems separately.

In a dictionary, how are irregular Persian verbs quoted? Normally, the infinitive “to” form is quoted. From this, one can remove the “an” and get the past stem to which personal endings can be added to conjugate in the past tense. But, for irregular verbs this is not enough. There are stem changes in the yellow part of the verb. So, in dictionaries, the present stem is given in the brackets for irregular verbs. If the verb is regular, one needs to just simply remove the “īdan/dan/tan” in the infinitive to get the present stem. After which, the prefix “mē” and the personal endings are added to get the forms of the present tense. But, for irregular verbs, the yellow part has to be learnt separately, and this is what is quoted in brackets in the present stem.

# Compound Verbs

Okay, let me tell you a secret now that no Persian wants you to know !! Persian does not have that many verbs! How can that be the case? There are lots of actions possible, right? Thing is, variety in actions do not always translate to variety in action words (verbs) in Persian. For example, instead of travelling, one can say “making journey” where in one recycles the verb for “make” and use a noun “journey” to give the action that we want. In this sense, Persian is very weird language. It hardly has verbs at all. Most verbs in the language are basically combinations of nouns plus other elementary verbs (like become, do, go, etc...). So, this is why the verbs that you have learnt in the last section are actually super important. Why this lack of verbs? I conjecture it is because of Arabic influence, and when Arabic words infiltrated the language, they preferred to sit in Persian as nouns rather than verbs**.** But this thing also diffuses over to Persian nouns as well. Note below some very common verbs that would be simple and single verbs in many languages are compounds in Persian. For example, you don't travel in Persian, you make a journey! You do not like something in Persian, but you have it as a friend!

> راما چای **دوست می دارد**  
> Rāma chāy **dōst mēdārad  **
> Rama **likes** coffee  
> (Lit) Rama **has** coffee (as a) **friend**

This sentence looks very weird when it is literally translated in English but this is the normal way of saying “likes” in Persian and there is no verb that directly means liking. Not all compound verbs are this indirect but this is the kind of logic when it comes to Persian compound verbs. The same thing happens with Urdu also, which uses Arabic verbal nouns with simple Urdu verbs.

Let me illustrate Persian compounds with more examples. For convenience, I have given the literal meaning of each compound verb so that you can see how it connects to the actual meaning.

# Future Tense formation

How to form the future tense?? This is very simple. In Persian, to form the future tense, one does the following.

- Conjugate the verb ***khwāstan*** (to want) in the present tense
  without the “mē” prefix

- Take the past tense stem of the verb for which the future is required

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!lJUM!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F786ffb4e-d80c-4177-a6ae-9ad61baae289_1390x746.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!lJUM!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F786ffb4e-d80c-4177-a6ae-9ad61baae289_1390x746.heic)

If you find this surprising, English’s future tense formation also works similarly. The word “will” used in future tense means “wanting” when used as a noun ! Think of the proverb “*Where there is a will, there is a way*”. There, the word “will” is a noun that means “desire” or “wanting”. So, “*I will go*” literally means “*I want go*” in English as well. Just that, in English, one only rarely uses the word “will” as a noun meaning wanting nowadays.

# The Evolution of the Marker Rā : Direct & Indirect Objects

Now, we will learn about a small word **را rā**. In Classical and Poetic Persian (poetic language preserves archaisms a lot), this was a postposition (inserted after a word) to signify an indirect object and translated into English as “for” or “to”, and sometimes also by “of”. It signified reception or beneficiary, kind of like the Hindi postpositions **kī** and **kō**.

> *این مژده ما **را** نیست  
> īn muzhda mā-**rā** nēst*  
> This news is not **for** me  
> - Sa’di
>
> پادشاهی **را** شنیدم  
> *pādshāhī **rā** shunīdam*   
> I heard **of** a king  
> - Sa’di
>
> حوران بهشتی **را** دوزخ بود اعراف   
> *hūrān-e bihishtī **rā** dōzakh buwad a’rāf*  
> **For** the heavenly (bihishti) hoors, purgatory (a’raaf) would be
> hell (dozakh)  
> -Sa’di
>
> شتران بود ما **را**  
> *shuturān būd mā-**rā*** (Note : shutur-ān is the plural form of
> shutur “camel”)  
> There was **to** me, camels  
> (Natural English : I had camels)  
> - Jami
>
> هیچ راهی نیست کو **را** نیست پایان   
> *he̅ch ra̅h-ī nēst k(i) ō-**rā** nēst pāyān*   
> There is no road **for** which there is no end (paayaan)   
> (Natural English : There is no road which has no end)  
> - Hafiz
>
> وی **را** بنده ای بود فتح نام  
> *vay **rā** banda-ī būd Fath nām  *
> To him, there was a slave named Fath  
> (Natural English : He had a slave named Fath)  
> - Qabusnama

But at the time when Persian literature was undergoing a renaissance (after centuries of eclipsing by Arabic), this word **rā** also was undergoing a change in meaning. In usage, it was changing from the indirect object marker (to/for) above to a definite direct object marker (the - object). What is a direct object, first?

All verbs have a subject doing them. But some verbs also have a recipient that suffers the action of the verb. For example, if I say that someone killed, then it is natural to ask “whom did he kill?”. If I say that someone hit, then it is natural to ask “whom/what did he hit?”. If I say that someone helped or ate, it is natural to ask back “whom did he help?” or “what did he eat?”. Such verbs that require one to ask “whom did he X?” are called **transitive verbs** and the nouns that suffer the action of the verb are called **direct objects** in grammar. Not all verbs are transitive though ; for example, if I say “he laughed”, you would not ask “whom/what did he laugh?”. So, that clears up what is the direct object. English conveys what is direct object and what is subject by word order alone. If I say “Rama kills Ravana”, I know that Rama is the subject and Ravana is the direct object of the killing simply because Rama comes before the verb and Ravana comes after the verb in the sentence.

For the most part, English does not use a special ending for a direct object of a verb. But, the Persian marker **rā را** eventually came to denote a **definite direct object**. We already saw what is the direct object before ; it is the one on which the action is done. It becomes definite when the direct object is not something arbitrary but already known to the speaker and listener. English marks definiteness by the article “the”. So, whenever you see an object of the verb being marked by “the” in English, it becomes a definite direct object. For example :

> He killed **a man**   
> a man : indefinite direct object
>
> He killed **the man**   
> the man : definite direct object

In the new usage, Persian came to use the marker **rā را** as a definite article for direct objects of verbs. For example, the above sentences would be

> He killed a man  
> او مرد کشت   
> ō mard kusht
>
> He killed **the** man   
> او مرد **را** کشت  
> ō mard-**rā** kusht
>
> Dictionary entry of the verb : کشت (کش) kushtan (kush) = to kill

So, during the time of the rise of Persian literature, the marker rā in Persian was undergoing a change in function ; turning from an indirect object marker (for/to/of) to a definite direct object marker (the - object). The indirect object marker function was already felt to be archaic by the time of the emergence of the poets and hence fell out of usage after the 10th century or so, but since poetry continued to retain these archaic usages, this archaic function remained alongside the new usage. Since Indian Persian writing was not that much influenced by Iran, and since it also adhered to the classical norms more strictly, we see more of the indirect object marker function for the most part.

Last time, we saw some suffixes used to convey possession by pronouns. They are recollected below.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!_SCc!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F44c0f106-3d4c-4239-a68a-10279f9922a5_1494x376.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!_SCc!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F44c0f106-3d4c-4239-a68a-10279f9922a5_1494x376.heic)

Turns out that these suffixes can be attached to verbs as well, after the personal endings (shaded blue) have been added. When adding these suffixes to verbs, again the function can mean two things :

1.  **(Archaic) - Indirect Object Marker** : It can be translated into
    English by the preposition “to/for/of” ; same as the archaic
    function of the marker rā.

2.  **(Modern) - Definite Direct Object Marker** : It can simply be
    translated as the definite direct object. Since pronouns are always
    definite in meaning (one always assumes that when he uses the word
    “he”, the listener understands who is exactly being referred to),
    even though pronouns do not have definite articles in English (“the
    him” does not make sense), the modern function of the pronominal
    suffix on verbs corresponds to the modern function of the marker rā.

But note that by the time of classical poetry, the archaic usage was in flux, and was slowly fading away to give way to the new meaning. So, do not be surprised if you find the new modern usage (definite direct object) even in classical poetry. As a guiding rule, translate the rā particle or the verbal pronominal suffixes by an indirect object for the most part. But, if it sounds awkward, then try the modern definite direct object translation. Here is an example from the Shāhnāma where it only makes sense when we translate the rā particle and the pronominal suffix, in the modern way as a definite direct object.

> (ی)پرستنده ای ک **ش** به بر داشتی ؛ زمین **را** به پی هیچ نگذاشت   
> *parastindá-ī **k(i)**-**ash** bah bar dāshtī ; zamīn **rā** bah pēy
> hēch na-g(u)żāsht(i)*  
> A servant **who** always had **him** \[direct object\] in \[his\]
> embrace ; he used to never allow **the** ground \[to be touched\] by
> \[his\] feet
>
> Verb : **گذاشتن (گذار)** ***guẓāshtan (guẓār)*** “to allow, to permit”
> (think of the Hindi-Urdu word *guzārish*)  
> Note : Ignore the final “i” in *g(u)żāsht(i)* ; will explain it more
> in the next article

# Examples & Exercises

Following are some examples to illustrate whatever concepts we have learnt

>  دوست داشتن ***dōst dāshtan*** (to love / like)  
> **من این کتاب را بسیار دوست دارم.**  
> *man īn kitāb-rā bisyār dōst dāram  *
> I like this book very much.
>
> حرف زدن ***ḥarf zadan*** (to speak)  
> **او در بازار با دوست خود دربارهٔ سیاست حرف زد.**  
> ō *dar bāzār bā dōst-e khud dar-bāra-ye siyāsat harf zad  *
> He spoke about politics with his friend in the market.
>
> تصمیم گرفتن ***taṣmīm giraftan*** (to decide) \[lit : take decision\]
>   
> **بعد از فکر بسیار، او تصمیم گرفت که به هند سفر کند.**  
> *baʿd az fikr-e bisyār, ō taṣmīm giraft ki be Hind safar bikonad  *
> After much thought, he decided to travel to India.
>
> سؤال کردن ***sawāl kardan*** (to ask)  
> **دانشجو از استاد دربارهٔ این شعر سؤال کرد.**  
> dānishjū *az ustād dar-bāra-ye īn sheʿr soʾāl kard  *
> The student asked the teacher about this poem.
>
> جواب دادن ***jawāb dādan*** (to answer)  
> **استاد به سؤال دانشجو با صبر بسیار جواب داد.**  
> *ustād bah sawʾāl-e dānishjū bā ṣabr-e bisyār jawāb dād  *
> The teacher answered the student’s question with great patience.
>
> خبر دادن ***khabr dādan*** (to inform) \[lit : give news\]  
> **دوستم خبر داد که فردا جشن بزرگی در شهر خواهد بود.**  
> *dōstam khabar dād ki fardā jashn-e buzorgī dar shahr khwāhad būd  *
> My friend informed that tomorrow there will be a big celebration in
> the city.
>
> فکر کردن ***fikr kardan*** (to think)  
> **او همیشه دربارهٔ آیندهٔ دنیا فکر می‌کند.**  
> ō *hamēsha dar-bāra-ye āyande-ye dunyā fikr mēkunad*  
> He always thinks about the future of the world.
>
> کمک کردن ***kumak kardan*** (to help)  
> **مردی در بازار به پیرزن فقیر کمک کرد.**  
> *mardī dar bāzār be pīr-zan-e faqīr kumak kard*  
> A man helped the poor old woman in the market.
>
> نگاه کردن ***nigāh kardan*** (to look)  
> **کودک با شگفتی به آسمان پرستاره نگاه کرد.**  
> *kūdak bā shigiftī bah āsmān-e pur-setāra nigāh kard  *
> The child looked at the starry sky with wonder.
>
> یاد گرفتن ***yād giraftan*** (to learn) \[lit : take {into} memory\]  
> **او زبان فارسی را در دانشگاه یاد گرفت.**  
> ō *zabān-e fārsī-rā dar dānishgāh yād giraft  *
> He learned Persian at the university.
>
> یاد دادن yād dādan (to teach) \[lit : to give memory\]  
> **استاد شعر حافظ را به دانشجوان یاد داد.**  
> *ustād sheʿr-e Ḥāfeẓ-rā be dānishjūān yād dād  *
> The teacher taught Hafez’s poetry to the students.
>
> پیدا کردن paydā kardan (to find) \[lit : to make something found\]  
> **او پس از جستجوی بسیار کتاب گمشده را پیدا کرد.**  
> ō *pas az justo-jū-ye bisyār kitāb-e gumshuda-rā paydā kard  *
> After much searching, he found the lost book.
>
> شروع کردن shurū kardan (to begin) \[lit : make beginning\]  
> **باران شدید ناگهان شروع کرد.**  
> *bārān-e shadīd nāgahān shurūʿ kard  *
> The heavy rain suddenly began.
>
> تمام کردن tamān kardan (to finish) \[lit : to make completion\]  
> **او کار دشوار خود را پیش از غروب تمام کرد.**  
> ō *kār-e dushwār-e khud-rā pēsh az ghurūb tamām kard  *
> He finished his difficult work before sunset.
>
> باور کردن bāvar kardan (to believe) \[lit : to do trusting\]  
> **من این داستان عجیب را به آسانی باور نکردم.**  
> *man īn dāstān-e ʿajīb-rā bah āsānī bāvar nakardam  *
> I did not easily believe this strange story.
>
> گریه کردن girya kardan (to cry) \[lit : to do crying\]  
> **کودک پس از شنیدن این خبر غم‌انگیز گریه کرد.**  
> *kūdak pas az shunīdan-e īn khabar-e gham-angīz girya kard  *
> The child cried after hearing this sad news.
>
> 17. خنده کردن ***khanda kardan*** (laugh) \[lit : to make laughter\]  
> **دوستان در باغ به حکایتش خنده کردند.**  
> *dōstān dar bāgh bah ḥikāyat-esh khanda kardand  *
> The friends laughed at his story in the garden.
>
> دیدار کردن ***dīdār kardan*** (to visit)  
> **او پس از سال‌ها مادر خود را دیدار کرد.**  
> ō *pas az sālhā mādar-e khud-rā dīdār kard  *
> After years, he visited his mother.
>
> دعا کردن ***daʿā kardan*** (to pray)  
> **پیرمرد در مسجد برای صلح دنیا دعا کرد.**  
> *pīr-mard dar masjid barā-ye ṣolḥ-e dunyā daʿā kard  *
> The old man prayed for peace in the world in the mosque.
>
> قبول کردن qubūl kardan (to accept)  
> **او نصیحت پدر دانای خود را با دل قبول کرد.**  
> ō *naṣīḥat-e pidar-e dānā-ye khud-rā bā dil qabūl kard  *
> He accepted the advice of his own wise father wholeheartedly \[lit :
> with heart\].

Translate the following from English to Persian as an exercise :

**Simple Sentences**

1.  The teacher asked the student a difficult question.

2.  My brother made an important decision yesterday.

3.  The child looked at the moon with great wonder.

4.  The old man gave good advice to his friend.

5.  The woman helped the poor child in the street.

6.  The king believed the words of the wise minister.

7.  The students began their lesson early in the morning.

8.  The traveler finished his journey in the evening.

**Complex Sentences**

9.  The young man asked the merchant about the price of the book in the
    market.

10. The student thought about the meaning of the poem all night.

11. The imam prayed in the mosque for peace in the world.

12. The child learned a beautiful story from his grandmother.

13. The wise teacher taught the students the secrets of language.

14. The traveler found a small village near the river.

15. The king sent news to the people of the city.

**Slightly More Complex Sentences**

16. The poet spoke about love and sorrow in the garden.

17. The merchant accepted the advice of his old friend.

18. The student did not understand the difficult answer of the teacher.

19. The traveler suddenly saw a strange light in the desert.

20. The children laughed loudly at the funny story.

✔ **Tip:** When translating, try to think in terms of **Persian compound verbs** such as:

- decision **take** (تصمیم گرفتن)

- question **do** (سؤال کردن)

- answer **give** (جواب دادن)

- help **do** (کمک کردن)

- prayer **do** (دعا کردن)

- look **do** (نگاه کردن)

- thought **do** (فکر کردن)
