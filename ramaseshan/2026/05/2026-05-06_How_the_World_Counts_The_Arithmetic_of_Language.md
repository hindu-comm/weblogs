# Quirks of French Numerals

It all began when I recently started learning the French language from about a month ago after enrolling in a course from the famous institute [Alliance francaise de Madras](https://madras.afindia.org/) in Chennai. One of the first things we were taught in the first few days of French class was to count in French from 1 to 100. I already knew quite a bit of Latin and French is derived from Latin ; how hard can it be, right? Or so, I thought, until it made me crazy and slide into a rabbithole of comparative numerical vocabulary across languages.

### Counting from 1-10 in French

It was not that hard to learn the words for numerals 1-10 in French. Anyone who has took science in high school can immediately recognise them.

### Counting from 11-20 in French

Upto this, the numerals in French are easy and straightforward, right?? They are just little phonetic modifications of the Latin words. The words for each numeral are unique. As we move from 11-20, things get a little complicated in Latin, and much more so in French. Let us first give the numerals in both the languages below. Where French deviates from Latin, the result is shaded in yellow.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!L7q3!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F887097d4-ed80-4703-8199-273ca2ddfde8_1314x1132.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!L7q3!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F887097d4-ed80-4703-8199-273ca2ddfde8_1314x1132.heic)

Note : The hyphenation is purely for your convenience to dissect the etymology. Most numbers quoted here are not written with hyphens and of course there are no hyphens when you speak and hear the words

Things are straightforward in Latin from 11 to 17. The words for these numerals are formed by combining the numeral for 1-10 (which is simplified sometimes) with the numeral for 10. For example, the Latin word for 5 is ***quinque*** and the word for 15 is *quin-decim* which is formed by combining the word ***quin*** (5) and ***decim*** (10). The etymology behind these words reflect the arithmetic logic behind them. For example :

*Latin* : **quinque** (5) + **decem** (10) = **quin**-**decim** (15)

This is the logic for all numbers from 11 to 17. But something funny happens for the numbers 18 and 19. The Latin word “***de***” literally means “*from*”. The words for 18 and 19 in Latin are formed by conveying that they are obtained from 20 by subtracting 2 and 1 respectively.

*Latin*:

**viginti** = 20

**un**-**de**-**viginti** (*lit* : 1 from 20) = 19

**duo**-**de**-**viginti** (*lit* : 2 from 20) = 18

This happens just for the number 19 in Sanskrit and Hindi too (but not for eighteen)

*Sanskrit* :

**viṃśati** = 20

**eka-ūna-viṃśati** (*lit* : 1 deficient 20) = 19

*Hindi* :

**bīs** = 20

**unnīs** = 19

Let us come back to French again. It follows the Latinate pattern from 11 to 16. Pasting the same image again for your convenience.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3Rqm!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6246cc4a-9780-4d5b-bfe4-317f6ba88f21_1291x727.jpeg)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3Rqm!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6246cc4a-9780-4d5b-bfe4-317f6ba88f21_1291x727.jpeg)

The words for the numerals 11-16 in French are formed by using a modified form of the numerals from 1 to 6 and then attaching the suffix “-ze” to it. This suffix “-ze” is a truncation of the Latin suffix “decim”. For example :

*Latin* **quin**-**decim** → *French* **quin**-**ze**

But, French breaks this pattern from 17 to 19 wherein it does not follow the subtraction pattern for 18 and 19 like Latin but instead attaches the full word for 10 in front of the words for the numerals 7 to 9 (instead of placing it at the back after weakening it to “-***ze***” like it did before).

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!L7q3!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F887097d4-ed80-4703-8199-273ca2ddfde8_1314x1132.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!L7q3!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F887097d4-ed80-4703-8199-273ca2ddfde8_1314x1132.heic)

*French* :

**cinq** (5) + **dix** (10) = **quin**-**ze** (*lit*. 5-10)

**six** (6) + **dix** (10) = **sei**-**ze** (*lit*. 6-10)

But, on the other hand

**sept** (7) + **dix** (10) = **dix**-**sept** (lit. 10-7)

**sept** (8) + **huit** (10) = **dix**-**huit** (lit. 10-8)

**sept** (9) + **neuf** (10) = **dix**-**huit** (lit. 10-9)

So, we see that French has ditched the subtraction from 20 pattern of Latin for the numbers 18 and 19 and instead shifts to the same addition pattern but that the word for 10 comes first in the front in the words for 17, 18, and 19 while it weakens into a suffix “-***ze***” and comes last.

But, French is not alone in this regard and many other Romance languages like Italian and Spanish do exactly this (in fact, Spanish breaks and shifts to the ten first pattern from 16 onwards till 19). This is not that tough although the word for ten suddenly coming first from 17 to 19 is little quirky. But, the real and unique quirks of French are seen in the higher numerals.

### Counting from 21-69 in French

As expected, the words for 30, 40, 50, and 60 in French are derived from the words for 3, 4, 5, and 6 which are given below, along with a suffix that signifies multiplication by 10. English, Latin, Sanskrit; all work like that. The words for the numerals 30, 40, 50, 60, 70, 80, and 90 are given below in English, Latin, and for French below as well until it follows the usual pattern, after which a warning sign is shown. The constant suffix that signifies multiplication by 10 is underlined in the words of all the three languages.

The suffixes “-ty” in English, “-ginta” in Latin, and “nte” in French signify multiplication by 10

French does the same thing upto 60 except that the suffix for “ten” in this case is reduced to “**nte**”. (*Latin* : **ginta** → *French* : **nte**). The words for an intermediate numeral 64 in English, French and Latin are formed in an obvious way. For example :

Numeral : 64

Arithmetic Logic : 60 + 4

English : **sixty**-**four**

Latin : **sexaginta**-**quattuor** (*lit* : sixty-four)

French : **soixante**-**quatre** (*lit* : sixty-four)

So, upto the number 69, French and Latin follow this usual way. What happens from the number 70 onwards till 99? Latin carries on usually. But, French does something superweird !!

### Counting from 70-99 in French

The French word for 70 is not the expected “**septa**-**nte**” but it is “**soixante**-**dix**” which literally is “sixty-ten” !! The words in French for the numbers from 70 to 79 are shown below.

This is something weird. The normal pattern for the words for numbers from 20 to 100 in English, French, Sanskrit, and Latin is summarized below.

Normally, the words for numerals 20, 30, 40, ..90 and the multiples of 10 are formed by conveying that they are obtained by multiplying the numerals 2, 3, 4, … 9 with 10. In some languages, the word or morpheme for 10 comes first and then the multiplication factor from 1 to 9 comes next (e.g. *English :* 30 = **3\******10** =* **thir-ty***,* where ***-ty** = 10, Sanskrit : 30 = **3*****\*10** = **triṃ-śat**, where **-*****śat*** = 10 ) whereas in some other languages, the word or morpheme for 10 comes last and the multiplication factor from 1 to 9 comes first.

The numbers in between the multiples of 10 from 20 to 99 are normally constructed in batches of 10. We count from 1 to 9 in each batch after the multiple of 10. In some languages, the word for multiple of 10 comes first and then the counting from 1 to 9 comes next (e.g. *English :* 34 = **30 + 4** = **thirty**-**four**) whereas in other languages, the counting from 1 to 10 comes first and the multiple of 10 comes next (e.g. *Sanskrit :* 34 = **4 + 30** = **catus**-**triṃśat,** where ***catus*** = 4 ** ). But, this is just a minor difference.

But, French does something superweird. From 21 to 60, it looks the same as Latin or English or Sanskrit. As usual, till 60, it forms words for the numerals in batches of ten with each multiple of ten forming a suffix that signifies product of its quotient with ten. But from 61 onwards, instead of counting in batches of 10, it starts counting in batches of 20 !!! That is why, after 69, we see that the word for 70 is not on the expected lines of 7\*10 but is 60+10 !! But, it is a continuation of counting from 60 only and the words from 61 to 79 are constructed by counting in batches of 20, which is again shown below.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!c5NO!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F52662293-b28d-482c-85e1-b18461b0d4f4_1394x1186.heic)

What about the word for eighty in French, which comes after the batch strength of twenty numbers from 61 to 79 end? The word for “eighty’ in French is not formed by the expected pattern along the lines of “8\*10” but now it is formed on the lines of “4\*20” as “**quatre-vingt**” which literally means “**four-twenty**”. To count from 81 to 99, one again counts in another batch of twenty !!!

# Basque : The Clue to French’s Arithmetical Mess

Why does French look like this? Why can’t it fully count in batches of ten (like its parent language Latin and most Indo-European languages do). If not, why can’t French be consistent and count in batches of twenty always, from 21 to 99? Why suddenly shift from batches of ten to batches of twenty from 61 onwards? To know this, we must probe what the French people were speaking long long ago, much before they adopted Latin as their spoken language. France was called as **Gaul** in Roman times and the people who inhabited the area were a part of the broader community of speakers who spoke the **Celtic** family of languages. After the Romans conquered **Gaul** (thanks to Julius Caesar), Latin began to replace the Gaulish dialects of Celtic family of languages spoken in many parts of Western Europe. But, Celtic itself is an Indo-European language like Latin and even its quirks (if any) cannot be explained. For this, we have to go a language called **Basque**, that is spoken in a small region bordering Spain and France.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!YASW!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4bbc59ca-6b03-49aa-812a-4fd64dc012cb_3840x1767.png)

![undefined](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!YASW!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4bbc59ca-6b03-49aa-812a-4fd64dc012cb_3840x1767.png "undefined")

The area where Basque is spoken is shown in red.

This language is the only language in Western-Europe that is not in the Indo-European family and is unrelated to any known language. Scholars believe that Basque was once spoken in a much larger area in Europe before the arrival of the Indo- European speakers which gradually eclipsed the language to now survive only in a mountainous border area between France and Spain. The words for numerals in this language holds the clue to the behaviour of French numerals.

Unlike the messy hybrid model of French, Basque is consistent always counts in batches of twenty and forms words for 40, 60, and 80 by expressing them as how many twenties they have.

For example, the first batch of Basque numerals from 20 to 39 are given below.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!2lE8!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffbe0eb4d-c08f-4d9a-bce3-818e140f3379_1400x1098.heic)

Note that what French does from 61 onwards, Basque starts doing it right from the beginning which is 20 onwards!!! For example, let us continue to keep counting from 40 to 59. The word for forty in Basque is not formed as for-ty like in English which signifies 4\*10 but as 2\*20.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!2Xf2!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F580f9283-7fa6-4097-a956-0e27c9fe242c_1380x1092.heic)

As usual, the numbers from forty-one to fifty-nine are counted instead as forty-one to forty-nineteen in Basque. Now, when we enter sixty, unlike English’s six-ty which signifies 6\*10, Basque’s word signifies 3\*20 ! And then, one counts in batches of twenty from sixty-one to seventy-nine as sixty-one to sixty-nineteen in Basque. In French, the word for sixty is formed like in English as soix-ante (six-ty) which signifies 6\*10 but from sixty-one onwards, French starts counting in batches of twenty too like Basque.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!mfyQ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F02add7d7-18b5-4940-a8a8-074010c7148b_1374x1078.heic)

The word for eighty in Basque is formed as usual by the logic 4\*20 which is exactly imitated in French as **quatre-vingt** and as usual counts starting in batches of twenty till 99 which is also imitated in French.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!dDM3!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc19639fa-5d4b-4ebc-a706-560c3879138c_1362x1068.heic)

Turns out this same logic is followed in many modern surviving Celtic languages too. When the Indo European speakers who spoke Celtic arrived and spread their language on the Basque speaking Europeans, these people who had previously speaking languages ancestral to Basque adopted the Celtic vocabulary but kept their counting system and formed the words for numerals in the same way as in Basque, except that they now used the Celtic words for the numerals from 1 to 20.

Even today, Celtic languages that survive in the fringes of the **British Isles** have this same Basque-like system for their words for numerals, except that they now use Celtic words for the lower numerals. For example, traditional **Welsh** had a strong vigesimal system too as reflected in the following words of its numerals :

- 20 = ***ugain***

- 40 = ***deu-gain*** (*lit* : “two twenties”)

- 60 = ***thri-gain*** (*lit* : “three twenties”)

and:

- 50 = ***deg a deugain*** (lit : “ten and forty”)

- 70 = ***deg a thrigain*** (*lit* :” “ten and sixty”)

So historically Welsh was heavily vigesimal and this system continues in only traditional usages while in modern times, this endangered Celtic language has adopted to decimal system. **Breton** (a Celtic language of Brittany) also historically used vigesimal counting very strongly. Hence, we can strongly surmise that the Celtic languages too which Julius Caeasar encountered when he conquered Gaul, would have had this vigesimal counting system adopted from contact with Basque. Eventually, Latin began to replace Gaulish Celtic in the area of what became France, as Celtic itself had replaced Basque. This time though, some damage did indeed happen to the vigesimal system of Basque that survived intact as a substrate influence in Celtic. We have seen that the French vocabulary for numerals is a hybrid between the **decimal** counting system (batches of 10, multiples of 10) of Latin and the **vigesimal** counting system (batches of 20, multiples of 20) of Basque. For the lower numerals (21 to 60), it follows the decimal system of Latin and for the higher numerals (61 to 99), the vigesimal substrate influence from Basque survives. The mess of French numbers is due to the layered linguistic pre-history of France !!! Other Romance languages that replaced Celtic (e.g. Spanish) were not so lucky though and this vigesimal system is fully lost in Spanish and even in the French spoken in areas like Belgium.

Basque is especially fascinating because it is not Indo-European, and its vigesimal counting in twenties is often thought to reflect a counting tradition possibly linked to finger-and-toe counting (10 fingers in hand + 10 toes = 20 total). Many anthropologists suspect this is one of the natural origins of base-20 systems worldwide. Other famous languages which are vigesimal include the Classical **Maya** language of the Mayans of Latin America, the **Nahuatl** language spoken by the **Aztecs**, the **Chukchi** language spoken by the Chukchi people in the easternmost extremity of **Siberia**, **Georgian**, the **Yoruba** language of Western Africa, some **Sino-Tibetan** languages, and the **Ainu** language spoken in Japan. Languages like **Old Norse** and its modern descendant **Danish** has a hybrid system like French where the counting changes to vigesimal after a certain number.

If decimal counting arises from counting with fingers and vigesimal comes from counting with fingers and toes, can we have a **quinary** system where we count in batches of five using only the five fingers of only one hand? Examples include some indigenous American, Oceanic, and **Papuan** systems. Britannica notes that pure base-5 systems are rare today, but quinary often combines with decimal or vigesimal systems. In this system, six will be called as five-one, and nine will be called as five-four and so on.

We also hear of rare cases like WALS giving the **Ekari** language spoken by Indonesian Papua as a modern language using base 60 (but also incorporates sub-bases of 10 and 20 for numbers below 60), and hence for example, rendering 71 as “eleven and sixty”.

# Counting in Dravidian

You might wonder the following. Sanskrit and Latin use subtraction from 20 to convey the numeral 19 by saying that 19 is 1 unit deficit of 20. Why not have the same convention for the number 9 as well and let the word for 9 be rendered in words as something that is 1 unit deficit of 10. Would not that be more consistent? This does not happen in Sanskrit and Latin which have independent words for 9 and 10. But this does happen in Dravidian languages. For example, here are the words for 10 and 9 in Tamil.

*Tamil* :

**pattu** = 10

**oṉ**-**patu** (*lit* : close to 10) = 9

From here on, Tamil is regular (unlike Sanskrit and Latin) in forming the numerals from 11 to 19 by literally having the word for 10 in the genitive case followed by the remainder from 1 to 9. For example :

*Tamil* :

**nāṉku** = 4

**pattu** = 10

**pat**-**in**-**nāṉku** (lit : 10’s 4) = 14

This pattern works for the words of all numerals from 11 to 19.

In the words for ninety and nine-hundred too, Tamil conveys it by subtracting from hundred.

*Tamil* :

**nūṟu** = 100

**toṇ-ṇūṟu** (*lit* : close to 100) = 90

**pat**-**in**-**nāṉku** (*lit* : 10’s 4) = 14

**āyiram** = 1000

**toḷḷ-āyiram** (*lit* : close to 1000) = 900

For example, the collection of three sets of Sangam Tamil texts that contain 900 poems each is called in Tamil as **Muttoḷḷāyiram** which means “three - ninehundred” where the poems are sung in praise of the three ancient Tamil rulers of the kingdoms of **Cēras**, the **Cōḻas**, and the **Pānḍiyas**. This is in line with an ancient Tamil custom of writing 900 verses to complete a work, as in several other works.

Kannada and Telugu later regularized 90 more toward “nine-tens” while Tamil preserves the archaic flavor more visibly.
