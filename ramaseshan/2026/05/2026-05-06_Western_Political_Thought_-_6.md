In the last two articles in the series, we saw the origins and important waypoints in the history of conservatism and socialism in the Western political thought. This time, we will chart the course of **liberalism** - an ideology that has been used by so many people in so many ways. We also saw in those articles that conservatism eventually adopted the ideas of classical liberalism and that aspects of socialism entered liberalism as time evolved. In this article, we will connect all the dots together.

# Classical Liberalism

The liberal response emerged as a reaction to the French Revolution that posited individual freedom as a cornerstone of a just society, fighting against the rigid hierarchies of feudalism and absolutist monarchies in the ancien regime. If socialism stands on the left end of the political spectrum and conservatism stands on the right end of the political spectrum, the position of liberalism has been moving with time. Initially they were centrists and this kind of liberals are known as **classical liberals**. You will be surprised to find out that these classical liberals would sound like today’s conservatives while the term “liberal” has attained a leftist connotation in modern times. This liberal shift to left was gradual as the Industrial Revolution progressed and as conservatism in the original sense slowly turned irrelevant and got marginalized to the fringe of mainstream Western political thought (due to the collapse of the old order and hierarchy of Europe with time). Also as socialism fell out of fashion in Europe, liberalism later evolved to fill that vacuum in the left by incorporating into itself many aspects of socialism gradually.

**Classical liberals** held dear what modern right wing conservatives hold dear now -

1.  Free markets

2.  No privileges to anyone based on their identity

3.  Rule of Law

4.  Minimum government

5.  Individual Liberty

Classical liberals argued that all individuals are equal before the law and possessed inherent rights to life, liberty, and property, and that the role of the government is to protect these rights and maintain law and order, interfering as little as possible in personal and economic affairs of the people.

The roots of classical liberalism go all the way back to the Renaissance and the Enlightenment. **John Locke**, one could argue, was the first proto-liberal as he focused on minimum governance and toleration. **Adam Smith** is the root of the classical liberal idea of free market and free trade without government interference. Classical liberalism was also represented by thinkers like **Thomas Paine** and **Montesquieu.** It is classical liberalism that dominated Wesern politics throughout the eighteenth and the early nineteenth centuries, despite its early evolution away from its classical roots. What caused the classical liberals to move away from classical liberalism? There are four main reasons:

1.  The extremely bad condition of the workers in the Industrial
    Revolution

2.  Monopolization in market and exploitation by such firms

3.  The rise of nationalism

4.  Wars

The suffering of the workers and the rise of stark inequalities, market monopolies, and exploitation in the course of the progress of the Industrial Revolution is what triggered the shift of liberalism to the left end of the political spectrum. Critics of classical liberalism saw that ***laissez-faire economics*** (free market capitalism) could not solve these ills and in fact accelerated them. These prompted rethinking the idea of the meaning of liberalism and propelled its shift to the left.

The 1790s to the 1820s saw increasing signs of labor unrest, especially in Britain. Between 1811–1817, the **Luddites** (handcraft experts who were upset that the industrial revolution stole their traditional job and made them miserable workers) broke up machines in this period. In 1812, the British government passed the **Frame Breaking Act**, making machine breaking a capital offense! The factory owners were using Adam Smith and other economic thinkers to justify their negligence in their fair treatment of workers. After all, isn’t capitalism all about the free market and employers choosing their own wages and working conditions for their employees simply based on their supply and demand? Hence, in the beginning of the nineteenth century, in response to the horrible plight of the industrial working class, classical liberalism would be challenged by new brands of liberalism. We will see representative thinkers or schools and trace the development of liberalism chronologically. The shift away from classical liberalism can be seen in the works of a single thinker like Jeremy Bentham or John Stuart Mill about whom we will explore in the coming section.

# Utilitarianism : Greatest Happiness for the Greatest Number

**Utilitarianism** served as the first bridge between classical liberalism and modern liberalism. It was founded by **Jeremy Bentham** (1748 - 1832), an Enlightenment product who believed in the power of reason and using it to discover the best laws for human society. **Bentham believed that the best society, the best government, the best institutions were those that brought the greatest happiness to the greatest number** - the defining axiom of utilitarian ethics. Any law, belief, or institution that strayed from this goal should be abolished, including the aristocracy, state religion, and slavery. Less radically, other institutions had the potential to be useful but needed reform: English common law, schools, the Poor Law, and prisons. Utilitarians advocated ending press censorship and universal manhood suffrage. So far this all looks like classical liberalism. Bentham argued that regarding any moral decision, we must ask ourselves: *What is the total set of people likely affected by it, and what is the likely effect on each of them? We must add up all the increases and decreases of every different quality of pleasure for each affected person or class of person. If the net effect is an increase in total pleasure, then we are okay to do that action*.

The main problem with utilitarianism is that it seems as if the philosophy would make it morally acceptable or even obligatory to harm a minority or an individual if doing so overwhelmingly benefits the majority. For example, what if it would make the poor majority happier to expropriate the property of the rich or to turn a minority into slaves? It seems as if utilitarianism could lead to violations of rights. Then, utilitarians argue that loss of personal liberty of a minority affects everyone and not just those minorities and hence any act that infringes on personal liberties of anyone can not be justified even if it benefits the majority overwhelmingly. So, personal liberties of a person should be infringed only when it harms another person. Mill did not consider offence or insult or emotional damage as harm - he considered only physical injuries and legal repurcussions as harms. Equivalently, paternalism—limiting an agent’s liberty for the agent’s sake—is prohibited. To limit an agent’sliberty, it must be shown that his or her liberty is likely to harm others. Hence, freedom of thought and expression of anyone can never be restricted as it does not harm anyone. Even private immoral behaviors like gambling, pornography, prostitution, fornication, etc, cannot be restricted by the state as it does not harm anyone else.One might argue that a gambler or fornicator does not harm anyone else but he harms himself neverthless. But Mill thinks that it is better for people to learn from their own mistake than from bans from a paternalizing state.

But, Bentham too, later in his life, advocated a degree of government intervention in some spheres that was not a part of classical liberalism. His later followers like **John Stuart Mill** (1806 - 1873) took this up further and argued for a far more governmental interventionism later in their lives. But Mill explained that this was simply a logical outgrowth of classical liberalism itself. Mill argued that it was not enough to give people the liberty to vote, trade, and choose their work, if their living conditions were so horrible and they could not even read and know enough to be aware and knowledgeable to choose what is good for themselves in society. Thus, he supported things like free public education, housing, and healthcare to be provided by the only institution large enough to make a difference: the government.

Mill thus became a new type of liberal. He still believed in personal freedom, as long as others were not hurt, and in small government. But that freedom would no longer include the right to exploit the poor or deny them education, housing, or votes. Rather, to ensure the maximum amount of freedom for all, the government had an obligation to ensure welfare for all - educate, house, and protect all. If that meant restricting the economic freedom of factory owners, so be it; because a free market should not mean free exploitation as a truly free market is possible only when there is information symmetry between the employer and the worker. If that meant higher taxes on the aristocracy and rising middle class, also let it be.

The major difference between Mill and Bentham was that Bentham, being more scientific, made differences between all pleasures solely quantitative. Any pleasure according to Bentham that you might experience can be equaled by a larger quantity of another pleasure. In principle, eating enough ice cream could equal another kind of pleasure like that gotten by reading a beautiful poem or falling in love. Mill thought that there are different qualities of pleasure and although the pleasures within one such class of quality can be arranged in a hierarchy, it cannot be pleasures that are qualitatively higher cannot be equalled by any amount of pleasure with quality lower in this hierarchy. Mill justified this hierarchy by saying that people in fact do choose higher-quality pleasures once they have access to them and are familiar with them. This was also a social point: The poor, if they had access to higher cultural tastes, would want it.

So basically Mill’s argument is that only well-informed and healthy people can be truly free and be able to choose and make the decisions that are the best for themselves in a free market society without being exploited and hence for enabling this, the state should provide education and healthcare for all. This should remind us of socialism. In his own autobiography, Mill wrote that as his beliefs had evolved, he could be classed “under the general designation of Socialists”! This also shows up in the later editions of his work "**Principles of Political Economy**". How can Mill be a socialist and a proto-libertarian? The clarification comes in an unfinished work titled “**Chapters on Socialism**” published after his death. Here, Mill says that he had always been upset that English life was dominated by inherited wealth, which he thought unjust. He argued that property rights were historically highly variable and should be decided by social utility. He believed that the problem of production had been solved by the free market economy by supply and demand; but not the problem of distribution, and government action was necessary for redistribution, especially in education, healthcare, and old-age pensions.

Still, he rejected state-centralized socialism. Production requires division of labor; competition and differential wages or rewards are just and necessary as incentives. Taking away the capitalist’s profits would not benefit the worker as this would destroy the capitalist’s incentive for production. He continued to argue against state paternalism. But he was open to local experiments in what some have called industrial democracy. For Mill, socialism meant local workers co-operations, worker-management cooperation, and some of what we would later call progressive or new liberal social legislation. And he emphasized its experimental, empirical character, whose outcomes would have to be judged over time. He was certainly sure that ideologies like revolutionary socialism would be a disaster for all.

Also Mill argued that only a democratic government with universal adult franchise and shifting of people in power is the only legitimate form of government because it prevents a tiny minority with power influencing the politics of the state at the cost of the majority of the members of the community. While direct democracy would be the best case scenario, he agrees that it would be infeasible in a large nation state and hence representative democracy is the next best thing that practically is feasible and comes close to protecting the interests of the community as a whole.

This brand of liberalism itself took some time to have an effect on politics and society and when it did so, it was mostly classical liberalism. It would take most of the 19th century to persuade the British middle class that workers deserved the vote and still longer before they came to embrace the ideas of John Stuart Mill. It is because of this reason that socialism thrived and filled the void in this period.

# Social Liberalism / Modern Liberalism / New Liberalism : Positive Liberty and Reform

We already saw the gradual incorporation of socialist elements into Utilitarianism. In this section, we examine the school of **Social Liberalism** or **New Liberalism** or **Modern Liberalism** that incorporated further more aspects of socialism following the precedent set by the Utilitarians. Social liberalism emerged in the late 19th century as a response to the limitations of classical liberalism, and Utilitarianism. Classical liberals like Adam Smith and Herbert Spencer believed competition and individual effort would solve most problems, but industrialization revealed glaring inequalities: workers toiled in unsafe factories, children went uneducated, and wealth concentrated in few hands. Social liberalism sought to preserve liberal values—freedom, democracy, individual rights—while using government to address these issues. We will examine this school through an important representative thinker named **Leonard Trelawny Hobhouse** (1864–1929), who published a long essay, “**Liberalism**” in 1911, “**The Elements of Social Justice**” in 1922, and “**Democracy and Reaction**” in 1904.

To grasp Hobhouse’s ideas, imagine Britain in the late 1800s and early 1900s. The Industrial Revolution had transformed society: factories churned out goods, cities swelled with workers, and wealth grew—but so did inequality. While some lived in luxury, many labored long hours in dangerous conditions for meager wages. Child labor, overcrowded slums, and lack of education were common. Classical liberalism, the dominant philosophy of the time, championed individual freedom and free markets, arguing that the government should stay out of people’s lives and let competition drive progress.

Thinkers like Adam Smith and Herbert Spencer believed this “hands-off” approach maximized liberty. But to many, including Hobhouse, this system seemed to favor the wealthy and leave the poor behind. At the same time, socialism was also gaining traction, calling for collective ownership of industries to end exploitation. Hobhouse, however, believed liberalism could offer a better path—one that preserved individual freedom while addressing inequality. As a sociologist and professor at the London School of Economics, he studied real-world conditions and drew on philosophy, ethics, and social science to craft a new vision of liberalism. His work was deeply influenced by earlier thinkers like John Stuart Mill, who began to explore social reforms, and **T.H. Green**, who introduced the idea that freedom means more than just being left alone.

Hobhouse developed Green’s ideas further who argued that 19th-century classical liberalism, often called **laissez-faire liberalism**, was flawed. Its concept of liberty was negative—focused only on removing barriers like government rules—and mechanical— treating people as isolated individuals competing in a market. To Hobhouse, this missed the bigger picture: humans are social beings, shaped by and dependent on their communities. He proposed a positive and organic liberalism that saw society as a living, interconnected whole, like a body where every part works together. Individual liberty thus depends on society and cannot be guaranteed merely by eliminating state or social controls on the individual.

### Positive Liberty as Freedom to Thrive, Not Just Survive

Classical liberals defined freedom as negative liberty: the absence of interference, coercion, etc.. from government, laws, or taxes - so that you can do what you want. For example, if no one stops you from starting a business, you’re free. But earlier social liberalist thinkers like **Thomas Hill Green** pointed out that this doesn’t help if you’re too poor to afford tools, education, or even food to survive. He championed positive liberty, which means having the resources and opportunities to actually achieve your goals. In 1881, Green delivered a famous public lecture called ‘***Liberal Legislation and Freedom of Contract**’* where he said:

We shall probably all agree that freedom, rightly understood, is the greatest of blessings—that its attainment is the true end of all our effort as citizens. But when we thus speak of freedom, we should consider carefully what we mean by it. We do not mean merely freedom from restraint or compulsion. We do not mean merely freedom to do as we like irrespectively of what it is that we like. We do not mean a freedom that can be enjoyed by one man or one set of men at the cost of a loss of freedom to others. When we speak of freedom as something to be so highly prized, we mean a positive power or capacity of doing or enjoying something worth doing or enjoying, and that, too, something that we do or enjoy in common with others.

Imagine having the talents and wanting to be a doctor but growing up in a slum with no access to capital to afford education—that’s not freedom, even if technically no law or individual is stopping you from becoming a doctor. But still, you cannot become a doctor. Positive liberty requires society to provide things like education, healthcare, or a decent wage so that everyone has a real opportunities and hence actual freedom to pursue their dreams. In “**Liberalism**” (1911), Hobhouse wrote:

*Liberty is the positive power or capacity of doing or enjoying something worth doing or enjoying*.

This means the government has a role in creating the conditions—like schools or hospitals—that make freedom meaningful. Negative liberty is like having no one lock your door—you’re free to leave. Positive liberty is having a car, gas, and a map to actually go where you want. Hobhouse said both matter, but positive liberty needs the help of the society. He argued that for the sake of thus, it is okay for the government to tax rich people to fund basic amenities like education, housing, and food for the poor people. Although this would not be pleasing to the rich people, it is beneficial to the society as a whole and maximizes liberty for the society as a whole. Hobhouse supported such a liberalism and justified it by its benefit to the society, not natural rights. He also viewed this as compatible with nationalism for what demonstrates nationalism more than a rich person helping a poor person from his own nation? So, in this conception, an individual depends on social conditions to develop personality and achieve the fullest liberty

### The Social Nature of Individuals

Hobhouse believed that no one is truly independent. Your ability to succeed—whether as a business owner, artist, or parent—depends on social conditions: the roads you travel, the schools you attend, etc. He called this an anthropological view, meaning it’s about the fundamental nature of humans as social creatures. He challenged John Stuart Mill’s idea of “*self-regarding conduct*” (actions that only affect you). To Hobhouse, every action ripples through society. For example, if you neglect your health, you might burden public hospitals. This interconnectedness means individual freedom cannot be discussed in isolation from society. In practical terms, this idea justified policies to support everyone, because helping others ultimately helps you too. A society with educated, healthy citizens is one where everyone can thrive. Think of society like a team sport. You can’t score a goal alone—you need teammates, a field, and rules. Hobhouse saw society as a team where everyone’s success depends on working together.

### Social Justice and Redistribution

Hobhouse argued that wealth isn’t just the result of individual effort—it’s created by society. A factory owner’s profits depend on workers, infrastructure, and customers, all supported by public systems. He called for a social conception of property, meaning those who benefit from society owe something back. This led to his support for redistribution through taxation - taxing the wealthy to fund public services. For example, a millionaire’s taxes could build schools, giving poor kids a chance to succeed. This wasn’t about punishing success but recognizing that success relies on social contributions. In “**The Elements of Social Justice**” (1922), he outlined a system where resources are distributed to ensure fairness, not absolute equality, but enough to give everyone a shot at a good life. If you make millions running a company, it’s not just your hard work—workers, roads, and laws helped you. Hobhouse said you should pay taxes to give back, like tipping the team that supported you.

### Social Liberalism

Hobhouse coined the term social liberalism to describe his blend of liberal freedom and socialist concern for equality. Unlike socialism, which might nationalize industries, his approach kept private property and markets but used the government to regulate them for the common good. For example, he supported minimum wage laws, workplace safety rules, and public welfare programs like unemployment benefits. These policies aimed to give workers dignity and opportunity while preserving the liberal value of individual choice. This balance made his ideas appealing to those who wanted change but feared socialism’s radical overhaul of society. Thus, social liberalism is a middle ground between liberalism and socialism: you keep your business (unlike socialism), but the government makes sure everyone gets a fair start, like providing free schools or healthcare using taxes collected from your profit. The modern notion of a welfare state as exemplified in Scandinavian countries comes from this school of thought

### Liberty as a Means, Not the End

Hobhouse made a bold claim: “*Liberty itself is not fundamental, it is a means to an end*.” By this, he meant that negative liberty—freedom from coercion—isn’t the ultimate goal. Instead, liberty serves a higher purpose: enabling people to develop their self-directing power of personality, or their unique ability to think, create, and contribute to society. For Hobhouse, the true goal of liberalism is a society where everyone can grow into their best selves, contributing to the common good. Negative liberty alone can’t achieve this if social conditions like poverty or ignorance hold people back. This idea flipped classical liberalism on its head, prioritizing social progress over unchecked individualism.

### Utilitarianism with a Social Twist

Hobhouse was inspired by utilitarianism, the philosophy of Jeremy Bentham and Mill, which judges actions by their ability to create the greatest happiness for the greatest number. However, he criticized early utilitarianism for treating people as isolated units, ignoring how their happiness depends on others. His version of utilitarianism focused on the benefit to society as a whole, not just individual gains. For example, taxing a rich person to fund schools might reduce their personal wealth but increases overall happiness by educating future generations. Unlike Locke’s liberalism, which rested on natural rights (inherent entitlements like property), Hobhouse justified liberalism by its social outcomes.

### Compatibility with Nationalism

Hobhouse believed liberalism could work hand-in-hand with nationalism, the idea of a shared national identity. He saw nations as communities that foster social cohesion, providing the context for liberal values like democracy and justice to flourish.

However, his nationalism was not aggressive or exclusionary. He opposed imperialism, arguing that true liberalism respects all peoples’ right to self-determination. In “**Democracy and Reaction**” (1904), he criticized Britain’s colonial policies for betraying liberal principles, advocating a nationalism that supports global fairness.

Thus, Hobhouse transformed liberalism by arguing that freedom isn’t just about being left alone—it’s about having the chance to thrive in a fair society. His vision of social liberalism rejected the “*negative and mechanical*” laissez-faire of the 19th century, proposing a positive and organic approach where the government ensures equality and opportunity. Inspired by utilitarianism but critical of its individualism, he justified liberalism by its benefits to society, not abstract rights. His ideas—positive liberty, social justice, redistribution, and social liberalism—shaped the welfare state and remain vital in modern debates. As a key figure in social liberalism, Hobhouse bridged classical liberalism’s individualism to a progressive philosophy that balanced freedom with fairness, leaving a lasting legacy in liberal thought.

# American Progressivism

**American Progressivism**, a movement that arose in the late 19th century and the early 20th century, shaped by **William Jennings Bryan** (1860–1925), **Theodore Roosevelt** (1858–1919), **Woodrow Wilson** (1856–1924), and **John Dewey** (1859–1952), builds on social liberalism’s focus on fairness and government intervention, infusing it with moral activism, pragmatic governance, and democratic optimism. Bryan’s moral fervor, like advocating for fair taxes to protect struggling farmers, adds a passionate ethical drive to social liberalism’s practical reforms. Roosevelt’s bold experimentation, such as regulating dangerous factories, and Wilson’s systemic reforms, like stabilizing banks, transformed social liberalism’s incrementalism into dynamic, proactive governance. Dewey’s philosophy of education as a tool for collaborative problem-solving, envisioning schools where students tackle issues like unemployment, elevates social liberalism’s welfare focus into a vision of continuous democratic growth. Progressivism extends these principles globally through liberal internationalism, with Wilson’s call for self-determination and Dewey’s global citizenship promoting democracy and cooperation, as in fostering peace between conflicting nations. Together, Progressivism and liberal internationalism expand social liberalism into a morally charged, experimental, and globally-oriented philosophy of collective progress. It formed the basis for the idea of the **Welfare State** where the government is socialist as far as providing education, healthcare, and public infrastructure is concerned (by collecting taxes from high income citizens) and otherwise capitalistic.

At its core, American Progressivism was a response to the rapid changes brought by the Industrial Revolution. By the late 1800s, the United States was transforming from a rural, agrarian society into an urban, industrial powerhouse. However, this progress came with problems: rampant poverty, unsafe working conditions, child labor, political corruption, and monopolistic corporations that stifled competition. Progressivism sought to address these issues through reforms that empowered government to regulate the economy, protect workers, and expand democracy. Progressives believed that society could be improved through reason, science, and collective action. Unlike earlier laissez-faire ideologies that favored minimal government involvement, Progressivism embraced an active state to promote the public good. It was not a single, unified movement but a coalition of reformers—politicians, intellectuals, journalists, and activists—who shared a commitment to change. Their goals included curbing corporate power, improving labor conditions, expanding suffrage, and promoting social justice.

### What Progressivism Added to New Liberalism

1. **Pragmatism**: American Progressivism was deeply influenced by the wider philosophy of **pragmatism**, which emphasized practical solutions over rigid ideologies. Unlike New Liberalism’s more theoretical bent, Progressives focused on experimentation—trying new policies and adjusting them based on results.

2. **Anti-Corruption and Democratic Reforms:** Progressives sought to clean up corrupt political systems. They introduced reforms like the secret ballot, direct primaries, and the initiative and referendum processes, which gave citizens more direct control over government—ideas less prominent in New Liberalism.

3. **Muckraking and Public Awareness**: American Progressives used journalism to expose societal ills. Muckraking journalists like **Ida Tarbell** and **Upton Sinclair** revealed corporate abuses and unsafe working conditions, galvanizing public support for reform. This focus on public engagement was less pronounced in New Liberalism.

4. **Regulation of Big Business:** While New Liberalism advocated for state intervention, American Progressives aggressively targeted monopolies and trusts. They pushed for anti-trust laws and regulatory agencies to curb corporate power, reflecting America’s unique struggle with industrial giants.

5. **Social and Moral Reforms**: Progressivism incorporated moral crusades, such as Prohibition and women’s suffrage, which were less central to New Liberalism. These reflected America’s religious and democratic traditions. Three figures—**Theodore Roosevelt**, **Woodrow Wilson**, and **John Dewey**—played pivotal roles in shaping American Progressivism. Each brought unique ideas that expanded the movement’s scope and influence.

### Theodore Roosevelt

Roosevelt argued that the economic world had changed since the American founding and the early 19th century. He claimed that the country faced large, monopolistic corporations whose power was too great. The suffering of the poor was the result. Unlike in Mill’s time, in the new century, he claimed that people witnessed a “***tyranny of minorities***,” namely, the wealthy few and their representatives. In response, he proposed a new version of the three R’s: **Reform**, **Regulation**, and **Re-distribution**.

Roosevelt called his program the **Square Deal** and the **New Nationalism**. Although he insisted that he was no opponent of capitalism, his rhetoric implied some principled limitations on it. Unearned possessions and privileges have to be removed so that practical equality of opportunity could be promted. Property rights are good, he claimed, but only when they benefit society.

Most importantly, Roosevelt and other progressives argued that the power of the presidency should be magnified, especially against the Supreme Court, which was protecting property rights of major corporations as per the Constituion, which he claimed, was written for a very different time. Roosevelt believed that the president should be able to act in any way for the public good that is not “forbidden” by the Constitution, as opposed to possessing only those powers explicitly granted by the Constitution. Progressives wanted more democracy, but in their time, this meant more power in the executive branch.

### Woodrow Wilson

Wilson took this further in his book, “***The New Freedom**”*, where he specifically criticized the “*mechanical Newtonian*” view of politics characteristic of ***The Federalist papers***. The division of powers and checks and balances are too mechanical and make the executive power too weak to serve the best interests of the nation.

In place of this “*mechanical balancing*,” he felt the need for a biological, Darwinian, evolutionary conception of politics that recognizes political society as a functioning organic system. Likewise, the Constitution, hence, must be regarded as a living document. The system needs hence greater centralized direction. The idea of “*balance of power*” had to be rethought.

In practice, this meant two major shifts: The president is the sole national representative and, as such, must have more power, and the Supreme Court has too much power. The Supreme Court was enforcing the 18th-century Constitution in such a way as to prevent the executive from flexibly dealing with the needs of the new age.

### John Dewey

In political philosophy, Dewey opposed the old “*asocial individualism*.” Individuals develop through interaction based on social conditions, especially education. They are formed by, and remain dependent for growth upon, interaction with others. The old liberalism is “static” and sees individuality in conflict with social action. But individuality and sociality are coordinated, interactive, and symbiotic.

Dewey is not against individuality, but he gives it a social definition as the unique contribution the individual makes to society. Society is not fixed either; it is a result of the interactions of masses of individuals and is rightly changed by them.

For Dewey, democracy is, above all, a process of ever-increasing interaction or communication between social elements. It is democracy that permits the widest possible range of interactive experiences and, hence, the greatest possible learning. Democratic freedom means breaking down the barriers to novel and wider experiences and encouraging opportunities for growth.

National economic life needs planning through intelligence, Dewey believes, not laissez-faire policies. Dewey sometimes said that he was a socialist, but today, we would say that he was a progressive or, perhaps, a social democrat in the European sense.

### Effects of the New Liberalism & Progressivism

Progressivism was largely successful and responsible for some reforms in the West, to which virtually no one today objects and takes for granted. Its legacy includes :

- legislation and government agencies for trust-busting

- the creation of the income tax (16th Amendment in the USA);

  senators \[in the USA\] (17th Amendment);

- the eight-hour work day

- minimum wage \[in the USA\]

- social insurance for the elderly, disabled, and unemployed

### Critique of Progressivism

**Christopher Lasch** (1932–1994), a left-wing American historian, in “***The True and Only Heaven: Progress and Its Critics**”*, faulted the progressive movement and its descendants for an over-reliance on experts. Turning to the executive branch to manipulate the economy and social welfare inevitably required solving social problems by hiring educated experts to manage society, instead of the earlier impulse of populism, which was concerned with empowering the rural and urban poor with some manner of direct and participatory democracy.

Some people think of laissez-faire capitalism and socialism as a battle between liberty and equality, (which indeed it does seem to be), and progressivism as a trade-off. But the conflict can be complex depending on what type of equality one seeks - equality of outcome or equality of opportunity. But all progressives agree that equality before the law is always unnegotiable. But, the new liberals argue that some degree of equality beyond the equality under the law is is necessary for any truly positive liberty and differ on what that degree should be.
