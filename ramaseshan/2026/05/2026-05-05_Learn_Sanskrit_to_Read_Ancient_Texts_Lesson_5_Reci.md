In this lesson, we will observe the **gerund** which is a verb form that is used to make very long sentences. It is a very frequently occurring form that occurs universally in all texts that an understanding of it can’t wait for long. The gerund form of a verb X can be translated as: “*after doing X / after having done X*”.

They are easy to recognise - they end in त्वा / इत्वा (or sometimes ट्वा, ध्वा).

> Example sentences:  
> राजः वनं **गत्वा** मृगं मारयति  
> The king, **after going to** the forest, kills a deer
>
> वालकः **पठित्वा** लिखति  
> The boy writes **after reading**

Compared to the present tense form of the verb, the gerund uses a weakened form: E.g. करोति = he does, कृत्वा = after doing (अर् =\> ऋ).

- What kind of roots take इत्वा and what others get just त्वा?

- What kind of roots lose their nasal sounds (e.g. नम् =\> नत्वा) and what
  others don’t? (e.g. क्रम् =\> क्रान्त्वा)?

- What kind of roots change their त्वा to ट्वा or ध्वा?

There are no rigid rules for all these and hence every form is best remembered on a case-by-case basis by beginners and recognized and recollected from experience. But worry not! **Recognising gerunds is easy - it has only one form that always ends in इत्वा / त्वा / ध्वा / ट्वा**. For now, just recognize to translate every word that ends thus as “after doing ….”. And remember that the vowel gets weakened so that you can relate to a present tense form.

**The exact rules of weakening to form the gerundive is complicated - hence, the gerund form of a verb is best learnt by practice & experience**. **However, it is easy to recognise by the ending (इ)त्वा / ट्वा / ध्वा**.

> ⚠️⚠️⚠️ The root **वह्** (pres. **वहति**) (meaning : *to drag, to
> transport, to ride, to carry*) has a completely unexpected irregular
> gerund **ऊढ्वा**.
>
> ⚠️⚠️⚠️ The root दृश् that has a regular gerund **दृष्ट्वा** has an
> unexpected present tense form **पश्यति** (meaning : *to see***)**.

Sanskrit can convey any number of a sequence of actions before the main verb by successively nesting gerunds. That is why I referred to it as one of the secret recipes for forming long sentences.

> बालकः **जागरित्वा** देवान् **पूजयित्वा** प्रातराशं **खादित्वा** विद्यालयं
> **गत्वा** पठति  
> The boy, **after waking up**, **saluting** the gods, **eating**
> breakfast, and **going** to school, reads.

# Prefixes for verbs

Verbs in Sanskrit admit prefixes which are attached before them that change their meaning. Many times, the meaning change caused by a prefix is somewhat predictable but sometimes the meaning change is random or sometimes, the meaning does not change at all.

For example, the prefix **प्रति** indicates reciprocity or doing something in return or doing something again.

> करोति *does*, vs प्रतिकरोति *does in return* / *revenges  *
> गच्छति *goes*, vs प्रतिगच्छति *goes back  *
> ददाति *gives*, vs प्रतिददाति *gives back  *
> वदति *speaks*, vs प्रतिवदति *replies*

The prefix **अव** normally means “down” and adds a descendant sense to the meaning of the verb.

> तरति *crosses*, vs अवतरति *crosses down, descends   *
> कृन्तति *cuts*, vs अवकृन्तति *cuts down* (we saw this verb in week 2’s
> story)  
> रोहति *climbs*, vs अवरोहति *climbs down*

The prefix **परि** normally means “around” or “about” or “thoroughly” and adds a sense of circulation or thoroughness to the meaning of the verb. Through Proto-Indo-European, it is cognate to the Greek prefix “peri” that is seen in words related to boundary like “perimeter” , “pericycle”, etc.

> गच्छति *goes*, vs परिगच्छति *goes around, revolves  *
> ददाति *gives*, vs परिददाति *gives around, assigns  *
> त्यजति *gives up*, vs परित्यजति *gives up everything around (completely
> abandons forever)  *
> पृच्छति *asks*, vs परिपृच्छति *asks about  *
> वदति *tells* vs परिवदति *tells about / gossip*

The prefix **अप** normally means “away” and adds a sense of separation to the meaning of the verb. Sometimes, it also adds a morally bad connotation to the verb.

> गच्छति *crosses*, vs अपगच्छति *goes away , departs   *
> हरति *takes*, vs अपहरति *takes away, kidnaps, snatches  *
> करोति *does*, vs अपकरोति *does badly*

But sometimes, the actions of prefixes on verbs alters the meaning in a very unpredictable manner or it does not alter the meaning at all.

> लोकयति *looks*, vs अवलोकयति *notices, observes* (not “looks down”!!)  
> गच्छति *goes*, vs अवगच्छति *understands* (not “goes down”!!)  
> नयति *leads*, vs परिणयति *man marries* (not “leads around”!!)

Interestingly, English also does the same thing. The word “understand” does not mean what its constituents might suggest. Its meaning cannot be derived by splitting it as “under+stand” which means standing under something !!

The prefix आ sometimes means the verb performed in reverse. But many times, it is unpredictable and the meaning of the आ prefixed verb has to be learnt separately. Popular among them are given below.

> गच्छति *crosses*, vs आगच्छति *comes   *
> नयति *leads*, vs आनयति *brings  *
> करोति *does*, vs आकरोति *assumes form, disguises as  *
> ददाति *gives*, vs आददाति *takes   *
> चरति *moves*, vs आचरति *conducts, behaves  *
> नन्दति *is happy*, vs आनन्दति *rejoices*

Two important points are to be kept in mind while using a prefixed verb.

1.  **Past tense**: The past tense augment अ comes after all the
    prefixes and just before the root of the verb. In this process,
    sound alterations called sandhi changes may occur about which we
    will study in detail in the next level.  
    Eg. वदति (says) =\> प्रतिवदति (replies): अवदत् (said), प्रति + अवदत् =
    **प्रत्यवदत्** (replied)

2.  **Gerund**: The gerund suffix changes to य or sometimes त्य when the
    verb takes a prefix  
    Eg. कृ =\> कृत्वा (after doing) : नमस् + कृ =\> नमस्कृत्य (after saluting)  
    गम् =\> गत्वा (after going): आ + गम् =\> आगम्य (after coming)

# Relative Clause & Main Clause

Up until this point, we have studied how to form and understand very basic Sanskrit sentences. I don’t mean that they are basic just because of our limited vocabulary or our lack of fancy verbs (although it is true that we have covered only a limited set of verb and noun patterns). But, what I mean when I say that they are basic is that they are not powerful enough to express complex interconnected ideas. We can describe the action of one subject, and we can chain several simple actions together with the gerund*,* form a complex nominal (noun) idea by using compounding; but ultimately, we’re still just considering one subject and one main action at a time per sentence. We have no good way to relate two ideas to each other. Here are simple (not many words) but yet interconnected sentences that we simply cannot say at this stage, like :

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zDVb!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F97720535-9597-4d6e-8726-7703c2527063_2048x1039.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zDVb!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F97720535-9597-4d6e-8726-7703c2527063_2048x1039.png "Screenshot 2025-10-03 at 22.10.53.png")

Observe the sentences above. Some part of each sentence here is in bold and some part is in italics. First, try to think yourself what is the difference between the bold and italic part of each sentence? Turns out :

The sentences in **bold** , are also called the **main clause**, because they tell the main matter of the sentence.

The *italicised* parts are the *relative* *clause,* because it give more information about the main clause by telling the time or place or condition or reason or description or manner with which main clause is happening.

For example :

> **he cooks hot snacks** → main matter (main clause)  
> \[when\] *it rains* → time in which the main matter happens (relative
> clause)
>
> **He gets happiness** → main matter (main clause)  
> \[who\] *remembers the name of God* → describing / characterising the
> “he” in the main matter (relative clause)
>
> **he did not go** → main matter (main clause)  
> \[because\] *it rained* → giving the reason why the main matter
> happened (relative clause)
>
> **the citizens behave** → main matter (main clause)  
> \[as\] *the king behaves* → describing the manner in which the main
> matter happens (relative clause)
>
> **he does not go** → main matter (main clause)  
> \[if\[ *it does not rain* → gives the condition under which the main
> matter happens (relative clause)

So, when we encounter any such interconnected sentence in English, as beginners, it is better to split them into two first. We split each sentence into two parts and use some new words to relate them. We usually call one part the **relative clause** because it is only important *relative* to the other part; here, the word **clause** means about the same thing as the word “sentence”, except that it is a sentence that forms a part of a larger sentence. We also call the other part the **main clause** because it can stand on its own.

English uses some words to signal the beginning of a main clause and other sets of words to signal the beginning of a relative clause. From the previous sets of sentences, they are. :

> *if*….**then**   
> *when*…..**then  **
> *where***…there  **
> *as***…..likewise  **
> *since***…hence /** *because***…..therefore  **
> *until..***…till then  **
> *who***…he**

But actually, Sanskrit is much more transparent in English in the sense that the form of these connectors give us the kind of clauses they are starting. In Sanskrit,

> - All connectors that signal the beginning of a *relative clause*
>   start with the letter Y य्.
>
> - All connectors that signal the beginning of a **main clause** start
>   with the letter T त् / its weaker variant S स् (Note that S and T are
>   both uttered using the tigue touching the tip of the teeth).
>
> - Other than this difference, the forms of these connectors are same
>   or almost the same in most cases.

The Sanskrit equivalents are shown below :

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!6Lc0!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4697a2d3-0ca4-4938-bf9b-f21bb51e8862_1318x620.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!6Lc0!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4697a2d3-0ca4-4938-bf9b-f21bb51e8862_1318x620.png "Screenshot 2025-10-03 at 10.47.20.png")

Hindi also has a similar structure. All relative clause connectors in Hindi start with the letter “j” (think of words like jo, jis, jin, jab, jab, jidhar, jaise) and main clause connectors start with the letter “t” or “u” or “v” (vo, us, tab, udhar, vaise, tab).

# Honouring the Guru with Relative Pronouns

Now, we will specifically focus on the last kind of sentence.

> **Who** remembers the name of God, **he** gets happiness  
> **यः** भगवन्नाम स्मरति **सः** आनन्दं प्राप्नोति

Here, the connection is between nouns and hence the connecting pairs are called **relative pronouns** and **main pronouns**. In relative clauses involving pronouns like यः and सः, it is important to know that because they refer to nouns, they possess gender, number, and grammatical case like nouns in general. Their endings mostly follow the रामः and फलम् pattern for masculine and neuter nouns but sometimes differ. Their endings are given below and the deviants are bolded.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!b_CP!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F70fd396b-131d-441e-8185-41b6edad0847_1294x612.png)

We see here that most of the endings here are familiar from the thematic endings of nouns and hence they are unbolded. It is okay if you remember only the unbolded endings for now and gradually imbibe the deviant endings in context and they will anyways be reviewed in a future chapter that covers pronouns exclusively.

Sentences involving relative pronouns are difficult to translate at first if the cases of the two connecting pronouns do not match and hence let us spend some time looking at them in detail. Consider the following sentence:

> Rāma kills the man who kidnapped his spouse.

This is made up of two sentences:

- Main clause: Rāma kills **the man** (obj)

- Relative Clause: **Who** (**The man)** (sub) kidnapped his spouse

The connectors tell us that the same man whom Rama kills in the main clause is also the same man who kidnaps his spouse. So, as a rule, **the relative pronoun and the main pronoun always refer to the same entity.**

Here, the main clause talks about Rāma killing a man whereas the relative clause provides more information about the man by saying that the man did something. But, although the relative pronoun and main pronoun always refer to the same entity in a sentence, the grammatical roles performed by them in their own sentences are different and need not be the same. In this example, in its own relative clause (the man kidnapped his spouse), “the man” is the subject of the sentence (he is doing the kidnapping). But in the main clause (Rama kills the man), the same man is the object of the sentence (he is getting killed). So, the relative pronoun here takes the subject case and the main pronoun takes the object case and the sentence in Sanskrit comes out as

> **तं** रामः मारयति , **यः** कलत्रं अपाहरत्   
> Rama killed **him** **who** kidnapped \[his\] spouse (Literally in
> English).

So we see that :

> **While the gender and number of the relative and the main pronoun
> match in a sentence (because they refer to the same entity), their
> grammatical cases do not match in general and depend on what
> grammatical role they are playing in their own respective
> (relative/main) clauses.**

Let us dissect and analyse more such sentences to get more practice.

> He meets the man whose money he took.

- Main clause: He meets **the** **man** (obj) - use object singular form
  of the main pronoun **तं**

- Relative Clause: He took **the man’s** money (of) - use possessive
  singular form of the relative pronoun **यस्य**

- Sanskrit translation: **यस्य** धनं आददात् **तं** मिलति

> Rāma sees the boy who is reading a book.

- **Main clause:** Rāma sees **the boy** (object) → **तम् बालकम्**

- **Relative clause:** **who** (the boy) reads a book (subject) → **यः**

- **Sanskrit: यः** पुस्तकम् पठति, **तम्** बालकम् रामः पश्यति

> She calls the child whose brother is playing.

- **Main clause:** She calls **the child** (object) → **तम्**

- **Relative clause:** **whose (child’s)** brother plays (possessive) →
  **यस्य**

- **Sanskrit:** यस्य भ्राता क्रीडति, तम् बालकम् आह्वयति

> He goes to the place where children are playing.

- **Main clause:** He goes **to** **that place** (destination of motion)
  → **तत्र**

- **Relative clause:** **Where (at that place)** children are playing
  (destination) → **यत्र**

**Sanskrit: यत्र** बालकाः क्रीडन्ति, **तत्र** सः गच्छति

> Rāma calls the man to whom he gave money.

- **Main clause:** Rāma calls **the man** (object) → **तम्**

- **Relative clause:** **To whom (to the man)** he gave money (dative) →
  **यस्मै**

- **Sanskrit: यस्मै** सः धनम् अददात्, **तम्** रामः आह्वयति

> The teacher praises the student by whom the work was done.

- **Main clause:** The teacher praises **the student** (object) → **तम्**

- **Relative clause:** **By whom (by the student)** the work was done
  (instrumental) → **येन**

- **Sanskrit:** येन कार्यं कृतम्, तम् आचार्यः प्रशंसति

> He remembers the friend from whom he received help.

- **Main clause:** He remembers **the friend** (object neuter gender) →
  **तत्**

- **Relative clause:** **From whom (from the friend)** he received help
  (ablative) → **यस्मात्**

- **Sanskrit: यस्मात्** सः सहायम् आददात्, **तत्** मित्रम् स्मरति

> The king rewards the servant whose son studies in the village school.

- **Main clause:** The king rewards **the servant** (object) → **तम्**

- **Relative clause:** **Whose** **(the servant’s)** son studies…
  (genitive) → **यस्य**

- **Sanskrit : यस्य** पुत्रः ग्रामे विद्यालये पठति, **तम्** **दासम्** राजा
  पुरस्करोति

> She gives food to the old man who is sitting near the temple and
> watching people.

- **Main clause:** Arjuna gives food **to the old man** (dative) →
  **तस्मै**

- **Relative clause:** **Who (the old man)** is sitting and watching
  people (subject) → **यः**

- **Sanskrit: यः** मन्दिरस्य समीपे उपविश्य जनान् पश्यति, **तस्मै** अर्जुनः अन्नं
  ददाति

> Rāma helps the boy whose mother is working in the house and preparing
> food for guests.

- **Main clause:** Rāma helps **the boy** (object) → **तम्**

- **Relative clause:** **Whose mother (the boy’s mother)** is working
  and preparing for the guests (genitive) → **यस्य**

- **Sanskrit: यस्य** माता गृहे कार्यं करोति अतिथिभ्यः भोजनं च पचति, **तम्**
  बालकम् रामः सहायं करोति

> The teacher shows the students the book in which a wise man shows many
> examples for understanding of life.

- **Main clause:** The teacher shows **that book** (object) → **तत्**

- **Relative clause:** **In which** **(in the book)** a wise man
  explains (locative) → **यस्मिन्**

- **Sanskrit: यस्मिन्** पुस्तके बुद्धिमान् पुरुषः जीवनस्य बोधाय अनेकानि उदाहरणानि
  दर्शयति, **तत्** आचार्यः छात्रान् दर्शयति

> The man speaks with the friends who live in that village.

- **Main clause:** He speaks **with the friend**s (instrumental, plural)
  → **तैः**

- **Relative clause:** **Who (the friends)** live in the village
  (subject, plural) → **ये**

- **Sanskrit: ये** ग्रामे वसन्ति, **तैः** वदति

> The teacher gives knowledge to the students in whom there is interest
> in learning.

- **Main clause:** The teacher gives knowledge **to the students**
  (dative, plural) → **तेभ्यः**

- **Relative clause:** **In whom** (in the students) there is interest
  (locative, plural) → **येषु**

- **Sanskrit: येषु** अध्ययनस्य रुचिः अस्ति, तेभ्यः आचार्यः ज्ञानं ददाति

> People respect the man from whom they receive guidance in difficult
> times.

- **Main clause:** People respect **the man** (object, singular) →
  **तम्**

- **Relative clause:** **From whom (from the man)** they receive
  guidance (ablative, singular) → **यस्मात्**

- **Sanskrit: यस्मात्** कठिनेषु कालेषु मार्गदर्शनं प्राप्नुवन्ति, **तम्** जनाः
  सम्मानयन्ति

> Literal Sentence : Rāvaṇa was killed by Rāma to whom there are three
> brothers.  
>   
> Rāvaṇa was killed by Rāma who has three brothers (Sanskrit does not
> have verb that means “have” (refer syntax points in the second
> article)

- **Main clause**: Rāvaṇa was killed **by Rāma** - **तेन (रामेण**)

- **Relative clause**: **Of whom**, there are three brothers - **यस्य**

- **Sanskrit** : रावणः **तेन** हतः आसीत् **यस्य** त्रयः सहोदराः सन्ति

> He writes with the pen which falls on the ground.

- **Main clause:** He writes **with the pen** (by, singular, neuter) →
  तेन लेखनदण्डेन

**Relative clause:**  
Which (the pen) falls on the ground (subject, neuter/masculine) → यः

**Sanskrit:**  
यः भूमौ पतति, तेन दण्डेन सः लिखति

**Observe carefully again :** In every sentence:

**Relative** pronoun (यः…) and **main** pronoun (सः/तम्…) **refer to** the **same person/thing**

Hence, their **gender & number match**

Their grammatical case however can **differ** depending on their grammatical role in each clause

> एकं नित्यं विमलम् अचलम् सर्वधीसाक्षीभूतम् भावातीतं त्रिगुणरहितं सद्गुरुं तं नमामि (I bow
> down) ॥

अखण्डमण्डलाकारं व्याप्तं **येन,** चराचरम् तत्पदं दर्शितं **येन,** **तस्मै** श्रीगुरवे नमः॥

Main clause: तस्मै श्रीगुरवे नमः  
→ Salutations to that Guru

Relative clause 1: **येन** चराचरम् अखण्डमण्डलाकारं व्याप्तम्  
→ By whom the entire moving and unmoving universe is pervaded

Relative clause 2: **येन** तत्पदम् दर्शितम्  
→ By whom that pada (tat tvam asi) is shown

Connection: येन (instrumental) ↔ तस्मै (dative)

Full meaning: Salutations to that Guru by whom the entire universe is pervaded and by whom that tat tvam asi pada is shown.

**अज्ञानतिमिरान्धस्य ज्ञानाञ्जनशालाकया चक्षुः उन्मीलितं येन तस्मै श्रीगुरवे नमः॥**

Main clause: **तस्मै** श्रीगुरवे नमः  
→ Salutations to that Guru

Relative clause: **येन** अज्ञानतिमिरान्धस्य ज्ञानाञ्जनशालाकया चक्षुः ज्ञानाञ्जनशालाकया उन्मीलितम्  
→ **By whom** the eyes of one blinded by ignorance are opened with the collyrium-stick of knowledge

Connection: येन ↔ तस्मै

Full meaning: Salutations to that Guru by whom the eyes of the ignorant are opened.

**स्थावरं जङ्गमं व्याप्तं येन कृत्स्नं चराचरम् तत्पदं दर्शितं येन तस्मै श्रीगुरवे नमः॥**

Relative clause 1: **येन** कृत्स्नं चराचरम् स्थावरं जङ्गमं व्याप्तम्  
→ By whom all moving and unmoving beings are pervaded

Relative clause 2: **येन** तत्पदम् दर्शितम्  
→ By whom that tat tvam asi pada is shown

Connection: येन ↔ तस्मै

Full meaning: Salutations to that Guru by whom all beings are pervaded and the supreme truth is revealed.

**चित्तरूपेण परिव्याप्तं त्रैलोक्यं सचराचरम् तत्पदं दर्शितं येन तस्मै श्रीगुरवे नमः॥**

Relative clause 1: **येन** चित्तरूपेण त्रैलोक्यं सचराचरं परिव्याप्तम्  
→ By whom the three worlds are pervaded by the form of consciousness

Relative clause 2: **येन** तत्पदम् दर्शितम्  
→ By whom that state is shown

Connection: येन ↔ तस्मै

Full meaning: Salutations to that Guru by whom the three worlds are pervaded as consciousness and the supreme state is revealed.

**सर्वश्रुतिशिरोरत्नसमुद्भासितमूर्तये वेदान्ताम्बूजसूर्याय तस्मै श्रीगुरवे नमः॥**

(No explicit relative clause; descriptive phrases)

सर्वश्रुतिशिरोरत्नसमुद्भासितमूर्तये  
→ To the one whose form shines with the jewel of the Upaniṣads

वेदान्ताम्बूजसूर्याय  
→ To the sun of the lotus of Vedānta

Full meaning: Salutations to that Guru who shines as the essence of the Upaniṣads and is the sun of Vedānta.

**(यः) चैतन्यः शाश्वतः शान्तः व्योमातीतः निरञ्जनः बिन्दुनादकलातीतः तस्मै श्रीगुरवे नमः॥**

→ One who is consciousness, eternal, peaceful, beyond space, pure, beyond sound and form

Full meaning: Salutations to that Guru who is pure consciousness and beyond all limitations.

Relative clause: **यः** ज्ञानशक्तिसमारूढः तत्त्वमालाविभूषितः भुक्तिमुक्तिप्रदाता च  
→ Who is established in knowledge and power, adorned with truth, and gives both enjoyment and liberation

Connection: यः ↔ तस्मै

Full meaning: Salutations to that Guru who grants both worldly enjoyment and liberation.

→ By whom the fuel of karma accumulated over many births is burnt by the fire of self-knowledge

Connection: येन ↔ तस्मै

Full meaning: Salutations to that Guru who burns accumulated karma through knowledge.

**\[यस्य\] शोषणं भवसिन्धोः च \[यस्य\] प्रापणं सारसम्पदः यस्य पादोदकं सम्यक् तस्मै श्रीगुरवे नमः॥**

→ Whose foot-water dries the ocean of worldly existence and leads to true wealth

Connection: यस्य ↔ तस्मै

Full meaning: Salutations to that Guru whose grace removes worldly suffering and grants true wealth.

**न गुरोः अधिकं तत्त्वं न गुरोः अधिकं तपः तत्त्वज्ञानात् परं नास्ति; तस्मै श्रीगुरवे नमः॥**

Statements (no relative clause):

न गुरोः अधिकं तत्त्वम् → No principle \[is\] higher than the Guru  
न गुरोः अधिकं तपः → No austerity \[is\] greater than the Guru  
तत्त्वज्ञानात् परं नास्ति → Nothing \[is\] beyond the knowledge of truth

**(यः) मद्नाथः श्रीजगन्नाथः मद्गुरुः श्रीजगद्गुरुः मदात्मा सर्वभूतात्मा तस्मै श्रीगुरवे नमः॥**

Relative clause:

यः मद्नाथः श्रीजगन्नाथः मद्गुरुः श्रीजगद्गुरुः मदात्मा सर्वभूतात्मा  
→ One who is my Lord, the Lord of the universe, my Guru, and the Self of all beings

Connection: यः ↔ तस्मै

Full meaning: Salutations to that Guru who is the Self of all beings.

**गुरुः आदिः अनादिः च गुरुः परमदैवतम् गुरोः परतरं नास्ति; तस्मै श्रीगुरवे नमः॥**

Statements (no relative clauses ):

गुरुः आदिः अनादिः च → Guru is the beginning and without beginning  
गुरुः परमदैवतम् → Guru is the supreme deity  
गुरोः परतरं नास्ति → Nothing higher than Guru

Also, the Upaniṣads shorten the main relative clause connectors like ततः and तत्र to तत् very often ; be aware of that. इतरः means “another one” (cognate with the Latin word *iter* that gives us words like *iteration*). इतरः इरतं पश्यति means “*one sees the other*”. # More Complex Relative Clauses

In this small section, we will see few examples of sentences that involve nested relative clauses.

> Sanskrit to English : **यतः** भूरीणि कर्माणि विभागशः श्रोतव्यानि सन्ति ,
> **ततः** सारं समुद्धृत्य ब्रूहि **तत्** मे **येन** आत्मा सुप्रसीदति

भूरीणि = many, विभागशः = part by part, श्रोतव्यानि = have to be heard, समुद्धृत्य = after selecting, ब्रूहि मे = tell me, सुप्रसीदति = becomes happily situated

Using the vocabulary help above, try translating the big sentence from Sanskrit to English.

> English to Sanskrit : **If** he does **as** he said, **then**, it will
> rain for sure (निश्वयेन)

Note : Use present tense for “will rain”(वर्षति) in your Sanskrit translation

> Sanskrit to English : हे भगवन् प्रभो, अद्य त्वं **तान्** भक्तान् जिहाससि
> **येषाम्** न अन्यत् \[अस्ति\] तव पादाम्बुजात् शरण्यम्

अद्य = today, त्वं = you, जिहाससि = want to leave, पादाम्बुजात् = than lotus-feet (here, आत् = than), तव = your, शरण्यम् = refuge

Using the vocabulary help above, try translating the big sentence from Sanskrit to English.

> English to Sanskrit : The Yajna **that** you look after from all
> sides, O Agni, **it** goes unto the devas

You look after = परिभवसि, From all sides = विश्वतः

Using the vocabulary help above, try translating the big sentence from English to Sanskrit.

> Sanskrit to English : **यः** छात्रः आचार्येन **यत्** ज्ञानं दत्तं , **तस्य**
> मूल्यं जानाति , सः एव जीवनस्य तस्मिन् स्थाने यस्मिन् अन्ये जनाः भ्रमन्ति सम्यक् निर्णयं
> करोति ।

स्थान = situation / position, सम्यक् निर्णयं करोति = makes good decision, अन्ये जनाः भ्रमन्ति = other people become confused

Using the vocabulary help above, try translating the big sentence from Sanskrit to English.

> English to Sanskrit : The words said by Lord Krishna are befitting
> **for those** Pandavas by **whose** good qualities, He was attracted.

Said = उक्त, Word = वचन, Befitting = युक्त Good quality = गुणः, Attracted = आकर्षित

> Sanskrit to English : **तस्य** नारायणस्य गणनाम् कः अन्यः अर्हति इह, **यः**
> कविः पार्थिवानि अमात् , (**यः**) अस्कभ्नोत् स्वपादेन त्रिलोकम्, **यस्मात्**
> त्रिसाम्यसदनात् उरुकम्पयानम् अस्ति ?

गणना = counting/measuring/estimating, इह = here, पार्थिव = lands, माति = measures, स्कभ्नोति = supports, त्रिसाम्यसदन = abode of three-fold-harmony, उरुकम्पयानम् = the great cosmic motion

> English to Sanskrit : The scholar **who** wrote **the** book **that**
> every student in the university admires, (**he**) is the same
> **person** **whom** the students approached (**then**), **when** they
> got a difficult problem which they did not solve.

Scholar = पण्डितः, Writes = लिखति, Book = पुस्तकम्, University = विश्वविद्यालय, Student = छात्रः, Admires = प्रशंसां ददाति, He is the Same = सः एव, Person = पुरुषः, Approaches = उपगच्छति, Got = प्राप्नुवन्ति, Solve = उत्तरयन्ति =\> (past : अ + उत्तरयन्ति = ??)

Don’t worry if you are stuck at some places. Try hard and do not give up. If you still cannot figure out, peep and look at the answers at the end.

> O God Lord, today you want to leave those devotees of whom there is no
