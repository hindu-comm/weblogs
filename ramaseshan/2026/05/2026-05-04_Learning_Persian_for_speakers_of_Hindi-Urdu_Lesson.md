# Main Clause & Relative Clause

Up until this point, we have studied how to form very basic Persian sentences. I don't mean that they are basic just because of our limited vocabulary or our lack of fancy verbs (in fact we covered a lot of tenses and talked a lot about how to form and recognise compound verbs); But, what I mean when I say that they are basic is that they are not powerful enough to express complex interconnected ideas. We can describe the action of one subject, and we can chain several simple actions or sentences together with words using conjunctions like ō و and یا *yā,* form a complex nominal (noun) idea by using a complex e-z̈afat nesting; but ultimately, we're still just considering one subject and one action at a time per sentence. We have no good way to relate two ideas to each other. Here are simple (not many words) but yet interconnected sentences that we simply cannot say at this stage:

- I go if you go.

- You smile when he stands.

- I go where you remember.

- You see the man who goes.

The answer is this: we split each sentence into two parts and use some new words to relate them. We usually call one part the **relative clause** because it is only important *relative* to the other part; here, the word **clause** means about the same thing as the word “sentence”, except that it is a sentence that forms a part of a larger sentence. We also call the other part the **main clause** because it can stand on its own. Observe the following sentences.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zDVb!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F97720535-9597-4d6e-8726-7703c2527063_2048x1039.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zDVb!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F97720535-9597-4d6e-8726-7703c2527063_2048x1039.png "Screenshot 2025-10-03 at 22.10.53.png")

Here, the sentences in bold are the main clauses because they tell the main matter of the sentence. The italicised parts are the relative clauses that give more information about the main clause by telling the time or place or condition or reason or description or manner with which main clause is happening. For example :

> **he cooks hot snacks** → main matter (main clause)  
> \[when\] *it rains* → time in which the main matter happens (relative
> clause)
>
> **He gets happiness** → main matter (main clause)  
> \[who\] *remembers the name of God* → describing / characterising the
> “he” in the main matter (relative clause)
>
> **he did not go** → main matter (main clause)  
> \[because\] *it rained* → giving the reason why the main matter
> happened (relative clause)
>
> **the citizens behave** → main matter (main clause)  
> \[as\] *the king behaves* → describing the manner in which the main
> matter happens (relative clause)
>
> **he does not go** → main matter (main clause)  
> \[if\[ *it rains* → gives the condition under which the main matter
> happens (relative clause)

How do we express ideas like these in Persian? In the first section of this entire article, we will learn how to form sentences of each type in Persian. In this entire article, the main clauses are in bold and the relative clauses are in italics for your convenience. Of course, real texts do not have this luxury and hence you have to gradually let this crutch go at some point by practising to separately identify them yourself without any visual cues.

# Restrictive Relative Pronouns

In these type of sentences, the relative clause restricts the noun and narrows it down to someone or something that satisfies a certain criterion. In English, this is exemplified by phrases like

- **the man** *who came ……*

- **the letter** *that arrived yesterday ……*

- **the student** *whom I was talking about* …..

In all these phrases, the relative clause in italics narrows down the noun in the main clause in bold that precedes it. English uses “who/that/whom” to signal that what follows is a relative clause. What does Persian do?

Here, we need to ask two things. Consider a sentence like

- **the student** *whom I was praising yesterday* **is giving a
  presentation today**

The italics is the relative clause and the bold part is the main clause. Splitting them, we get

- Main Clause : **the student is giving a presentation today**

- Relative Clause : *I was praising the student* (whom) *yesterday*

Both the “whom” and “student” refer to the same entity. The same student about whom the teacher was talking about is the one who was giving a presentation today. But even though they refer to the same person, the grammatical role played by “the student” is different in both the clauses. In the main clause, the student is the subject of the verb and is giving a presentation. In the relative clause, it is the direct object (in fact, definite direct object !!) of the verb praising because the “I” is the subject in the relative clause who is doing the verb of praising and the student is the object or direct recipient of the verb praising. The particle “whom” that signals the beginning of the relative clause describing the student is “whom” and not “who” to precisely convey this object role in the relative clause.

In Persian, the particle, that marks the beginning of the relative clause regarding a noun that immediately precedes it, is ***ki*** که, and is the same always ; whether it is a boy or girl or non-living thing. But, one should be mindful about the difference in role that the same noun plays in the relative clause vis-a-vis the main clause to ensure that the grammar is tight. Let us now how see how Persian forms these kind of phrases.

> **the man** *who came*

The equivalent of the English’s “who” is **ki** که. But, in addition, since the relative clause is constraining the noun by narrowing it down in a certain category, the noun “the man” in the main clause gets an extra “i” at the end (do not confuse this with the indefinite article ; this restrictive marker is always immediately followed by the relative clause marker **ki** که). So, the phrase here in Persian would be

> **مردی** *که* *آمد*  
> **mard-i** *ki* *āmad*  
> The man-(i) who came

Let us now express a full sentence.

> **مردی** *که آمد* **هندی** **است**  
> **mard-i** *ki* *āmad* **hindi ast**  
> The man-(i) who came is Indian

To give another more complex example

> **دخترهای** *که دیروز شما را دیدند* **به من گفتند**   
> **dukhtar-hā-i** *ki dīrōz shumā rā dīdand* **bah man guftand  **
> The daughters who saw you yesterday said to me

In all these cases, the main and relative pronouns are subjects in their own clauses. What if the noun in the main clause is an object but still a subject in the relative clause? Let us see an example for that below :

> **مردی را** *که آمد* **ندیدم  
> mard-i rā** *ki āmad* **nadīdam  **
> Literally : **the man** *who came*, **I did not see**  
> Naturally : **I did not see the man** `(definite direct object)` *who
> came*

Since Persian always wants to keep the verb last, the word order is convoluted compared to English. Note here that in the main clause “**I did not see the man**”, it is the definite direct object of the sentence and hence it is getting the marker را rā. But, in the relative clause “*who* (the man) *came*”, the man is the subject of the verb coming and hence nothing changes the “ki” که here. Let us see some more examples.

> New verb : شناختن (شناس) shinākhtan (shinās) “to recognise, to know”  
> **دخترهای** **را** *که آنجا زندگی میکند* **نمیشناسم  
> dukhtarhā**-**i rā** *ki ānjā zindagī mēkunad* **namēshināsam  **
> Literally : **The daughters**, *who live here*, **I do not know**
> \[them\]  
> Naturally : **I do not know the daughters** *who live here*
>
> New verb : فرستادن (فرست) firistādan (firist) “to send, to dispatch”  
> **نامه ی را** *که روی میز بود* **فرستادم  
> nāma-i rā** *ki rōy-e mēz būd* **firistādam  **
> Literally : **The letter**, *that was on the table*, **I sent**
> \[it\]  
> Naturally : **I sent the letter** *that was on the table*

How about the case when the relative pronoun itself is the object of the sentence (like English : **the student** ***whom I was praising*** **is giving a presentation today**). English just changes the connecting relative pronoun marker “who” to “whom” (for human and animate nouns) to convey this change. This is the point it gets confusing and deviates from English and Hindi-Urdu. Persian has a strict principle that the connectors like “ki***”*** که do not get modified or receive any endings, and they stay constant. Note that English and Hindi do not have any qualms regarding this and their relative pronouns easily change forms and take endings. For example,

> English : who → whom → whose → to whom → from whom → with whom   
> Hindi - Urdu : jō → jis ne → jin ko → jis se → jis kē bāre mēṃ → jis
> kē

So, the marker “ki” که cannot change forms or take any extra prepositions or suffixes like above. So, what does Persian do? It puts an extra dummy pronoun “ān” آن (meaning : that) wherever appropriate in the relative clause, and makes it take all the necessary case endings. Let us see an example

> **I sent the letter** *that I wrote yesterday*

Here, the letter is the object for the relative clause “*I wrote that* \[letter\] *yesterday*. Persian takes the pronoun ān آن (meaning : that) and slaps the ending rā on that. So, we have

> **نامه ی را** *که دیروز آن را نوشتم* **فرستادم**   
> **nāma-i rā** *ki dīrōz {ān rā} navishtam* **firistādam  **
> Literally : **The letter**, *that yesterday , {that definite object} I
> wrote*, **I sent \[it\]**  
> Naturally : I sent the letter that I wrote yesterday

Consider another example.

> پولی را که به دوستش آن را داده است پس میگیرد  
> **pūl-i rā** *ki bah dōstesh ān rā dādá ast* **pas mēgīrad**  
> Literally : **The money**, *that to his friend, {that definite object}
> he has given*, **he takes back**   
> Naturally : He takes back the money that he has given to his friend

This same style is also followed when the noun in the relative clause demands some other preposition or a suffix. For example, consider the sentence

> **The city** *from which I have come* **is far**

Here, the city is the subject in the bolded main clause but is actually the object of the preposition “from” in the relative clause (*I have come from which* \[the city\]). Persian as usual does not do anything to the که ki marker but inserts a dummy “that” (آن ān) and lets it take the preposition. So, we have

> **شهری** *که از آن آمده ام* **دور است**  
> **shahr-i** *ki az ān āmadá am* **dūr ast  **
> Literally : **The city** *which {from that} I have come* **is far**   
> Naturally : **The city** *from which I have come* **is far**

This holds for other prepositions as well. Let us see some more examples.

> **اطاقی** *که در آن نشسته ایم* **خیلی کوچک است**  
> **otāq-i** *ki {dar ān} nishastá ēm* **khaylē kōchak ast**  
> Literally : The room which {in that} we have sat is very small  
> Naturally : The room in which we have sat is very small

When the preposition is complex to require an e-z̈afat to connect to its object, then instead of “ān” آن, Persian uses the pronominal suffixes to connect to it. That is:

> Preposition-e-ān → preposition-pronominal suffix  
> Noun-e-ān → Noun-pronominal suffix

An example to illustrate this is given below.

> New compound verb : صحبت کردن (کن) ṣuḥbat kardan (kun) “to converse”  
> **کتابی** *که* *درباره اش صحبت میکردیم* **کجا است**؟  
> **kitāb-i** ki *{darbāre-ash} ṣuḥbat mēkardēm* **kujā ast**?  
> Literally : **The book**, *which {about that} we were talking*,
> **where is \[it\]**?  
> Naturally : **Where is the book** *about which we were talking about*?
>
> زنانی که دخترهایشان به تهران رفته اند دلواپسند  
> **zanān-i** *ki {dukhtarhā(y)-eshān} bah tahrān raftá and*
> **dilvāpasand  **
> Literally : **The women** *whose {their daughters} to Tehran have
> come* **are worried**  
> Naturally : The women whose daughters have come to Tehran are worried

# Descriptive Relative Pronouns

Consider the following two sentences in English

> **The Ahmad**, *who came yesterday*, **is here**  
> **Ahmad**, *who came yesterday*, **is here**

In the first sentence, the relative clause constrains who the preceding Ahmad is. Thus, it restricts the Ahmad to a specific category. Thus, it is called a **restrictive relative clause**. All the sentences that we formed so far in the previous section are of this category. But, in the second sentence, the relative clause just happens to describe what Ahmad did which is that he came yesterday. This is more of a circumstantial descriptor rather than a description constraint. It is hence called a **non-restrictive** or **descriptive** relative clause. How does Persian distinguish between these two usages? Through the tiny marker ی -i. When the marker is there (as with the examples of previous section), it signifies a restrictive relative clause. If it is not there, then it signifies a non-restrictive and just circumstantially descriptive relative clause. In Persian, the above two sentences would look as follows :

> **The Ahmad**, *who came yesterday*, **is here  
> احمدی ک**ه *دیروز آومد* **اینجا است  
> aḥmad-i** *ki dīrōz āmad* **īnjā ast**
>
>   
> **Ahmad**, *who came yesterday*, **is here**   
> **احمد** *که دیروز آومد* **اینجا است  
> aḥmad** *ki dīrōz āmad* **īnjā ast**

For non-living nouns, English uses “that” and “which” to distinguish between these two usages (restrictive vs descriptive). Again, let us see some examples.

> **I put out the lamp** *that* *was smoking*. (Restrictive : what kind
> of lamp I put out)  
> **چراغی** **را** *که دود میزد* **خاموش** **کردم**  
> **chirāgh-i rā** *ki dūd mēzad* **khāmōsh kardam**
>
> **I put out the lamp** *which* *was smoking*. (Descriptive : what was
> the lamp doing?)  
> **چراغ** **را** *که دود میزد* **خاموش** **کردم**  
> **chirāgh rā** *ki dūd mēzad* **khāmōsh kardam**
>
> New compound verbs :   
> **خاموش** **کردن** (کن) khāmōsh kardan (kun) “to make quiet”  
> **دود** **زدن** (زن) dūd zadan (zan) “to hit \[with\] smoke”

# The Imperative & Archaic Optative Moods

Till now, we have seen 14 tenses for Persian verbs. All of these were used to express some action that factually happened at some point in time in some manner. But, if we have to start forming complex sentences like those involving relative clauses (that we saw previously), we need to express wishes, hypotheticals, conditions or commands. For that, we are going to learn four more verb forms in this article. They are :

- Imperative

- Present Subjunctive

- Past Subjunctive

- Archaic Optative

### Imperative

The imperative is the command form that is used to give commands. You can give commands only to those who are in front of you. So, the imperative in Persian has only second person singular and plural forms. In English, the imperative verbs do not have any endings on them like “*Go*!” or “*Do*!” or “*Sit* *down*”.

In Persian, for simple verbs, the second person singular imperative does not have any personal ending as well but has a prefix which is “bi” ب , which we already encountered to form the archaic simple past tense. But this time, for the imperative, this prefix is attached to the present stem. So, to form the second person singular imperative, just slap the “bi’ ب prefix on the present stem and that is it - you are ready to give orders (***dastūrhā*** دستورها) in Persian!

> بکن *bikun* = Do !  
> بنشین *binishīn* = Sit down !  
> بزن *bizan* = Hit !

When followed by a “o” or “u”, the prefix “bi” changes to “bu” or even becomes silent but this reflects only in pronunciation and not in the Perso-Arabic script because it does not capture short vowels.

> بگذار *b(u)guẓar* = Put !  
> بگذر *b(u)guzar* = Pass (by) !

When followed by a present stem that starts with a vowel, the imperative prefix becomes “bi” only but this time the “i” is shown in writing in the Perso-Arabic script.

> بیا *biā* = Come!  
> بیانداز *biandāz* = Throw!  
> بیفت *biuft* = Fall (down)!

The plural imperative is formed by adding the familiar second person plural personal ending ید ēd to the singular imperative.

> بگیرید *bigīrēd =* (You all) Take!  
> بروید *birawēd =* (You all) Go!  
> بیاید *biāēd* = (You all) Come!  
> ببرید *bibarēd* = (You all) Take/Carry (it)!

How are imperatives of compound verbs formed? Turns out Persian feels that prefixing is too much work for compound verbs. There is no prefix and this is just the naked present stem of the main verb with the noun element. The fact that a present stem is naked (without any mē prefix for other indicative tenses) is supposed to tell you that the verb is imperative for compound verbs. Some examples are given below.

> گم شو *gum shaw* = Become lost! (like English : Get lost!)  
> در را باز کن *dar rā bāz kun* = Open the door!

The negative imperative is formed by adding the prefix “na”ن, which is the same prefix used to negative other indicative verbs. This time though, even for simple verbs, the negative imperatives do not take the “bi” prefix. Imagine the imperative “bi” prefix as so shy that it will stay only with simple positive verbs. It goes away when any other prefix like “na” or any other noun comes before the verb (to compound with it).

> نرو naraw = Don’t go  
> گم نشوید *gum nashawēd =* (You all) don’t get lost!  
> در را باز نکن *dar rā bāz nakun* = Don’t open the door!  
> نیاید *nāyēd* = (You all) don't come!  
> نیندازید *nayandāzēd* = Don’t throw (it)!

In Classical Persian poetry though, instead of “na”, a special prefix “ma” م was used to negate imperatives. Otherwise, everything else remains the same.

> مرو *maraw* = Don’t go!  
> گمان مبر کی *gumān mabar ki* = Don’t think that
>
> Verb : گمان بردب (بر) ***gumān burdan (bar)*** “think/assume” (lit :
> “to carry opinion”)  
>   
> Note : Do not confuse **گم شدن (شو)** ***gum shudan (shaw)*** “to
> become lost” with **گمان** **بردن** ***gumān burdan (bar)*** “to
> assume” or “to think”

The imperative of two verbs is formed very irregularly. They are the verbs of being and having.

- The imperative of بودن (است) *būdan (ast)* is formed by a special stem
  **باش** ***bāsh*** that never takes the bi-prefix.

> مرد **باش** ! گریه نکن  
> *mard **bāsh** ! girya nakun*  
> **Be** a man ! Don’t cry !
>
> فعلاً همینجا **باشید**  
> *fa’lan hamīnjā **bāshēd***  
> (For) now, be right there

- The imperative of the verb داشتان (دار) *dāshtan (dar)* is irregularly
  formed from combining its past participle with the special imperative
  stem of *būdan*. This occurs only for the simple verb.

> این را **داشته باش**  
> *īnrā **dāshtá bāsh***  
> **Hold** (**have**) this !
>
> این نکته را در نظر **داشته** **باش**  
> *īn nukta dar naẓar dāshtà bāsh*  
> **Keep (have)** this point in \[your\] mind (view)

### Optative

This mood is used to convey wishes and is archaic. It is still used in certain formulaic expressions but is no longer productive in the modern language but was a regular process in archaic Persian. The optative is often formed in the third person singular. Take the present form of the verb and replace the “ad” د personal ending by “ād” اد (vowel lengthening) and you get the optative third person singular. The optative is used to convey wishes and hopes.

> ماند *mānad* (Present) = It remains   
> **ماناد** ***mānād*** (Optative) = May it remain !
>
> آید āyad (Present) = It comes   
> **آیاد** **āyād** (Optative) = May it come

The optative of the verb “to be” again is irregular and formed from a special short stem “b” ب. Hence, its third person singular optative expression would be **باد** ***bād*** (may it be). This word is famous in many familiar formulaic expressions in Persian and Urdu like

> زنده باد *zindá bād* = May he/she be alive (Long live!!)  
> مبارک باد *mubārak bād* = May he/she be blessed (Bless you!)  
> مرده باد *murdá bād* = May he be dead (Drop dead !!)

A couple of examples from poetry are given below.

> که رستم منم کم **ماناد** نام ؛ **نشیناد** بر مأتمم پور سام  
> *ki Rustam man-am, k(i) am mamānād nām ; nishēnād bar ma’tam-am pūr-e
> (pusar-e) Sām*  
> For I am Rustam, **may** \[my\] name **not endure** ; **May** the son
> of Sam **sit** at my funeral
>
> چونین داد پاسخ که این نیست داد ؛ بدین روز خرشید روشن **مباد**  
> *chūnēn dād pāsukh ki “īn nēst dād ; bidēn rōz khurshīd rawshan
> **mabād**”*   
> Thus, he gave reply (lit : justification) “This is not just ; On this
> day, **may** the sun **not** shine (literally : **may** the sun
> **not** **be** bright)
>
> \- Shāhnāma

Note that the archaic negative prefix for optative is “ma” which is same as the archaic negative prefix of the imperative that we saw in the last section. The optative is also formed occasionally from other singular present forms by lengthening the “a” in the personal ending to “ā”. The optative is no longer used and productive in Modern Persian but its usage is familiar in poetry and in formulaic expressions like we saw above. Next to cover are just the two tenses of the subjunctive moods which are important in Persian. Before that, we need to analyse certain relationships.

# Types of Compound Relationships

We saw that few verbs in Persian are simple verbs that stand alone but most are compound verbs that take a non-verbal element along with them. Compound verbs can be further classified based on the strength of the relationship between the non-verbal element and the verbal element. Why this matters is because the **type of compound** determines:

- how tightly the two parts behave

- whether they can be separated

- how the **subjunctive** is formed

### Close Compounds

Here, the non-verbal element is usually a **particle, prefix, or preposition-like element**, and the compound behaves almost like a **single verb**. These are **tight units** and are rarely broken. Some important examples are

> بر داشتن — to pick up  
> **bar dāshtan (dār-)**
>
> در آوردن — to take out  
> **dar āvardan (āvar-)**
>
> باز کردن — to open  
> **bāz kardan (kon-)**
>
> بر گشتن — to return  
> **bar gashtan (gard-)**
>
> فرو رفتن — to sink  
> **furū raftan (rav-)**
>
> بر خوردن — to collide / encounter  
> **bar khwardan (khwar-)**

### Closed Fractured Compounds

Here, the non-verbal element is a **noun**, and this is the **most common type in Persian**. This noun can in turn, like any other normal noun :

- can take adjectives

- can take plural markers

- can take e-zāfat

- can be expanded

This is why we call it **fractured** — it can “open up internally”.

> کار کردن — to work  
> **kār kardan (kun-)  **
> Fractured usage example : کار کارگاه کردن **kār-e kārgāh kardan** — to
> do office work  
> Fractured usage example : کار خوب کردن **kār-e khub kardan** — to do
> good work
>
> صحبت کردن — to speak  
> **suhbat kardan (kun-)  **
> Fractured usage example : صحبت کارگاه کردن **suhbat-e kārgāh kardan**
> — to speak about office
>
> فکر کردن — to think  
> **fikr kardan (kun-)  **
> Fractured usage example : فکر خوب کردن **fikr-e khub kardan** — to
> think good
>
> جواب دادن — to answer  
> **javāb dādan (dah-)   **
> Fractured usage example : جواب سؤالم را دادن **jawāb-e sawāl-am rā
> dādan** — to answer my question

### Open Compounds

These only represent a loose connection between the verb and the non-verbal element and the complements are often adverbial. The non-verbal element is often:

- adverbial

- locative

- prepositional

The meaning is more **compositional** than lexical. These are **syntactically loose**:

- elements can move

- modifiers are free

- meaning is transparent

For example, we have :

> مدرسه رفتن **madrāsa raftan (raw)** — to go to school  
> کتاب خواندن **kitāb khwāndān (khwān)** — to read books

# The Present Subjunctive Verb

The two subjunctive verb forms in Persian (present, past) are used only as secondary verbs in Persian ; means that they are used for complementary constructions to give :

1.  more information about another main verb

2.  inside a relative clause that gives information about a main clause
    with a main verb

To illustrate the first usage, there are some verbs that require another verb to tell more information. Some examples :

> - I can …… (can do what???) I can **swim**
>
> - You must …. (must do what???) You must **drink**
>
> - He wants …. (wants to do what???) He wants **to eat**
>
> - It is possible for her …… (possible to do what???) It is possible
>   for her **to win**

Unlike in English, the infinitive (to do / کردن / kardan) cannot serve as complements of these verbs. For example, the following is not allowed :

> He wants **to eat** (literally translating to Persian) → او میخواهد
> ~~خوردن~~ **ō mēkhwāhad ~~khwardan~~**

Persian does not use the infinitive in this way as we saw in the last section of this previous article. Instead, it rephrases all of these sentences as a composite sentence where the complementary verb is put in a separate relative clause. The above four sentences in Persian, when literally translated, would be :

> - I can **swim** → (Persian : ) I can \[that\] I **swim**
>   ***(subjunctive verb)***
>
> - You must **drink** → (Persian : ) You must \[that\] you **drink**
>   ***(subjunctive verb)***
>
> - He wants **to eat** → (Persian : ) He wants \[that\] he **eats**
>   ***(subjunctive verb)***
>
> - It is possible for her **to win** → (Persian : ) It is possible
>   \[that\] she **wins** ***(subjunctive verb)***

This is how the above sentences would literally sound in Persian (no matter how awkward or ungrammatical they are in English) and the verb in the complementary clause (which are bolded above) would be put in the subjunctive mood if it is in the present tense. The verbs in the main clause (can / must / want / possible) that demand other verbs as their complements are called main verbs and put in any indicative tense that you have learnt so far and the verb in the relative clause that complement the main verbs are put in the subjunctive mood.

How to form the present subjunctive? Again, we have the present stem and the present tense personal endings. Instead of the prefix mē می, we either have :

- Simple Verbs : Prefix ب bi :: **بگیرم** → میگیرم :: mēgīram →
  **bigīram** (pres. subj)

- Closed Compounds : No Prefix in Subj :: **گم شوند**→ گم میشوند :: gum
  mēshawand → **gum** **shawand** (pres. subj)

- Fractured / Open Compounds : Prefix ب bi :: **حرف بزنم** → حرف میزنم
  :: ḥarf mēzanam → **ḥarf bizanam** (pres. subj)

- Negative Subjunctive : Prefix “na” ن is compulsory and it replaces any
  “bi” prefix if there

This almost is same of the imperative which takes the “bi” prefix for simple verbs and vanishes for compound verbs and for negatives. But the crucial difference is that the imperative is there only for the second person and the singular form of the second person imperative does not take any personal ending. So, the possibility to confuse between imperatives and subjunctives happens only for the second person plural case. Also like imperative, the subjunctive of **būdan (ast)** and **dāshtan (dār)** are specially formed using the irregular stems **bāsh** and **dāshtá bāsh** respectively.

Do not worry too much about the formation of the present subjective. First, learn to recognise them. They are either with the “bi” prefix or without any prefix, unlike the present indicative which has the “mē” prefix. Now, let us study all of its uses.

### Uses of the Present Subjunctive - I : Expressing Possibilities & Necessities

The complements of the following fixed impersonal verbs

- **شاید** ***shāyad*** (it is possible / maybe),

- ***bāyad*** **باید** (it is necessary / must / should),

- ***nabāyad*** (it is not necessary / should not) are the present
  subjunctive verbs.

> باید بروم  
> *bāyad birawam*  
> I must go (lit. It is necessary I do)
>
> شاید بمانید  
> *shāyad bimānēd*  
> You all may stay (lit. It is possible you stay)
>
> نباید بگویٔیم  
> *nabāyad bigōīm*  
> We should not say (lit. It is not necessary we say)
>
> شاید نیایند  
> *shāyad nayāyand*  
> Maybe they won’t come (lit. It is possible they don’t come)

### Uses of the Present Subjunctive - II : Expressing Desire, Ability, & Permission

The subjunctive also serves as the complement of the following verbs :

- **خواستن** (**خواه**) ***khwāstan (khwāh)*** want

- **توانستم** (**توان**) ***tawānistan (tawān)*** can, able

- **گذاشتن** (**گذار**) ***gużāshtan (gużār)*** allow, permit

> میخوستم بروم  
> *mēkhwāstam birawam*  
> I wanted to go (lit. I wanted I go)
>
> میتواند بیاید  
> *mētawānad biāyad*  
> He can come (lit. He can he come)
>
> پدرم نگذاشت بروم  
> *pidaram nagużāsht birawam*  
> My father did not allow me to go (lit. My father did not allow I go)

Sometimes, when the complement is a verb that takes objects and other non-verbs as complements, then the marker **که** **ki** (which we already saw as denoting the starting of a relative clause that beings with a relative pronoun) is added to indicate that a complementary clause has begun.

> پدرم نگذاشت که به خانه دوستم بروم  
> *pidaram nagużāsht ki bah khāna-ye dōstam birawam*  
> My father did not allow me to go to my friend’s house   
> (lit. My father did not allow that, to my friend’s house, I go

This is because the verb always has to come last in every clause in Persian. The main verb has to come last in the main clause and the complementary verb should come last in the end of the sentence where the complementary clause ends. If there is a change in the subject from the main clause to the relative clause (like in the previous example), then the marker **که ki** is compulsory.

> میخواهم که شما بیاید  
> *mēkhwāham ki shumā biāyēd*  
> I want you to come (lit. I want that you come)

### Uses of the Present Subjunctive - III : Independent Jussive & Deliberative Interrogative Use

The subjunctive when used alone as the main verb alone in a sentence can be translated as :

- “Let …” / “May …”

> برویم   
> *birawēm  *
> Let us go!
>
> خدا نکند  
> *khudā nakunad*  
> (Lit. Let God not do \[it\]) God forbid!
>
> خدا بیامرزدش  
> *khudā biāmurzad-esh*  
> Let God have mercy on him

- “What….should”

> چه کار کنم؟  
> *chi kār kunam?*  
> What should I do?
>
> چه بگوید  
> *chi bigōyad?*  
> What shall/should he say?
>
> برویم؟  
> *berawēm?*  
> Shall/Should we go?
>
> چرا نرویم؟  
> *chirā narawēm?*  
> Why shouldn’t we go?

### Uses of the Present Subjunctive - IV : Complements in Relative Clauses

Whenever in English, you use the conjunction “that” to create a relative clause that gives more information about the main clause, the verb in the relative clause is put in the present subjunctive mood.

> امکان دارد که نیایم  
> *imkān dārad ki nāyam*  
> It is possible that I won’t come (lit. It has possibility that I don’t
> come)
>
> لازم بود که بروید  
> *lāzim būd ki birawēd*  
> It was necessary that you go (lit. There was necessity that you go)
>
> احتمال دارد که همانجا بمانند   
> *iḥtimāl dārad ki hamānjā bimānand*  
> It is probable that they will stay here (lit. It has chance that they
> stay here)
>
> نیل ندارم که به شهر بروم  
> *mayl nadāram ki bah shahr birawam*  
> I am not inclined to go to the town (lit. I don’t have inclination
> that I go to town)
>
> قصد داشتند که آن کار را بکنند   
> *qaṣd dāshtand ān kār rā bukunand*  
> They had intention to do that work (lit. They had intention that they
> do that work)
>
> ترجیح میدهیم کی آن طور نباشد  
> *tarjīḥ mēdahēm ki ān ṭawr nabāshad*  
> We prefer that it not remain like that

### Uses of the Present Subjunctive - V : Commands, Hopes & Prohibitions

While the imperative is used for direct commands and prohibitions in the second person, the subjunctive is used for indirect commands and prohibitions and also for expressing hopes, opinions, worries, joys, and doubts.

> به او گفتم که بنشیند  
> *bah ō guftam ki binishīnad*  
> I told him to sit (lit. I told to him that he sit)
>
> فرمودند که ما نرویم  
> *farmūdand ki mā naravēm*  
> They ordered us not to go (lit. They ordered that we not go)
>
> امیدوارم که بازهم شما را ببینم  
> *umīdwāram ki bāz ham shumā rā bibīnam*   
> I hope that I will see you again (lit. I am hopeful that again I see
> you)
>
> امیدوار بودم که بتوانم بروم  
> *umīdwār būdam ki bitawānam birawam*  
> I hoped that I would be able to go (lit. I was hopeful that I can I
> go)
>
> شک دارم که بیایند یا نه  
> *shakk dāram ki biāyand yā na*  
> I doubt whether they would come or not (lit. I have doubt that they
> come or not)
>
> فکر میکنم که آنجاست  
> *fikr mēkunam ki ānjā-ast*  
> I think that he is here
>
> میترسم که او را پیدا نکنم  
> *mētarsam ki ō rā paydā nakunam*  
> I am afraid that I won’t find him/her
>
> خوشحال میشویم شما را ببنیم  
> *khushḥāl mēshawēm ki shumā rā bibīnēm*  
> We are happy to see you (lit. We become happy that we see you)

# The Past Subjunctive Verb

The formation of the past subjunctive is easy. Take the past participle and then add the present subjunctive of *būdan* to it.

> Example of past subjunctive : رفته باشم *raftá bāsham*

What are the uses of the past subjunctive verb? It does not have much use in modern Iranian Peraian but is very productive in the literary language.

### Uses of the Past Subjunctive – I : Hypothetical or Unreal Past

It is used for doubtful realisation of past verbs of necessity (must have), possibility (might have), desire, wishing, regret, etc in a complementary clause.

> باید رفته باشد  
> *bāyad raftá bāshad*  
> He must have gone
>
> شاید آمده باشند  
> *shāyad āmadá bāshand*  
> They might have come
>
> واقعهٔ مهمی باید پیش آمده باشد  
> *wāqa’-e ye muhammi bāyad pēsh āmadá bāshad*  
> An important event must have come up
>
> نمیتواند آن کار را کرده باشد   
> *namētawānad ān kār rā kardá bāshad*  
> He cannot have done that work (lit. He can not that he could have done
> that work)
>
> امیدوارم به شما (که) خش گذشته باشد  
> *ummīdwāram bah shumā (ki) khush gużishtá bāshad*  
> I hope that you had a good time (lit. I am hopeful in you that good
> time would pass)

### Uses of the Past Subjunctive – II : After Conjunctions of Condition & Concession

Normally, in clauses of conditions and concession where the main verb itself is put in the present subjunctive (because it is hypothetical), the complementary verb is put in the past subjunctive.

> به شرطی متوانید بروید که کارتان را کرده باشید  
> *bah sharṭi mētawānēd berawēd ki kāretān rā kardá bāshēd*  
> You can go provided you have done your work (lit. In a condition you
> can go that you may complete your work)
>
> نمیتوانید یاد گرفته باشید مگر این که مدرسه رفته باشید  
> *namētawanēd yād giraftá bāshēd magar īn ki madrasa raftá bāshēd*  
> You can’t have learnt unless you would have gone to school
>
> حتی اگر هم این کتاب را خدش نوشته باشد افکارش مال او نیست  
> *hatta agar ham īn kitāb rā khudash nivishtá bāshad, afkārash māl-e ō
> nēst  *
> Even though he might have written this book himself, the ideas of it
> are not his

### Uses of the Past Subjunctive – III : Literary and Archaic Narrative Style

In elevated or classical Persian (such as epic or poetic texts), the past subjunctive can be used to give a sense of:

- **uncertainty in narration**

- **distance from factual assertion**

- **stylistic elevation**

> چنان گفتی که گویی ندیده‌ای  
> chenān guftī ki gōyī nadīdá’ī  
> You spoke as if you had not seen

This usage is mostly restricted to **literary Persian**.

In modern standard Persian:

- The **past subjunctive is largely obsolete**

- Its functions are replaced by:

  - **present subjunctive**

  - **past perfect**

  - **indicative constructions**

# Forming Conditional Sentences

Now, let us see how to form sentences of the type that we saw in the motivating initial section of this article.

### 1. If … then (Conditional Sentences)

Persian uses **اگر (agar)** for “if”. The “then” is often **not explicitly stated**, but can be expressed using **آن‌گاه āngāh / پس pas**.

**Structure:**

**اگر (agar) + clause 1 ……….. (پس / آن‌گاه) clause 2**

⚠️ Note:

- The verb in the **if-** part of the clause is usually:

  - **present subjunctive** when the if-condition is irreal and
    hypothetical

  - **indicative** when the if-condition is already assumed to have
    happened

> اگر بیایی، خوشحال می‌شوم  
> *agar biyāyī, khushḥāl mēshawam*  
> If you come, I become happy
>
> اگر میدانید چرا نمیگویید؟  
> *agar mēdānēd, chirā namēgōēd?*  
> If you know (indicative - already know), why are you not telling?
>
> اگر او را ببینی، به من بگو  
> *agar ō rā bibīnī, bah man bigō*  
> If you see him, tell me (lit. tell to me)
>
> اگر دیر برسد، ما می‌رویم  
> *agar dir birisad, mā mērawēm  *
> If he arrives late, we go
>
> اگر وقت داشته باشی، آن‌گاه با هم صحبت کنیم  
> *agar vaqt dāshta bāshī, āngāh bā ham ṣuḥbat kunēm*  
> If you have time, then let us talk together

### 2. Where … there

Persian uses **هر جا (har jā)** or **جایی که (jāyī ke)**.

**Structure:**

**(jāyi ki) هر جا / جایی که (harjā) + clause ، آنجا + clause**

⚠️ Note:

- **هر جا** = general “wherever” (lit. anywhere)

- **جایی که** = specific “the place where” (lit. a place that)

> هر جا بروی، آنجا آرامش پیدا می‌کنی  
> *har jā birawī, ānjā ārāmesh paydā mēkunī*  
> Wherever you go, there you find peace
>
> جایی که آب هست، آنجا زندگی هست  
> *jāyī ki āb hast, ānjā zindagī hast*  
> Where there is water, there is life
>
> هر جا او برود، ما هم می‌رویم  
> *har jā ō birawad, mā ham mērawēm*  
> Wherever he goes, we also go

### 3. When … then

**Persian uses:**

- **وقتی (waqtī) (**lit. a time)

- **هر وقت (har waqt)** (whenever) (lit. any time)

**Structure:**

**(har waqt) وقتی / هر وقت (waqti) + clause …….. (then) clause**

⚠️ Note:

- “then” is usually **not expressed explicitly**

- sequence is understood from context

**Examples :**

> وقتی بیایی، شروع می‌کنیم  
> *waqtī biyāyī, shurūʿ mēkunēm*  
> When you come, we begin
>
> هر وقت او را ببینی، سلام برسان  
> *har waqt ō rā bibīnī, salām birisān*  
> Whenever you see him, convey greetings
>
> وقتی باران ببارد، زمین خیس می‌شود  
> *waqtī bārān bibārad, zamīn khīs mēshawad*  
> When it rains, the ground becomes wet

### 4. Until / As long as … till then

Persian uses:

- **تا (tā)** = until / till

- **تا وقتی که (tā vaqtī ke)** = until when (lit. till a time when)

- **تا زمانی که (tā zamānī ke)** = as long as (lit. till a time when)

**Structure:**

**تا / تا وقتی که + clause ……… clause**

⚠️ Note:

- verbs after **تا** are often in **subjunctive**

- especially for future reference

> تا بیایی، اینجا می‌مانم  
> *tā biyāyī, īnjā mēmānam*  
> Until you come, I stay here
>
> تا وقتی که او حرف نزند، ما صبر می‌کنیم  
> *tā waqtī ki ō ḥarf nazanad, mā ṣabr mēkunēm*  
> As long as he does not speak, we wait
>
> تا زمانی که کار تمام شود، نمی‌رویم  
> *tā zamānī ki kār tamām shawad, namērawēm*  
> Until the work is finished, we do not go

### 5. Because … therefore

Persian uses:

- **چون (chūn)** / **زیرا (zīrā)** = because

- **پس (pas)** / **بنابراین (banābarīn)** = therefore

**Structure:**

**چون / زیرا + clause …… پس / بنابراین + clause**

⚠️ Note:

- “therefore” is often **omitted**

- logical relation is understood

> چون دیر آمده، پس ما رفته‌ایم  
> *chūn dīr āmade, pas mā rafte’īm*  
> Because he came late, therefore we have gone
>
> زیرا او بیمار است، بنابراین نمی‌آید  
> *zīrā ō bīmār ast, banābarīn namēyāyad*  
> Because he is ill, therefore he does not come
>
> چون خسته بودم، زود خوابیدم  
> *chūn khasta būdam, zūd khwābīdam*  
> Because I was tired, I slept early

### 6. As … likewise

Persian uses:

- **همان‌گونه که (hamān-gūne ke)** = just as

- **چنان‌که (chinān-ke)** = as

- **همچنین (hamchinīn)** = likewise

**Structure:**

**همان‌گونه که / چنان‌که + clause …… همچنین + clause**

> همان‌گونه که گفتی، همچنین عمل کردم  
> *hamān-gūne ki guftī, hamchinīn ʿamal kardam*  
> As you said, likewise I acted
>
> چنان‌که او آمد، ما نیز آمدیم  
> *chinān-ki ō āmad, mā nīz āmadīēm*  
> As he came, so we also came
>
> همان‌طور که می‌خواهی، همان‌طور عمل می‌کنم  
> *hamān-tawr ki mēkhwāhī, hamān-tawr ʿamal mēkunam*  
> As you want, I act likewise

### 7. … otherwise …

Persian uses:

- **وگرنه (vagarna)** (lit. and if not)

- **در غیر این صورت (dar gheyr-e īn ṣūrat)** (lit. in not that face)

**Structure:**

**clause …… وگرنه / در غیر این صورت + clause**

> زود بیا، وگرنه دیر می‌رسیم  
> *zūd biyā, vagarna dīr mīrisēm*  
> Come quickly, otherwise we will be late
>
> درس بخوان، وگرنه موفق نمی‌شوی  
> *dars bikhwān, vagarna muwaffaq namēshawī*  
> Study, otherwise you will not succeed
>
> باید برویم، در غیر این صورت مشکل پیش می‌آید  
> *bāyad birawīm, dar ghayr-e īn ṣūrat mushkil pēsh mēāyad*  
> We must go, otherwise a problem will arise

To summarise, in Persian:

- Logical relations are often marked by:

  - **particles (agar, chūn, tā, etc.)**

- The **verb still tends to come at the end of each clause**

- One of the connector pairs, which is the main clause connector (e.g.
  “then” / “therefore” / “there”) are often **optionally omitted** and
  understood from context

### The Uses of Chūn

The particle **چون** ***chūn*** usually occurs as a part of **چونکه** ***chūnki*** meaning “because”. In Classical Persian, the particle **چون** ***chūn*** and its shortened version ***چو chū*** denotes a lot of meanings and is a generic connector of a relative clause that can mean :

- Because, Since

- As, Just as, Like

- When

- If

- How? (Question)

Only context can tell us how it is used and hence special attention is needed in translating it.

> چو قاصد شد پیام او برد / شیشهٔ مهر در میان خرد  
> *chū qāṣid shud, payām-e ō bord: / shud shīshe-ye mehr dar miān
> khurd.*  
> When the messenger left, he took his message; thus the vial of love
> between them was shattered. - Nizami
>
> شخصی همه شب بر سر بیمار گریست / چون روز شد، او بمرد و بیمار بزیست  
> *shakhṣī hame shab bar sar-e bēmār girēst / chūn rōz shud, ō bimurd o
> bēmār bizēst.*   
> A man wept all night over a sick person; when day came, he died and
> the sick man lived. - Sa’di
>
> چو پیش یوسف آمد ابن یمین / نشاندش هم‌نفس بر تخت زرین  
> *chū pēsh-e yūsuf āmad ibn-e yāmīn / nashāndash ham nafas bar takht-e
> zarrīn.*  
> When Benjamin came before Joseph, he seated him beside himself upon
> the golden throne. - Attar
>
> اگر صد سال گبر آتش فروزد / چو یکدم اندر آن افتد بسوزد  
> agar ṣad sāl gabr ātesh furōzad / chū yekdam andar ān uftad,
> bisōzad.  
> Even if a Zoroastrian (gabr) tends the fire for a hundred years, when
> /if he falls into it for a moment, he will be burned. - Sa’di
>
> چه باشد ار شود از بند غم دلش آزاد / چو هست حافظ مسکین غلام و چاکر
> دوست  
> *chi bāshad ar shawad az band-e gham dil-ash āzād / chū hast ḥāfiẓ-e
> maskīn ghulām-o chākar-e dōst?*  
> What would it matter if his heart were freed from grief, since poor
> Hafez is a slave and servant of the beloved? - Hafiz
>
> تنها نه منم ستم ‌رسیده / کو دیده که صد چو من ندیده  
> *tanhā na manam sitam rasīda / kū dīda ki ṣad chū man nadīda?*  
> It is not I alone who have suffered oppression; where is the eye that
> has not seen a hundred like me? - Nizami
>
> دوران بقا چو باد صحرا بگذشت / تلخی و خوشی و زشت و زیبا بگذشت  
> *dawrān-e baqā chū bād-e ṣaḥrā bigzasht / talkhī-o khushī-o zisht-o
> zēbā bigzasht.*  
> The age of existence passes like a desert wind; bitterness and
> sweetness, ugliness and beauty all pass away. - Sa’di
>
> پادشاه خویش را دانسته‌ام / چون روم تنها چو نتوانسته‌ام  
> *pādishāh-e khvēsh rā dānistam / chūn rawam tanhā, chū
> natavānistam?*  
> I have known my king ; but how can I go alone, when I am unable? -
> Attar

From the next article onwards, we will start reading lot of actual Persian. In this section of this article, we will start reading two extracts from the famous prose work ***Gulistan*** written by **Sa’di Shirazi**. The *Gulistan* (lit : “Rose Garden”), completed in 1258 by the great Persian poet and moralist Saʿdi of Shiraz, is one of the twin masterpieces of classical Persian literature alongside his poetic work ***Bustan*** (lit : “Garden of Fragrance”). Written in the turbulent decades after the Mongol invasions had devastated much of Iran, the book draws on Saʿdi’s decades of travel across the Islamic world, from India to North Africa, distilling his observations into short, vivid prose anecdotes interwoven with graceful verse. In Persian culture it has long been far more than a literary work: for over seven centuries it has served as a practical handbook of ethics, social wisdom, and human understanding. Generations of Iranians have memorised its elegant maxims, which still colour everyday speech; it remains a staple of school curricula and a symbol of the Persian genius for combining moral depth with wit, humanity, and linguistic beauty. Reading extracts from the *Gulistan* therefore opens a direct window onto the heart of Persianate civilisation. For example, let us start with the one that hangs in the United Nations.
