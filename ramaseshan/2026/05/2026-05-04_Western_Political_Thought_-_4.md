# Against the optimism of Enlightenment thinkers

Conservative ideology is criticised by non-conservatives as an anti-ideology that is born out of the self-interest of the traditionally privileged class who are simply afraid of change. But in this article, we will see that it is on the whole, a positive ideology and when taken in moderation, has lots of positive alternatives to offer.

If there are some things that characterises and unites all of conservative thought, it is the distrust in the following Enlightenment beliefs which are:

- The creation of a perfectly ordered fully rational society is
  feasible.

- Political action is able to achieve the creation of the same.

- Human nature is highly malleable, with human will having the capacity
  to refashion history and society in whatever ways scientific and
  rational ideals may require.

- Society is the artificial product of a contract between autonomous
  individuals.

- Evil is a contingent feature of human society that is caused only by
  eliminable factors like social oppression that are in turn perpetuated
  by irrational superstition, and hence will disappear from human
  society if these underlying reasons are addressed by political action.

Conservatives view the above beliefs and assumptions of the Enlightenment thinkers with distrust and claim them to be mistaken. The person widely regarded as the father for conservative ideology in the Western tradition is **Edmund Burke**. Hence, we will start with him.

# Edmund Burke & Conservatism: Defending Tradition & Culture

The first round of political argument surrounding the French Revolution was the **Anglo-American battle of the pamphlets**. Englishman **Richard Price** published a discourse approving the revolution as an expression of English political principles. Edmund Burke, the classic source of conservatism in Anglo-American politics, argued against Price that the revolution was a violation of English principles. Thomas Paine, the most famous political pamphleteer in American history, supported the revolution against Burke, as did Mary Wollstonecraft. In “**A Discourse on the Love of Our Country**” (1789), the English philosopher and cleric **Richard Price** (1723–1791) praised the revolution, believing that the French had fulfilled the promise of the Glorious Revolution of 1688 more completely than the English had done. Price decried excessive fealty to the king and declared that the king’s crown depends on the choice of the people, who have the right to choose their governors and “cashier” them for misconduct. It is in response to this that **Edmund Burke** (1729 - 1797) wrote his seminal work that would serve as the source for Western conservatism.

Burke, in his seminal work, "**Reflections on the Revolution in France**”, (that was published just a few months after the revolution), articulates his eloquent opposition to the French Revolution and his defense of tradition, while also providing insights into his views on feudalism. Burke was terrified over the revolution’s increasing radicalism and its Enlightenment inspired rejection of all historical traditions which would destabilise society.

### Gradual Reform instead of Revolution

Burke viewed as reckless the attempts of the French Revolution to dismantle centuries-old institutions—like the monarchy, the aristocracy, and the Church—in favour of abstract, utopian, untested principles such as liberty, equality, and fraternity. He argued that these traditional institutions, though imperfect, were the product of gradual historical development and embodied the collective wisdom of several generations of the past. He argued that the accumulated wisdom of the past cannot be disregarded uncritically. He literally wrote

“We are afraid to put men to live and trade each on his own private stock of reason; because we suspect that this stock in each man is small, and that the individuals would do better to avail themselves of the general bank and capital of nations and of ages.”

The revolution’s radical break with tradition, exemplified by the abolition of the monarchy in 1792, horrified Burke, who believed that societal change should be incremental and organic, not sudden & destructive.

### Dangers of Abstract Rationalism

Burke criticised the revolutionaries’ reliance on abstract philosophical ideals, particularly those of Enlightenment thinkers like Rousseau, who emphasised vague untested notions like the “*general will*.” He saw these ideas as dangerously utopian, detached from the practical realities of human nature and society. In Reflections, he mocks the revolutionaries’ obsession with “*metaphysical abstraction*,” arguing that their attempt to rebuild society based on theoretical principles ignored the complexity of human behaviour & the need for established norms. For example, he derides the National Assembly’s Declaration of the Rights of Man as “*a sort of institute and digest of anarchy*,” suggesting that such universal claims to rights without corresponding duties would lead to chaos.

### Violence and Social Upheaval

Burke was appalled by revolutionary violence, particularly the mistreatment of King Louis XVI and Queen Marie Antoinette. In a famous passage in Reflections, he laments the queen’s plight:

“*I thought ten thousand swords must have leaped from their scabbards to avenge even a look that threatened her with insult. But the age of chivalry is gone*.”

He saw the revolution’s mob violence as a sign of its moral bankruptcy, predicting that it would spiral into greater bloodshed—a prophecy fulfilled during the Reign of Terror, when thousands were executed by guillotine.

### Destruction of Social Order & Hierarchy

Burke believed that society required a natural hierarchy to function, with the monarchy, aristocracy, & clergy providing stability & moral guidance. The Revolution’s egalitarian ideals, which sought to abolish feudal privileges & level social distinctions, threatened this. He argued that the revolutionaries’ attack on the nobility & clergy, such as the confiscation of Church lands & the abolition of feudalism, would undermine the social bonds that held society together, leading to anarchy. Burke feared that without these traditional structures, France would descend into tyranny, as power would fall into the hands of military leaders (it came true with Napoleon).

### Contrast with the Glorious Revolution

Burke contrasted the French Revolution with England’s Glorious Revolution of 1688, which, although initially radical, eventually restrained and became a model for conservative change. The Glorious Revolution preserved the monarchy and aristocracy while establishing constitutional limits, whereas the French Revolution sought to uproot these institutions entirely.

Burke writes in Reflections,

“*We are not the converts of Rousseau; we are not the disciples of Voltaire… We have not been drawn and trussed, in order that we may be filled, like stuffed birds in a museum, with chaff and rags.*”

He believed England’s approach demonstrated the value of reforming within the framework of tradition, not destroying it. His defense of tradition and conservatism is rooted in a belief that society is a complex, organic entity that evolves slowly over time, guided by inherited wisdom rather than radical innovation. He accepted the view that England’s freedoms depended on a set of balancing institutions—royalty, nobility, Parliament, the church, and the traditional rights of the people. But the proper balance is the result of historical learning, not a philosophical argument from natural rights. History grants traditional freedoms and responsibilities to all citizens and to their rulers, including the king. England’s political norms are not deduced from reason or philosophical principles; they are embodied in its historical practices and institutions.

### Society as a Partnership Across Generations

This is my favorite extract from Burke. Borrowing from the contract theory of politics, he modified it to defend tradition and continuity following which he famously described society as a

“*partnership not only between those who are living, but between those who are living, those who are dead, and those who are to be born*”.

So, he says that society is not a contract between just those who are alive now but also with our dead ancestors and our future descendants and hence our ancestor’s voices also should weigh in whenever we make any decisions and also the long term consequences of our decisions for our future generations should also weigh a lot. Thus, society is not the product of a moment’s choice to contract. If at all there is a “contract” among the people, it is eternal and unstated and includes also the dead and the yet unborn. Political society is like an organism that exists over time. It has traditions that give it legitimacy. The local loyalties of the people—family, neighborhood, and church—are what keep the organism together and well functioning. He argued that traditions and institutions—such as the monarchy, the Church, and the legal system—are the accumulated wisdom of past generations, tested by time and experience. To discard them blankly, as the French revolutionaries did, is to reject this delicate intergenerational contract and risk societal collapse. This idea became a cornerstone of conservative thought, emphasising the importance of preserving what works rather than experimenting with untested theories.

### The Value of Prejudice and Custom

Burke used the term “prejudice” positively, meaning the ingrained habits, customs, and moral instincts that guide human behaviour. He writes,

“*Prejudice is of ready application in the emergency; it previously engages the mind in a steady course of wisdom and virtue, and does not leave the man hesitating in the moment of decision, skeptical, puzzled, and unresolved*”.

For Burke, these prejudices—such as respect for authority, religious piety, and social hierarchy—are essential for maintaining order and stability, as they provide a framework for action that doesn’t rely on individual reason alone.

### Critique of Radical Change

Burke’s conservatism is defined by his belief in gradual, organic change over radical revolution. He argued that societies should reform slowly, addressing specific grievances while preserving the core structures that ensure stability. He praises the English constitution for its ability to evolve incrementally, contrasting it with the French Revolution’s attempt to “*begin the world anew*.” He writes :

“*A spirit of innovation is generally the result of a selfish temper and confined views. People cannot look forward to posterity when they can never look backward to their ancestors*”.

This defence of incrementalism became a hallmark of conservative ideology.

### Defence of Established Institutions

Burke saw institutions like the monarchy, aristocracy, and Church as vital to social order. He believed they provided a moral and cultural framework that restrained human passions and ensured continuity. For example, he defends the role of the aristocracy as a stabilising force, arguing that their inherited wealth and status gave them a vested interest in the nation’s long-term well-being, unlike the “new men” of the French Revolution, whom he saw as opportunistic and self-serving. He also valued the Church for its role in fostering morality and social cohesion, lamenting the revolution’s confiscation of church property as an attack on the moral fabric of society.

### Emphasis on Practical Wisdom Over Abstract Theory

Burke prioritized practical wisdom over the abstract rationalism of the Enlightenment thinkers. He believed that human nature is complex and imperfect. Hence, traditions evolved to accommodate these imperfections. In Reflections, he criticizes the revolutionaries for their “*geometrical approach to governance*" —trying to design society based on theoretical principles like equality—arguing that such methods ignore the nuanced realities of human behavior. That is, thinkers in their “*armchairs*” cannot derive a small set of “*rational principles*” to reform all of society; this is just plain arrogance. Instead, he advocated for a pragmatic conservatism that respects what has proven to work over time. To quote his own words:

*Instead of casting away all our old prejudices, we cherish them ... ; we cherish them because they are prejudices; and the longer they have lasted, and the more generally they have prevailed, the more we cherish them. We are afraid to put men to live and trade each on his own private stock of reason; because we suspect that this stock in each man is small, and that the individuals would do better to avail themselves of the general bank and capital of nations, and of ages. Many of our men of speculation, instead of exploding general prejudices, employ their sagacity to discover the latent wisdom which prevails in them. If they find what they seek, and they seldom fail, they think it more wise to continue the prejudice, with the reason involved, than to cast away the coat of prejudice, and to leave nothing but the naked reason.*

### Appreciation for Feudal Hierarchy

He expresses nostalgia for the “*age of chivalry*,” which he associates with feudalism’s hierarchical structure, where knights, nobles, and the Church provided a moral and social framework. He writes, “*The age of chivalry is gone. That of sophisters, economists, and calculators has succeeded; and the glory of Europe is extinguished forever*.” For Burke, feudalism’s hierarchical relationships—between lords and vassals, or monarchs and subjects—created a sense of mutual obligation and loyalty that stabilised society. He believed these relationships, rooted in tradition, were preferable to the revolutionary egalitarianism that sought to erase all distinctions.

Burke viewed the aristocracy, a remnant of the feudal system, as a crucial element of social stability. He argues that the inherited status of nobility and their wealth gave them a long-term stake in the nation’s well-being. The aristocracy’s role in governance, as seen in feudal systems like the English monarchy, ensured continuity and prevented the kind of radical upheaval he witnessed in France.

### Critique of Feudal Abuses

Despite his appreciation for feudal hierarchy, Burke was not an uncritical apologist for feudalism. He accepted that feudal systems could be oppressive, particularly when lords abused their power over serfs or when the system failed to adapt to changing circumstances. He implicitly supports reforms to feudal practices—like those that had occurred in England over centuries—but argues that these should be gradual, not revolutionary. For example, he praises the English system, which had evolved from feudalism into a constitutional monarchy by the 18th century, balancing tradition with necessary changes. Thus, Burke was conservative but not absolutely rigid, believing that change is inevitable. His conservatism was devoted to keeping up the goods of traditional society and he admits that this will come out different when applied to different societies. He approved of the American Revolution, believing that the British crown had violated the traditional rights of Americans. He also disapproved of the treatment of India by the British East India Company. And he accepted Smith’s view on the economy and the desirability of free trade over protectionism. In Burke's own words:

A true politician always considers how he shall make the most of the existing materials of his country . A disposition to preserve , and an ability to improve , taken together , would be my standard of a statesman.

To put it differently, Burke argued that laws should reflect changes in society rather than using laws to re-engineer society from scratch.

### Feudalism as a Source of Moral Order

Burke saw feudalism’s moral framework, particularly its emphasis on chivalry and mutual obligation, as a source of social cohesion. He believed that the feudal code of honor—where knights protected the weak and served their lords—instilled virtues like loyalty, duty, and reverence for tradition. In Reflections, he laments the loss of this moral order in revolutionary France, where he saw the revolutionaries’ rejection of feudal privileges as a rejection of the values that underpinned them. He writes, “*The unbought grace of life, the cheap defence of nations, the nurse of manly sentiment and heroic enterprise, is gone!*” For Burke, feudalism’s moral ideals, even if not always realized in practice, were worth preserving as part of the traditional order. Burke also denied legal equality. The estates have different legal statuses. It’s true that all have equal rights but “not to equal things.” Each estate or class has its traditional prerogatives, all of which presume obligations and duties. Rights always came with obligations in traditional societies and hence it is not correct to have blank equality for everyone's rights to everything.

### Comparison with Revolutionary Egalitarianism

Burke contrasted feudalism’s hierarchical stability with the French Revolution’s egalitarian ideals. While he recognized feudalism’s flaws, he believed that its gradual evolution into modern systems (as in England) was preferable to the revolution’s attempt to abolish all hierarchy. He saw the revolution’s confiscation of feudal lands and abolition of titles as an attack on the social order, arguing that such radical changes would lead to chaos rather than justice. For Burke, feudalism, despite its inequalities, provided a framework for social stability that the revolutionaries lacked.

The essence of Burke’s conservatism can be described as follows:

“***Authority, rank, and inequality are good when deserved and legitimate. Social life is made possible by a cultural softening of the harsh facts of power and nature. Submission to the elegant and dutiful is a virtue, and each finds respect in that unequal relation. The new bourgeois men want to reduce us to animal equality and tear away the drapery that makes life livable and honorable***”.

### Political philosophy as inherently historical

Inherent in this conservative conception of prejudice is a philosophy reminiscent of Aristotle and other thinkers like Cicero, that views that **knowledge of the society and politics is essentially historical rather than individual reason;** that it is the result of the ideas and practices of many generations. **This view that philosophy is historical does not necessarily mean that it is either static or irrational unless one thinks that history itself is irrational and static**. It is therefore to history, and not pure armchair reasoning, that we must turn to if we want to seek the knowledge of how to organize society and state. This is the antithesis of the Enlightenment view that sees history as a cesspool of irrationality and individual reason as the sole basis for any kind of philosophy. Burke’s view is that ***any revolution which attempts to eliminate grievances by altering the whole structure of society causes more harm than good because most of the institutions of the society are more rational than the scheme of any individual politician.***

### Organismic metaphor of conservatism

Also, conservatives see human society as an organism whose parts are intricately interconnected to each other. Society is a mutually interdependent set of institutions and traditions that encompass the individual and that are locked to each other in much the same way as are the various organs of a living body. And just as any radical alteration in one organ of the body must cause changes in all the rest, by analogy the same results occur when alterations are made in the social body. Changes in one part of society alter the whole, and in ways that seldom can be anticipated fully. This does not mean that conservatives are against medical treatment to fix a diseased body but they are against radical surgeries and drug treatments of the body without strong reason as they conceive that our knowledge of the complexities of the human body is not complete no matter how large it may be.

### Mass society leads to more centralisation and tyranny

Burke argues that the various traditional institutions present in the society with their privileges and obligations form a balance of power and resist absolute centralisation. Institutions are buffers between the state and the individual. In the complete collapse of all traditional institutions and all group identities destroyed, the society will be just a mass of homogeneous lonely identity-less men. **Far from transforming society for the better, Burke reasoned that, the revolutionary replacement of age-old spontaneous traditional ties by ever-increasing state regulation of an otherwise atomic society, would ironically lead to newer forms of despotism and oppression that would be far more hostile to liberty than the ancient regime had ever been**. While the progressives thought the destruction of traditional ties in society to be a good idea because each man would be equal to any other man, Burke argued that this will in fact lead to tyranny because in this situation, an individual becomes powerless in front of the state which would find nothing in the way to establish its absolute despotism. This was prophetic as this was what happened as the French Revolution progressed. According to Burke, lone individuals can never be successful in breaking the tyranny of the state and only traditional institutions like the church, the parliament, etc. have the capacity to do so. It was institutions and divisions with a high inertia sanctioned by age-old traditions that stand as a bulwark between the individual and the state.Since Burke predicted all these merely months after the French Revolution, this reasoning became a fulfilled prophecy as the French Revolution progressed in the direction of radicalism.

### Conservatism after Burke

While Burke represents a balanced conservative and progressive reactions to the French Revolution respectively, there were also people who were far to the right and far to the left. In fact, the very terms right and left wing in politics discourse is a legacy of the revolution because people supporting the revolution sat on the left and people supporting the monarchy sat on the right side in the French National Assembly in 1789.

While Burke was fairly open to the idea of gradual reform but guided by tradition, there were far more diverse conservative reactions to the French Revolution during and after Burke. Although all of these share a rejection of the Enlightenment belief of reordering society based on pure reason, their solutions are extremely diverse, and hence conservatism split into many diverse forms of thought and has changed a lot with time. We will see the detailed evolution in another article but in the remainder of this article, I am giving a very brief overview of the developments.

As we saw in the beginning of the article, conservatives reject the Enlightenment assumption that evil is a contingent and eliminable feature of human society that is caused only by factors like social oppression that are in turn perpetuated by irrational superstition. They argue that

- There are **limits** to the scope of political action in reforming
  societies

- There are ineliminable **tensions** in human society that are a part
  of the human existence itself

To put in simpler words, the Enlightenment thinkers viewed politics as controls to steer human society to a place of liberty and rationality. But conservatives think that there are some tensions (like inequality, evil) that are always part of the human condition and hence an ineliminable (although undesirable) part of society and that politics has limited power in moulding the minds of masses. If the Enlightenment philosophes wanted to steer society to a place of liberty, equality, and fraternity using politics, the conservatives argue that politics is of limited steering capacity and the place where these philosophes want to steer the society to, does not exist at all. But conservatives disagree on what are exactly the limits of political action and what exactly are the ineliminable tensions of human society. They can be grouped into three major kinds

1.  Reactionary Conservatism

2.  Radical Conservatism

3.  Moderate Conservatism

In modern times, a new group arose called the New Right.

# Reactionary Conservatism

**Reactionary conservatism** is given less attention in many places because it is viewed as outdated and rejected as far-right and intellectually bankrupt, considering its simplistic demand to push the clock back before the Revolution and undo all the changes brought about after the Revolution. Although this criticism is true and deserved, there are some thoughts and contentions of these reactionary conservatives that merit attention.

For example, the Spanish reactionary thinker Juan Donoso Cortes writes:

*There are only two possible forms of control (in society): one **internal** (through religion, culture, and traditions) and the other **external** (political state control) - religious control and political control. They are of such a nature that when the religious barometer rises, the political barometer of control falls and likewise, when the religious barometer falls, the political barometer, that is political control and tyranny, rises. That is a law of humanity, a law of history.*

Since modern democracies tend to have secular cultures that encourage liberty of thought and diversity of any form of self-expression, they inevitably destroy the kind of religio-spiritual control that Cortes advocates. In this situation, ‘*The way is prepared for some gigantic and colossal tyrant’*, a prospect which cannot be averted by optimistic liberal tactics such as ‘*granting more liberty, more guarantees* \[for rights, etc.\] *and new constitutions’*. The only hope is ‘*a salutary reaction - a religious reaction*’, about the possibility of which, however, Donoso Cortes confessed that he was skeptical.

The core of reactionary ideology, then, is the claim that at the heart of modern democracy is a spiritual void created by false optimism about the ability of man to abandon religion and pursue happiness through creative political action. The most articulative and extreme among the reactionary conservatives would be **Joseph-Marie, comte de Maistre** (1753–1821). While agreeing with a Hobbesian idea that the state of nature is inherently evil and drenched in blood (he attributes it to the Christian doctrine of original sin), he argued that the only way out for men would be to submit their souls to God and their bodies to the ruler. All power including that of the ruler comes from God and hence any revolution can generate only adverse consequences like the Terror of 1793 - 1794. For Maistre, the order of the cosmos is determined by God, who is not rational and who is incomprehensible. Hence, man’s reason is tiny and puny for anything important to be designed based on it. Power, fear, and tradition are far superior to reason. This is exactly the antithesis of the Enlightenment view. Others of similar thought like **Hugues-Félicité-Robert de Lamennais** (1782–1854) and **Louis-Gabriel-Ambroise de Bonald** (1754–1840) who were religious traditionalists, supported what is called ***ultramontanism*** - the acceptance of the superior political authority of the pope and a restoration of the pre-revolutionary authority of the Catholic Church.

Another reactionary criticism of modern democracy with a liberal capitalistic society is that it perpetuates and legitimates an ethic of self-seeking and makes any consensus on important foundational values difficult to achieve. For e.g, **Charles Mauras** (who founded the **Action Française** movement in 1899) argued that modern capitalism and democracy simply replaces the old form of oppression with control of our desires by a new business plutocracy of companies. **Maurice Barrès** extends this further and argues that this liberal capitalism would lead to the death of civilisation itself as it becomes impossible to stably transmit a common cultural heritage to future generations. The fact is that this end of civilisation is accompanied by mass prosperity conceals this bitter end, according to **Ortega y Gasset**. One can also find expressions of such sentiments in **T.S. Eliot as well**.

Reactionary conservatism has been widely criticised for being anti-innovation and for its intellectual snobbery for the large part and is deservedly so. In modern politics, it has become extinct in its original form as it speaks against democratic governments. While the Enlightenment thinkers are criticised by conservatives to be overly optimistic about what political action could achieve to bring about an utopian and rational society, reactionary conservatism is criticised for being overly nostalgic about the hierarchical pre-modern societies that existed in the *ancien regime*. When confronted with the facts about the problems in such societies, they have turned to blame it on a scapegoat for which Jews have been a favourite example.

Since reactionary conservatism rejects democratic modernity wholesale, they are now confined only to the fringes of modern politics. But they have also sometimes adopted many strategies to come out of this.

### Using extra constitutional methods to overthrow the established order

- “**The Friends of Morès**” was a small, militant group founded in 1891
  by the Marquis de Morès, a French nobleman inspired by the rugged
  cowboy culture he encountered while ranching in North Dakota. Dressed
  in distinctive sombreros and purple shirts, the group engaged in
  street brawls to challenge France’s then democratic government, which
  Morès saw as weak and corrupt. Their ideology, rooted in reactionary
  conservatism, rejected modern democracy in favor of a hierarchical
  society led by strong, masculine figures. They aimed to restore
  traditional values and national pride, often with antisemitic
  undertones. The group had little lasting impact and remained a fringe
  movement in French politics.

- “**The Black Legion**” was a secret, far-right vigilante group in the
  United States, active in the Midwest during the 1930s, with roots in
  the Ku Klux Klan. Composed mainly of white Protestant men, it opposed
  labor unions, immigrants, Catholics, Jews, and African Americans,
  viewing them as threats to a "pure" American society. The group used
  extra-constitutional methods like bombings, arson, and assassinations
  to intimidate and eliminate political opponents, including targeting
  left-wing activists and union organizers. Their ideology blended
  ultranationalism, white supremacy, and anti-communism, aiming to
  impose an authoritarian order over democratic institutions. The group
  was dismantled after high-profile murders led to arrests and trials in
  1936–37.

- The **Lapua Movement** was a Finnish far-right, anti-communist group
  that emerged in 1929, seeking to suppress leftist movements and
  establish a nationalist, conservative order. Driven by fears of
  socialism and Soviet influence, it used violent tactics like
  kidnappings, beatings, and forced deportations to silence communists
  and social democrats. The group’s ideology centered on
  ultranationalism, anti-communism, and a vision of a hierarchical,
  Christian Finland, rejecting democratic pluralism. In 1932, it
  attempted the **Mäntsälä Rebellion**, an armed uprising to overthrow
  the government, but it was quickly suppressed by state forces. The
  movement was banned in 1932, though its influence persisted in Finnish
  right-wing politics.

- The **Iron Guard**, also known as the **Legion of the Archangel
  Michael**, was a Romanian fascist movement founded in 1927 by
  **Corneliu Zelea Codreanu**, blending ultranationalism, Orthodox
  Christianity, and antisemitism. It sought to overthrow Romania’s
  democratic government, which it viewed as corrupt and dominated by
  Jewish and liberal elites, in favor of a totalitarian, theocratic
  state. The group used extra-constitutional methods like
  assassinations, street violence, and pogroms against Jews and
  political rivals, culminating in the 1940–41 rebellion against the
  government. Its ideology romanticized a mythical Romanian past,
  emphasizing ethnic purity and authoritarian rule. The Iron Guard was
  suppressed in 1941 by dictator **Ion Antonescu**, who saw it as a
  rival to his own regime.

- The **Conservative Revolutionary Movement** was a loose coalition of
  German intellectuals and activists in the **Weimar Republic**,
  including figures like **Carl Schmitt** and **Ernst Jünger**, who
  rejected democracy and modernity. They advocated for a nationalist,
  authoritarian state based on a mythical "German essence," opposing the
  liberal Weimar Republic as weak and decadent. While not a single
  organization, some factions supported extra-constitutional actions
  like coups and street violence by right-wing paramilitaries to
  undermine the democratic order. Their ideology combined romantic
  nationalism, anti-liberalism, and a disdain for democratic
  institutions, influencing early Nazi ideology. The movement’s ideas
  were absorbed into Nazism, though many of its thinkers later distanced
  themselves from Hitler’s regime.

- **Action Française** was a French monarchist and nationalist movement
  founded in 1899 by **Charles Maurras**, aiming to restore a
  traditional monarchy and overthrow the Third Republic’s democratic
  system. It viewed democracy as chaotic and corrupt, promoting a
  hierarchical society rooted in Catholicism, nationalism, and
  antisemitism. The group initially used extra-constitutional tactics
  like street riots, propaganda, and support for violent anti-republican
  plots, including backing the 1934 far-right riots that nearly toppled
  the government. Its ideology emphasized order, tradition, and a strong
  state over liberal democratic values. It was banned in 1944 for
  collaborating with the **Vichy regime** which in turn collaborated
  with the Nazi government in Germany during World War II.

### Forming or supporting revolutionary political parties

- **Oswald Spengler**, a German historian and philosopher, was a key
  figure in the Conservative Revolutionary Movement, known for his book
  *The Decline of the West*, which argued that civilisations rise and
  fall in cycles and that Western democracy was decaying. Disdainful of
  liberal democracy, Spengler sought a strong, hierarchical state led by
  an elite, but he initially saw himself as above partisan politics,
  focusing on grand cultural theories. By the 1920s, he gave qualified
  support to the **Nazi** Party, believing it could overthrow the weak
  Weimar Republic and restore German greatness, despite his personal
  contempt for Hitler’s vulgarity and rejection of Nazi racism. This
  reflects the second strategy, as Spengler aligned with a revolutionary
  political movement to achieve his anti-democratic goals, compromising
  his ideological purity for practical impact. However, he grew critical
  of the Nazis by the 1930s, wary of their populism and totalitarian
  methods, and died in 1936, disillusioned with their direction. His
  support illustrates how reactionaries might back a political party to
  gain influence, even if they disagree with aspects of its ideology.

- While **Action Française** initially used extra-constitutional methods
  like street riots (the first strategy) to achieve their goal, Maurras
  shifted to the second strategy during World War II by supporting the
  **Vichy Regime**, a collaborationist government established after
  France’s defeat by Germany in 1940. He saw Vichy, led by **Marshal
  Philippe Pétain**, as an opportunity to replace the Third Republic
  with an authoritarian, traditionalist state aligned with his vision.
  Maurras backed Vichy’s anti-democratic policies, including its
  antisemitic laws, believing they could restore French greatness,
  though he was wary of their full collaboration with Nazi Germany. This
  strategic alignment with a revolutionary regime allowed Maurras to
  influence policy, but Vichy’s collapse in 1944 led to his arrest and
  conviction for collaboration. His support for Vichy illustrates how
  reactionaries might join a political movement to achieve their goals
  when direct action proves insufficient.

- **Gabriele D’Annunzio**, an Italian poet, nationalist, and adventurer,
  was a reactionary figure who romanticized a heroic, militaristic past
  and despised liberal democracy for its perceived mediocrity. Initially
  a cultural icon with no formal political affiliation, he led the 1919
  occupation of **Fiume** (modern-day Rijeka, Croatia), a proto-fascist
  experiment in nationalist governance that defied international
  treaties. By the early 1920s, D’Annunzio gave tacit support to
  **Benito Mussolini’**s Fascist movement, seeing it as a powerful force
  to overthrow Italy’s weak liberal government and restore national
  glory. This shift to backing a revolutionary political party again
  reflects the second strategy, as he aligned with fascism to amplify
  his influence, despite differences with Mussolini’s methods. His
  flamboyant style and ideas, like the Fiume occupation’s theatrical
  governance, influenced Fascist aesthetics, though D’Annunzio never
  fully joined the party and later withdrew from active politics. His
  example shows how reactionaries might support a revolutionary movement
  to achieve anti-democratic aims when their own efforts lack traction.

### Converting the ideal past to a visionary future under a charismatic leader

Reactionary conservatism often rejects modern democracy for its pluralism and perceived chaos, favouring traditional systems of hierarchy . However, this third strategy recognizes that outright rejection of democracy can lead to political isolation. Instead, reactionaries adapt by working within democratic frameworks, promoting a **charismatic leader** who can rally the masses, override divisive party politics, and restore a sense of national unity. This leader is seen as a figure above ordinary politics, embodying the nation’s spirit and guiding it toward a new order that aligns with reactionary values like order, tradition, and national strength. Unlike the first strategy (violent, extra-legal action) or the second (joining revolutionary parties), this approach is more subtle, operating within legal democratic structures while subtly undermining their pluralistic nature. This strategy appears to move away from nostalgia for a “golden age” by converting it into a forward-looking vision, making it more appealing to modern audiences. At the level of practical politics, it was this form of reactionary thought upon which the **French Gaullist Party** relied after the Second World War, although the charismatic ideal was qualified in this case by a commitment to constitutional politics.

### Adopting politics and shifting to purely non-political activities

Abandoning politics altogether and adopting purely non-political activities - ***spiritual*** activities, ***aesthetic*** activities, and ***adventurous*** activities is another reactionary strategy that serves as a symbolic rebellion against the decadence created by modern democratic liberal societies.

- **Julius Evola** (1898–1974), an Italian philosopher and esotericist,
  was a reactionary thinker who rejected modern democracy for its
  materialism and egalitarianism, advocating a return to a hierarchical,
  spiritual order based on what he called the “tradition”, although his
  view of tradition and values were unconventional. Disillusioned with
  political movements like Italian fascism, (which he initially
  supported but later criticized as too populist), Evola abandoned
  politics for a focus on spiritual and metaphysical pursuits by the
  mid-20th century. In works like *Revolt Against the Modern
  World* (1934) and *The Doctrine of Awakening* (1943), he drew
  inspiration from Buddhism, Hinduism, and other non-Western traditions
  to propose a radical revision of Western values, emphasizing spiritual
  elitism and transcendence over democratic mediocrity. He argued that
  only a select few could achieve spiritual awakening, rejecting the
  mass-oriented nature of democracy as spiritually bankrupt. His
  esoteric writings, blending mysticism with reactionary ideals, reflect
  this fourth strategy by seeking meaning and tradition outside politics
  in a private, intellectual quest for a higher order. However, his
  ideas, often tinged with elitism and contempt for modernity, have been
  criticized for their detachment from practical reality and flirtation
  with nihilistic rejection of societal norms.

- **José Ortega y Gasset** (1883–1955), a Spanish philosopher and
  essayist, was a conservative intellectual who critiqued modern
  democracy for its “**revolt of the masses**,” arguing in his famous
  work *The Revolt of the Masses* (1930) that mass culture eroded elite
  values and cultural excellence. Disenchanted with democratic politics,
  which he saw as dominated by mediocrity, Ortega turned to aesthetic
  and intellectual pursuits, focusing on philosophy, literature, and
  cultural criticism. His essays, such as those in *Meditations on
  Quixote* (1914), celebrated art and high culture as domains where
  individual distinction could resist the leveling effects of democracy.
  By emphasizing the role of an intellectual elite in shaping cultural
  values, Ortega sought meaning in a private sphere of aesthetic
  creation, distancing himself from political engagement. This too
  aligns with the fourth strategy, as he expressed contempt for
  democratic uniformity through refined, creative work rather than
  political action. His focus on aesthetics, however, risked moral
  posturing, as it prioritized cultural critique over practical
  solutions, sometimes appearing detached from societal realities.

- **T. S. Eliot** (1888–1965), an American-born British poet and critic,
  was a reactionary figure who lamented the spiritual and cultural
  decline of modern democratic societies, which he saw as fragmented and
  lacking tradition. In works like *The Waste Land* (1922) and essays
  such as *The Idea of a Christian Society* (1939), Eliot turned to
  aesthetic and religious pursuits to counter the “mediocrity” of
  democratic life, advocating for a return to Christian values and
  classical literary traditions. Disillusioned with the political chaos
  of the interwar period, he largely withdrew from political activism,
  focusing instead on poetry, criticism, and cultural commentary as a
  means to preserve elite cultural standards. His emphasis on aesthetic
  activity reflects the fourth strategy, as he sought meaning in art and
  spirituality rather than engaging with democratic politics, which he
  viewed with skepticism. Eliot’s work, while influential, has been
  critiqued for its elitism and nostalgia for a hierarchical past,
  occasionally veering toward a dismissive attitude toward modern
  society that borders on nihilistic detachment. His conversion to
  Anglicanism and focus on cultural renewal underscore his attempt to
  find purpose outside the political sphere.

- **Henri de Montherlant** (1895–1972), a French novelist and essayist,
  was a reactionary writer who felt alienated by the conformity and
  safety of modern democratic life, which he saw as stifling individual
  heroism and vitality. He embraced bullfighting as a dangerous,
  life-affirming activity, detailed in his semi-autobiographical novel
  *The Bullfighters* (*Les Bestiaires*, 1926), written after he was
  seriously injured in a bullfight. In the novel, the protagonist Alban
  overcomes his sense of alienation due to democratic modernity, through
  the mortal combat of bullfighting, which Montherlant portrayed as a
  noble, aristocratic pursuit that defied democratic mediocrity. This
  reflects the fourth strategy as well, since Montherlant abandoned
  political engagement for a private, high-risk activity that symbolized
  traditional notions of courage and authenticity against the backdrop
  of a “boring” democratic society. His obsession with bullfighting,
  however, has been interpreted as a form of moral posturing, glorifying
  danger to an extreme that risks nihilism by prioritizing personal
  thrill over societal contribution. Montherlant’s later works and
  eventual suicide in 1972 further highlight the existential despair
  underlying his rejection of democratic norms.

> *It is to be noted that reactionary conservatism is not a form of
> **fascism**, to which it is, in principle, opposed to. The aim of
> fascism (as we will see in a later article) is to transform the social
> order by instituting a dynamic cult of mass participation under a
> charismatic leader. Whereas reactionary conservatism is static and
> rejects mass dynamism in any form, it prefers traditions and
> traditional institutions over any kind of new cults and cultic
> movements. However, from the above examples, one sees that reactionary
> conservatism has tended to serve as a bridge to fascist type regimes
> which is why it has fell into distaste after the Second World War.*

# Radical Conservatism

At the other end of the conservative spectrum are the **radical conservatives** who find it necessary for conservatism to embrace democratic modernity positively and stop viewing the mass society with complete hostility and distrust. Radical conservatives get along in the democratic system by rallying the masses behind conservative leaders who convince them to reject the liberal commitment and embrace their conservative ideology by fusing it with nationalism. This works out better when the nation is under threat by an external adversary. If reactionary conservative ideology is instituted “*from above*”, radical conservative ideology is instigated “*from below*”. While reactionary conservatism has served as a bridge to fascist regimes, radical conservatism has led directly led to such regimes when the powers of the charismatic conservative leader go unchecked. This is what actually happened in inter-war Germany and Italy that led to the rise of **Nazism** under **Hilter** and **fascism** under **Mussolini**. Radical conservatism rejects representative parliamentary democracy in favour of mass movements and direct involvement of the people in politics by mass mobilisation.

Since inter-war radical conservatives were sufficiently tainted by their association with Nazism and fascism, its post-war defenders have tried adopting three kinds of intellectual strategies to make them more respectable.

- Rejection of a leader in the form of an individual.

- Rejection of nationalism in favour of a supra-national ideal like
  Christianity or European Unity.

- Rejection of extra-constitutional methods and instead adopting
  gradualist approaches like mass political education.

> *The result is that although radical conservatism claims to be a
> movement of national unification, in practice it is a slippery slope
> which offers no protection against a slide into totalitarian
> dictatorship of the kind pioneered by fascism and communism.*

# Moderate Conservatism

The moderate school of conservatism is the only form of conservatism that has made it into mainstream politics today, and is a descendant of the project started by **Edmund Burke**, who himself was a moderate in his position who supported the need for reforms and checks on power and balances in governance. But it has changed a lot in the process of its evolution that it gradually moved away from many things most of what conservatism originally stood for and has embraced many ideals of liberalism, despite claiming to be against the Enlightenment reasoning that birthed them.

While reactionary conservatism commits itself to a static ideal and radical conservatism commits itself to active mobilisation of the populace, the moderate school is committed to the ideal of a limited state ruled by law, representative institutions, and a constitution that checks executive power. We will see in the article on liberalism that this was originally the view of the first liberals, also called as classical liberals. While moderate conservatives do adopt liberal ideas, they reject the underlying abstract principles that liberals use to justify them. Moderate conservatives all want to keep the powers of government in check but disagree on what would constitute effective checks. Since this brand of conservatism is influenced by liberalism, I will discuss this after discussing classical liberalism.
