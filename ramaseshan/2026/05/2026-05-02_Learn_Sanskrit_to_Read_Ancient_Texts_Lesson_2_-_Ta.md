# Introducing Mahābhārata and neuter nouns

In this reading, you will be introduced to neuter gender nouns through readings on the biggest epic poem in the world *Mahābhāratam*. Sanskrit nouns come in three grammatical genders. So far, we have seen the masculine gender nouns that ends in अः like

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!BO5P!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1bc06453-09ce-4610-8214-5d337a62d35a_1536x222.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!BO5P!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1bc06453-09ce-4610-8214-5d337a62d35a_1536x222.heic)

Note that in languages like Sanskrit, gender does not always correlate to biological gender. In the above list, the word गन्थः that means text is masculine although there is nothing masculine about texts. For every noun that does not have biological gender, its gender has to be memorized. Often, the endings like अः gives a clue about gender. For example, nouns that have the normal ending as अः are masculine in gender. But for some, the gender has to be memorized - for example, नाम name. There are three genders in Sanskrit - masculine, feminine, and neuter. So far we have seen masculine gender nouns that normally end in अः . Now we will see neuter nouns that end normally in अम् / अं . Their possessive forms and location forms have the same endings as the masculine - अस्य and ए respectively. The names of the two epics in Sanskrit Rāmāyaṇam and Mahābhāratam are neuter gender nouns. The question word for neuter nouns is किम् what (neut).

# Man Bites Dog / Dog Bites Man

By now, you would have realised that Sanskrit nouns take various endings to convey various functions in a sentence. The three endings that we have seen so far are recollected below for masculine and neuter nouns.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!BUWJ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9c999b81-5b38-4067-a726-c0ad6abbfe33_1226x226.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!BUWJ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9c999b81-5b38-4067-a726-c0ad6abbfe33_1226x226.heic)

In this section, we will see another ending and examine how to distinguish between subject and object. In English, look at the following sentences.

1.  The dog bites the man

2.  The man bites the dog

In the first sentence, the dog is the doer of the verb biting whereas the man is the receiver of the action of biting and is getting bitten. Grammatically, the doer of the verb in a sentence is called the subject in Sanskrit and the receiver of the verb in a sentence is called the object in Sanskrit. In English, the word order alone conveys this distinction - the verb is in the middle with the subject coming before it and the object coming after it. But in Sanskrit, it is the endings on nouns that conveys the distinction between these two roles. Therefore, the word order of the sentence is not rigid in Sanskrit. Take the above two sentences in Sanskrit. The word for dog in Sanskrit is शुनकः and the word for man is मनुष्यः . Both are masculine words as evident from their endings. The word दशति means “bites” in Sanskrit. Now, these two sentences in Sanskrit come out as follows:

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!lvVC!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1257ef48-1a9a-4888-94e0-0086c7aaae16_2048x1083.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!lvVC!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1257ef48-1a9a-4888-94e0-0086c7aaae16_2048x1083.png "Screenshot 2025-09-18 at 13.03.35.png")

Can you see what happened above? The masculine noun retains its normal अः ending while acting as the subject of the verb but becomes अम् / अं when acting as the object of the sentence. Since the endings convey the role of the nouns, any permutation of these three words means the same in Sanskrit.

The man bites dog

1.  मनुष्यः शुनकम् दशति

2.  शुनकम् मनुष्यः दशति

3.  मनुष्यः दशति शुनकम्

4.  शुनकम् दशति मनुष्यः

5.  दशति मनुष्यः शुनकम्

6.  दशति शुनकम् मनुष्यः

As long as the endings of the words for dog and man do not change, the meaning of the sentence remains the same.

Another example to illustrate this would be the sentences

1.  The man throws a stick

2.  A stick throws the man

In Sanskrit, look at these pairs of sentences. The words दण्डः for stick and नरः for man changes endings to convey the distinction in the pairs of sentences and not the relative position of them relative to the verb क्षिपति for *throws*.

We already seen this ending to answer the question “where” to convey location. Here, it is used appropriately. Now, let us make this sentence richer by adding adjectives to all the nouns. Read below.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!iDyP!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd77d38b0-fa55-4491-ae7f-cff356e179bb_2048x1157.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!iDyP!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd77d38b0-fa55-4491-ae7f-cff356e179bb_2048x1157.png "Screenshot 2025-09-18 at 13.07.07.png")

What novel thing do you observe here? In many languages including English, Hindi, and Tamil, an adjective describing a noun has to come immediately next to it and cannot wander far from the noun. For example, in English:

> Brave Rāma kills cruel Rāvaṇa

The adjective “brave” that describes Rāma has to come right before it and similarly the adjective “cruel” that describes Rāvaṇa. But something nice happens when you translate this sentence in Sanskrit.

> वीरः रामः क्रूरं रावणं मारयति

One sees that the adjectives take endings too just like the nouns!!! What is the advantage of this? It means that the adjective can be placed anywhere in the sentence even far away from the noun and still the endings tell you what noun it describes.

वीरः has to describe a noun that is the subject of the sentence because of its ending and hence wherever it is, it will describe only रामः ! Similarly, क्रूरम् has to describe रावणम् because of its ending.

The order of any of these words in Sanskrit can be permuted and the meaning of the sentence remains the same! For example, consider this longer sentence

> वीरः रामः क्रूरं रावणं सुन्दरे लङ्कानगरे भयङ्करेण शस्त्रेण सनातनस्य धर्मस्य रक्षणाय
> मारयति  
> Brave Rāma kills cruel Rāvaṇa in beautiful Laṅkā city with a
> terrifying weapon for the protection of sanātana dharma

`The order of any of these words in this huge sentence in Sanskrit can be permuted any way you like and the meaning will remain the same, thanks to the endings coded in every word that determine exactly what word is doing what and goes with what other word!!`

So each adjective in Sanskrit comes in three genders depending on the gender of the noun it describes and agrees with the noun in gender, number, and the grammatical role endings!

# Syntax Points

### 1. Verb of Being is Optional

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!h-HZ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3a03b13c-f086-4214-bc04-674999e7b5aa_2048x1105.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!h-HZ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3a03b13c-f086-4214-bc04-674999e7b5aa_2048x1105.png "Screenshot 2025-09-18 at 13.12.19.png")

In English, every sentence has to have a verb and a subject. Other things may be optional but at the minimum, there should be a subject noun and a verb. But in Sanskrit, the verb forms of the verb “to be - am/is/are/was/were” (e.g. अस्ति = is) can be omitted. Consider the sentence below:

> Valid Sanskrit sentence : मम नाम रामः  
> Literal translation: *My name Rāma*  
> Actual translation: *My name **\[is\]** Rāma.*

This alone just would make a complete and perfectly grammatical sentence in Sanskrit. The verb अस्ति “*is*” understood from the context. This is like Bengali and the Dravidian languages where the verbs for being are omitted (e.g. Bengali: *amar nam ram*, Tamil: *en peyar rāman*). In Sanskrit, if needed, the verb can be added back as

> Also valid Sanskrit sentence : मम नाम रामः अस्ति

But the word अस्ति (is) is optional. Such sentences are called verbless sentences where the appropriate verb of being (am/is/are/was/were) has to be supplied from context.

### 2. Omission of the Subject

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jJ3T!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2395635e-5c77-43a8-99c2-c5187ddb87c6_2048x1009.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jJ3T!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2395635e-5c77-43a8-99c2-c5187ddb87c6_2048x1009.png "Screenshot 2025-09-18 at 13.12.37.png")

Similarly, the subject too can be omitted if the verb is there and understood from the context. Consider the below sentence

> मारयति रावणं  
> Literal translation: *kills Rāvaṇa*  
> Actual translation: *He/she/kills Rāvaṇa*

In this sentence, there is a verb and an object and hence we know that somebody is killing someone. But we do not know who that subject of the verb is that is doing the killing. If the subject is understood from context or the same as the previous sentence, it is omitted in Sanskrit. For example,

> रामः अवतरति । रावणं मारयति ।  
> Literal translation: Rama takes avatāra. Kills Rāvaṇa  
> Actual translation: Rāma takes avatāra. \[He\] kills Rāvaṇa.

In this sentence, the killer is understood to be Rāma from the previous sentence. While English would use a pronoun “He” in that sentence instead of “Rāma”, it is simply omitted in Sanskrit.

### 3. Omission of Dummy Subjects

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!8f3E!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe7c93902-b365-4d84-9c08-9052d8b014ad_2048x1016.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!8f3E!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe7c93902-b365-4d84-9c08-9052d8b014ad_2048x1016.png "Screenshot 2025-09-18 at 13.13.05.png")

Also consider an English sentence like:

> **There** is a monkey.

In this sentence, “there” actually does not refer to anything and is called a dummy subject in English grammar since English cannot permit omission of subject. This sentence conveys the existence of the predicate of the dummy subject which is a monkey in this case and tells us that a monkey exists. In Sanskrit, the verb अस्ति can also function as this dummy subject of English apart from he, she, or it. Hence, the sentence

> वानरः अस्ति  
> Literal translation: Monkey is  
> Actual translation : can mean any of the following:

1.  He is a monkey (Sub: He)

2.  She is a monkey (Sub: She)

3.  It is a monkey (Sub: It)

4.  There is a monkey (Sub: There - dummy subject)

### 4. Adjectives as Nouns

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!AWs2!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa828b670-acc9-437f-b3a3-7389cbd63f48_2048x1132.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!AWs2!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa828b670-acc9-437f-b3a3-7389cbd63f48_2048x1132.png "Screenshot 2025-09-18 at 13.13.24.png")

In English too, sometimes, adjectives can function as nouns in a sentence in the absence of any other noun to describe. Consider this sentence.

> *Give charity to the poor.*

In the above sentence, the word “poor” is actually an adjective. But there is no other noun in the sentence. But it is understood that the adjective “poor” is describing a noun like “people” and that noun is understood from context. Sanskrit does this too but more often than in English. This sentence above will come out like

> दरिद्राय दानं देहि

Here, the adjective दरिद्रः that means poor is actually functioning as a noun and means “poor person”. The gender, number, and the case ending of such an adjective would be the same as that of the noun that is implied by the adjective from context.

### 5. Destination of Motion Takes Direct Object

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!0hdo!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Feefc9b33-b690-4de1-8a00-1cbac32e3171_2048x1117.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!0hdo!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Feefc9b33-b690-4de1-8a00-1cbac32e3171_2048x1117.png "Screenshot 2025-09-18 at 13.13.46.png")

In English, consider verbs of motion like “go” or “come” or “lead”. Mostly, they need to take the preposition “to” to convey the destination of the motion.

> John goes **to** the *monastery*  
> James comes **to** *the field*  
> Stuart leads the army **to** *the city*

But in Sanskrit, they take the direct object, which means the अम् ending. So, the sentence “Rama goes to the forest” in Sanskrit would be

> रामः वनं गच्छति  
> Rāma goes \[**to**\] *the forest*
>
> कृष्णः अर्जुनं कुरुक्षेत्रं नयति  
> Kṛṣṇa leads Arjuna \[**to**\] *the Kurukṣetra*

The \[to\] is supplied in the translation only because English requires it. Note that in the verb “lead”, there are two objects to answer two things - who is being led, and what is the destination for the leading. Such double object constructions are okay for these kind of situations in Sanskrit.

### 6. Having & Giving

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!A7k_!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F34f086f5-cb9d-4e40-b553-f83d29698a7c_2006x1008.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!A7k_!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F34f086f5-cb9d-4e40-b553-f83d29698a7c_2006x1008.png "Screenshot 2025-09-18 at 13.14.30.png")

In Sanskrit, there is no verb that means “to have”. Possession is conveyed indirectly by the following syntactical replacement.

> English : Arjuna has one brother  
> Sanskritic style : ***Of Arjuna**, \[there\] is a brother*   
> Sanskrit : **अर्जुनस्य** एकः भ्राता अस्ति

This might sound strange initially. But Hindi also does the same thing and so do many Indian languages. For example :

> English : Arjuna has one brother  
> Sanskritic-Hindi style : ***Of Arjuna**, \[there\] is a brother*   
> Hindi : **अर्जुन का** एक भाई है

For verbs related to giving, normally, the object being given is in the object form, the person receiving the object is in the “for/to” form. But sometimes, a double object form is also allowed, like in English. Both are okay below.

> **Double Object :**   
> English : Arjuna gives Govinda a book   
> Sanskrit : अर्जुनः गोविन्दं पुस्तकं ददाति
>
> **Single object** :   
> English : Arjuna gives a book to Govinda  
> Sanskrit : अर्जुनः गोविन्दाय पुस्तकं ददाति

### 7. Means vs Accompaniment

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jxed!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F85621679-dbd9-4ec2-955b-9834add546d1_2048x1104.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jxed!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F85621679-dbd9-4ec2-955b-9834add546d1_2048x1104.png "Screenshot 2025-09-18 at 13.14.50.png")

Consider the following two sentences in English :

> *He breaks the fruit **with a hammer*** (hammer : means)  
> *He breaks the fruit **with his friend*** (friend : company)

Here, we see that the preposition “with” performs two different functions in these two sentences. In the first sentence, it conveys an instrumental function using which the action is performed while in the second sentence, it conveys accompaniment. The corresponding Sanskrit ending एन is ambiguous too but to remove the ambiguity and emphasise that it is in the second sense, a word सह is added after the word ending in एन.

As an exercise, in the below sentences, add सह when required.

> युधिष्ठिरः कृष्णेन राजसूयम् आचरति  
> राजा रथेन नगरं गच्छति  
> राजा पुत्रेण नगरं गच्छति  
> सीता वने सदा रामेण अस्ति  
> रावणः तापस्येन वरं प्राप्नोति

### 8. Translational Ambiguities

Another potential ambiguity that arises in Sanskrit translation is that adjectives can also take the role of relative clauses. Consider the following phrases in English.

1.  The dark monkey……

2.  The monkey that is dark…..

In Sanskrit, both of the above can be captured by the phrase

> वानरः कृष्णः  
> Literal translation: *Monkey dark*

Sanskrit has a construction that is equivalent to the second sentence in English which is

> वानरः यः कृष्णः  
> Literal translation: Monkey that-is dark

But in Sanskrit, the particle like यः that does this conditioning can also be optionally omitted as well. So, an innocent pair of Sanskrit words

> वानरः कृष्णः

can be translated as:

1.  The monkey is dark =\> full sentence

2.  The dark monkey….. =\> phrase: combination of a noun and adjective

3.  The monkey that is dark….. =\> phrase: relative clause - adjective
    conditions the noun

A set of three Sanskrit words

> वानरः कृष्णः अस्ति

can be translated as:

1.  The monkey is dark

2.  There is a dark monkey

3.  The dark one is the monkey

4.  There is a monkey that is dark

5.  There is a dark one that is a monkey

When the sentence is long and has many parts like

> वानरः कृष्णः प्रमत्तः  
> Literal translation: *Monkey black intoxicated*

It can be translated as a full sentence with the following possibilities:

1.  The dark monkey is intoxicated

2.  The intoxicated black one is a monkey

3.  The intoxicated monkey is black

4.  The monkey that is dark is intoxicated

5.  The dark one that is a monkey is intoxicated

6.  The monkey that is intoxicated is dark

7.  The intoxicated one that is a monkey is dark

8.  The intoxicated one that is dark is a monkey

9.  The dark one that is intoxicated is a monkey

It can be translated also as a phrase in many ways some of which are:

1.  The intoxicated monkey that is dark…….

2.  The dark monkey that is intoxicated….

3.  The intoxicated dark one that is a monkey…….

And the possibilities increase for phrases and sentences with larger numbers of words! But do not let it scare you. Some of these translations turn out to be the same without any consequence in our understanding of the situation and the more convoluted word orders become less probable and hence you will, based on the context, be able to pick a smooth translation based on the context. I am just telling you here so that you understand these possibilities and also take part in translation debates that you see regarding various texts. In the next article, we will see plural forms. Till then, revise and attempt the exercises below.
