The first thing to be relieved about is that Persian nouns do not have gender like in Hindi or Arabic or French and hence it makes it easier. Also, Persian shares lots of grammatical features with Hindi and Urdu. Some of them are :

- The word order is subject + object / predicate + verb. In other words,
  the verb comes last in Persian and the subject comes first, like in
  Indian languages.

- The question words start with the letter **K** (sometimes **Ch**).

# The e-**z̤āfat**

Let us start with something that is light and familiar to us. The first topic that we are going to see is called technically as the **e-z̤āfat** (literally means : e-connector) in Persian grammar. It is familiar to anyone who has heard even a little Persian. Consider some phrases like ***Sulṭān-e-Hind***, ***Sazā-e-Moṭ, Gustākh-e-Nabi, Nān-e-khub , Kishwar-e-buzorg,*** and ***Mughal-e-ʕazam***, etc. The “e” that comes between the words is what is called as the e-connector. It serves two functions in Persian.

1.  “Of” : It connects a noun to someone or who possess it, or something
    that is related to it. In this regard, it is best translated by the
    English preposition “of”. For example, ***Sulṭān-e-Hind*** means
    “sultan of India” and ***Sazā-e-Moṭ*** means “the punishment of
    death” and ***Gustākh-e-Nabi*** means “insult of the prophet”. Here,
    we see that the “-e-” is functioning as the English preposition
    “of”.

2.  “Descriptor” : Alternatively, the “-e-” connector can also connect a
    noun to its adjective. Note that the adjective comes after the noun
    in Persian. For example, the phrase ***Nān-e-khub*** (literally :
    bread-**e**-good) simply means “good bread” and
    “***Kishwar-e-buzorg”*** simply means “big country”.

These two functions might look weird in Persian but note that in both cases, the noun/adjective that follows the “-e-” connector gives more information about the noun. One can connect multiple such “-e-” connectors serially to form long phrases of nouns and adjectives. Some examples are given below :

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!yk_c!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F67d90f10-d68d-42fe-a49a-45448a8cf03d_1500x352.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!yk_c!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F67d90f10-d68d-42fe-a49a-45448a8cf03d_1500x352.heic)

The famous epic Shāhnāma begins with the phrase

> **nām-e khudāvand-e jān o khirad**  
> *the name of the God of life and wisdom*

Note that the “-e-” connector is not shown in Persian writing since it is a short vowel and Persian script does not have provision to denote short vowels. So, the presence of the “-e-” connector has to be inferred from context. In this article, since I also give transliteration of everything, this should not be a problem for you.

# The Plural Markers -hā & -ān

Persian words are mostly regular in forming their plurals.

- The universal suffix for forming plurals for all words that are
  inanimate is -**hā** **ها**-.

- The suffix for forming plurals that is only used for human nouns and
  for some body parts is -**ān** **ان**-.

- Sometimes, the Arabic feminine plural suffix -**āt** **ات**- is also
  used : popular e.g. **dēh** (*village*) → **dēhāt** (*villages*).

- The only complication that may arise is that in Arabic, the plurals of
  many words are formed by changing the internal structure of the word
  (like English : man → men). In Arabic grammar, these plurals are
  called “***broken plurals***”. Since Persian has lots of Arabic
  loanwords, sometimes these Arabic broken plurals are also used in
  Persian. But, this does not happen often or all the time or with all
  words. The most frequent ones will get familiar soon. For the most
  part though, when Arabic loanwords are borrowed into Persian, their
  plurals are formed in the Persian way only. However, in overly
  religious and formal contexts, one may see Arabic broken plurals (like
  in overly formal contexts, English uses Latinate plurals for Latin
  words like *focus* → *foci*, *larva* → *larvae*, and *medium* -\>
  *media*). But, at the beginner stage, one need not worry about Arabic
  broken plurals.

# The Indefinite Article Marker -ī

Persian normally does not have any word corresponding to the definite article “the” (although one important exception will be seen soon). Hence, any word mostly translates with a definite article in English, if it is in the singular. For denoting the indefinite article though, Persian has a suffix “-ī” attached to the end of a word. For plurals, the Persian indefinite article marker is best translated as “some”.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!fgZv!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fafdbddc2-5b7f-4751-bf36-50589c31da3c_1470x258.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!fgZv!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fafdbddc2-5b7f-4751-bf36-50589c31da3c_1470x258.heic)

But there is an important rule :

> When the indefinite marker falls on a noun, it loses its ability to
> take the “-e-” connector.

So, what do I do if I want to say indefinite phrases with adjectives like “a big old house”?

- A word with an indefinite article marker cannot take the “-e-”
  connector to connect to its possessor and adjectives. The adjectives
  in this case are placed without the “-e-” connector and are separated
  by the word “o” meaning “and”.

- To make an indefinite adjectival phrase, another option is that the
  indefinite article marker “-ī” can come at the very end of a complex
  “-e-” connector nested phrases to make the entire phrase indefinite.

Both the ways are illustrated using an example below :

> noun.

# To Be or Not To Be

To make full sentences, we need verbs like *is*, *are*, and *am*. Notice how the third person singular form “**ast**” meaning “**is**” is cognate to Sanskrit “**asti**”. Persian verbs of being have three forms. The first form denoted with “-” is used only as a suffix attached to the previous word. The second form is the full form used normally for non-living beings. The third one with an extra “h” is even more emphatic and is used only in emphatic situations. Although the usages of these three forms are different, the meanings are exactly the same. Note how all these verbs come at the end of the sentence like in Hindi-Urdu.

Example :

> من خوب**م** *man* *khub**am***
>
> من خوب استم *man khub **astam***
>
> من خوب هستم *man khub **hastam***
>
> All three above mean : **I am** good

Some examples from the Shāhnāma :

> تویی کرده کردگار جهان   
> *tū-(y**)ī** kardá-ye kardgār-e jahān*   
> Literal translation : You **are** the created \[one\] of the creator
> of the world   
> Smooth translation : You are the creation of the creator of the world
>
> به سالار گفتند “ما بنده**-ایم** ؛ خود اندر جهان شاه را زنده-**ایم**  
> *bah sālār guftand “mā **bandà-īm** ; khud andar-e jahān shāh rā
> zindá-**īm**”*  
> Literal translation : They said to the king “**We are** slaves ;
> **We** ourselves **are** alive for the king \[who is\] inside of this
> world”.   
> Smooth translation : They said to the king “We are slaves. We keep
> ourselves alive for the king in this world”

# Possession using Pronouns & Suffixes

If you want to say stuff like “my book” or “your pen” in Persian, there are two ways. One way is to use the standard “-e-connector” and say stuff like “***kitāb-e-man***” (lit : book of I) and ***qalam-e-tū*** (lit : pen of you). Another way is to use special suffixes that can be attached directly to the noun which are called the possessive suffixes. These are given below. Again, once you know the singular ones, the plurals are gotten by simply adding the human plural suffix ان- -ān to the singular suffixes/

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!CAlN!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F371c7888-bd18-463b-a189-ea1c683f954c_1492x372.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!CAlN!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F371c7888-bd18-463b-a189-ea1c683f954c_1492x372.heic)

So, to say “My book” in Persian, you can either say “***kitāb-e tū***” or “***kitābat***”, both of which are exactly equivalent in meaning.

To say something like “*This book is mine*” or “*That pen is yours*” , what are the counterparts in Persian? Here is where the familiar word **مال māl** (meaning : stuff, thing, property, etc..) comes in handy. In Persian, you literally say it as follows :

- ***īn kitāb māl-e man ast*** (this book is the stuff-of me)

- ***ān qalam māl-e-tū ast*** (that pen is the stuff-of you).

# Prepositions

The only part of Persian that would be completely new to you in Persian would be the prepositions. Even here, some of these words (e.g. bē, ba’ad, barāy-e) will be familiar with you from Hindi usages and Urdu especially. Commit these important prepositions below to memory as soon as possible as these are the bread and butter of Persian. The prepositions are divided into three types in Persian.

- Simple single syllabled ones

- Prepositions that have to take the “-e-connector” before connecting to
  their objects

- Compound prepositions that use some other word along with a simple
  preposition
