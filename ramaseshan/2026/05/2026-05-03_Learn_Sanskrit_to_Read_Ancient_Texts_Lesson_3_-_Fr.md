# Subject-Verb Agreement

When the subject changes from singular to plural, something happens to the verb as well. This change happens in English and Hindi too.

> The man (*sing*) **goes** to the forest  
> The men (*plu*) **go** to the forest
>
> आदमी *(sing)* **जाता है  **
> आदमी *(plu)* **जाते हैं**

Technically, this phenomenon is called as **subject-verb agreement** (you do not need to know this term though, unless you want to become a linguist or impress scholars!). This same thing happens in Sanskrit as well which is illustrated below. We already saw one example of this :

> Singular subject : अस्ति (is) → Plural subject : सन्ति (are)

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-dSl!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F536ec3d4-c1f6-4e73-8844-64819c2f1236_2048x1107.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-dSl!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F536ec3d4-c1f6-4e73-8844-64819c2f1236_2048x1107.png "Screenshot 2025-09-15 at 11.05.32.png")

For many verbs, we have

> अति (singular subject) → अन्ति (plural subject)

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!UMmr!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F513cf040-1fad-456c-9deb-4ce08f695a7a_2048x1130.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!UMmr!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F513cf040-1fad-456c-9deb-4ce08f695a7a_2048x1130.png "Screenshot 2025-09-15 at 11.23.11.png")

अन्नम् = food, भवति = अस्ति, पर्जन्यः = rainfall, सम्भवः = possibility, समुद्भवः = arising, कर्म = Vedic karma rituals

# Best among the gods : Plural Possessives

In this section, we will explore the plural ending of the possessive form (singular ending : अस्य) by reading a short passage. The verb वदति means “says” and वरः means desirable which normally used to mean best.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!YHIL!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3a54eef4-da06-44e1-82bb-70695beb18a5_2048x1131.png)

> अस्य =\> आनाम्.

# Brāhmaṇa invokes the devas sacrificially

We saw the plural forms for the subject pronouns last time. For neuter gender nouns, as in the singular, the plural forms are identical for both the subject and the object forms. Therefore, वनम् forest, and वनानि forests, can stand for both subject and object. How about the masculine gender nouns? Turns out they have a plural that is almost identical to the neuter plurals with a slight truncation. Observe below:

वनम् (sing) =\> वनानि (plu)

देवः (sing) =\> देवान् (plu)

So you see that the object plural ending for masculine nouns आन् is a truncated version of the neuter plural ending आनि. Take the example of the following sentence that uses the verb यजति that means invoking by sacrifice.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!PAQE!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F58f1a28b-3a71-4f98-a968-10b7cf7a5146_2048x1118.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!PAQE!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F58f1a28b-3a71-4f98-a968-10b7cf7a5146_2048x1118.png "Screenshot 2025-09-15 at 11.06.05.png")

Did you see how the ending changed here?

> By/with : एन (sing) → ऐः (pls)  
> Object Masculine : अम् (sing) → आन् (plu)

# Gender & Number Identification

Apart from singular and plural, Sanskrit nouns and adjectives also have a **dual** form that is used for exactly two of something. But I am skipping them now and will teach the dual forms of all nouns and verbs at the 3rd level because they occur less frequently than the singular and the plural. You will see soon that **the singular forms of various noun patterns are diverse and need to be relearned for different categories of nouns**. For example, the pattern for the four nouns राम, सीता, मुनि, आत्मन् are very different and are unrelated. But **the plural endings are the same or highly similar for all nouns in Sanskrit**.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!7_pB!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0b50d12c-eda8-49c7-91f6-fcaf04d8ee55_1758x950.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!7_pB!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0b50d12c-eda8-49c7-91f6-fcaf04d8ee55_1758x950.png "Screenshot 2026-02-21 at 00.29.58.png")

Suppose you already know a Sanskrit word because it is directly borrowed into an Indian language. Then, its endings are removed. रामः becomes राम in Hindi. Seeing only the Hindi word राम, it is difficult to figure out whether it masculine or neuter in Sanskrit. For the specific case of राम, it is obviously masculine as it refers to a male person. But what about a word like धर्म? Is it masculine or neuter? Here are some thumb rules to decide that.

- Nouns ending in the suffixes -**अन , -त्र , -त्व** are always neuter.
  E.g. दर्शनम् , स्नानम्, सोपानम् , नयनम् , नेत्रम् , शस्त्रम् , शास्त्रम् , नक्षत्रम्,
  हिन्दुत्वम्

- Nouns ending in -**त** and -**य** are actually adjectives. But if they
  are used as generic abstract nouns, they tend to be neuter. E.g.
  सुभाषितम् , स्वागतम्, ऋतम्, कार्यम् , धैर्यम् , वीर्यम्.

- Many nouns that have a **male god** behind them are also grammatically
  masculine. E.g. सूर्यः , चन्द्रः , पर्जन्यः , यमः

- **The gender of a compound word is always the gender of the final part
  of it** that takes the case endings - e.g. the gender of वनराजः is
  masculine as राजः is masculine even though वनम् is neuter.

- But if all these fail, ultimately you have to just place a bet. But
  know in that case that statistically, there are much more masculine
  than neuter nouns in Sanskrit so that a random word outside of those
  special neuter suffixes is always more likely to be masculine in
  gender.
