# Components of Aṣṭādhyāyī

In the next section, we will survey the genius text of **Aṣṭādhyāyī** as a whole first and then work through the full derivation of a sample sentence from scratch using the rules from the text. Before that, let us survey the parts of Aṣṭādhyāyī. Traditionally, it is described as being made up of five parts or limbs (*pañcāṅga*).

### Sūtrapāṭha

This is the core part or, (to use computer science jargon), the program that has the 4000 rules of derivation. The core of Aṣṭādhyāyī is a list of about 4000 aphorisms, that are a highly condensed set of abbreviated technical rules which describe as well as derive the formation of grammatically correct Sanskrit words and phrases. It is like a computer program that generates legit Sanskrit words.

### Dhātupāṭha

This is a list of around 2000 verbal roots (along with meanings) that form the base for deriving almost any word in the Classical Sanskrit language. The roots are arranged into ten classes with various other sub-groupings. These roots do not stand alone but contain extra data with them called anubandhas that give us more grammatical information about the root and also inform us about the constraints regarding the type of grammatical operations that the verb root can undergo. For example, when **Śaṅkarācārya** sees an old man sitting on the banks of the river Ganges, fine-tuning his grammar knowledge so that he can debate effectively, he scolds the old man asking why he was sharpening his grammar when it will not save him while death comes to him. This verse is from the fourth line of the first stanza of the famous ***Bhaja Govindam*** song, that is heard in many Indian households today, and goes as follows :

> s*amprāpte sannihite kāle nahi nahi rakṣati **ḍukṛñ karaṇe***
>
> When your time is over (death comes), “***ḍukṛñ karaṇe***“ won’t save
> you

Here, the bolded part is an entry of a verb root from the dhātupāṭha corresponding to the verb root in Sanskrit that means “to do”. It breaks down as follows :

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Q_A2!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F93c63799-e03b-492c-8e70-374ed93630a1_676x250.heic)

The yellow shaded part “***kṛ***” is the actual verb root that means doing or making that derives famous and well-known Sanskrit words like *kṛta* (done), *kartavya* (to be done), *kara* (doer), and *kartṛ* (doer). The violet parts “***ḍu***” and “***ñ***” are called ***anubandha-s*** in the Indian grammatical tradition and **indicatory letters** by the Western translators. They are not a part of the verb root and only indicate the important grammatical properties of the verb root. For example, in English, different verbs form their past tenses in different ways. Many verbs form past tense by adding an extra “(e)d” at the end (e.g. educate → educated) while other verbs change vowels inside their root to mark the past tense (e.g. sing → sang , think → thought). It is not easily predictable from the root as to how it will form the past tense ; for example, both the words “ring” and “bring” have an almost similar phonetic structure but both form their past tense in entirely different ways as seen below :

> ring → rang (not “~~rought~~”!!)
>
> bring → brought (not “~~brang~~”!!)

So, such grammatical information in English as to how the vowel changes in the past tense has to be learnt on a case-by-case basis for many English verbs. In the Indian grammatical tradition, the anubandha or indicatory letters in violet contain such valuable grammatical information about the verb root in Sanskrit.

The part in red which is “***karaṇe***” tells the meaning and the application of the root in Sanskrit and it literally translates as “in doing” which tells us that this verb root has to be applied while talking about doing something.

Also, it is likely that the dhātupāṭha as we now have it has been augmented since Pāṇini’s time, by the addition of many extra roots to keep up with new developments of the Sanskrit language, which stopped only around the time of Patāñjali after which the grammatical tradition froze. It is also clear that Pāṇini was drawing on earlier grammatical traditions and he might not have constructed the entire body of dhātupāṭha by himself.

### Gaṇapāṭha

Parallel to the dhātupāṭha, is the *gaṇapāṭha* which translates literally as “list of groups” which derives the bases of underived words and pronominal stems. Pāṇini derives all the nouns and adjectives of the Sanskrit language by the verb roots by addition of appropriate prefixes and suffixes. But there are some other words in the Sanskrit language like prepositions, basic adverbs, conjunctions, etc that are not from any verbal base but still cannot be reduced to any verbal base either, and since these are all listed in the gaṇapāṭha.

### Uṇādi Sūtras

Another part of Pāṇinian grammar are the *Uṇādisūtras* ‘the rules for (suffixes) beginning with ***uṇ***’. The Uṇādisūtras specify series of marginal suffixes which can be used to derive small groups of words, the derivation of which is not directly addressed in the Aṣṭādhyāyī. We all know that even in English, some suffixes can be applied to only a small group of words. For example, the suffix “*-let*” that forms words for smaller versions of things like *booklet*, *leaflet*, *droplet*, etc, but can’t be applied on other words to give *~~chairlet~~* or *~~cardlet~~*. The Uṇādi are those kind of suffixes in Sanskrit that are applicable only for a very restricted kind of verb roots or nominal stems. Pāṇini refers to an uṇādi list twice in the Aṣṭādhyāyī. However, the Uṇādisūtras, as we now have them today, contain mostly post-Pāṇinian material ; it is likely that many of the small groups of words whose derivation is treated in the Uṇādisūtras were simply treated as underived by Pāṇini.

### Liṅgānuśāsana

It translates as “instructions on gender”. Like many North Indian languages and other European languages, Sanskrit nouns and adjectives too have grammatical genders even for inanimate nouns and this part of the grammatical tradition attempts to specify the rules for determining the gender of nouns (masculine / feminine / neuter). This part is short and also not comprehensive in its coverage, and hence modern scholars consider that it was not an original part of the tradition. It appears that for Pāṇini, noun gender did not need to be taught since it is known from common usage; that is,nouns come into the grammar pre-specified for gender; this view is first explicitly stated by Patañjali himself, who also hence seems to not have considered any Liṅgānuśāsana as a part of Pāṇini’s grammar in his own day.

### Śivasūtras

Although not a part of the five traditional limbs, it serves as the phonological base by listing the sounds of the Sanskrit language. Read my first article to explain its rationale in more detail. But the way in which the sounds are arranged are not the same as that of the varṇamālā that organises the sounds neatly by type and manner of articulation in a grid. The śivasūtras have the following sounds :

> A I U **Ṇ**
>
> Ṛ Ḷ **K**
>
> E O **Ṅ**
>
> Ai Au **C**
>
> Ha Ya Va Ra **Ṭ**
>
> la **Ṇ**
>
> Ña Ma Ṅa Ṇa Na **M**
>
> Jha Bha **Ñ**
>
> Gha Ḍha Dha **Ṣ**
>
> Ja Ba Ga Ḍa Da **Ś**
>
> Kha Pha Cha Ṭha Tha Ca Ṭa Ta **V**
>
> Ka Pa **Y**
>
> Śa Ṣa Sa **R**
>
> Ha **L**

There are some things to note here :

- The letters in the bold at the end of each line again are anubandhas
  or indicatory letters that serve as short hand for summarising the
  grammatical properties of the sounds it includes. Thus, each of the
  fourteen sūtras begins with a list of one or more phonemes and ends
  with an anubandha letter.

- A sequence of sounds, consisting of the first phoneme of the desired
  grouping (followed by a if a consonant) followed by the **anubandha**
  which follows the final phoneme of the desired grouping, is called as
  a ***pratyāhāra***. For example, the syllable “***aK***” refers to the
  five vowels a, i, u, ṛ, and ḷ, (starting form “**a**” in the list
  above and ending in ‘**K**’) while “***ec***” refers to the four
  vowels e, o, ai, and au (excluding the anubandhas **Ṅ** and **C**). If
  you want to refer to all vowel, then you can simply say “**aC**”. If
  you want to refer to the sounds y / v / r / l, you can say “**yaṆ**”
  and for summoning the vowels i / u / ṛ / ḷ , one can use the aphorism
  “**iK**”. In short, a ***pratyāhāra*** is an aphorism like “xZ” that
  refers to all sounds starting from “x” to “"Z” in the śivasūtras,
  excluding all the anubandha indicatory letters (bolded in the list).

- The arrangement of sounds may be haphazard but this is actually the
  most concise and optimal way to arrange sounds to refer to a specific
  group of sounds concisely using the initial and final way. For the
  collection of letters that Pāṇini wants to refer to, this ordering is
  the most optimal, as per the analysis done by the mathematician Wiebke
  Petersen in his paper
  [here.](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10849-004-2117-7)

# Divisions of the Sūtras

### Overall Organisation : Adhyāyas and Pādas

Let us now focus on the sūtrapāṭha of the text and examine how it is divided. It is first divided into eight chapters (hence the name *aṣṭādhyāyī*) and each chapter is divided into four ***pada***-s (feet / sections). So, when I mention something like “Rule 5.2.6”, it means Chapter 5, Section 2, Rule 6. In terms of the overall organisation, the chapters are roughly and broadly organised as follows :

**Chapter 1**

- Definitions

- Rules of Interpretation

- Rules dealing with parasmaipada and ātmanepada verbal conjugations

- rules dealing with noun functions (**kāraka**-s)

**Chapter 2**

- rules for forming compound words

- case endings of nouns

- number and gender of compounds

- replacement of roots/stems

- deletion by **luK**

**Chapter 3**

- derivation of of secondary verb roots like **saN** , etc.

- derivations of items ending in a **kṛt**

- the derivation of verbs

**Chapter 4-5**

- derivation of nouns and adjectives

- ***strī pratyaya-***s (feminine suffixes)

- derivation of ***taddhita*** suffixes (suffixes attached to non-verbal
  stems)

**Chapter 6-7**

- ***dvitva*** - doubling or reduplication

- ***samprasāraṇa*** (weakening of certain verb roots / root ablaut
  reduction)

- ***saṃhitā*** (rules for connected speech)

- ***svara***-s (pitch accents)

- non-deletion by **luK**

- Modifications conditioned by the following ***pada***

- phonological operations relative to an ***aṅga*** (presuffixal base)

**Chapter 8**

- dvitva (doubling) relative to a ***pada***

- accent relative to a ***pada***

- other phonological operations

Notes however that this is not strict and you do find few rules in one topic scattered in other places throughout the text.

### Structural Division : Sapāda-saptādhyāyī and Tripādī

Another major functional division of the Aṣṭādhyāyī in the tradition is a two way division.

1.  **Sapāda-saptādhyāyī** : The first seven chapters and the first pāda
    of Chapter 8

2.  **Tripādī** : The second, third, and the fourth pāda of Chapter 8.

Imagine the aṣṭãdhyãyĩ as a recipe for cooking words based on roots, prefixes, and suffixes. The first major part of the aṣṭãdhyãyĩ which contain the seven chapters and the first quarter of the eighth chapter consists of those rules that can be applied in any order (provided the conditions they demand are satisfied). But, the last three-quarters of the last chapter constitute a special class of rules and the aṣṭãdhyãyĩ begins 8.2.1 as

> 8.2.1 ***pūrvatrāsiddham***
>
> Every subsequent rule from here on, is **not effected** (**asiddha**)
> by any previous rule

This means that the rules starting from 8.2.1 , once when they begin to be applied on to any output, becomes ineligible for the previous rules (before 8.2.1) to act on. Technically, this phenomenon is a type of what is technically called **asiddhatva** which refers to important control mechanisms for the sequential application of rules in Pāṇinian grammar. What we do in grammar to derive any Sanskrit word is starting with a verb root and then applying a series of rules that apply one after another ; i.e. the output gotten by applying to the input of one rule is eligible for going as the input of the next rule in the sequence. What this 8.2.1 does is mainly two things :

> 1.  If we apply the rules after 8.2.1 to get any result, then that
>     result is not eligible to get operated on by the rules before
>     8.2.1 ; i.e. the output gotten after applying the rules after
>     8.2.1 are invisible or unrecognised (asiddha) to the rules before
>     8.2.1.
>
> 2.  Even within the tripādī (last three **pāda**s**)**, any output
>     gotten by application of a later rule is not eligible to get
>     operated on by a preceding rule ; i.e. the output gotten after
>     applying a latter rule (within the tripādī) are invisible or
>     unrecognised (asiddha) to the preceding rules (within the
>     tripādī).
>
> Or equivalently, any rule after 8.2.1 (in the tripādī) will take as an
> input, the output from any other rule that comes before it, and
> nothing from the output of the tules that come after it. Or, any rule
> after 8.2.1 blocks its input to the outputs of all other rules after
> it
>
> E.g. The rule 8.4.39 recognises for its input, any output gotten from
> the application of any rule before 8.4.39 only. The output gotten
> after the application of 8.4.39 can be acted upon as an input, only by
> the rules after 8.4.39 and nothing before it. From the point of view
> of 8.4.39, every rule from 8.4.40 onwards is asiddha (their outputs
> invisible to it). **Each rule within the tripādī (8.2.1 onwards) does
> not recognise the operations carried out by their successors.**
>
> Note that this is not applicable in the sapāda-saptādhyāyī at all. A
> rule, say 4.7.1 in the sapāda-saptādhyāyī can act on the result of a
> later rule, say 4.8.2 within the sapāda-saptādhyāyī.

### Functional Division : Four types of Rules and Three Types of Operations

Now, let us classify the rules into four types, and the fourth type into three sub-types, based on what they do through a neat digram below.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!PNIx!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2e0cc257-a1f9-4b3c-ac4c-85fd3197b040_1574x816.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!PNIx!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2e0cc257-a1f9-4b3c-ac4c-85fd3197b040_1574x816.heic)

The four broad types of rules are :

1.  ***Saṃjñā*** / **Definition** : These rules introduce and define the
    technical jargon of Sanskrit grammar.

2.  ***Paribhāṣā*** **/ Meta-Rule** : These kind of rules govern and
    control how other rules are to be applied and talk about other rules
    ; kind of like a rule for other rules and hence the term meta-rule.

3.  ***Adhikāra*** **/ Heading** : These are basically headings that are
    to be applicable into every rule in the section they head. We saw an
    example of this already with the rule 8.2.1 ***pūrvatrāsiddham***
    which is to be applicable for all the succeeding rules after 8.2.1
    (throughout the *tripādī*) and demands that they are invisible to
    the inputs of any rule that precede them.

4.  **Operations** : These rules are the bulk class that actually do
    something and actively drive the derivational process of words. They
    are further subdivided into three sub-types depending on what kind
    of operations they do.

- ***Vidhi*** **/ Positive Injunction** : These injunctions aid the
  process of deriving correct and legit Sanskrit words

- ***Niyama*** **/ Restriction** : These kind of rules prevent the
  deviation of wrong words by restricting certain operations to certain
  data (roots, suffixes, prefixes etc..).

- ***Pratiṣedha*** / **Exception** : These kind of rules give exceptions
  so that the positive injunctions are applicable despite being
  restricted by other rules.

Having seen this, let us now proceed to examine the generic structure of the positive injunctions or vidhis.

# Meta-Language & Structure of a Vidhi / Injunction

In this section, we will examine the structure of a *vidhi* or an injunction.

### Nominal Case Endings : A Gentle Introduction

Before that, it is important to get familiarised with some aspects of the language. Consider the following sentences in Sanskrit which has the word Devadatta, a very popular name used by grammarians when they have to give examples.

1.  **Devadatta** *sees* a horse

2.  The horse *sees* **Devadatta**

3.  The horse *stands* **with Devadatta**

4.  The owner of the horse *gives* it away **to Devadatta**

5.  The horse *takes* food **from Devadatta**

6.  The horse however does not *eat* the food **of Devadatta**

7.  The horse *trusts* **in Devadatta**

The following are the roles played by the noun Devadatta, in relation to the verb in italics in each sentence.

1.  **Nominative /** ***Kartṛ*** : Here, Devadatta is the subject of the
    verb “sees” and is doing the seeing. Devadatta answers the question
    : Who/What is doing the action?

2.  **Accusative /** ***Karman*** : Here, Devadatta is the object of the
    verb “sees” and is the one being seen. Devadatta answers the
    question : Who/What is suffering the action?

3.  **Instrumental /** ***Karaṇa*** : Here, Devadatta accompanies the
    subject of the verb “stands” in the sense that he is standing along
    with the horse. Devadatta answers the question : Who/What is
    accompanying the action?

4.  **Dative /** ***Sampradāna*** : Here, Devadatta is the benefactor of
    the verb “give” in the sense that he is the sake for whom the giving
    is done. Devadatta answers the question : Who/What is benefiting
    from the action?

5.  **Ablative /** ***Apādāna*** : Here, Devadatta is the originator of
    the verb taking in the sense that he is the source from which the
    horse is taking food. Devadatta answers the question : Who/What is
    the source of the action?

6.  **Genitive /** ***Sambandha*** : Here, Devadatta is not at all
    related to the verb “eat” but is related to another noun which is
    “food” in the sense that the food belongs to Devadatta.

7.  **Locative /** ***Adhikaraṇa*** : Here, Devadatta is the location of
    the verb “trust” in the sense that he is the place where the trust
    is located. Devadatta answers the question : Where is the action
    taking place?

English is able to convey and distinguish between these seven roles by word order (subject comes before the verb, objects comes after the verb) and prepositions (with, for, to, from, of, in, on, at, etc…). Same with Hindi which uses postpositions (ko, kā, kī, ke, se, meṃ, etc..). But Sanskrit nouns instead uses case endings that are a part of the word itself to coney and distinguishes these roles. Take the Sanskrit word ***pariṣad*** that means “assembly”. When it performs the seven roles above, it takes the following endings that are highlighted in yellow below.

### The General Structure of a Vidhi

The most generic structure of a vidhi or injunction looks like this:

> *Structure of a Vidhi :*   
> **A is replaced by B when it is preceded by C and succeeded by D.**
>
> **C-A-B → C-B-D**

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!p2NV!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb29d26b3-a878-479f-84e5-59039fafe536_1232x1034.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!p2NV!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb29d26b3-a878-479f-84e5-59039fafe536_1232x1034.heic)

Of course, not all the four elements need to be there in every vidhi. For some rules, we might simply want B to replace A irrespective of what is there before or after A and hence there is no need to mention any C or D. For other rules, B replaces A depending only on what precedes A and in that case D is unnecessary to mention. The reverse can also happen. But the most generic vidhi has all the four elements.

### Meta-Language for Encoding Vidhis

How does the Sanskrit grammatical tradition convey succinctly what is A, B, C and D in a vidhi? It is here that the nominal case endings come in. We saw that the case endings of nouns, normally conveys the role played by a noun with respect to the verb of the sentence in the normal usage of Sanskrit language. But in the rules of the Aṣṭādhyāyī, the case endings are hijacked and are instead used to encode the generic structure of the vidhis.

**Nominative** (Case 1) : This case ending normally conveys that the word is the doer of an action ; but in the rules, it conveys what is the one that replaces (B).

**Genitive** (Case 6) : This case ending normally conveys that the word is in relationship to another word; but in the rules, it conveys what is the one that is being replaced (A).

**Ablative** (Case 5) : This case ending normally conveys that the word is the source of the action of the verb; but in the rules, it conveys what should precede for the replacement to happen (C).

**Locative** (Case 7) : This case ending normally conveys that the word is the location of the action of the verb; but in the rules, it conveys what should succeed for the replacement to happen (D).

So, we have :

\\C\_{abl} - A\_{gen} - D\_{loc} \rightarrow C\_{abl} - B\_{nom} - D\_{loc}\\

This is something interesting because the case endings are foregoing their normal roles and are instead taking up new computational roles for encoding the structure of rules. This is what is called as a meta-language in computer science and formal language theory. ***What Pāṇini has done through his magnum opus is creating a meta-language (meta-Sanskrit) to describe the grammar of a natural language (natural-Sanskrit)***. To know in detail and lots of examples, read my first part introductory article.

> Pāṇini hijacks the normal Sanskrit language (at least some part of
> it - the grammatical case endings here) for his coding purposes ; in
> computer jargon, this is called **metalanguage!** Pāṇini is
> essentially creating a metalanguage out of a natural language
> (Sanskrit) for his coding purpose!!!!

As an example to illustrate this, consider a sandhi rule. Sanskrit speakers were very conscious of the sound changes happening at word boundaries (since their language flourished in a mostly oral culture) and hence minutely noted these changes. When the vowel that ends the first word is **i / u / ṛ / ḷ** , and it is followed by another vowel in the next word immediately, Sanskrit converts the initial vowel into its appropriate semi-vowel consonant **y / v / r / l**. These transformations are:

- i → y

- u → v

- ṛ → r

- ḷ → l

Some familiar examples of this is given below :

- prat***i*** + uṣā → prat***y***uṣā

- s***u*** + āgatam → s***v***āgatam

- māt***ṛ*** + aṃśaḥ → māt**r**aṃśaḥ

So, the transformations here can be summarised as follows from the Śivasūtras :

- What is replaced : **i / u / ṛ / ḷ** → referred to by “ik”

- What replaces : y / v / r / l → referred to as “**yaṇ**”

- What should precede : Does not matter

- What should follow : Any vowel → referred to by “**ac**”

> 7.3 **chidIṂ̆R dvaidhīkaraṇe**

What are the capital letters? Once again, they are the anubandhas or indicatory letters that convey grammatical information about the verb. What does the indicatory **IṂ̆R** convey?

First, the letter **Ṃ̆** conveys that the preceding **I** is just an indicatory vowels because this is a special type of nasal vowel in Sanskrit that is otherwise not there in the naturally spoken language and is hence here used to indicate that the vowel having that special nasal is just indicatory of grammatical information and not a part of the root. Note that Pāṇini did not have any access to colour coding like we have and hence had to use sounds only to convey what is part of the root and what is not. Hence, this class of special nasals.

The actual indicatory part **IR** conveys that this root is among those special class of roots that receives a nasal infix into its present tense stem. Look at the Sanskrit root chid (meaning : to split) that gives us popular words like chedaḥ (cut), chedyaḥ (object to be cut), and so on. But in the present tense verb form alone, we suddenly get chi**na**tti (he cuts), with an extra “**na**” inside the root.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!psBO!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb499b318-4207-4717-9bde-c69245de790c_1292x272.heic)

The presence of the indicatory IR is to ensure that this root becomes eligible eventually to attract the nasal infix “na” before to form the present tense form that is our target which is “chinatti”, as visualised in the figure above.   

There are other roots that behave like this, for example, the related root “bhid” that also means to tear open or blow apart, and takes the present tense form analogously as “bhinatti”. If you refer to the dhātupāṭha, you will see the root at 7.2 as

> 7.2 **bhidIṂ̆R vidāraṇe**

For example, here is a famous application of the past tense of the word in Ṛg Veda 1.32.1 which uses the past tense of “**bhinatti**” which is “**abhinat**” (final “ti” dropped from the present and an extra “a” added at the front)

Having seen the exact role of the indicatory IR, it is easy to derive the word “chinatti” from the verb root “chid”. It is easy and let us proceed step by step.

**Step 1 :** Verifying that **chidIR** is a valid root :

> Aṣṭādhyāyī 1.3.1  
> **bhvādayo dhātavaḥ  **
> Translation: “The roots beginning with **bhū** are called dhātus.”

This rule defines what counts as a verbal root. √**chid** is one such root. Summarising it in a pseudo-code format below :

    INPUT: verbal root = "chid"
    STATUS: valid dhātu (1.3.1)

**Step 2 :** Choosing the Present Tense

We want present time.

> Aṣṭādhyāyī 3.2.123  
> **vartamāne laṭ**  
> Translation: “The affix **LAṬ** is attached (to the verb root) for
> present time.”

So we select the tense-marker **LAṬ**. Note that the entire affix is in caps because it will soon disappear (it is not a part of the word and is there to just attach the appropriate ending). Hence, LAṬ itself is not visible in the final word (**chinatti**). It just triggers the attachment of appropriate personal endings (that will be a part of the final word) and then it will disappear.

    if tense == present:
        apply lakāra = "laṭ"   (3.2.123)

**Step 3 :** Choosing the Parasmaipada ending

> Aṣṭādhyāyī 1.3.78  
> **śeṣāt kartari parasmaipadam**  
> In the agentive sense (kartari - active voice), parasmaipada endings
> are used for remaining verbs (unless otherwise specified).”

For now it is important to understand that our root falls under the category of “remaining verbs”. So we choose parasmaipada endings.

    if usage == kartari:
        voice = parasmaipada   (1.3.78)

**Step 4 :** Choosing the appropriate Person and Number

In the sentence “*Devadatta cuts…*”, Devadatta is the subject of the verb and is referred to in third person and is singular and the verb is used in the active voice. Hence, we want:

- 3rd person

- singular

- parasmaipada (active voice)

> Aṣṭādhyāyī 3.4.78  
> **tip tas ji sip thas tha mip vas mas**  
> Translation: “These are the parasmaipada personal endings.”

For 3rd singular → **tip**

So now we attach : **chid** + **tiP**

    if person == 3rd and number == singular and voice == parasmaipada:
        ending = "tip"    (3.4.78)

    form = "chid" + "tip"

**Step 5** : Apply Verb Class (Gaṇa) Rule

√chid belongs to the 7th class (rudhādi group). This Class 7 verbs insert a nasal “**n**" before the ending, as we expect it to get the word “**chinatti**”.

> Aṣṭādhyāyī 3.1.78  
> **rudhādibhyaḥ śnam**  
> Translation: After roots belonging to the rudhādi group, the affix
> “**ŚnaM**” is added.

Note : There are two indicatory letters in the affix - **Ś , M** and hence the actual thing added is just “na” which is what we expect too, from the final form of the word “**chinatti**”. The dummy indicatory letter M in **ŚnaM** actually tells us that this “na” is not to be placed a suffix but to be placed in between the root after its vowel (according to rule 1.1.47)!

> Aṣṭādhyāyī 1.1.47   
> **midaco’ntyātparaḥ**  
> The augment that has an indicatory “**m**” comes after the last among
> the vowels and becomes the final position of that which it augments.

Note : This sutra explains the use of the indicatory “**m”**. The augments having an indicatory “**m**”, technically called “**mit**” augments, are placed immediately after the final vowel of a word

So, we have :

**chid → chi + na + d**

Keeping the **tiP** attached, we get :

**chi-na-d-tiP**

    if root in rudhādi_class:
        insert "śnam" (3.1.78) in the middle (1.1.47)
       

    apply tasya_lopaḥ (1.3.9)
        "śnam" → "na"

    form = "chinad" + "tip"

**Step 6** : Simplify the ending

Now we have: **chind** + **tiP**

Aṣṭādhyāyī 1.3.9  
**tasya lopaḥ**  
Translation: Its (the marker’s) deletion.

Removing the indicatory marker “**P**” from “**tiP**”. The visible ending becomes: **ti.**

So: **chinad + ti**

    apply tasya_lopaḥ (1.3.9)
        "tip" → "ti"

    form = "chinadti"

**Step 7** : Sandhi

We now examine: **chinadti**

There is a consonant cluster:

**d + t**

Aṣṭādhyāyī 8.4.55  
**khari ca**  
Translation: “Before a ‘**khaR**’ (voicless) consonant, (a voiced consonant becomes voiceless)”.  
Note : The affix “**khaR**” refers to all voiceless consonants using the enumeration from the Śivasūtras. So, we have

Since **t** is voiceless:

“**d**” (voiced dental stop) → becomes voiceless “**t**”

So: d → t

Thus: **chinadti** → **chinatti**

    if voiced_stop followed_by voiceless_stop:
        devoice first consonant   (8.4.55)

    "d" + "t" → "t" + "t"

    "chinadti" → "chinatti"

The entire sequence is summarised as :

    Goal:  tiṅanta (finite verb) = "chinatti"  [present, kartari, parasmaipada, 3sg]

    (0)  √chid   “to cut”
         │  (1.3.1) bhvādayo dhātavaḥ
         │         “Roots beginning with bhū etc. are called dhātus.”
         ▼
    (1)  dhātu: √chid
         │
         │  (3.2.123) vartamāne laṭ
         │           “Laṭ is used for present time.”
         ▼
    (2)  √chid + LAṬ (present-time operation chosen)
         │
         │  (1.3.78) śeṣāt kartari parasmaipadam
         │          “In kartari (agentive sense), parasmaipada (active) endings apply (by default).”
         ▼
    (3)  √chid + (parasmaipada selection active)
         │
         │  (3.4.78) tip tas ji sip thas tha mip vas mas
         │          “These are the parasmaipada endings.”
         │          Choose: tip (3rd person singular)
         ▼
    (4)  √chid + tip
         │
         │  (3.1.78) rudhādibhyaḥ śnam
         │          “After rudhādi-class roots, śnam is added.”
         │          (√chid is treated as in this class ; insert "śnam" in the middle (1.1.47))
         ▼
    (5)  √chid + śnam + tip
         ├───────────────────────────────────────────────┐
         │                                               │
         │  Branch A: śnam processing                    │  Branch B: tip processing
         │                                               │
         │  (1.3.9) tasya lopaḥ                          │  (1.3.9) tasya lopaḥ

# Rule Interactions in Pāṇinian Linguistics

### Asiddhatva

In the previous section, we saw an assortment of tules drawn from different parts of the grammar applied sequentially (not necessarily correspondent with the order of the presentation of the rules in the text). The following is the sequence of rules we used to derive the word “chinatti”.

> 1.3.1 → 3.2.123 → 1.3.78 → 3.4.78 → 3.1.78 → 1.3.9 → 8.4.55

Not we jumped back and forth between chapters 1 and 3 until we reach the eighth chapter where asiddhatva holds.Although not explicitly stated by the grammarians, there is an implicit metarule that is understood by everyone in the tradition :

> **Sarvatra Siddham Principle** : (although not explicitly stated) Any
> rule in the *sapādasaptādhyāyī* can apply whenever it can (unless
> explicitly stated as optional). It is only from 8.2.1 with the
> “**pūrvatrāsiddham**” where the rules can apply only sequentially

In fact, 8.2.1 rule of “**pūrvatra asiddham**” would make sense only if the other rules in the previous (pūrvatra) *sapādasaptādhyāyī*’s rules were taken as “**sarvatra** **siddham**”. Thus, the **pūrvatrāsiddham** applicable from 8.2.1 onwards has the effect of making the last three tripādas of the text into a linearly ordered set of rules until the grammar is terminated after the last possible rule is applied. Why are these “asiddham” rules needed? Let me give an example as to why the asiddhatva rules are needed. The ending is **bhiḥ** for most nouns except for stems of nouns ending in “**a**” like “**deva**” in which the expected sequence “**deva + bhiḥ**” is replaced by “**devaiḥ**” ⚠️⚠️. In the stem “**rājan**”, the “**n**” is elided (deleted) 🚨🚨🚨 and then only the “**bhiḥ**” is added.

> **rājan - n** 🚨🚨🚨 → **rāja** + **bhiḥ** → **rājabhiḥ**  
> **pariṣad** + **bhiḥ** → **pariṣadbhiḥ**  
> **deva** + **bhiḥ** → **devaiḥ ⚠️⚠️⚠️**

The rule 7.1.9 takes care of **⚠️⚠️⚠️** case as follows :

>  7.1.9 **ato bhisa ais** ⚠️⚠️⚠️  
> The suffix “ais” replaced “bhis” after a nominal stem ending in short
> “a”

The rule 8.2.7 takes care of the 🚨🚨🚨 case as follows :

> 8.2.7 **nalopaḥ prātipadikāntasya** 🚨🚨🚨  
> There is deletion of a final “n” at the end of a nominal stem
> (converts **rājan** → **rāja**)

Now, the 🚨🚨🚨 rule is in 8.2.7 and hence it has *asiddhatva*. Why is it needed? Because if not, then the elision of “n” in **rājan** will produce **rāja.** Then, the ⚠️⚠️⚠️ rule will act on this **rāja** to give an expression **rājaiḥ** which is not a legit word in Sanskrit. This should not happen and hence the output of the🚨🚨🚨 rule should be made invisible to the ⚠️⚠️⚠️ rule which is why asiddhatva helps. So, after applying 8.2.7, it is impossible to go back up to apply rule 7.1.9 due to asiddhatva.

### Multiple Rules Applicable

What happens when multiple rules are applicable in the same scenario at a time? This scenario of conflict is called **vipratiṣedha** in the jargon of grammar. What if application of all of them give different results based on the sequence in which they are applied? Here also, we need to constraint this generic “**sarvatra siddham**” principle. The grammatical tradition says that if there are two rules are applicable for a given form, then what rule to apply is decided upon these four following criteria in the order of their decreasing importance.

1.  **Utsarga** vs **Apavāda** Relation : **Blocking**

2.  **Antaraṅga** vs **Bahiraṅga** Relation : **Cyclicity** or
    **Word**-**Integrity**

3.  **Nitya** vs **Anitya** Relation : **Bleeding**

4.  **Para** vs **Pūrva** Relation : **Linear** **Precedence**

#### 1. The *Utsarga-Apavāda* Principle

The first thing to do if two rules can apply at the same time is very simple to understand. This principle is not stated in the Aṣṭādhyāyī explicitly as a meta-rule (*paribhāṣā*) but understood implicitly and explicitly first formulated only by Patañjali. Let us define an important concept before stating the actual principle.

> If the domain of application of rule A is a strict subset of the
> domain of application of rule B, then B is said to be “**utsarga**”
> (generic rule) relative to B which is an “**apavāda**” (specific rule
> / exception).
>
> In this case, when B is a generic rule and A is a specific rule, and
> both cannot apply, then we have that the specific rule A wins over and
> is applied. That is, a more specific rule or an exception leads to the
> blocking (**bādhana**) of the application of a more generic rule.

Note that this is easily generalised for more than two rules. A general rule might be blocked by a more specific rule and that more specific rule may be blocked by a yet more specific rule (which may license the application of the general rule, or instead license a separate and even more specific exception of its own). Also, this extends to more complex cases as well.

> If the domain of application of rule A is a strict subset of the
> combined domain of applications of rules B and C, then A is said to be
> an exception to the combined domains of B and C and takes precedence
> over B and C.

All these are illustrated below.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!tKQb!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F879dfed9-7c78-4e66-9f24-bb39492800ba_920x1252.heic)

To illustrate, let me give an example. Consider the following three sandhi rules in Sanskrit, one of which you have already seen in a previous section.

**Rule A:** Like Vowels lengthen when combined ;

Vowel + Vowel (same pair) → Long Vowel

Examples :

- brahma + astram = brahmāstram (a + a → ā)

- yogi + indraḥ = yogīndaraḥ (i + i → ī)

- guru + uttamaḥ = gurūttamaḥ (u + u → ū)

**Rule B** : a + vowel = guṇa grade (medium grade of the vowel)

Examples :

- rāja + indraḥ = rājendraḥ (a + i → e)

- maha + utsavaḥ = mahotsavaḥ (a + u → o)

- sapta + ṛṣiḥ = saptarṣiḥ (a + ṛ → ar)

**Rule C :** i / u / ṛ / ḷ + vowel = y / v / r / l + vowel (already seen previously)

Examples :

- prati + uṣā = pratyuṣā (i → y)

- su + āgatam = svāgatam (u → v)

- mātṛ + aṃśaḥ = mātrāṃśaḥ (ṛ → r)

We see that rule A conflicts with rules B and C. In the same rule A where we have the example of “**yogi + indraḥ**”, an “**i + i**” is encountered, I can also apply Rule C because it falls under the domain of “**i** + any vowel → **y** + vowel”. If we did, we would get wrong words like “**~~yogiyindraḥ~~**” which is not a legit word in Sanskrit. So, how does Pāṇini ensure that it is only Rule A that applies in all those examples and not rules B and C. It owes to blocking because the domain of the like-vowels sandhi in rule A (shown in yellow) is a subset of the combined domains of the guṇa sandhi in rule B (shown in blue) and yaṇ sandhi in rule C (shown in red). Hence, A automatically blocks the application of B and C and hence Pāṇini does not explicitly need to mention that the rule C has to be applied only in the case of rule A not applying.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!vobo!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc2c17d98-a594-42ab-a1e4-afd82b6d0ca2_1456x742.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!vobo!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc2c17d98-a594-42ab-a1e4-afd82b6d0ca2_1456x742.heic)

Note that these rules of blocking only apply to rules and in general, not to all definitions (*saṃjñā sūtras*). Because, a single object can belong to many categories and hence intersecting definitions need not block each other because they need not be mutually exclusive. For example, take the definitions of tall vs thin. If a person happens to be both tall and thin, then this is not a problem as the two pairs of definitions are not mutually exclusive. So, a person can be assumed to be both tall and thin if both criteria apply to him and there is no need for blocking. But on the other hand, there are mutually exclusive definitions where blocking has to happen. For example, in the classification of syllables as metrically short (**laghu**) and metrically long (**guru**). A single syllable cannot be both short and long metrically. In this case, there needs to be blocking. One has to be specific about what definitions are exclusive and what are not, and also what overrides what, in case of incomptatibility. This is where the metarule 1.4.1-2 comes into the picture.

> 1.4.1 **ā kaḍārādekā saṃjñā**  
> (From this rule) upto (the word) kaḍāra (which occurs in rule 2.2.38),
> only one definition applies (to any item).
>
> 1.4.2 **vipratiṣedhe paraṃ kāryam**   
> In case of incompatibly (**vipratiṣedha**), the later rule (of
> definition) should be used.

#### 2. The *Antaraṅga*-*Bahiraṅga* Principle

What if the domain of applications of rules are not strict subsets at all and hence the first criterion of general-vs-exception becomes inapplicable in deciding what rule is blocked and what rule applies? It is here that the second criterion comes in. The terms **antaraṅga** and **bahiraṅga** roughly translate to “internal unit” and “external unit” respectively. This principle again is not explicitly stated by Pāṇini himself in his sūtras but understood implicitly and elucidated by his successor **Kātyāyana**. Simply put, this states that :

> Word internal operations take precedence over word-external operations

A standard example to illustrate this is the Sanskrit sentence “**dadhy atra**” which means “*curd (is) here*”. This sentence actually splits as “**dadhi atra**” and the change i→y of the final vowel of the first word “**dadhi**” happens because of the familiar yaṇ sandhi rule. But, we have another rule (8.2.23) in Sanskrit grammar that says that word internally, if there are two or more consonants together at the end, then all except the first have to be removed.

> 8.2.23 **saṃyogāntasya lopaḥ**  
> When a word ends in a double consonant the last consonant is dropped.

Applying this to the output of yaṇ sandhi which is “**dadhy atra”,** we would erroneously get “~~dadh atra~~”. How is this 8.2.23 prevented from applying here? It is here this principle comes in. 8.2.23 applies purely to word-internal bases whereas the yaṇ sandhi applies to boundaries between two words. So, a rule that applies to word-internal bases cannot feedback on a rule that applies to word-external operations. This more generally applies as follows :

Consider a sequence \[Root-A-B\], where A and B are morphological affixes. Operations relevant to the more internal sequence \[Root-A\] should take precedence over operations that apply to \[\[Root-A\]-B\]. As an example, consider the noun stem **kartṛ** (means male doer) which accepts the feminine suffix **ī** to become **kartrī** (female doer) and then accepts **ā** suffix to form the instrumental singular (case 3) form **kartryā** (meaning : by the female doer). Before any kind of sandhi, this is just **\[\[\[kar + tṛ\] + ī\] + ā\]**. The yaṇ sandhi rule should now apply to ṛ first where we have ṛ→r because it applies to the more internal portion of the word. Only after this, we apply the ī→y rule to finally get the correct form of the word “**kartryā**”. If we had applied it in reverse, we would have gotten instead “~~kartṛyā~~” in the first step and there is no need to apply further sandhi here and the derivation concludes with an erroneous form of the word.

#### 3. The *Nitya-Anitya* Principle

If two rules A and B both can apply to a form at a given point, neither one is more generic than the other, and both are applying at the same stage of a derivation (so that the above two criteria do not apply), then what should be done? Let us define some terms.

> **A is nitya over B** : Let the application of rule A first on the
> given form change it in such a way that rule B cannot apply on it
> after. But, let the application of rule B first on the given form
> change it in such a way that rule A can still apply on it after. If
> this is the case, then A is said to be **nitya** over B. In other
> words, if A is nitya over B, then it can apply even after B is applied
> but it disables the application of B after itself.
>
> **Nitya rule takes precedence over a non-nitya rule** : The
> grammatical tradition assumes that if A is a Nitya-rule and B is a
> non-nitya rule, then the nitya-rule A always takes precedence over a
> non-nitya rule.

This is illustrated below.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!eZx2!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdc85c19c-1a22-4609-a51a-44739339dc94_1420x864.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!eZx2!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdc85c19c-1a22-4609-a51a-44739339dc94_1420x864.heic)

Let me give an example of this. Verbs in the command form in Sanskrit take three types of endings in the second person singular.

> Ending “**a**” : √**bhaj** (worship) → **bhaja** **govindam**   
> Meaning : Worship Govinda !  
>   
> Ending “**hi**” : √**dā** (give) → **bhikṣām dehi**   
> Meaning : Give alms !  
>   
> Ending “**dhi**” : √**vid** (know) → **avināśi tu tad viddhi**   
> Meaning : Know it \[the soul\] to be indestructible - Gītā 2:17

The root √**rud** (cry) is of the second type and assumes an imperative second person singular ending of the second type and the word is “**rudihi**” (meaning : Hey, cry!). When the suffix “**hi**” is added to the root “**rud**”, there comes a stage where the following two rules can apply at the same time on it.

> 6.4.101 **hujhalbhyo herdhiḥ (A)**  
> “**dhi**” replaces “**hi**” after the root “√**hu**” and roots ending
> in “**jhaL**” sound (refer the Śivasūtras for knowing what all sounds
> are covered in “**jhaL**”.
>
> 7.2.76 **rudādibhyaḥ sārvadhātuke (B)**  
> After the roots in the list beginning with the root “√**rud”,** the
> augment “**iṬ**” is inserted before **sārvadhātuka** (a type of)
> suffixes beginning with any consonant except “**y**”. (note that
> “**Ṭ”** in “**iṬ”** is just an indicatory letter and hence the actual
> augment is just “**i**”)

Here, we have two rules A and B. Rule A can apply on “rud+hi” can give “**ruddhi**” and after this, rule B can apply to give “**~~rudidhi~~**” (not a legit Sanskrit word). But when rule B applies on “rud+hi” to give “**rudihi**”, rule A cannot apply because now the “**hi**” is not directly following the verb root (an intervening “**i**” is there) and hence is not (immediately) after the root, as demanded by A. So, here B is nitya over A. So, only rule B applies and hence we have only “**rudihi**” as the final form which is indeed the correct word for commanding someone to cry.

#### 4. The *Para-Pūrva* Principle

What if all these three criteria above fail? If two rules A and B are equally specific, applicable at the same place, and no rule is nitya with respect to the other? It is now here the final tie breaker rule comes. The statement in Sanskrit is :

> 1.4.2 **vipratiṣedhe paraṃ kāryam**  
> In case of conflict, \[the rule that is\] later (param) has to be
> applied.

Traditionally, this has been understood to mean that t**he rule later in the Aṣṭādhyāyī has to be applied**. But this has led sometimes to the derivation of some erroneous words. But, recently, a researcher named **Rishi Rajpopat** (picture below) has rejected this, and ha instead argued instead that Pāṇini meant that between rules applicable to the left and right sides of a word respectively, Pāṇini wanted us **to choose the rule applicable to the right side**. Employing this interpretation, Rajpopat claims that he has found Pāṇini’s language machine produced grammatically correct words with almost no exceptions (read a news article regarding this by clicking [here](https://phys.org/news/2022-12-ancient-grammatical-puzzle-years.html)).

# Rule Interactions in Modern Linguistics

The modern study of formal language began in the mid-twentieth century with attempts to model natural language using precise mathematical systems. In 1957, Noam Chomsky’s ***Syntactic Structures*** introduced the idea that human language can be described by a formal generative grammar: a finite set of rules capable of producing an infinite number of sentences. Chomsky later distinguished different levels of grammatical power (the “Chomsky hierarchy”) and argued that human syntax requires at least context-free, and in some respects more powerful, mechanisms. In ***The Sound Pattern of English*** (Chomsky & Halle 1968), this generative approach was extended to phonology: **surface forms** (how words were actually pronounced) were derived from **abstract underlying representations** (their lexical structure) by ordered rules. This derivational view raised questions about rule interaction, ordering, and abstractness. In the late 1970s and early 1980s, scholars such as **Paul Kiparsky** and **K. P. Mohanan** developed **Lexical Phonology**, proposing that phonological rules apply cyclically within the lexicon, and are interleaved with morphological operations, and are distinct from later post-lexical processes. Strikingly, many of the architectural ideas behind this layered interaction which are :

- rule ordering b

- blocking (the Elsewhere Principle)

- domain restriction

- the interleaving of morphology and phonology

All these have clear parallels in Pāṇini’s *Aṣṭādhyāyī* (c. 4th century BCE). Pāṇini’s system anticipates and influenced key concerns of generative grammar: formal explicitness, rule interaction, derivational structure, and constrained rule application. Modern linguists, including Kiparsky, have explicitly acknowledged these structural similarities, and the dialogue between Pāṇinian grammar and generative theory remains an important chapter in the history of formal linguistics. In this section, we summarise the important points of their work on lexical phonology by simple examples.

### Trisyllabic Shortening in English

In researches on modern phonology too, the interactions between rules and constraints on rule systems has been a very important focus of study. Modern Western generative grammar pioneered by people like **Noam Chomsky**, are explicitly derivational like that of Pāṇini. Derivational means : You start with an underlying abstract form, then apply rules one after another, until you reach the final spoken form.

    Start with raw ingredients
    Apply Transformation Rule 1
    Apply Transformation Rule 2
    Apply Transformation Rule 3
    → Final dish

To illustrate the issue, consider the example of “trisyllabic” shortening in English where in a long vowel is shortened if it occurs two or more syllables from the end of the word. Consider the following sets of words :

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!KM8q!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4d54b212-ae1c-4828-b64f-614afa1e219b_396x210.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!KM8q!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4d54b212-ae1c-4828-b64f-614afa1e219b_396x210.heic)

Notice: The long vowel in the first word becomes short in the second in pronunciation (marked yellow). Chomsky proposed a theory that says:

> **Shortening Rule** : If a word becomes three syllables or more (by
> addition of suffixes), then shorten the vowel.

But, consider a word like “**nightingale**”, technically derived from the word “**night**”. In pronunciation, the long vowel in “**igh**” (pronounced “**ai**”) is not shortened in the full long form word “**nightingale**”. So why doesn’t shortening apply here? This is where things become abstract. **Chomsky** and **Halle** propose that the underlying phonetic form of the word *nightingale* is not simply something like /niːtingæːl/. Instead, they suggest something like /ni**x**tingæːl/. There is an underlying consonant /x/ (which is the pronunciation of the “gh” in the spelling of the word). But in modern English, that sound (written as “gh” in the spelling and /x/ in the phonetic form does not exist in the surface pronunciation at all in modern English and has completely disappeared.

So what happens? They propose two further rules: A rule that lengthens a vowel before /x/ and another rule that deletes /x/. So the derivation works like this:

    Start: /nixtingæːl/
    Rule 1 : Shortern long vowels after two syllabes from the end
    Rule 2 : Lengthen vowel before /x/ → /niːxtingæːl/
    Rule 3 : Delete /x/ → /niːtingæːl/

Crucially, the trisyllabic shortening rule is ordered in the beginning itself before the lengthening rule. But, since the starting form has the “x” and a short vowel “i”, the shortening rule never “sees” a long vowel in the relevant position to shorten because the lengthening comes only later. Therefore, it does not apply. This preserves the correct pronunciation. But, the phonetic symbol /x/ (manifesting as “gh” in the written spelling of English writing):

• Is never heard in modern English  
• Does not appear in pronunciation in any surface form of any word in the modern English language  
• This exists only in the underlying abstract representation  
• From the point of view of purely spoken modern English, it is a grammarian’s fiction that is inserted by Chomsky only to prevent another rule (vowel shortening) from applying. It is hence, a purely abstract device.

Although the insertion of an underlying /x/ is historically justified (Old English indeed had such sounds which is reflected in the spelling as “gh” in words like night, light, might, right, sight, bright, etc..); but seeing only the modern form of the spoken language (in the grammar of a modern speaker), it has no surface evidence at all. That is why this is controversial.

Now, why is this an issue in modern linguistics? Because once you allow invisible elements like /x/, you open the door to unlimited abstraction. In the previous exampe, you could at least justify the insertion of an unpronounced ghost phoneme /x/ based on the history of the language and the writing system that preserves the historical pronunciation of this ghost consonant by the letter “gh”. But, assume you are researching a previously unstudied knowledge from a remote tribe that never has writing and all that you have access too are the sounds of their currently spoken language only, with no way to figure out any historical pronunciations. Then, you could explain almost anything in the language by inserting hidden segments and ghost sounds that never surface. So linguists asked:

> How abstract should a grammar be allowed to become?

Should we allow elements that:

• Never appear in surface form (actual pronunciation)?  
• Exist only to control and direct the application of other rules for deriving correct forms of final words?

That is the debate. In Pāṇini’s grammar, there are also abstract elements, which are called *anubandhas* or indicatory letters which exist only to regulate the operation of grammatical rules. But here is the big difference.

Anubandhas are:

• Explicitly acknowledged to be separate meta-linguistic symbols  
• Explicitly accepted as non-phonological  
• Explicitly deleted by a meta-rule (1.3.9 tasya lopaḥ)  
• Explicitly taught by the grammarians themselves to not be a part of the spoken language

They are part of the grammatical machinery, not the phonological representation. That is very different from the English /x/ case and what Chomsky is claiming which is that the /x/ in nightingale is supposed to be a real phoneme (just hidden in actual pronunciation). But, anubandhas are never claimed to be phonemes that enjoy any status of real existence in the actual spoken language. They are technical markers. But, Chomsky does not claim that the /x/ in nightingale is just like that and instead he postulates that it is : a hidden consonant that never appears in speech but is assumed to exist ad hoc in the mental representation of just some words (having the “gh” in the spelling) in order to block another rule from applying.

### *Tugāgama* in Pāṇinian grammar

Pāṇini also allows **abstraction** like Chomsky in his grammar, but it is a controlled abstraction. Phonemes that are purely fictitious and serve only grammatical purposes are acknowledged as indicatory by him. But, every other non-indicatory intermediary phoneme that he uses has basis in the real spoken language. Let me explain this with an example.

There are situations in Sanskrit where a word-initial “**ch**” doubles to become “**cch**” when preceded by a short vowel. Following are some examples :

> **pari + chedaḥ = paricchedaḥ  
> vi + chedaḥ = vicchedaḥ  
> pra + channaḥ = pracchannaḥ**

How does Pāṇini enable this to happen? You might think that the obvious way to do this would be to directly write a rule that doubles word-initial “**ch**” doubles to become “**cch**” after a short vowel. But he does not and instead inserts an augment called “**tUK**” between the vowel and the next word (rule 6.1.73). The “UK” part is indicatory and hence the real phonetic part of this augment is “t” !! This may be surprising initially but it does do the required job because another standard sandhi rule (8.4.40) converts a “**t**” before a “**c/ch**” to “**c**” and hence we have “**t + ch → cch**”. Why is a “**t**” being inserted to lengthen “**ch”** to “**cch”** here?? This “t” is not an indicatory letter and hence cannot be adhoc and cannot be without having any phonetic basis in the Sanskrit language. Was Pāṇini inserting “t” here like Chomsky inserted /x/ in “nightingale”? Turns out, even though the “**t**” eventually changes by subsequent transformations to some other phoneme at most times whenever the calling of “**tUK**” happens ; we also have that the “**tUK**” does manifest as a “**t**” intact in one other formation. To illustrate this, for example, take the following words in Sanskrit on the right derived from the following roots on their left :

> √**ji** (conquer) → indraji**t** (one who conquers Indra)   
> √**kṛ** (do/make) → bhayakṛ**t** (one who induces fear)   
> √**bhṛ** (carry/support) → bhūbhṛ**t** (one who supports the land →
> king)   
> √**cit** (collect/arrange) → agnici**t** (one who arranges for fire)
>   
> √**su** (press) → somasu**t** (one who presses the soma)

We see that these roots ending in short vowels, while forming suffixes with other words add an extra “**t**” to them.

> 6.1.71 **hrasvasya piti kṛti tuk**  
> After ending in a short vowel, when a **kṛt** suffix (a type of
> suffix) having an indicatory “**p**” (**pit**) follows, the augment
> **tUK** is inserted.

The suffix of interest here is “**KVIP**” which has an indicatory **P** and this hence triggers an insertion of the augment “**tUK**” (the same that we saw before, which converts “**ch**” to “**tch**” and then to “**cch**” after a short vowel !!) in between and hence we have :

> √**kṛ + KVIP →** √**kṛ + tUK + KVIP**

The next sequence of rules delete all the indicatory letters and what remains is just hence

> √**kṛ + t = kṛt**

So, here we see that the “**t**” in “**tUK**” is not adhoc but indeed has a basis in the actual spoken language because it does appear as “**t**” without any change in one particular formation. Hence, Pāṇini recycles the same “**tUK**” augment when he wants to double “**ch**” to “**cch**” after a short vowel instead of creating a separate system and rule for enabling that. Also, “tuK” augment is used for effecting many other desired phonetic changes as well. So, we must be clear.

The “**t**” in “**tUK**” is:

- It is inserted by rule in many cases.

- It always produces a real phonological effect.

- It is not freely posited on an adhoc basis for very specific cases,
  because it does manifest its real form of the phoneme in some cases
  (e.g. “jit” compounds) and its positing can derive a wide range of
  words through its single transformation (can simultaneously explain
  “short vowel + ch → short vowel + cch” , “root ending in a short vowel
  → short vowel + t” and many other formations (about which we don’t
  have enough space to cover here but do look up **tugāgama** in
  Sanskrit grammar to learn more about this topic).

- It is not an underlying ghost phoneme whose original form is not seen
  anywhere in the spoken language.

- It is a realistically motivated controlled augment to effect a lot of
  desired phonetic transformation.

English /x/ in nightingale, on the other hand :

- Never surfaces and exists only in underlying representation and never
  manifests its original sound in any word in the spoken language .

- Is not reused elsewhere in the grammar and used adhoc just form some
  words (that happen to have “gh” in the written spelling but look
  arbitrary from the point of view of the spoken language)

### Alternation Condition

While the introduction of /x/ as a hidden phoneme in the word “nightingale” is somewhat reasonable by historical phonology (the preservation of “gh” in writing) , how abstract is too much? What constraints do we need to place on introducing such elements of abstraction? One such condition was proposed by **Kiparsky** in 1968 and goes as follows :

> **Alternation Condition** : Obligatory neutralisation rules cannot
> apply to all occurrence of a morpheme (Kiparsky 1968)
>
> To put it without jargon : **If some sound that is proposed is erased
> everywhere in the language (after applying any kind of transformation
> rules), then that sound probably was never there, and should not be
> given any real status.**
>
> To put it even more simply : I**f you claim something exists
> underneath, it must show up somewhere in the spoken language.**

This is basically forbidding what Chomsky did and is strongly grounded on Pāṇinian realism. It says that the morpheme proposed must manifest itself as it is, at least at once instance in the grammar and come out in the surface pronunciation intact. If /x/ in nightingale never appears anywhere in the English language as it is spoken today, then maybe it should not be in its grammatical description at all.

### Strict Cycle Condition : Lexical & Post-Lexical Rules

Then linguists noticed another problem. Some rules should only apply when something new has really been added. For example, the vowel-shortening rule applies in “divine → divinity”, but not in “nightingale”. Why? The best way to explain this is not to rely on adding ghosts phonemes in the English language (historically there but now lost) but to look at grammatical derivations itself. Note that the word “divinity” is clearly built from the word “divine” and is a derived word from “divine (word) + ity (suffix)” whereas “nightingale” is not exactly derived from “night” from the point of view of modern English. Although historically, the suffix “ingale” was a productive suffix in the English language that derived many words but now it is just a vestige and the word “nightingale” is no longer perceived to be felt to be derived from the word “night”. Hence, this rule of vowel shortening should not apply here because the word “nightingale” in modern English is a single unit and not derived with a productive morphological process. So, this condition was formulated by Kiparsky in 1982 and stated as :

> **Strict Cycle Condition** : Cyclic rules apply only to derived
> representations. A representation P is derived with respect to the
> rule R in cycle “j” if and only if P meets the structural analysis of
> R by virtue of a combination of morphemes introduced in cycle “j” or
> the application of a phonological rule in cycle “j”.
>
> To put it without jargon : **Certain phonological rules apply only to
> newly derived structures. Rules apply when morphology adds something.
> They do not apply randomly to simple, underived words.**

So shortening applies in divin-ity, but not in nightingale. This was a way to limit rule over-generation without any abstraction. What does this tell us? This laid the groundwork for what is today called as **lexical phonology** which claims that there is a fundamental distinction between phonological rules operating on the word as a whole (called **post-lexical rules**) and phonological rules operating to strings of parts of words or words which have undergone derivation and concatenation (called **lexical rules)**. The trisyllabic shortening rule that we have been seeing here is a clear instance of a lexical rule because it applies only to derived sequences of 3+ syllables which are words and hence it has a limit in scope and cannot apply indiscriminately to all three-syllabled words.

**Lexical rules**

- Apply inside word formation.

- Can have exceptions.

- Can see morphological structure.

- They are “***inside the word***” rules

Example:  
Trisyllabic shortening.

**Post-lexical rules**

- Apply after words are fully formed.

- Cannot see internal structure.

- Have no exceptions.

- Apply across word boundaries.

- They are “***after the word is built***” rules.

Example : When the negative prefix “in-” is added to English words, the “n” changes to “l” or “m” or “r” depending on the sound of the word that follows it and this is applicable to whatever word that follows, independent of its details and internal structure. The rules are :

- in + p, b, m → **im** (e.g. impossible, imbibe, immutable)

- in + l → **il** (e.g. illegal, illoyal)

- in + r → **ir** (e.g. irreversible, irrelevant)

Where did the linguists draw from and where have we seen this before? Again, it is Pāṇini!! While most rules of Sanskrit grammar are lexical rules, the external Sandhi rules of Sanskrit are a prime example of post-lexical rules that apply to ready made words without looking at their internal structure and such rules are qualified in the sūtras by the adhikāra “**saṃhitāyām**” (meaning : in connected speech). The asiddhatva restricts the interaction between the post-lexical rules and the lexical rules!

There is also a more refined three-way classification of rules too. Among the lexical rules, some rules may get applied in the intermediate stages of word formation whereas another rule may just apply once after all the morphemes are arranged as required. The former are called “**cyclic** **rules**” that keep on repeatedly get applied (as more suffixes keep getting added) and the latter are called “**post-cyclic rules**” that apply once just before the word formation process. Stress in spoken English and pitch accents in Sanskrit are examples of cyclic rules because as suffixes keep getting added, the stress or pitch accent keeps getting shifted after re-evaluation. An important aspect that Chomsky and Halle demonstrate in their 1968 paper is that morphological rules and phonological rules are intertwined with each other. A suffix may cause certain sounds to change and the sound changes can in turn determine what suffixes the result can or cannot take. For example, in English grammar, among Latinate verbs. only those that are stressed on their final syllable are eligible to take the suffix -al. For example, we have revérse → revérsal , arríve → arríval , propóse → propósal, and withdráw → withdráwal ; because the stress is on the last syllable. But verbs like édit and depósit which do not have the stress on their last syllable are not eligible to take the -al suffix and hence we do not have words like ~~edital~~ and ~~deposital~~. So here, the phonology influences the morphology. In turn, the morphology can influence the phonology like our stress rule that shortens vowels like seréne -\> serénity. In Sanskrit grammar too, this is very well-known phenomenon. For example, taking the causative suffix “**nic**” in Sanskrit grammar triggers the vowel change inside the root from “**chid**” to “**ched**” and ultimately derives “**chedayati**”. This is an example of morphology influencing phonology. Take the noun stems “**jāta**” (male born) and “**yogin**” (male ascetic). Since the former ends in “**ta**” and the latter ends in “**in**”, they form their feminines using different suffixes to give “**jātā**” (female born) and “**yoginī**” (female ascetic). This is an example of phonology influencing morphology.

Thus, lexical phonology assumes that there are fundamentally two types of phonological rule, lexical and post-lexical, with the former being lexically specific, cyclic in application, and restricted in domain to the word/stem level, and the latter having the converse properties. Phonological and morphological rules are interleaved in the lexicon, but the fundamental divide is between the lexical and postlexical levels. In all domains, phonological rules must still be ordered with respect to each other, and/or their interaction must be subject to at least one meta-principle, the Elsewhere Principle (exceptions blocking generic rules).

John J Lowe in his book writes :

> *The similarities of this model to the Pāṇinian system are noteworthy.
> Morphological and phonological processes are interleaved in both
> systems. There is no explicit distinction between lexical and
> post-lexical levels for Pāṇini, nor between strictly morphological and
> strictly phonological processes, but there is a clear distinction
> between rules which have scope only over individual words or sub-word
> units, and those which apply to complete words **saṃhitāyām** ‘in
> connected speech’, that is in a given syntactic context. Moreover,
> there is a final set of phonological rules in the grammar which cannot
> feed earlier rules, that is,the rules of the Tripādī, and thus
> function like the final group of a set of ordered postlexical rules.
> Furthermore, following the interpretation of Joshi and
> Kiparsky(1979), the fundamental principle of rule interaction in the
> Aṣṭādhyāyī is the siddha principle, that any rule which can apply
> should apply at any point, and the same principle is true of Lexical
> Phonology. The fundamental constraint on the siddha principle in the
> Aṣṭādhyāyī, the Elsewhere Principle (blocking), is likewise the
