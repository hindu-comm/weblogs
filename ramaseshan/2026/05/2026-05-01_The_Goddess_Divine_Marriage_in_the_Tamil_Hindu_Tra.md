In the Tamil region, the marriage between a goddess and a god serves as the foundational creation story of many major temples and forms the basis for the cults associated with them. Although both the god and the goddess cohabitate in such temples as equal partners, it is the goddess usually that locates the god physically in the temple space and also gives the place name and identity where the temple is located. For instance, we talk of the **Mīṉāṭci** (Mīnākṣī) temple located at **Maturai** even though her consort **Sundarēśvarar** is also in the same temple. The same holds in many other instances like **Kamāṭci** (Kāmākṣī) temple at **Kāñcipuram** , **Nīlāyatāṭci** (Nīlāyatākṣī) temple at **Nākapaṭṭiṉam** (Nāgapaṭṭana), the **Akilāṇḍēśvari** (Akhilāṇḍeśvarī) temple at **Tiruvāṉaikkā**, the **Apītakucā** temple at **Tiruvaṇṇāmalai** and so on, even though all the goddesses in these temples reside with their consort. In the myths, it is the goddess who is shown as always having been rooted in the temple with the god later being drawn to her at some time and becomes rooted subsequently with her by marriage.

In the Sanskritic myth, as is well known, we have the story of **Pārvatī**, daughter of the mountain king **Himavat**, performs intense **tapas** (austerities) to win **Śiva** as her husband, who is absorbed in deep meditation. To aid Pārvatī, the gods send **Kāma**, the god of desire, to distract Śiva by shooting his arrow of love. Enraged by the disturbance, Śiva incinerates Kāma with his third eye but later revives him. Pārvatī’s steadfast devotion ultimately wins Śiva’s heart, leading to their marriage. Their union, celebrated in texts like the ***Śiva Purāṇa*** and ***Kumārasambhava*** by **Kālidāsa**, leads to the birth of their son **Kārttikeya**, who fulfills the gods’ purpose by slaying the demon **Tāraka**.

# The virgin awaiting marriage : Kaṉṉiyākumari

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!xWcD!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff6666c9c-c759-4f09-bf63-0e8f1ad3a3c2_715x1050.jpeg)

![undefined](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!xWcD!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff6666c9c-c759-4f09-bf63-0e8f1ad3a3c2_715x1050.jpeg "undefined")

The exact storyline associated with the goddess who gives the name to the southernmost tip of India - **Kaṉṉiyākumari** (Sanskrit : Kanyākumarī) - varies but let me first present to you the one from the text **Kanyākṣetramāhātmya**. **Bāṇāsura**, the ruler of the land that today is called **Kaṉṉiyākumari**, performed tapas for **Brahmā** who granted his desired his wish that he could not be killed except by a woman. He then conquered the worlds and wreaked havoc on the world. When he heard that the sage **Mārkaṇḍeya** was still sacrificing to the gods, he arrogantly demanded a share for himself which made him curse the asura that he would be killed by a woman. Alarmed, **Bāṇāsura** sought the counsel of the asura guru **Śukra** who advised him to perform additional tapas and get the boon modified from Brahmā that he would only be killed by a virgin woman. The reason he gives is that virgins are rare on this earth. Then, **Śiva** sends his consort **Pārvatī** to incarnate on earth to kill Bāṇāsura which she does on the southernmost tip of the Indian Ocean. It is said that she awaits the pralaya (cosmic destruction) during which Śiva will appear and make her his bride.

In another version of the story present in the Tamil text **Cucīntiram**, we have **Imayamātu** (Tamil reference to Pārvatī) who takes the form of a virgin named **Puṭpakācai** (**Puṣpahāsā**) and worships Śiva**.** When Śiva does indeed appear to her and asks her what she desires, she replies “*I want to be with you without cease*”. Śiva promises that he would marry her after thousand cycles of the four *yuga*s after taking the name of **Rudra** and asks her to keep performing tapas until then by the shore of the sea and that he would also await her as a brahmacārin in the **Pūraṇacapai** (**Cucīntiram**). The goddess then bowed to him and went to the spot hence giving name to the place as **Kaṉṉikākettiram** (Kanyākṣetra).

In yet another version of the myth, Śiva promises to marry the goddess immediately but the gods conspire to prevent the wedding. Here, we have the lord of **Cucīntiram** (a form of Śiva) wishing to marry the goddess **Kaṉṉiyākumari** but the gods are alarmed because they want her to remain virgin to retain her power to fight the demon **Bāṇāsura** who, as we saw before, could only be killed by a virgin. The asura had been giving tough time to the devas and hence the order of the world is being upset and hence this is a serious situation. In order to thwart the wedding, the sage **Nārada** requests Śiva to bring as presents on the wedding day - coconuts without eyes, betel leaves without veins, and the like before midnight (which was the auspicious moment at which the marriage was fixed). Śiva manages to bring these gifts which causes Nārada to adopt an alternate plan where he assumes the form of a cock and coos to announce the dawn prematurely making everyone believe that the midnight had passed and turn back in disappointment. Kaṉṉiyākumari waits in bridal clothes till dawn and when no one had come, she cursed the items assembled for the weeding feast turn to sea shells and sand. With unbearable insult, pain, grief, and anger, she then destroys everything she sees. She breaks her bangles. The food particles that she scattered around are said to be the source of the colourful sands of Kaṉṉiyākumari. When she finally gained her composure, she undertook continuous penance. Ages later, Bāṇāsura tried to lure and approach Kumari, without realising who she was. The infuriated Kumari slaughtered him at once. Only moments before his death, the asura realised that the one before him was the Goddess herself, after which he prayed to her to absolve him of his sins.

According to Vaiṣṇavite tradition though, the davita philosopher **Vādirāja Tīrtha** states in his ***Tīrtha Prabandha*** that Kaṉṉiyākumari is a form of the goddess **Lakṣmī** herself, who descended upon the earth to kill the demon Bāṇāsura, who here is described as a devotee of Śiva!

So, here we see a reversal of the classical Sanskritic story. There, the gods induce Śiva to marry Pārvatī so that they can create a son to defeat a demon. Here, Śiva is willing to marry while the gods want Pārvatī to stay a virgin so that she can destroy a demon.

# The chastity of Anasūyā : The Tāṇumālayaṉ temple at Cucīntiram

This temple at **Cucīntiram** is just located near the Kaṉṉiyākumari in the same district which we saw in the last section itself, is the home of the waiting bridegroom Śiva. But in this temple, he also cohabitates with the two other main gods Brahmā and Viṣṇu. The myths associated with this temple are interesting and unique.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SySl!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1f71e3ba-f299-4731-ac66-c2c6337eaa0e_2560x1920.jpeg)

![undefined](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SySl!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1f71e3ba-f299-4731-ac66-c2c6337eaa0e_2560x1920.jpeg "undefined")

The major myth of this temple too centres on a virgin who is the Vedic figure **Anasūyā**, the wife of sage **Atri**. Several Sanskrit purāṇas tell the story of how she gave birth to the three gods Trimūrti (Brahmā, Viṣṇu, Śiva) who are the presiding deities of the Tānumālayan temple in Cucīntiram. The very name **Tāṇumālayaṉ** is made up of the Tamil names of the three major Hindu gods - **Tāṇu** \[Sanskrit : Sthāṇu\] (Śiva), **Māl** (Viṣṇu), and **Ayaṉ** (Brahmā).

First, let us examine two myths attributed to Anasūyā in the Sanskrit purāṇas. One myth found in *Mārkaṇḍeyapurāṇa* & *Skandapurāṇa* goes as follows :

A brahmin was being carried by his chaste wife to the house of a prostitute when he brushed against Māṇḍavya, an innocent woman impaled on a stake. **Māṇḍavya** curses the brahmin to die at sunrise which prompts his wife to prevent the sun from rising by utilising the power of her chastity. The gods get distressed and appeal to Anasūyā who persuades the lady to allow the sun to rise after promising her that she would revive her husband. As a reward, the gods grant a boon to Anasūyā that the three deities Brahmā, Viṣṇu, Śiva would be born to her as sons. Atri, just by mental contact with his wife Anasūyā (*manasā bheje*) causes a wind to impregnate her womb with **Soma** (moon form of Brahmā), with Viṣṇu then putting his incarnate **Dattātreya**, and with Śiva incarnating into her womb as **Durvāsas**.

The *Bhaviṣyapurāṇa* offers a much more dramatic story. One day, the three gods (Brahmā, Viṣṇu, Śiva) came to visit the sage Atri to offer him a boon. Atri was immersed in tapas and hence the gods go to his wife Anasūyā where the gods are seduced by her and want to rape her. Anasūyā curses them to become her sons and also curses them to be worshipped in various humiliating form - Śiva to be worshipped in the form of a liṅga, Brahmā to be worshipped in the form of a head, and Viṣṇu to be worshipped in the form of feet. The gods are struck with terror, bow to her, who then promises them contentment when they would be born as her children.

Now, let us return to the Tamil myths associated with the Tāṇumālayan temple where we see another variant of this myth, attested in the Talapurāṇam of Cucīntiram. Here we see Atri leaving Cucīntiram (also referred in Tamil as Ñaṉaraṇiyam) for the Brahmāloka. The gods Brahmā, Viṣṇu, Śiva want to test the chastity of Anasūyā who take the form of three old men and visiting her house begging for food. They then demand that she serve the food to her naked, refusing to eat the food otherwise. Perceiving this as a test, she said thought of her husband and through the power of her chastity, converts the three old men to infants before becoming naked and then breastfeeding them and singing lullabies to them. Meanwhile, the three wives of these gods become maidens and come to the āśrama of Anasūyā to request her to restore their husbands. Anasūyā refuses to do so until her husband returns and hence the goddess go off to do tapas at Ñānatīrrtam to make Atri return which he does. Anasūyā restores the gods who then say “*There are no householders who can equal you two, and there is no loosening of your love. Choose a boon*”. Atri is already contended but Anasūyā asks that the love for her husband should always increase and that the three gods should incarnate in her womb as her children which they do. It is also described in this same myth that ***after being restored to their true forms***, ***the three gods marry their spouses again who have come as maidens, whose site marks the Tāṇumālayaṉ temple***. The representation of the three central gods of Hinduism together in one liṅga makes it a unique temple in India.

Another Tamil Talapurāṇam (Tirimūrttimalai) gives yet another slightly different version of this myth. In this version, the three gods come down as naked hungry beggars and ask Anasūya for food who refuses to attend to them until her husband returns from bathing as they are naked. Upon insistence, she uses the power of her chastity to convert them into infants and feeds them with breastmilk. When Atri comes home and finds out about the true identity of the infants, he begs to the gods for forgiveness and bathes their feet with the water of the **Toṇi** river and with his tears. In yet another oral version of this myth, the sage Atri becomes completely irrelevant and Anasūyā is described as a virgin and she converted the three gods to calves (instead of three infants) before they demand her to get naked.

# The Goddess who took the form of a Peahen : Tirumayilai

In Chennai, **Mylapore** (anglicised form of the Tamil name **Tirumayilai**) is an important temple area. One such important temple is the **Kapālīśvarar** temple, named after the form of Śiva who resides with is consort **Kaṟpakāmpāḻ**. The temple’s name is derived from the words kapāla (head) and īśvara, an epithet of Śiva. According to the purāṇas, during the meeting of the deities Brahmā and Śiva at top of Mount Kailāśa, Brahmā failed to show the due respect to Śiva. Due to this, Śiva plucked off one of his five heads (kapāla). In an act of penance, Brahmā is said to have came down to the site of Mylapore and installed a liṅga to please Śiva.

The regional marriage myth associated with this temple describes to us that once when Śiva was expounding to Pārvatī, she got distracted by a peahen and here lost attention. Śiva is then said to have cursed her to be born as a peahen with beautiful eyes in its tail and said that the curse would end if she would worship and await him in the place that would eventually become the Kapālīśvarar temple. The goddess then went there and made all the sacred waters of the earth enter the **tīrtha** of the temple where she bathed and worshipped her, following which Śiva appeared to her on the bull and gave her back her divine form. The tīrtha can be still be seen inside the temple and the bull and peahen are recurring motifs in this temple.

# The Goddess with Eyes of Desire : Kāmākṣī of Kāñci

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!72xG!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa3263bce-e804-43d5-9dd1-3795ada98cf6_288x383.jpeg)

According to the myth associated with this temple at **Kāñci**, Pārvatī, in one of her playful moods, closed the eyes of Lord Śiva.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!6aE3!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd8859b2f-f894-4d7e-aabc-1e8d6ec794dd_1582x320.jpeg)

![Banner](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!6aE3!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd8859b2f-f894-4d7e-aabc-1e8d6ec794dd_1582x320.jpeg "Banner")

Darkness then enveloped the whole universe. Śiva got angry with her and cursed an otherwise golden complexion of hers to turn dark, proceed to the Earth and do penance till he would come and marry her again. Meanwhile, a sage by name **Kātyāyana** was doing tapas in the Himālayas. Pārvatī came to the sage as a small child who named her **Kātyāyanī** and brought her up. At the age of eight, she realised the secret of her birth and proceeded to the ***Satyavratakṣetra***, the present Kāñci. The girl dressed as a tapasvinī, carried along with her a Yoga Daṇḍa, Akṣamālā, Kamaṇḍala, Gaṅgā water and sand scooped from the holy river and other items for her penance. She stopped over at Kāśī, which was going through a period of famine. She assumed the form of Annapuraṇī and was doing Annadāna for twelve years. Later she resumed her journey towards the south and reached Kāñci. Here as she began her penance under a mango tree, all the things she carried changed into different divine forms. She prepared a liṅga out of the Gaṅgā sand and started doing tapas on **Pañcāgni** (fire surrounding her and the Sun above), standing with one foot on a needle. It continued for years. In order to again test the intensity of the tapas, Śiva sent Gaṅgā as river Vegavatī, which flooded the area. The goddesss held on to the liṅga close to her chest to protect it that was made out of earth. Even today, it is said that the liṅga carries the imprints of the breasts of Pārvatī and the bracelets she wore in her hands.

Pleased with her penance, he finally gave her darśana and promised to marry her on the full moon day of the month of **Paṅguni** (March-April) month. Brahmā, Viṣṇu and the other devas arrived for this divine wedding of Lord Ekāmbareśvara (Śiva) and Kāmākṣī. The place where Kāmākṣī did the initial fire penance is known as **Māṅkāṭu** (mango grove), a small temple town 23 km from Chennai (near my house actually!), where Kāmākṣī worshipped Śiva.

Since the liṅga was made of sand, Kāñci is held sacred as **Pṛthivī** (earth). The Pṛthivī Liṅga is worshipped as Lord Ekāmbareśvara. The legendary mango tree is worshipped on the temple even today. The four branches of the tree are said to represent the four Vedas. Kāmākṣī is enshrined in the Kāmakoṭi Pīṭa, which is the present temple.

**Ādi Śaṅkara** is credited for consecrating the current **Śrī Cakra Yantra** in front of this ambāḻ sculpture. The eight **Vāgdevī** deities who composed the Lalitā Sahasranāma can be seen around the yantra.

Śiva and Devī quarrel after a game of dice wherein he curses her to be black, have three mis-shapen eyes (*virūpanayanā bhava*), and a terrifying form. Then, the Devī proceeds to worship her brother **Vāmana** (an avatāra of Viṣṇu) at Kāñci who made her eyes beautiful, thus befitting the title Kāmākṣī. He also advised her to build a liṅga and worship it in order to win back the hand of Śiva. While Śiva did indeed descend to see her, he tries to burn her with the fierce heat of the sun wherein Vāmana comes to help by creating a mango tree to give shade to her. As the burning continues, Vāmana cools Devī with rays of nectar (sudhā) by assuming his moon form (pūrṇacandrākṛti) and creates a lake of nectar wherein the burnt mango tree is also regenerated. After this, the well known episode of the trail by flood happens as mentioned just now. Eventually, Śiva does marry the goddess after the trials.
