So far, we have seen the most frequent forms of masculine and neuter nouns and have learnt present and past tense of verbs. We also saw how Sanskrit creates long sentences using relative pronouns, connectors, and the gerund suffix. This time, we will see the forms of feminine nouns in Sanskrit and also read some interesting poems and stories.

# What does Sītā do?

So far we have seen the forms of masculine and neuter nouns ending in अः and अम् respectively. In this chapter, we will explore the forms of feminine nouns ending in आ, ई, इः, and ईः like सीता, जननी, भूमिः, and लक्ष्मीः. Rama marries Sita

In the above sentence, one sees that the object form of सीता is सीताम् which is obtained by adding म् as in the case of रामम्. So, this should not be easy to remember because this is exactly what happened for the masculine case as well.

> रामः (sub) → रामम् (obj)  
> सीता (sub) → सीताम् (obj)

Let us rephrase it now with जानकी, another name for Sītā.

> स्वयंवरे जानकी रामम् अपश्यत् । ततः रामः जानकी**म्** पर्यनयत् ।

So, we see that even the ई ending feminine nouns behave similarly under the object form in the singular. Let us now do it for लक्ष्मीः that ends in ईः which too behaves similarly.

> लक्ष्मीः नारायणम् पश्यति , ततः नारायणः लक्ष्मीम् प्रतिपश्यति ।

Now, let us proceed to learn another ending that is also just a little different from the masculine. Read this sentence.

This is also not that different from the masculine when we observe together as follows.

> सीता (sub) → सीतायै (for/to) ; the ending is आयै  
> रामः (sub) → रामाय (for/to) ; the ending is आय

प्रतिज्ञा = promise, बद्धं करोति = binds

The word कृपा (meaning: grace / ability / tolerance), has a “by” form which is कृपया (literal translation: by grace / ability / tolerance). This word is routinely used in Hindi for “please” which actually means “by \[your\] ability / grace / tolerance”. With these two examples in mind, see the following derivations.

> कृपा (grace/ability) → कृपया (by grace/ability)

How to say “from/than” for feminine nouns? Here, we see that even to say “*from Laṅkā*”, the form is still लङ्कायाः ! Yes, in the feminine singular case, the forms for “from” and “of” coincide. In masculine, we had रामस्य. How about the feminine? Turns out that you have already seen the “from” form of सीता. It is सीतायाः! In all the nouns that we are going to see hereafter, the “of” or the possessive form will always coincide with the “from/than” form! So, the word सीतायाः can either be translated as “of Sītā” or “Sītā’s” or “from Sītā” or “than Sītā” depending on the context.

For example, the phrase **नद्याः समुद्रपर्यन्तम्** means “***from the river to the sea***”. How about the plurals? We saw that in the masculine case, the “from” form and the “to” form coincide for रामः which is रामेभ्यः. The same rule and a similar ending hold true for feminine nouns and mostly all nouns in general. सीता, जननी, and मतिः are सीताभ्यः, जननीभ्यः, and मतिभ्यः respectively. The plural of the “of” forms are सीतानाम्, जननीनाम्, and मतीनाम् respectively, once again reminiscent of रामाणाम्.

> रामस्य (of/’s) and रामात् (from/due to) → सीतायाः (of/’s/from/due to)

Now, let us go to the plural forms. What are the plurals of the subject and object words like सीता and सीताम्? Learn them.

Turns out that for nouns that end in आ, the plural is same for both the subject and object form and it resembles the masculine subject plural forms (e.g. compare रामाः and सीताः). But it is not so for the other class of feminine nouns.

When the final vowel is short इ, there is an extra अ before that यः to give अयः whereas it is absent for the words ending in long ई.

Notice the absence of न् in the thirds person plural unlike the other cases न्ति / न्ते.

Take the following verse from the Bhagavad Gītā 14.19.

न अन्यं गुणेभ्यः कर्तारं यदा द्रष्टा अनुपश्यति, गुणेभ्यः च परं **वेत्ति** मद्भावं, सः अधिगच्छति ॥

Let me simplify this verse by making the word order convenient for English translation and expanding hidden subjects, objects, and connectors.

यदा द्रष्टा अनुपश्यति कर्तारं न अन्यं गुणेभ्यः \[इति\], \[यदा द्रष्टा\] वेत्ति मद्भावं परं गुणेभ्यः च , \[तदा\] सः \[द्रष्टा \] \[मद्भावं\] अधिगच्छति ॥

Let us translate each phrase separately first.

यदा द्रष्टा अनुपश्यति कर्तारं न अन्यं गुणेभ्यः

*When the seer sees the doer (obj) not another from the guṇas*

च \[यदा द्रष्टा\] **वेत्ति** मद्भावं परं गुणेभ्यः

*and \[when the seer\] **knows** my-nature distinct from the guṇas*

\[तदा\] सः \[द्रष्टा \] अधिगच्छति \[मद्भावं\]

*\[then\] he \[the seer\] attains \[my-nature\].*

Something still seems to be missing from this translation here even after filling in for the incomplete connectors, subjects, and objects.

*When the seer sees the doer not another from guṇas and \[when the seer\] knows my-nature distinct from the guṇas, \[then\] he \[the seer\] attains \[my-nature\].*

Turns out we need to add an “as” between the two object predicates of the verbs and that Sanskrit omits the word for “as” usually.

*When the seer sees the doer **as** not another from the guṇas and \[when the seer\] knows my-nature **as** distinct from the guṇas, \[then\] he \[the seer\] attains \[my-nature\].*

A more polished translation would look like

*When the seer sees the doer **as** constituted of nothing other than the guṇas and when the seer knows my-nature **as** distinct from the guṇas, then he attains my-nature.*

Let us now attempt to translate another slightly abridged verse from Bhagavad Gītā 2.40 that utilises another form of the verb.

न इह (here) अभिक्रम-नाशः अस्ति, प्रत्यवायः न **विद्यते**, स्वल्पम् अपि तस्य धर्मस्य त्रायते (rescues) महाभयात्।

Rearranging it in an English friendly word order gives

अस्ति न अभिक्रम-नाशः इह अभिक्रम-नाशः, प्रत्यवायः न विद्यते, अपि स्वल्पम् तस्य धर्मस्य त्रायते महाभयात्।

A literal translation gives:

*There is no endeavour-loss here, reversal is not there/found, even a little of this dharma rescues from great-danger.*

A more polished translation gives:

*There is neither loss of endeavour here, nor is there reversal. Even a little of this dharma rescues from great danger.*

Note that nested न...न...न… phrase structure in Sanskrit is best translated as “neither..nor…nor…” if it is appropriate as Sanskrit does not have distinct words for neither and nor.

Now let us practise another form of the verb through translating Gītā 7.29.

जरा-मरण-मोक्षाय माम् (me) आश्रित्य यतन्ति ये, ते ब्रह्म तत् **विदुः** कृत्स्नम् अध्यात्मं कर्म च अखिलम् ।

Rearrangement in an English friendly word order gives

ये यतन्ति जरा-मरण-मोक्षाय आश्रित्य माम्, ते **विदुः** तत् ब्रह्म, कृत्स्नम् अध्यात्मं, च अखिलम् कर्म ।

A phrase by phrase translation would be:

ये यतन्ति Those who all strive

जरा-मरण-मोक्षाय for liberation from old age and death

आश्रित्य माम् after taking refuge in me

ते **विदुः** they all **know**

ब्रह्म, कृत्स्नम् अध्यात्मं, च अखिलम् कर्म the brahman, the entire ātman, and all karmas.

Final translation: *Those who all strive for liberation from old age and death after taking refuge in me, they all know the brahman, the entire ātman, and all karmas*.

Let us now translate another verse Gītā 4.38 that uses two forms of this root simultaneously.

न हि ज्ञानेन सदृशं (in comparison) पवित्रम् इह **विद्यते**, तत् स्वयम् योग-संसिद्धः कालेन आत्मनि (in the ātman) **विन्दति** ।

विद्यते न हि पवित्रम् सदृशं ज्ञानेन इह

There is nothing indeed holy in comparison with knowledge here

योग-संसिद्धः विन्दति तत् स्वयम् आत्मनि कालेन

One who is accomplished in yoga gets that by himself in himself with time.

# The Abstract Action-Object Feminine Suffix

Consider the word “creation”. It can either refer to the act of creation or an object of creation. For example, consider the following sentences :

> God is involved in the process of **creation** → abstract noun  
> I am a **creation** of God → object

In Latin, the suffix “**-tion**” is the suffix that conveys this dual meanings - the noun of the process itself and the object of that process. In Sanskrit, the corresponding cognate suffix “ति” that can mean both the action and the process. This suffix sometimes becomes a “टि” or a “धि” due to the influence of previous sounds and sandhi. For example, consider this :

> दृश् (root) + तिः (suffix) = दृष्टिः (subject singular)

This can mean the process of seeing as well as the object of seeing. For example,

> मम दृष्टिः सम्यक् नास्ति   
> Translation 1 : My object of sight (the thing that I see) is not good
>   
> Translation 2 : My seeing is not good (the action)
>
> The English sentence that we saw in the beginning of this section in
> Sanskrit :  
> नारायणस्य सृष्टिः अद्भुदा (process)  
> अहं नारायणस्य सृष्टिः अस्मि (object)

All these short “ति” ending feminine abstract noun - object nouns are feminine in Sanskrit and are declined the same pattern as भूमि. Some famous and well known examples of this suffix ending words are given below. Only the abstract noun meanings are given.
