# Script & Phonology

Tamil language has 12 (+2) vowels, 18 consonants and a special letter called as *āyutam*. They are listed below. In the Tamil grammatical tradition, the vowels are compared to the soul (**uyir**) and the consonants are compared to the body (**mey**). The reason is because the soul can exist without the body (vowels can be pronounced alone) whereas the body cannot exist without the soul (consonants always need a vowel to be sounded).

### Extra short vowels : Kuṟṟiyalukaram & Kuṟṟiyalikaram

Apart from these paired vowels and two exclusively long vowels, there also occur a couple of extra short vowels in Tamil, that are held half the duration of the short vowels. They do not have a separate letter and are denoted by the same short இ (i) and உ (u) only. These occur only at the end of words.

In Tamil, no word can end in a hard consonant. When a word is forced to end in a hard consonant, then an overshort “u” vowel comes in and fills in at the end.

Note that this phenomenon continues even in modern Tamil where this overshort ‘u’ is added to the end of an English loanword that ends in a hard consonant and is borrowed into Tamil. For example, the English word “road” is rendered in Tamil as ரோடு. Hindi names like Arvind are rendered as அரவிந்து, again with an overshort ‘u’ at the end.

In Telugu, which is another Dravidian language related to Tamil, this overshort ‘u’ is not really that short and is normal and is heard so prominently that even a non-native barely exposed to Telugu can feel that many words end in the vowel ‘u’ !

This overshort ‘u’ is called by Tamil grammarians as குற்றியலுகரம்.

However, when the next word begins with a vowel, this extra overshort “u” vanishes. For example, the word “சிலம்பு” when it accepts the word “அரசன்” that starts with a vowel, in a compound word, the final overshort உ (U) in final பு vanishes and hence we have சிலம்பு + அரசன் = சிலம்பரசன். The overshort “u” becomes an overshort “i”, if the following word starts with ய். Eg. கோட்டு + யானை = கோட்டியானை. This overshort இ no longer survives in modern speech and is purely an archaic literary phenomenon.

There are some words that end with a genuine short “u” and not the overshort உ. But since Tamil writing doesn’t distinguish between this genuine short “u” and overshort “u”, be prepared to get surprised when you find that these genuinely short உ (U) simply don’t get omitted without having other side-effects. These classes of words (where the normal final “u'“ leaves before vowels but also creates side-effects) consists mainly of two syllable adjectives having both short vowels and single consonants. If that is the case and these adjectives are followed by a suffix or another word, two things can happen when the final short உ leaves.

- The first vowel is lengthened after the genuinely short U is omitted
    
  Eg.   
  சிறு + இலை = சீறிலை   
  அரு + இரவு = ஆரிரவு   
  ஒரு + ஊர் = ஓரூர்

- The second consonant is doubled   
  Eg. சிறு + இலை = சிற்றிலை.

However these lengthenings of vowels and consonants have ceased in modern speech except while imitating classical styles. However, the overshort U is retained always when it is followed by a consonant so that a consonant-consonant intercourse is prevented.

# Sandhi Rules : Puṇarcci

When two words meet, sound changes occur at the boundary to facilitate easier pronunciation. This is called sandhi in linguistics (the term is borrowed from Sanskrit). English has sandhi too in writing ; for instance :

> a + apple → an apple

You were not told that the a → an change is a sandhi change. Sandhi in Classical Tamil is complex because of the so many options that it allows which have led to different sets of rules standardised by different sets of authors over different times. Although modern Tamil has discontinued most of the sandhi rules of Classical Tamil in writing, it is compulsory in Classical Tamil to do sandhi between words whenever possible (like in Classical Sanskrit).

### Vowel Sandhi

No language likes two vowels clashing at word boundaries (e.g. English : a + apple → a an apple). Like in English, when the first word ends with a vowel and the next word begins with a vowel, Tamil too inserts a consonant between the two vowels to ease pronunciation (except that it is not “n”). Those consonants are either வ் or ய். What exactly is taken depends on the nature of the vowel that precedes. Final அ, ஆ, உ, ஊ, ஒ, ஓ take the consonant வ் and final இ, ஈ, எ, ஏ, ஐ take the consonant ய்.

> Examples :  
> கோ (*king*) + இல் (*place*) = கோவில் (literally : *king’s place* =
> *palace*) =\> *temple*  
> சேர்ப்ப + ஓம்புமதி = சேர்ப்பவோம்புமதி *Man from the coast, beware!  *
> கொன்றை + இணர் = கொன்றையிணர் *cluster of laburnum*

As said in the previous section, when the final vowel is an overshort u, or an overshort i, it gets omitted when the following word starts with a vowel.

> Examples :  
> வண்டு + இனம் = வண்டினம் *swarm of bees  *
> சிலம்பு + அரசன் = சிலம்பரசன் *king of stick dancing*

Now, how about when the final vowel is normal “u”? The first sandhi rule only applies (u → uv) as this is not an overshort “u”. A famous example would be the Tamil equivalent of the Sanskrit word Śrī which is திரு.

> Examples :  
> திரு + அடி = திருவடி *Śrīpāda*  
> திரு + அறை = திருவறை *room of Śrī*

Upto here, the above rule are carried forward and adopted in modern formal Tamil as well. But, the rules that are described in this section from here on are no longer productive in the modern language.

As also said in the previous section, overshort u becomes an overshort i, when followed by ய்.

> Examples :  
> கோட்டு + யானை = கோட்டியானை *tusked elephant*

If an adjective consists of just two short syllables, and the final vowel is a normal short உ, and if the adjective is followed by a noun that begins with a vowel, then two options are available.

- The second உ is dropped and the first vowel in the adjective lengthens
  and this is what happens most of the time

> Examples :   
> சிறு + இலை = சீறிலை *small leaf*  
> அரு + இரவு = ஆரிரவு *difficult night*  
> ஒரு + ஊர் = ஓரூர் *one village  *
> பெரு + அவை = பேரவை *big assembly*

This rule is also applicable when the first word is long but ends in the suffix ஒடு *with*.

> Example :   
> பெடையொடு + சேவல் = பெடையொடு சேவல் (no sandhi) *rooster with hen*  
> பிணையொடு + இரலை = பிணையோடிரலை (sandhi) *stag with doe*

- Sometimes, the second உ is dropped and the second consonant is
  doubled.

> Example :   
> சிறு + இலை = சிற்றிலை small leaf

Note that these rules are no longer productive in modern Tamil although sometimes done for said phrases to give an archaic feel.

One syllabled adjectives (which are very small) have their own peculiarities as well.

> Examples :   
> செம் + அடி = சேவடி *red foot*  
> செம் + இழை = சேயிழை *red leaf*  
> வெம் + வரை = வெவ்வரை *hot mountain*  
> அ + இடை = ஆயிடை *in between*  
> அது + ஏ = ஆதே *that only / that indeed*

An exceptional vowel elision of a final short vowel occurs when it is followed by a verb of quotation in reported speech.

> Example :   
> வருக / வருகி + என்றாள் = வருகென்றாள் *she said “I will/You may come”*

Sometimes when two particles merge, we have the exceptional transformation அ + அ → அ

> Example :   
> மறந்த + அனையேன் = மறந்தனையேன் I am like someone who as forgotten  
> மன்ற + அம்ம = மன்றம்ம

### Consonant Sandhi

No Tamil word can end in a hard consonant க் ச் ப் ட் த் ற். If they do, an overshort u is addded (which sometimes becomes an overshort i) to ease the transition when the word is followed by another word that begins in a consonant. This overshort u/i vanishes when the following word starts with a vowel. So, with that possibility removed, only nasals and liquids (semi-vowels) can end a Tamil word and hence let us see what happens in sandhi for those cases. All consonant sandhi rules in this section are no longer productive in modern Tamil atleast between words except for archaic words that survive in usage and contain these sandhis within the word.

#### Final ம்

The nasal ம் followed by a consonant, normally assimilates within the word and at word boundaries into the class nasal of that following consonant.

> பெரும் + குன்று = பெருங்குன்று *big mountain*  
> அம் + சிலம்பு = அஞ்சிலம்பு *beautiful anklet*  
> குறும் + தொகை = குறுந்தொகை *short anthology* \[of poetry\]

But when final ம் is part of case ending or compound, it normally simply vanishes and geminates the following consonant.

> கமலம் + கண் = கமலக்கண் *lotus eye*  
> நம் + துறந்து = நத்துறந்து *after abandoning us*

But, in the above circumstances, when followed by வ் or ய், it simply vanishes without geminating the following consonant.

> புகர்முகம் + வேழம் = புகர்முகவேழம் *elephant with a spotted face*  
> கானம் + யானை = கானயானை *forest elephant*

Final -ம், when followed by other nasals of the type ம், ந், ஞ் is simply dropped ; unless the ம் is at the end of a monosyllabic word with a short vowel, in which case it is assimilated to the class nasal.

> நாமம் + மாறும் = நாமமாறும் the name will change  
> மரம் + நீண்டது = மரநீண்டது the tree grew long
>
> செம் + மண் = செம்மண் red (fertile) land  
> வெம் + நீர் = வெந்நீர் hot water  
> தம் + ஞான் = தஞ்ஞான் their string

#### Final ண்

The retroflex word final ண் when followed by a dental த் or ந் causes retroflexive assimilation (like in Sanskrit).

> தண் + நீர் = தண்ணீர் *cold water (*modern Tamil *: water)*  
> கண் + நீர் = கண்ணீர் *eye water (tear)*  
> தண் + துறை = தண்டுறை *cool ghat*

When followed by other hard consonants like க், ச், ப் inside a word, it changes to a ட்.

> கடும் + கண் + பன்றி = கடுங்கட்பன்றி fierce eyed pig  
> பெரும் + பூண் + சென்னி = பெரும்பூட்சென்னி Cenni with large ornaments

#### Final ன்

A final ன் when followed by a dental ந் causes it to vanish and when the word is monosyllabic with a short monosyllabic word, it geminates as well.

> தோழியின் + நெஞ்சம் = தோழியினெஞ்சம் *friend’s heart*  
> என் + நெஞ்சம் = என்னெஞ்சம் *my heart*

When followed by the hard consonants க், ச், த், ப், it may optionally changes to ற்.

> அறத்தின் + காக்கும் = அறத்திற்காக்கும் *that is guarded by dharma  *
> இறும்பின் + பாம்பு = இறும்பிற்பாம்பு *snake in the thicket*

When followed by the hard dental த், it may sometimes change as ன் + த் = ன்ற் / ற்ற்

> என் + தோழி = என்றோழி *my friend*  
> நின் + துறந்து = நிற்றுறந்து *after abandoning you*

#### Final ல்

This is the most variant among all sandhis. Normally, a final ல் becomes ற் when followed by the consonants க், ப், and த். In the case of following by a dental த், it is also assimilated to ற் or is made to vanish.

> பல் + கள் (plural marker) = பற்கள் *teeth*  
> திரள்கால் + குவறை = திரள்காற்குவறை *round-stemmed blue water-lily*  
> இல் + செறிப்பு = இற்செறிப்பு *being confined in the house*  
> நுதல் + பேப்பு = நுதற்பேப்பு *pallor of the forehead*
>
> நல் + தாய் = நற்றாய் *good mother*   
> நல் + தமிழ் = நற்றமிழ் *good Tamil  *
> அழல் + துடங்கினாள் = அழறுடங்கினாள் *she began to cry*

But sometimes, other changes can happen like :

- ல் + ச் = ன்ச்

- ல் + ப் = ன்ப்

- ல் + த் = ன்ற் / ஃற்

> மெல் + சாயல் = மென்சாயள் *soft beauty*  
> வல் + புலம் = வன்புலம் *hard soil*  
> அல் + திணை = அஃறிணை *non class*

When followed by nasals ம் and ந், a final ல் transforms into a ன் and assimilates the folowing dental ந் to a ன் or makes it vanish.

> நுதல் + நீவி = நுதனீவி *stroking the forehead*  
> நல் + நகர் = நன்னகர் *good city*  
> சொல் + மாலை = சொன்மாலை *word garland*

#### Final ள்

Final retroflex ள் when followed by the hard consonants க், ச், ப், த் either transforms itself into the hard retroflex ட் or the nasal retroflex ண். In the case of dental த், it is forced to assimilate to the preceding ட். These happen within a word or a compound word but are optional in word boundaries.

> மகள் + கு (dative ending) = மகட்கு *for the daughter*  
> வெள் + குடை = வெண்குடை *white umbrella*  
> ஒள் + சுடர் = ஒண்சுடர் *bright glow*  
> கேள் + தல் (infinitive ending) = கேட்டல் *asking*  
> மகள் + போக்கிய தாய் = மகட் போக்கிய தாய் a *mother who left her child*  
> ஒளிறு வாள் + தானை = ஒளிறு வாட்டானை *an army with shining swords*  
> நாள் + தோறும் = நாடோறும் *daily*

When it is followed by a nasal ம் or ந், it does not become hard and becomes only ண் and and assimilates the folowing dental ந் to a ண் or makes it vanish.

> பைய்யுள் + மாலை = பைய்யுண்மாலை *sorrowful evening*  
> தெள் + நீர் = தெண்ணீர் *clear water*  
> வாள் + நுதல் = வாணுதல் *bright forehead*

For example, if modern Tamil still used classical sandhi rules, the phrase “பொங்கல் திருநாள் நல்வாழ்த்துக்கள்” would be rendered as “பொங்கற் றிருநா(ண்) ணல்வாழ்த்துக்கள்”.

### Doubling of Initial Consonant

This is the only sandhi that is still preserved fully intact in modern written Tamil. When a word ends in a vowel (or sometimes a semi-vowel consonant) and is immediately followed by another word starting in the hard consonants க், ச், த், and ப், these initial hard consonants undergo doubling in some cases. The rules that govern when their doubling occurs is the subject of this section. Most Tamil students in Tamil Nadu make their fair share of spelling mistakes in school by doing this sandhi wrongly either by forgetting to apply when it should be applied, or by doing it when it should not be done. No one teaches exactly when to do it and when not to do it; and the students are expected to pick this proper application of sandhi up by intuition that is obtained with reading and practice. But, there is a rule governing this process and the following are the vowels after which the four hard consonants க், ச், த், and ப் double.

- after the accusative (object) ending ஐ for nouns.  
  E.g. இராமனைக் கொன்றான் *he killed Rama*

- after the dative (to) ending கு for nouns.  
  E.g. தேர்வுக்குப் படி *study for the test*

- after உ that is preceded by a double consonant.  
  E.g. மாட்டுப் பால் *cow’s milk*

- after the demonstrative particles (that), இ (this), உ (this), and
  after the particles எ (what), after the longer demonstratives இந்த
  (this), அந்த (that) , and எந்த (which).  
  E.g. அச்சிறுவன் *that boy*

- after the final அ of the infinitive.  
  E.g. செய்யப் பெற்றேன் *I got to do*

- after the final இ of the absolutive verb (including the forms ஆய் and
  போய்).  
  E.g. ஆய்ப் போன *completed*, ஆகிச் சென்ற *that which* *went away*

- after the final ஆ of the negative peyareccam and positive absolutive
  (refer to a future section).  
  E.g. முடிவிலாப் பயணம் *endless journey*

- after monosyllabic words ending in a long vowel.  
  E.g. கைப்பேசி *hand-talk (mobile phone)*

- in compounds after mono- and disyllabic neuter nouns ending in a vowel
  or ய், ர், and ழ்.  
  E.g. தாய்ப்பசு *mother cow,* வீதிக்கதவு *street door*

### Tense System : Past & Non-Past

Modern Tamil has three basic tenses : simple past, simple present and simple future. But, what we now have as the present tense in modern Tamil developed out of another construction from the past tense that I will explain shortly. In Old Tamil, there was only a single tense for both the present and the future. Even today, Telugu and Kannada (which are other Dravidian languages related to Tamil), have the same form for simple present and simple future tense). So, in Old Tamil, what we now call as the simple future tense stood for both simple present and simple future.

To summarise :

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kba2!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe406c3ad-8c38-4897-a540-ff8b9a9967ff_1508x432.heic)

The red colored modern Tamil’s distinct present tense is an innovation and was not a part of the older language. So, in Old Tamil, it makes sense to call the tenses as **Past** vs **Non-Past (**as there is the same verb for both present and future tenses). Only towards the medieval period, a firm and distinct present tense comes to be established (shown in red in the picture). How did the present tense form come into modern Tamil? The present tense in later Tamil developed out of the following construction.

> Verb root + **கில்** (ability) + past tense endings

Let us now illustrate how a distinct present tense came to be formed in modern Tamil with the help of this verb.

> Example:  
> Root **செய்** *to do*  
> Root **கில்** *to have ability*

**செய்** + **கில்** = **செய்கில்** *to be able to do*  
  
Now, in modern formal Tamil, the past tense of the root **கல்** (to learn) is : **கற்றான்** (he learnt). Similarly, the past tense of **செய்கில்** becomes **செய்கிற்றான்** (he was able to do)  

> **கல் → கற்றான்  
> செய்கில் → செய்கிற்றான்**

Now, the following series of sound changes happened with time:

> **செய்கிற்றான்** → **செய்கின்றான்** → **செய்கிறான்**

Eventually, this new form, that originally meant “was able to do X”, changed its meaning and began to assume the meaning of a distinctively present tense. So, originally, the verb **செய்கிற்றான்** that meant “***he was able to do***” eventually came to mean the present tense “***he does***” after a sound change and a meaning change. In modern formal Tamil, both **செய்கின்றான்** and **செய்கிறான்** are acceptable present tense.

But we should remember while reading Old Tamil that this present tense form was not there and hence we should interpret the modern present tense form as a past tense with “was able to”.

### Tamil Verbs : Conjugation Classes

Tamil verbs are divided into about 12 classes (and a small set of irregular verbs that do not pigeon-hole into either of these classes) based on the modifications that they undergo to form the past and future tense forms. We can talk about conjugation classes for English verbs based on how they form their past tenses. The following verbs that add a “d” “ed” would be put in one class in English,

> educate → educated  
> ship → shipped  
> widen → widened  
> search → searched  
> melt → melted

The following verbs that change i→a to form their past tenses would be in one class in English.

> drink → drank  
> ring → rang  
> sink → sank  
> sit → sat

The following verbs that change i→o to form their past tenses would be in one class in English.

> drive → drove  
> ride → rode  
> strive → strove

The verbs that change to ought would be put in another conjugation class in English.

> bring → brought  
> buy → bought  
> seek → sought

There are also irregular verbs whose past tense is completely unrelated or very unpredictable from their present tense forms. Some of them are :

> go → went

So, we have seen some conjugation classes of verbs in English. Just that in English, we did not systematically classify verbs into groups and name them based on how they form their past tenses but Indian grammarians have been much more systemmatic in describing the grammar of their languages and hence Tamil grammarians have meticulously grouped verbs of their lanugage into twelve classes based on how they form their past tenses (Sanskrit grammarians too grouped their verb roots into ten conjuagation classes based on how they form their present tense from the root).

These groups or classes are called conjugation classes in linguistics; but don’t let that name bother you. Although there are patterns that decide what verb root belongs to what class, sometimes it can be arbitrary. I present these classes one by one now. I name each class by a famous example of a verb from each class so that you can easily remember.

#### Class 1 : **செய்** (to do) Class

**Prediction**: Verbs of this class normally have one syllable and end in the consonant **ய்** and have a **short vowel** in the root.

**Pattern**:  
Past tense → add **த்**  
Non-past tense → add **வ்**

Conjugated example in all the two tenses:  
செய்**தா**ன் = he did  
செய்**வா**ன்= he will do / he does

> Other examples of verbs that belong to this class:  
> **பெய்** *to fall, to pour, to spread, to discharge*

#### Class 2 : **ஆள்** (to rule) Class

**Prediction**: Verbs of this class normally have **one syllable** and end in the consonant **ள்**

**Pattern**:  
Past tense → replace **ள்** by **ண்** and add **ட்**  
Non-past tense → add **உவ்**

**Conjugated example in all the two tenses:**  
ஆ**ண்டா**ன் = he ruled  
ஆ**ளுவா**ன் = he will rule / he rules

> **Other verbs that belong to this class:**  
> **கொள்** *to attain, to possess*

#### Class 3 : **கொல்** (to kill) Class

**Prediction**: Verbs of this class normally have **one syllable, a short vowel** and end in the consonant **ல்**

**Pattern:  ** Past tense → replace **ல்** by **ன்** and add **ற்**  
Non-past tense → add **உவ்**

**Conjugated example in all the two tenses:  ** கொ**ன்றா**ன் = he killed  
கொ**ல்லுவா**ன்= he will kill / he kills  

> **Other verbs that belong to this class:**  
> **செல்** *to go, to leave, to move*  
> **மெல்** *to chew*  
> **நில்** *to stand*  
> **கில்** *to be able to* (this formed the present tense in modern
> Tamil)  
> **வெல்** *to win*

#### Class 4 : **அறி** (to know) Class

Prediction: Verbs of this class normally have **two syllables** , a short vowel and end in **ய், ர், ழ், இ, ஐ**

**Pattern**:  
Past tense → add **ந்த்**  
Non-past tense → add **வ்**

**Conjugated example in all the two tenses**:

#### Class 5 : **தூங்கு** (to sleep) Class

**Prediction**: Verbs of this class normally have **two syllables** and end in **உ**. However, if the verb ends in **டு/று**, it belongs to the next class (class 6), but sometimes can belong to in this class as well.

**Pattern**:  
Past tense → replace **உ** by **இ** and add **ன்**  
Non-past tense → add **வ்**

**Conjugated example in all the two tenses:  ** தூங்கி**னா**ன் = he slept

**Prediction**: Verbs of this class normally have two syllables and end in **டு/று**

**Pattern**:  
Past tense → remove the **உ**, **duplicate** the **ட்/ற்**  
Non-past tense → add **வ்**

**Conjugated example in all the two tenses:**  
வி**ட்டா**ன் = he left

**Prediction**: Irregular (the verbs belonging to this class have no regular pattern)

**Pattern**:  
Past tense =\> add **ட்**  
Non-past tense =\> add **ப்**

உண்**டா**ன் = he ate

**Prediction**: Verbs of this class normally end in ன்

Past tense =\> Replace **ள்** by **ட்ட்**  
Non-past tense =\> Replace **ள்** by **ட்ப்**

கே**ட்டா**ன் = he heard  
கே**ட்பா**ன் = he will hear / he heard

#### Class 10 : **கல்** (to learn) Class

**Prediction**: Irregular

**Pattern**:  
Past tense =\> Replace **ல்** by **ற்ற்**  
Non-past tense =\> Replace **ல்** by **ற்ப்**

**Conjugated example in all the two tenses:**  
க**ற்றா**ன் = he learnt  
க**ற்பா**ன் = he will learn / he learns

**Note** : Class 4 verbs are also conjugated in this class when they are used when they have a transitive meaning.

**Pattern**:  
Past tense → Add **த்த்**  
Non-past tense → Add **ப்ப்**

  
Conjugated example in all the two tenses:  
கொடு**த்தா**ன் = he gave  
கொடு**ப்பா**ன் = he will give / he gives

Old Tamil conveys case relations of nouns by using fixed postpositional suffixes. But, the noun does exhibit some degree of inflexion. The basic distinction is between the subject form and the other forms called the oblique forms which are used as bases in compound words and also the base that recieve case suffixes. The possible patterns are summarised below. The final உ in all these are the overhort u.

- ம் → த்த்

- doubling of the final consonant

- same as the nominative

- addition of the oblique suffix இன் to the nominative stem

The oblique suffix இன் is added for those words for which the noun stem remains the same between the nominative and the oblique. But, in a later stage, the oblique suffix இன் began to be added liberally to other cases to distinct oblique stems themselves, thus creating double obliques and this is why we have variant forms of the same word like மரத்தில் vs மரத்தினில் *on the tree*. The oblique stem is used for forming initial bases for compounds as well as the base that recieves the case endings.

> ஆறு (nominative) → ஆற்று (oblique) *river*  
> Compound : ஆற்றுநீர் *river-water*  
> Case suffix : ஆற்றில் *on the river  *
> Double Oblique : ஆற்றினில் *on the river*

### Demonstrative & Interrogative Pronouns

English has a two way distinction in its demonstrative pronouns which are :

- that (far) = noun located far

- this (near) = noun located near

Tamil however has a three way distinction in its demonstrative pronouns which are marked by the following prefixes.

- அ (e.g. அவன், அவள், அவர், அது, அதன், அவை) = noun located far from the
  speaker and listener

- உ ( e.g. உவன், உவள், உவர், உது, உதன், உவை) = noun located close to the
  listener

- இ (e.g. இவன், இவள், இவர், இது, இதன், இவை) = noun located close to the
  speaker

But, the உ prefix drops out of usage after the Sangam times and the three-way அ-உ-இ distinction of Old Tamil collapses to a two-way அ-இ distinction in post-Sangam Tamil like in English. Apart from these, an additional suffix marks interrogative prononuns.

- எ (e.g. எவன், எவள், எவர், எது, எதன், எவை) = question words

The demonstrative pronouns are formed by adding these prefixes to the following suffixes. Due to sandhi, a வ் intervenes between the prefix and suffix to prevent vowel-vowel hiatus.

- Masculine singular suffix : அன்

- Feminine singular suffix : அன்

- Plural or Honorary suffix : அர் (later : +கள்)

- Neuter singular suffix (nominative) : அன்

- Neuter singular suffix (oblique) : தன்

- Neuter plural suffix (nominative) : ஐ

- Neuter plural suffix (oblique) : அற்று

So, using these four prefixes (three demonstrative + one interrogative) and the seven suffixes, one can get 28 forms.

#### Present / Habitual

Verb root X **→ Participal Noun =** someone who does X / someone who is in the habit of doing X  
  
Old Tamil adds various suffixes to form nouns of agent. Only some of them survive into Modern Tamil. They are very similar to the pronominal suffixes that we saw in the previous section.  
  
**THIRD PERSON PRONOMINAL SUFFIXES FOR PARTICIPLE NOUNS :**  
**அன்/ஓன்** = he (male)  
**அள்/ஓள்** = she (female)  
**அர்**/**ஓர்** = they (plural)  
  
**The person who does :** Finally, let us see how to form a noun that means “he/she/they/that which does X” from a verb root X.  
  
1. **Root + வ்/ப்/ப்ப்** + third person pronominal suffix (**அன்** for male, **அள்** for female**, அர்** for plural**, அது** for neuter)  
  
Eg**. செய்** (to do)+ **வ்** + **அர்** (plural) = **செய்வர்** = people who do  
  
2. **Root** + **ப் /ப்ப்** + third person full pronoun (**அவன்** for male, **அவள்** for female, **அவர்** for plural, அது for neuter)  
  
Eg. **அணி** (to adorn) + **ப்** + **அவர்** (plural) = **அணிபவர்** = they who adorn  
  
3. **Root** + **(உ)ந்** + third person pronominal suffix (**அன்** for male, **அள்** for female, **அர்** for plural, **அது** for neuter)  
  
Eg. **விடு** (to leave) + **ந்** + **அர்** = **விடுநர்** = they who leave  
  
4. **Root** + **உம்** + third person full pronoun / pronominal suffix   
  
Eg. **அறி** (to know) + **உம்** + **ஓன்** (male) = **அறியுமோன்** = the (the man) who knows

#### Past

In the previous section, we saw in Old Tamil, how to convert a verb root X to convert into a noun that means "***a person who does X***". This time, we will do the same for the past tense and will see:  
  
Verbal Root X → **a person who did X / a person who used to do X**  
  
In this construction, for most of the roots, their past tense stem suffixes are used.  
  
For example, consider the root **அறி** that gives **அறிந்தான்** (he knew)**,** in third person singular past tense. We remove the pronoun suffix **ஆன்** and use the raw stem **அறிந்த்** for the noun formation. The various patterns of their formation are explained now.   
  
1. **Root** + **past stem** + **pronoun suffix** / **full pronoun** = ***a person who did X***  
  
Eg. **அறி** (to know) + **ந்த்** (its past tense stem from class 3)  + **அன்** (3rd male singular) = **அறிந்தன்** = he who knew / he who used to know / he who has known   
  
Words of this type survive in some epithets in modern Tamil like **பெரியார்** (respectul plural - he who has grown \[in stature\]). Sometimes, full pronouns are also used.   
  
Eg. **அறி** (to know) + **ந்த்** + **அவன்** = **அறிந்தவன்** (same meaning as above).  
This is very productive in modern Tamil too.   
  
Another suffix that performs the same function which is neither productive nor familiar in modern Tamil is:  
  
2. Root **(no class 5 allowed)** + past stem + **இசின்**+ prononoun suffix = a person who did X  
  
Eg. அறி (to know) + ந்த் + **இசின்** +ஓர் (third person plural) = **அறிந்திசிநோர்** (they who knew / they who have known / they who used to know)  
  
Note: Some roots conjugate in class 3 (except for class 5), just for the sake of taking this இசின் ending even if they are not in class 3 otherwise.

### Disambiguation for Participles and Verbs

Having seen the participle endings and personal endings for verbs, you would see that they can be very similar many times - especially for the ending having **அன், அள், அர், அம்**. For e.g, a word like **வருவன்** can mean "***he comes***" or as an agentive "***he who comes***". This is because the personal endings and the pronoun suffixes are so similar.

This is indeed the case with Classical Tamil : the border between nouns and verbs is fluid especially when one uses the first forms to form nouns. This is a major reason why people with only modern Tamil background find it difficult to parse Old Tamil texts even if they know the core verb root in a word. Classical Tamil can be amiguous in this way.

One way that Classical Tamil can explicitly shed this ambiguity and explicitly convey that something is a \[participal\] noun (and not a verb), is to use the second and/or third forms of the constructions in posts 17 and 18. For example, instead of using the ambiguous first form **வருவன்**, if the second form **வருபவன்** is used, this can **only** mean "***he who knows***".  
  
How about verbs? Does Classical Tamil have hat tricks to freeze the ambiguity and say that it is only a verb an not an agentive noun? Yes, one method is that it can use the personal endings of the ஓ/ஒ type that don't look like nouns. But another construction is also possible. Tamil adds another suffix after the stem and before the personal ending to explicitly convey that it is a verb. That prefix is:  
1. **கு** (for **non-past** tense)  
2. **அன்** (for **past** tense)  
  
Example:  
The word **செய்**+**த்** (past tense class 1A) +**அன்** (3rd person singular male) = **செய்தன்** can mean either  
Noun: "*He who did*"  
Verb: "*He did*"   
  
But, if we add the verbal marking additional ending அன் (for past tense), we have   
  
செய்+த் (past tense class 1) + **அன் (verbal disambigation ending)** + அன் (3rd person singular male) = **செய்தனன்**  
  
**செய்தனன்** only means "*he did*" and cannot mean "*~~he who did~~*" at any circumstances.   
  
Similarly, a word **வருவள்** in the non-past tense, can mean "(noun): she who comes \[post 17\]" or "(verb): she comes \[post 19\]"). But adding the non-past disambiguation suffix **கு**, we have  
  
வரு + **கு** + வள் = **வருகுவள்**  
  
This word வருகுவள் can only mean "*she comes*" in the strictly verbal sense and not "*~~she who comes~~*" in a noun sense.

# Absolutive : After Doing

Now, we look at another construction in Classical Tamil that is ubiquitous. It is called the ***absolutive***. What is that? In the sentence "*After reading, I slept*", the verb denoting reading would be in the absolutive. Classical Tamil has lots of ways for forming absolutives out of which only one survives into the modern languages. In this post, we will survey all of them.  
  
1. **உ / இ (used in modern Tamil)**  
If a verb belongs to class 5, then the raw past tense stem that changes the உ to இ to form the absolutive.

> Eg. **தூங்கு** (*to sleep*) =\> **தூங்கி** *after sleeping*

If a verb does not belong to class 5, then **உ** is added to the raw past tense stem is used as the absolutive.

> Eg.   
> **செய்** (*to do*) =\> **செய்த்** (past tense stem) + உ =\> **செய்து**
> *after doing*  
> **உணர்** (*to feel*) =\> **உணர்ந்த்** (past tense stem) + உ =\> **உணர்ந்து**
> *after feeling*

Thus, so far, everything is familiar from Modern Tamil. But, Classical Tamil has lots of other tricks in its sleeves.  
  
2. **Class 3 Irregulars ending in உவு**  
If a class 5 verb ends in **உவு** (like **தழுவு**), then apart from the usual absolutive form **தழுவி**, classical Tamil also has a form like **தழீஇ** = after embracing.  
  
3. **Root + (ப்)பு**  
A verb root can simply just take **பு** or **ப்பு** to become an absolutive. Class 3 roots always take only பு but others take either of the two.

> Eg.   
> **செய்பு** *after doing*  
> **உணர்ப்பு** *after feeling*  
> **அஞ்சுபு** *after fearing*

4. **Root + ஊ(உ)**  
Rarely, a verb root can take ஊ(உ) and can become absolutive.

> Eg. **தேடூஉ** *after searching*

5. **Root + ஆ : positive and negative absolute**

Something strange happens with this form. This suffix ஆ when added to a root X, not only means "after doing X" but also means its negative "after not doing X"!!

> Eg. **செய்யா** *after doing / after not doing*

Now, we will look at forms that serve as only negative absolutes. The first two are familiar from modern Tamil.   
  
**6. Root + ஆமல்**

> Eg. **செய்யாமல்** *after not doing*

7. Root + **ஆது**

> Eg. **செய்யாது** *after not doing*

This next form below is familiar in modern Tamil but is used as a negative abstract noun in modern Tamil. In Classical Tamil, it is both a negative absolute and as well as a negative abstract noun.   
  
**8. Root + ஆமை**

> Eg. **செய்யாமை** *after not doing* / *the act of not doing*

Some absolutives have acquired new meanings from bhakti era Tamil onwards. They are:  
  
1. **நின்று** (after standing → from)  
Literally it means "***after standing***". But it eventually acquires a prepositional meaning of "***from***".   
  
2. **கொண்டு / கொடு** (after possessing → with)  
Literally it means "***after having***" but eventually it became used as a preposition meaning "***with***".  
  
3. **நோக்கி** (after aiming → considering)  
Literally it means "***after aiming***" but later it came to mean either "***thanks to***" or "***in consideration of***".   
  
4. **பற்றி** (after attaching to → about)  
Literally the word means "***after holding on to***" or "***after attaching to***". Eventually, it came to mean "***about***" or "***regarding***".

# Infinitive : To Do

Next, we will see how classical Tamil conveys the meaning intended by the English prefix “to”. This form is called the infinitive. It is formed in four ways. The first three survive in modern Tamil but the last one does not and is rarer even in Old Tamil.  
  
**1. Verb Root / Present tense stem + அ**  
Here, the vowel அ is directly added to the verb root or it present tense form (with sometimes doubling of the final consonant). For the verbs of the latter type, modern Tamil converts (ப்)ப =\> (க்)க

> E.g.   
> செய் + அ = **செய்ய** *to do*  
> நில் =\> நிற்ப் + அ = **நிற்ப** =\> later in modern Tamil **நிற்க** *to
> stand*  
> தீர் =\> தீர்ப்ப் + அ = **தீர்ப்ப** =\> later in modern Tamil **தீர்க்க** *to
> complete*

**2. Verb root + மார்**  
Here, the verb root takes the suffix மார்.

> Eg. தரு + மார் = தருமார் =\> **தருமாரு** (to give)

**3. Verb root + (இ)ய(ர்)**  
Here, the suffix இய(ர்) is directly added to the verb root. இ is omitted when the verb root already ends in it. Roots ending in உ transform it into a ஈஇ(ர்).

> E.g.  
> பணி + (இ)ய = **பணிய** *to obey*  
> காண் + இய = **காணிய** *to see*  
> கொடு + இய(ர்) = **கொடீஇய**(ர்) *to give*  
> கடி + இயர் = **கடியர்** *to chase*

**4. Verb root + வான் / ப்பான்**  
This is a very rare formation even in Old Tamil and is homophonous to the third person masculine singular future tense.

> E.g. அள + ப்பான் = **அளப்பான்** *to measure / he will measure*

In English, verbs like “open” and “fill” that are used either transitively or intransitively have the same form but in Tamil and other Indian languages, they do not. We will see how Tamil distinguishes between transitive and intransitive verbs grammatically but note that in general, Tamil has separate modes of formation for transitive and intransitive verbs.

## Verbs : Intransitive → Transitive

Although some verbs are always transitive and some are always intransitive in Tamil (like in English), many verbs have separate forms and the following three are the methods by which Tamil converts an intransitive verb stem to a transitive verb stem.

### Consonant Hardening & Doubling

Verb roots that end in a hard consonant preceded by a vowel (like ஆடு *shake*, விடு *leave,* ஆகு *become,* திருந்து *become correct*, ) and those where the final consonant is preceded by a nasal (like அடங்கு *be controlled*, நிரம்பு *be filled*, திரும்பு *be turned*) form their transitive forms by doubling and hardening of that final consonant where the nasal disappears in the process of hardening and doubling.

> ஆடு → ஆட்டூ *shake*  
> விடு → விட்டு *leave*  
> ஆகு → ஆக்கு *become*  
> திருந்து → திருத்து *correct*  
> அடங்கு → அடக்கு *restrain*  
> நிரம்பு → நிரப்பு *fill*  
> திரும்பு → திருப்பு *turn*

### Hardening & Doubling of Tense Sign (Class 4 → Class 11)

There are some roots that become transitive by hardening and doubling the tense signs in their conjugated forms. Class 4 verbs for instance are conjugated in Class 11 when they become transitive. Some examples are shown below.

> நனைந்தான், நனைவான் → நனைத்தான், நனைப்பான் *wet* (Class 4 → Class 11)  
> சேர்ந்தான், சேர்வான் → சேர்த்தான் , சேர்ப்பான் *join* (Class 4 → Class 11)  
> எரிந்தாள், எரிவாள் → எரித்தாள், எரிப்பாள் *burn* (Class 4 → Class 11)  
> விரிந்தான், விரிவான் → விரித்தான் விரிப்பான் *spread* (Class 4 → Class 11)

### Extra Suffix

There is till another set of verbs like நட *walk*, தீர் *finish,* அறு *cut,* etc. which are converted into transitive by adding the particle த்து to the root. Examples are shown below :

> நட→ நடத்து *walk*  
> நிமிர் → நிமித்து *straighten*  
> தாழ் → தாழ்த்து *lower*  
> உழர் → உழர்த்து *raise*  
> உணர் → உணர்த்து *feel*

There are however some verbs in Tamil that do have the same form for both transitive and intransitive usages like in English. Also, there are some verbs that form their transitives irregularly. For example :

> உண் *eat* → ஊட்டு *feed  *
> காண் *see* → காட்டு *show*

## Verbs : Causative

Now we will see how Tamil converts verbs to their causatives to convey making someone do the verb. There are two methods.

### Suffix பி / வி

A suffix like வி or பி is added to the verb root and the resulting causative verb is now conjugated in Class 11. This method is fully productive and applicable for all verbs in Classical Tamil but no longer productive in modern written Tamil although it survives in vestiges. Some popular examples :

> தெரி *become visible* → தெரிவி *make* *visible*  
> அறி *know* → அறிவி *make* *known (announce)*  
> செய் *do* → செய்வி *make* *do*  
> காண் *see* → காண்பி *show* (*make* *see*) (Colloquial Tamil : kāmi)  
> முடி *finish* → முடிவி *make* *finish  *
> போ *go* → போவி *make go*  
> கல் *learn* → கற்பி *teach* (sandhi change : ல் → ற்)

When to use the பி and when to use the வி? This thumb rule helps. Whenever the future tense stem starts with ப், then the causative suffix is பி and whenever the future stem of the verb starts with வ், the causative adds the வி. (e.g. செய் → செய்வான் → செய்வி). Test this rule for all the examples above as an exercise.

Sometimes, this can continue on to form a double causative by adding another ப்பி to the causative as well.

> காண் *see* → காண்பி *show* → காண்பிப்பி *make show  *
> அறி know → அறிவி *make* *know (announce) →* அறிவிப்பி *make it
> announce*

- Any adjective used in the neuter form either singular or plural can
  stand for an adverb. Eg. சிறிது செய்தான் *he did little,* எளிதாக
  முடிந்தது *it got over easily*.

- The oblique form is also possible, sometimes even a double oblique.
  Eg. எளிதினின் செய்தான் *he did easily*.

- The enclitic particle -ஏ that is used for emphasis can be added to an
  adjective to form the adverb: Eg. வல்லே *strongly*.

- In isolated cases we see adverbs that are the datives of adjectives.
  E.g. நற்கு *well*.

- The infinitive -என of the quotative verb என்னுதல் *to speak*, is

- Absolutives sometimes can also be used as adverbs, especially when
  they stand in concatenation with another absolutive: விரைஇ
  *fragrantly*

# Peyareccam : Relative Participle Verb

Consider the following phrases in English like

- **the man** *who came ……*

- **the letter** *that arrived yesterday ……*

- **the student** *whom the teacher praised* …..

In all these phrases, the second part in italics narrows down the noun in bold that precedes it and gives more information about it. The italicised part is called the relative clause in English grammar and the rest of the sentence in bold is called the main clause in English grammar. In some sense, the entire italics part is acting as an adjective (describing more information about the noun in bold).

> **The student** *whom the teacher praised* **is absent today  
> Main clause : The student .. is absent today →** main matter**  
> Relative clause :** *whom* \[the student\] *the teacher praised →*
> more information

English uses “who/that/whom” to signal that what follows is a relative clause and then constructs a normal sentence as usual within the relative clause. What does Tamil do? It does not have any special word like “who/that/which/whom” to mark a relative pronoun. Instead, it uses a special form of a verb called the **peyareccam** when the verb is a part of the relative clause. It is this verb that takes the bolded noun as its complement. This word *peyareccam* literally in Tamil means “that which needs a noun as a complement”. English instead, uses the same form of the verb in both the main and relative clauses and relies on extra particles like “who/whom/that/which” to rely on conveying what action is in the main clause and what is in the relative clause.

> Eg. Verb : Praised   
> In Main Clause : **The student** *whom the teacher praised* **is
> absent today**  
> In Relative Clause : **The manager** *whom everyone hates* **praised
> Rohan**

But Tamil would use a different word for the same verb “praised” when it is occuring in an italic taking the role of a relative clause. This grammatical paradigm of using a separate verb form for relative clauses (with the ordinary verb reserved only for main clauses) is unique to Tamil and Dravidian languages and has no exact counterpart in other Indo-European languages of both India and Europe. This special form performs the role of the relative pronoun plus verb combination, as well as those of participles in other languages. So, since it does not have a straightforward equivalent in the grammar of Western languages, I name it by the native name given by Tamil grammarians which is **peyareccam**.

Consider the following example in detail involving a transitive verb.

English : **The student** *who read* `[past tense]` *books* **plays with others.**

This is made up of two clauses :

Main Clause : `The student` **plays with others**  
Relative Clause : *who* `[the student]` *read books*

Here, the connecting noun that is common to both the clauses is “the student”. The student is the one who is playing and the one who has also read books. The noun common to both the main clause and the relative clause is “the student” here. The verb in the relative clause is “read” (past tense). Let us call the common connecting noun X and the verb in the relative clause V. Then, Tamil does the following :

> *relative clause information* …… + *peyareccam form of V* +
> `connecting noun X` + **main clause information…..**

So, this sentence when translated into Tamil, will literally come out as :

> *books \[object\]* *read \[peyareccam form\]* + `the student` +
> **plays with others**

Thus, if we compare the English and Tamil clauses, we have :

> English : **The man** *who read books*….  
> Tamil : *books read* \[peyareccam\] **the man**

We see that the word order in English and Tamil are exactly reversed in the sense the italics part comes first and the bolded part comes next. Also, inside the relative clause, the peyareccam verb comes last and other things come to the left of it, which is also the opposite to that of English.

If we see the full sentences, we have

> English : **The man** *who read books* **is brilliant**  
> Tamil : *books read* \[peyareccam\] **the man** **is brilliant**

English gives the main noun, interrupts to give the italicised information about the main noun by inserting a relative clause, and then continues with the main clause. The relative clause in English is sandwiched between the main noun and the rest of the main clause. That is,

English interrupts the main clause immediately after the main noun to provide information about a noun in the main clause by starting a relative clause immediately after the noun (in the main clause) that needs to be described

**Main clause ……..** → **noun** → *relative clause* → **rest of the main clause**

But Tamil does not do that.

Tamil does not follow this syntax. The relative clause is fully given first with its verb in the form of peyareccam at the end. Then, the peyareccam relative verb is followed by the noun in the main clause (that needs to be described) as its complement. Then, the rest of the main clause proceeds, starting from this connecting noun. The main clause begins afresh and proceeds without interruption after the relative clause has fully ended.

*relative clause* … → *peyareccam verb* **+ main clause noun → rest of main clause**

So, in Tamil, the main clause begins after the relative clause ends and the noun that is common to both the clauses (the main clause noun that needs to be described by the relative clause) is sandwiched in the middle, between the two clauses. This noun is preceded by the peyareccam verb in the relative clause and succeeded by the rest of the main clause.

Let us see how the peyareccam form of the verb is formed for the past tense and the non-past tense.

**PAST PEYARECCAM :**   
The peyareccam for most verbs in the past tense is formed by adding **(ய்)அ** to the past stem of the verb.

**Pattern : Past tense stem of verb + (ய்)அ**

> E.g.   
> செய் =\> செய்த் =\> செய்த *done*  
> அஞ்சு =\> அஞ்சி =\> அஞ்சின *feared*  
> உணர் =\> உணர்ந் =\> உணர்ந்த *felt*

**Class 5 extra : Root** + **இய**  
This is only for class 5 verbs which also form an extra peyareccam as follows.

> E.g. அஞ்சு =\> அஞ்சி =\> அஞ்சிய f*eared*

As we saw, past tense peyareccam forms are used to convey a completed action of a subject before the main verb.

> E.g. புத்தகம் **படித்த** மாணவன் வீடு திரும்பினான்  
> *The student **who has read** books returned home.*

**NON-PAST PEYARECCAM :**  
This type of peyareccam is formed using the bare root as the base. Sometimes, the final consonant of the root may be doubled.

**3**. **Verb root + (ய்)உம்**  
For verbs from classes 1 to 7, the suffix is **உம்**. If the verb root already ends in உ, then it is omitted. If it ends in another vowel, an extra **ய்** is added. For verbs of classes 8 to 12, the suffix is (க்)கும் and it takes the peculiarities of the non-past stem.

> E.g.   
> பிரி + (ய்)உம் = பிரியும் *that which is separating / that which will
> separate  *
> கல் + கும் = கற்கும்

These non-past tense peyareccam forms are used to convey an action of a subject that is concurrent with the main verb or will happen after the main verb.

> E.g. புத்தகம் **படிக்கும்**  மாணவன் வீடு திரும்பினான்  
> *The student **who was reading books** returned home. (The student
> read before he left for home)*

**NEGATIVE PEYARECCAM :**  
In the negative peyareccam, there is no distinction between past and non-past cases.  
  
**4. Verb root + ஆ + optional த**  
E.g. செய் + ஆ(த) = செய்யா(த) *did not / does not / will not do*
