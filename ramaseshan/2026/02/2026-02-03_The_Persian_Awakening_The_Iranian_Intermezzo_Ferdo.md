# The Persian Awakening : The Iranian Intermezzo, Ferdowsi, and his Shahnamah (The Book of Kings)

### One of the most important yet neglected period in Islamic history, the Iranian Intermezzo, witnessed the overthrow of Arab dominance in Islam and the rising and enduring dominance of Persian Culture

There is a popular saying in Islam :

> Arabic is the language of Islam’s God, Persian is the language of
> Islam’s sword

This is a common, often unattributed, cultural proverb highlighting the traditional roles of these two languages in Islamic civilisation. The sentiment reflects Arabic as the language of the Quran, Islamic prayer, and faith, with Persian being associated with imperial power, poetry, and military administration. Why was this?

If you look at the expansion of Islam under the Arab conquests that started in the seventh century AD, except Spain and Iran, all other regions are now Arabic speaking and culturally Arab as well.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kE1U!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F78b949de-6560-4d83-9a9d-65f085a14f7c_556x390.heic)

Why did Iran not become Arabised even though it fell very quickly to the Arab Islamic conquests? Why do Iranians still speak Persian and other nations that fell to the Arab conquests speak Arabic? Why did the Turks who were a major power in the expansion of Islam use Persian (and not their own Turkish or Arabic) as the language of high culture in the court of their empires ranging from the Balkans in Europe to Bengal in India? For over a thousand years, to be educated and cultured in the Islamic world meant knowing the language of Persian and not Arabic. In this article, we will trace the reason for this through a Persian epic poem composed in the 10th century by a poet named Ferdowsi which is called the Shahnamah, or the Persian book of kings.

# Introduction to the Shahnamah

Imagine yourself to be living in a turning point in the history of the world known to you. One age is ending and another age is ensuing. You begin to realise that the world you grew up in, its history, its religion, its government systems, its social fabric, and even its very language is fading away, and your grandchildren may never know this world. Things that you feel at home with are gradually fading into becoming obsolete and obscure. What would you do if you found that happening to your entire society and way of life?

That was exactly what the Persians felt in the 9th century when it was more than a century since the Arab Islamic conquest of Iran (Persia) in 651 CE where the Persian Sassanid Empire decisively lost once and for all to the invading Arab armies. What followed was a brutal conquest and a rapid change in the Islamisation and Arabisation of Iran, under the first **Rashidun** caliphate and the later **Umayyad** caliphate (capital : Damascus) and **Abbasid** Caliphate (capital : Bhagdad). Many of the native elite of Iran who were following their ancestral Zoroastrian religion in Iran converted to Islam either under pressure or to maintain their status under the Arab caliphates. Like in all other lands that Arabs conquered, Islam, the Arabic language and culture, gradually began to replace the native languages, religions, and cultures of Iran. This was the world against which the Iranian poet **Hakim Abul Qasim Ferdowsi Tusi** was reacting by his epic poem **Shahmamah**, better known as the **Persian Book of Kings** in English. This epic poem, about 60,000 couplets long, became the most famous work of Persian literature and is widely regarded as the national epic of the worldwide Persian speaking community. Ferdowsi was born into a landed aristocratic family in the city of Tus, in the northeastern part of Iran in the Khorasan, rich in Persian culture. Ferdowsi lived in a time when the Persian language was being infiltrated with Arabic words and Arabic itself threatened to replace Persian as the preferred language of literature. Ferdowsi was undoubtedly a devoted Muslim but first and foremost, he regarded himself as a native Iranian with ties to classical Persian culture and took immense pride in his roots, identity and heritage. This is evident in his epic Shahnamah which does not contain much Arabic loanwords and instead uses native Persian words, even if they had fallen out of common use and became archaic by his time.

The origins of Persian empire go all the way back to Emperor Cyrus, the great, who founded the kingdom (that then expanded into the largest empire till its time) in the 5th century BCE. The cultural origins of Persia go back to a millennia further with its Zoroastrian ancient texts in the **Avestan** language (Ancient Persian) dated as far back as at least 1000 BCE. Tradition has it that Ferdowsi began composing the epic in 977 CE to raise money for his daughter’s dowry at age 36 and an apocryphal story tells us that he was promised at a rate of 1 gold coin for every one of his couplets. The historical context of his times is also important about which we will see in more detail in the next section. When Ferdowsi began writing, the region where he lived was under the control of a native Persian dynasty that still valued Persian culture. But by the time he completed his poem thirty-three years later in 1010 CE, Eastern Iran was under the control of the Ghaznavid Empire that was of Turkish origins and ethnicity, which was gradually expanding its control over Central and West Asia.

The epic Shahnamah tells the story of Iran, from the creation of the world till the fall of the last Sassanid ruler who fell to the Arab conquests. It should be noted that this poem is not strictly historical and fiction mingles freely with facts. The epic does talk about many Iranian kings and heroes who actually lived in history but it is also full of mythical creatures and magic.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!1sie!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F95de265c-6484-41a4-9295-baeac1273794_369x512.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!1sie!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F95de265c-6484-41a4-9295-baeac1273794_369x512.png)

The Shahnamah is an epic length legendary anthology of kings and heroes in Persian history. It is divided into three main sections in chronological order - mythical age, heroic age, historical age. Over these three sections, Ferdowsi traces 50 generations of Persian kings (both good and evil) from the creation of the world to the last Sassanid king. The treatments of the kings and heroes are uneven ; for example, King Kaus who ruled Iran for a hundred and fifty years covers three out of the standard nine volume divisions of the Shahnama! This is also interesting because Kaus is actually a bad king and hence he provides lessons for what not to do. Whereas, some other kings who ruled Iran are just treated within a few lines and many important kings and even entire dynasties in Iranian history are not even mentioned directly which include King Cyrus, the founder of the Persian empire from the Achaemeneid dynasty. In addition, the book spends a lot of time on figures who are not kings but heroes who lived in the time of kings and hence the epic should be more appropriately titled as “The Persian book of Kings and Heroes”. The three sections (mythical, heroic, and historical) cross-reference and weave intricately with each other ; thus creating an epic masterpiece that celebrates Iranian history and culture to serve as a national and nationalistic epic.

But what was the historical context in which Ferdowsi was writing that motivated him and supported him?

# Historical Background : The Iranian Intermezzo

By the mid 9th century, the Arabs began to lose direct control of Iran and the region gradually came under the domination of native ethnically Persian dynasties like the **Saffarids**, **Samanids**, and the **Buyids (**also spelt **Buwayhid)** that hence valued their Persian heritage. This period in Iranian history is called the **Iranian Intermezzo** and lasted from the **9th to the 11th century**. After the 11th century, the **Turks** would begin to emerge as the dominant imperial power in the Islamic world.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!cnWZ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7667f942-992f-4d31-a461-8c8e4d01a7cc_1075x710.png)

Figure : Iran in the 10th century CE

If they had wanted, these ethnically and culturally Iranian Persian speaking kings, could have reverted to Zoroastrianism (which was thriving much better than today in the rural areas of Iran then) and made it as their state religion. It was not like mass conversion was not possible ; if the **Safavid** empire in the 16th century could mass convert Iranians from Sunni Islam to Shia Islam, these Samanid or Buyid dynasties too could’ve converted the Iranians to Zoroastrianism, if they wanted. But, they did not. Instead, they decided to champion a "**Persianate Islam**" and appropriate it into the Persian language and culture.   
  
That is, the native Persian dynasties did push back against Arab dominance, but they chose to fight this **only on the battlefield of culture and language, not religion.** The Zoroastrian Sassanid kings of Persia had used Middle Persian (in the Pahlavi script) as their official language. The new dynasties, particularly the **Samanids, revived the Persian language** and even **resurrected Persian words** that were falling out of use and being increasingly replaced by Arabic ones, but they wrote it in the **Arabic script** (creating Modern Persian or Farsi). The resulting identity was a hybrid: Islamic in faith, but Persian in culture. The Samanids, who claimed descent from the Sasanian general Bahram Chobin, like King **Mansur**, sponsored poets like **Ferdowsi**, who wrote the **Shahnamah** (Book of Kings) that chronicles all the glories of the pre-Islamic kings of Persia.

Figure : Early 9th-century Sasanian-style silver plates from Merv in Iran

The Samanid bureaucracy used the New Persian language and commissioned translations of Pahlavi texts into New Persian. **Abu Mansur Muhammad**, a governor of Tus, had ordered his minister **Abu Mansur Mamari** to invite several local scholars to compile a prose *Shahnamah* ("Book of Kings"), which was completed in 1010. Although it no longer survives, Ferdowsi used it as one of the sources of his epic. Samanid rulers were patrons of such important Persian poets as **Rudaki** and **Daqiqi**, and Ferdowsi followed in the footsteps of these writers. But, Ferdowsi’s epic poem eclipsed all other works that came before him, and thus became the book that preserved pre-Islamic Persian history, myths, and its Zoroastrian ***cultural heritage***. Ferdowsi’s tomb can still be visited in Tus, in modern Iran.

The **Buyid** Dynasty (934–1062) is another great example of this paradox between Perisan nationalism and an Arabic Islam. They were Persians from the **Daylam** region (which had resisted Islam for centuries). But when they took power and even conquered Baghdad and revived the ancient Sassanid title "**Shahanshah**" (King of Kings) and claimed descent from pre-Islamic Sassanid kings for their legitimacy. But yet, **they remained Shi'a Muslims**. They used their Persian heritage for prestige but used Shi'a Islam to challenge the authority of the Sunni Caliphs dominating in Arab Islam, thus fighting against Arab dominance and for Iranian nationalism without leaving the fold of Islam.

The tension between following an Arab religion (Islam) while also wanting to be uniquely Persian by nation, ethnicity, language, and culture, seems to **not have had created any urge for the Iranian elite to revert to Zoroastrianism** (which was much closer to them in time) during the Persian Intermezzo. This tension is best seen in the epic poem of Shahnameh itself.

  
**Ferdowsi** in his Persian epic poem **Shahnameh**, views the Arab conquest of Persia as a catastrophe and vehemently hates the Arabs and portrays them as barbarians and uses slurs against them like **lizard eaters**. I will give some verses from the end of the epic where he describes the Arab conquest of Persia.   

"*From drinking camel's milk and eating lizards, it has come to this for the Arabs, that they aspire to the Kayanian (Persian) crown? Shame on you, O spinning wheel \[of fate\], shame!*"   
  
"***The artless (*****uncultured)** ***slave becomes the King; Lineage and greatness no longer serve any purpose.... Such will become this land of the Iranians, that the suspicious body will not fear evil***". (Implying a collapse of Iranian lineage and identity, and a moral breakdown where honor no longer matters).  
  
"\[They have\] *No throne, no crown, no golden shoes, No jewels, no diadem, no banner overhead... From* \[eating\] *dry barley bread and wearing rough wool, their* \[Arabs'\] *work has reached such a fervor* \[that they conquer us\]."  
  
"*A naked commander, a naked army, they cover the sun and moon with* \[the dust of their march\]. *No equipment, no horses, no armor, no helmets, Yet from the dark plain, a harvest \[of death\] arrives*".  
  
But despite his disdain for Arabs and the Arab conquest, Ferdowsi **praises Islam, Muhammad, and Ali,** and is seen to be a **devout Shia Muslim**. For e.g., he says. :  
  
"*If you have your eyes set on the Other World (Paradise), Take your place \[on the ship\] next to the Prophet and Ali. ... This is how it is, and this is my religion and my path*".  
  
"*I am the slave of the House of the Prophet, I am the praiser of the dust at the feet of the Vicegerent (Ali)*".   
  
"***I was born on this \[faith\], and on this I shall pass away, Know this: I am the dust at the foot of Heydar*** (a title for Ali, implying the Prophet's lineage)".   
  
"***Seek the way through the words of your Prophet, And wash your heart of darkness with this water*****"**.  
  
And he also views Zoroastrianism, the religion of the old kings that he is praising in his poem, as a precursor to Islam and is highly sympathetic towards it. For example, he says in a verse :  
  
*"**Do not say that they were Fire-Worshippers! They were worshippers of the Holy God (Yazdan). In that time, the bright fire was \[their direction of prayer\], just as the stone Mihrab (the Kaaba) is for the Arabs**" .*   
  
But while he respects the old Zoroastrian kings, he also accepts that their time had passed. He views the shift from Zoroastrianism to Islam as a shift in "Divine Fortune" (**Farr**) and ordained by Fate (**bakht**). He says that God took the fortune from the Persians and gave it to the Arabs; and hence to fight it (Islam) is to fight God. Here are some verses from the Shahnameh in this regard.   
  
*"Such has become the command of the revolving wheel \[of Heaven\], That the House of Sassan* (the last Zoroastrian dynasty of Iran) *shall vanish ...*  
  
*Alas for that head, that crown, that throne, and that royal seat. Alas for that greatness, and that **Farr** (Divine Glory) and **Bakht** (Fortune).*  
  
*Hope for no profit/result from your struggle (**koshish**), For **Ahriman** (disaster/evil) has come to the White Throne*".  
  
Even **Yazdegerd III,** the last Zoroastrian king who is defeated in the Arab conquest of Persia, eventually is portrayed by Ferdowsi as resigning himself to this new reality. In his final moments, fleeing to Khorasan, he frames his defeat as a decree from the "World Creator" (**Jahan Afarin**). Ferdowsi writes :   
  
*"Such was the judgment of the World Creator, that I should weep bitterly upon this plain of battle."*

# The Shu’ubiyya Movement

Why did the Persians adapt Islam rather than rejecting it? And how could a man like Ferdowsi write verses cursing the Arabs while praising Mohamed and Ali? For a more detailed answer, refer [here](https://x.com/Schandillia/status/2016855122433970580?s=20), to an article by Amit Schandillia but I am paraphrasing one important part of the answer written by him which lies in a movement known as the ***Shu’ubiyya***.

The name of the movement is derived from the use of the Arabic word *šuʿūb* for “nations” or “peoples” in the Quran:

> يَا أَيُّهَا النَّاسُ إِنَّا خَلَقْنَاكُم مِّن ذَكَرٍ وَأُنثَى وَجَعَلْنَاكُمْ **شُعُوباً** وَ**قَبَائِلَ**
> لِتَعَارَفُوا إِنَّ أَكْرَمَكُمْ عِندَ اللَّهِ أَتْقَاكُمْ إِنَّ اللَّهَ عَلِيمٌ خَبِيرٌ‎  
>  “*O mankind! Indeed, We have created you from male and female and
> made you **nations** and **tribes** that you may know one another.
> Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most
> righteous of you. Indeed, Allah is Knowing and Acquainted.*”
>
> — Quran 49:13, (*Sahih International*)

As Islam expanded from Arabs to non-Arabs, it prompted a literary and political reaction by non-Arab Muslims, primarily and successfully championed by the Persians, against the privileged status of Arabs within the Islamic empire. The Arabs had been treating Islamic religion as their tribal inheritance till then under the **Rashidun** and the later **Umayyad** Caliphate. That is, Islam was initially associated with the ethnic and cultural identity of the Arabs and being a Muslim required being a member of an Arab tribe or being formally associated and adopting the client status as *mawali* under one of the Arab tribes. That is, they believed that to be a Muslim, one had to be an Arab or a client subject (*mawali)* of an Arab tribe, with hence the Arab always being in the higher position of the hierarchy. Islamic scholars and Quranic exegetes of the Shu’ubiyya movement interpreted this Quranic verse 49:13 as a declaration of the fundamental equality of all human beings against ethnic, racial, social, cultural, or linguistic differences, with only faith in God serving as a valid measure of a person's value.

The Persians hence rejected this ethnically privileged gatekeeping of Islam by the Arab tribes. They argued that Islam was a universal faith that superseded Arab tribalism. Through the *Shu’ubiyya* movement, they launched a counter-offensive which was both intellectual and military. Instead of rejecting the message, they captured it. The Persian elites realised a powerful distinction that would define their history. They separated the universal theology of Islam from the specific culture of the Arabs.

- **The Message vs. The Messenger:** The Persians argued that while the
  Quranic revelation and the religion of Islam was divinely inspired and
  the expansion of Islam was divinely ordained, the Arab tribes through
  which the message was received and spread, were not perfect and in
  fact flawed.

- **Intellectual Superiority:** The Persians mastered Arabic grammar,
  Islamic jurisprudence, and theology during the Arab rule, better than
  the Arabs themselves and hence by doing so, they could take rightful
  ownership of the religion since they were now as qualified in the new
  religion as the Arabs were.

- **Cultural Synthesis:** They claimed and ensured that while the
  revelation of Islam was in the Arabic language to an Arab prophet, the
  political vessel that would house it need not be Arabic, and ensured
  that the cultural and linguistic vessel of the civilisation that would
  sustain it from now on, would be Persian.

This explains the “Ferdowsi Paradox.” He did not see Islam as the property of the “lizard-eaters” (Arabs) whom he despised. In his view, the Arabs were merely the vessels for a divine truth that they themselves were too uncultured to fully comprehend and undeserved to claim privilege over it. To Ferdowsi, the ***Ahl al-Bayt*** (the family of the Prophet) represented a spiritual nobility. This resonated with the Persian concept of royal lineage. He could hate the Arab general who burned the city of Ctesiphon. But he could simultaneously revere Ali, the Prophet’s cousin. This was not submission but digestion.

The Persians stripped the faith of its “Arab-ness” and clothed it in Sassanid robes. They replaced the Arab tribal structure with Sassanid-style bureaucracy. They replaced simple desert poetry with complex Persian mysticism. They built what is called the 'Islamic Golden Age' but more appopriately should have been the Persian Golden Age —figures like Avicenna (Ibn Sina), Al-Khwarizmi, and Al-Razi were Persians who elevated the religion intellectually.

By the time the Intermezzo ended, Islam was no longer just an Arab religion. It had become a Persianate civilization. The Persians had not failed to revert but had simply realised that conquering the religion was more effective than fighting it. This process ensured that whoever ruled the Islamic world from now on, would be Persianate in culture and language. Arabic would remain the language of scripture but Persian became the *lingua franca* of the Islamic world. From the Balkans to Bengal, to be a cultured Muslim meant to know Persian, not Arabic. The Caliphate in Baghdad went from an Arab tribal chieftaincy into a Sassanid-style despotism. When Islam finally entered places like Turkey and India, it was Persian in modes of thinking while Arabic in its message. The Ottomans and the Mughals were Turks by blood but they were Persians by culture.

The rejection of Arab dominance by no means implied that the Persians rejected Islam. While they admired Zoroastrianism as a historical source of their roots and saw it as a precursor to Islam, they did not want to revive it as a thriving state religion and patronised Persian heritage and up keeping within the fold of the Islamic faith. Many of the Zoroastrians in Iran who still refused to convert to the new faith gradually faced mounting pressures because of which many of them migrated to the state of Gujarat in India to keep their faith in peace, where they came to be known as the **Parsis**.

# Legacy of the Shahnamah

The stories in the Shahnamah were not only read but chanted and told loud by oral bards in Persia in all kinds of occasions. The Shahnamah is an epic that narrates the history of a specific kind of people but also about the general themes of heroism, valour, and honour. In this epic, Ferdowsi sought to capture the rich culture that he knew in a form that would endure forever. He writes in the epic :

> The houses that are the dwelling of today will sink beneath shower and
> sunshine to decay. But storm and rain shall never mar the palace that
> I have built with poetry.

Ferdowsi sought to capture the remnants of a golden age and using that to inspire successive generation of Persians. It is tremendously influential in the life of everyday Persians ; parents name their children after characters from this epic ; grandparents narrate and quote stories from these epics to their grandchildren. It is like the Mahabharata and Ramayana in Indian-Hindu culture.

**Azar Nafisi**, in an introduction to a translation of the epic (to read the epic in that translation, buy the translation from Penguin [here](https://www.amazon.in/Shahnameh-Persian-Kings-Penguin-Classics-ebook/dp/B0191WS04U/ref=tmm_kin_swatch_0)) writes how her father raised her with the stories in the epic and that he claimed that Persian culture had no homeland in any geographical space but in the epic Shahnamah. Whenever the Persians were attacked or displaced, the poets have acted as the custodians of their history, language, and culture. She says that :

> We have no home. The Shahnamah is our home. Our identity.

She also writes that when their land was taken away and the during the turmoil of the Iran-Iraq war, the Shahnamah served as their home, refuge, and identity.

After the *Shahnamah*, a number of other works similar in nature surfaced over the centuries within the cultural sphere of the Persian language. Without exception, all such works were based in style and method on the *Shahnamah*, although none of them could quite achieve the same degree of fame and popularity.

Some experts believe the main reason the Modern Persian language today is more or less the same language as that of Ferdowsi’s time over 1000 years ago is due to the very existence of works like the *Shahnamah*, which have had lasting and profound cultural and linguistic influence, and served as a force of linguistic stability of the Persian language despite the political turmoils. In other words, the *Shahnamah* itself has become one of the main pillars of the modern Persian language. Studying Ferdowsi’s masterpiece also became a requirement for achieving mastery of the Persian language by subsequent Persian poets, as evidenced by numerous references to the *Shahnamah* in their works.

Ferdowsi purposefully avoided Arabic vocabulary in *Shahnamah* despite the fact that a lot of Arabic loanwords and been in common use in the colloquial Persian of his own day and has always been so and even now continues to be so today. Despite that, the overwhelming majority of *Shahnamah’s* vocabulary used in it (about 91%) remains distinctly Persian in origin. Because of this, while colloquial spoken Persian still contained a lot of Arabic loanwords, the native Persian words used in works like Shahnamah (and others inspired from it) are still in usage and are known by Persian speakers because of reading this epic and referring to it frequently in works and speeches of high culture throughout the Islamic Persianate world.

The *Shahnamah* has 62 stories, 990 chapters, and some 50,000 rhyming couplets, making it more than three times the length of Homer’s *Iliad* and more than twelve times the length of the German epic ***Nibelungenlied***. According to Ferdowsi himself, the final edition of the ***Shahnamah*** contained some sixty thousand distichs. But this is a round figure; most of the relatively reliable manuscripts have preserved a little over fifty thousand distichs. Nizami Aruzi reports that the final edition of the *Shahnamah* sent to the court of Sultan Mahmud of Ghazni was prepared in seven volumes.

The Turks who constituted the dominant political force in Islam after the Persian intermezzo, still used Persian and patronised Persian as the language of their high culture. According to the Turkish historian Mehmet Fuat Köprülü:

> Indeed, despite all claims to the contrary, there is no question that
> Persian influence was paramount among the Seljuks of Anatolia. This is
> clearly revealed by the fact that the sultans who ascended the throne
> after Ghiyath al-Din Kai-Khusraw Iassumed titles taken from ancient
> Persian mythology, like Kai Khosrow, Kay Kāvus, and Kai Kobad; and
> that Ala’ al-Din Kai-Qubad I had some passages from the Shahname
> inscribed on the walls of Konya and Sivas. When we take into
> consideration domestic life in the Konya courts and the sincerity of
> the favor and attachment of the rulers to Persian poets and Persian
> literature, then this fact \[i.e., the importance of Persian
> influence\] is undeniable.

**Shah Ismail I** (d.1524), the founder of the **Safavid dynasty of Iran** (contemporaneous with the Mughal dynasty in India) was also deeply influenced by the Persian literary tradition, particularly by the *Shahnamah*, which probably explains the fact that he named all of his sons after *Shahnamah* characters. Even today, many Persian speakers name their kids after beloved Shahnamah characters like most Indians name their kids after beloved Hindu epic characters. Dickson and Welch suggest that Ismail’s *Shāhnāma-i Shāhī* was intended as a present to the young **Tahmasp**. After defeating Muhammad Shaybani’s Uzbeks, Ismail asked **Hatefi**, a famous poet from Jam (Khorasan), to write a Shahnamah-like epic about his victories and his newly established dynasty. Although the epic was left unfinished, it was an example of *mathnawis* in the heroic style of the Shahnamah written later on for the Safavid kings.

The *Shahnamah*‘s influence has extended beyond the Persian sphere. Professor Victoria Arakelova of Yerevan University states:

> During the ten centuries passed after Firdausi composed his monumental
> work, heroic legends and stories of Shahnameh have remained the main
> source of the storytelling for the peoples of this region: Persians,
> Kurds, Gurans, Talishis, Armenians, Georgians, North Caucasian
> peoples, etc.

Ferdowsi concludes the Shahnamah by writing these words that have turned true :

> ***I’ve reached the end of this great history  
> And all the land will fill with talk of me:  
> I shall not die, these seeds I’ve sown will save  
> My name and reputation from the grave,  
> And men of sense and wisdom will proclaim  
> When I have gone, my praises and my fame.***

**Goethe** was inspired by Persian literature, which moved him to write his *West-Eastern Divan*. Goethe wrote:

> *When we turn our attention to a peaceful, civilized people, the
> Persians, we must—since it was actually their poetry that inspired
> this work—go back to the earliest period to be able to understand more
> recent times. It will always seem strange to the historians that no
> matter how many times a country has been conquered, subjugated and
> even destroyed by enemies, there is always a certain national core
> preserved in its character, and before you know it, there re-emerges a
> long-familiar native phenomenon. In this sense, it would be pleasant
> to learn about the most ancient Persians and quickly follow them up to
> the present day at an all the more free and steady pace.*

Many other poets, including the famous Persian Sufi mystical poets Hafez, Rumi and others have used the imagery of Shahnamah heroes in their poetry.
