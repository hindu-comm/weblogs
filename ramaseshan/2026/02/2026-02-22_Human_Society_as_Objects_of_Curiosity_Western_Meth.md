Recall from the previous part what exactly is meant by a reductionist approach to religion. The reductionist approach assumes that religion is not a primary reality in itself, but a by-product of more fundamental “real” forces. Like a chemist breaking down and explaining all the properties of water arising due to a covalent bond between hydrogen and oxygen atoms, the reductionist breaks down the sacred into the secular. One should never talk about religion in religious terms, according to reductionists, but only in terms of economic terms, sociological terms, anthropological terms, or psychological terms, depending on what the reductionist approach reduces religion to.

- **The Reductionist Logic:** Religion is “nothing but” a manifestation
  or an example of **X** (where **X** is economics, psychology,
  sociology, or biology, or now culture through anthropology).

- **The Reductionist Goal :** To explain the cause of religious
  behaviour. For a reductionist, the “truth” of a religion is found in
  the functional utility it provides, or the mental error it represents.

- **Example:** “*You aren’t actually worshiping a deity; you are
  fulfilling a subconscious need for a father figure* (Freud) *or
  reinforcing your community’s social bonds* (Durkheim)”.

The opposite approach in religious studies is called the **phenomenological approach** which insists that religion is an irreducible, unique phenomenon that must be studied on its own terms. It demands that we set aside (or “bracket”) our judgments about whether a religion is true or false to understand how it is understood and experienced by the believer.

- **The Phenomenological Logic:** Religion is a “***sui generis***”
  (Latin for : its own type of) reality. The “sacred” cannot be
  explained away by other sciences without losing its essence.

- **The Phenomenological Goal:** To describe the meaning and structure
  of religious experience, a phenomenologist instead asks questions
  like, “What is it like to encounter the sacred?” rather than “What
  caused this delusion?”

- **Example:** “*We will study the ritual of prayer not as anxiety
  relief, but as a structural encounter between the human and the
  ‘wholly other’* (Otto), *respecting the internal logic of the
  practitioner*”.

Anthropology is also a reductionist approach as well and sadly, the Indian academia has not caught up with phenomenological approaches and instead has latched on to these modern avatars of reductionist approaches. The British colonial administration’s reductionist approach to understanding Indian religion still dominates Indian academia and policy making. Hence, our institutions continue to treat living Indian Hindu traditions as specimens to be dissected rather than as profound systems of meaning to be understood. In this article, we will see important reductionist approaches to religious studies that developed in the West from the end of the 19th century to the end of the 20th century that developed under the garb of anthropology. As usual, we will see only a couple of representatives from each important school of anthropology in detail. Remember, anthropology is a vast discipline by itself and hence what I cover is just a small part of the subject, even if only in its intersection with religion. But, after reading what I cover, you should be able to relate to any other school you might encounter in your further studies.

# Origins of Anthropology

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

In the 19th century, most people in the West believed in the following :

- The world was six thousand years old

- Humanity was put on the earth readymade by God during creation

- Humans were born in an original state of grace and perfection but had
  later fallen (garden of Eden story)

So, as travellers, who were exploring the world, began to bring to Europe, stories of Stone Age tribes living inside tents in forests, the natural assumption was that this was a part of the fall from grace. The state of savagery that these primitive tribes were found in, they concluded, was apparently due to the fall from Eden. This perspective is technically known as "**degenerationism**" which is the belief that "savages" were simply civilised people who had lost their way and regressed due to sin.

But, new sciences (like geology and evolutionary biology) began to develop that showed that the earth was very old (millions of years) and archaeologists were unearthing remains of ancient human beings who lived thousands of years ago without any sign of the so called original perfection. There was found to be no anatomical difference between the remains of ancient savage tribes and modern savage tribes. This led to the development of new ways of thinking about the history, progress, and nature of humanity. Questions like these were beginning to be asked :

- Were the primitive tribes same as European industrialised men?

- If yes, how does one account for the differences?

- How can these modern savage tribes be more like the ancient savage
  tribes (unearthed from archaeology) but very unlike modern
  industrialised Europeans?

Also, the sheer variety of data gathered based on information coming from missionaries, travellers, and other officials, who studied societies in other continents, exhibited both deep differences and also provocative patterns. This called for a thorough systemisation and analysis and from all these, the discipline was called anthropology (the study of man) was born. The goal of anthropology initially was to discover something about human nature and human development, in general. Hence, what became central was the notion of the "**comparative method**" which assumed that different cultures around the globe represented different "stages" of a single, universal trajectory of human historical evolution. This school of thought is often called **"unilineal classical social evolutionism**" as it posited that every culture must follow the exact same line of progress from 'savagery' to 'barbarism' and finally to the most advanced stage of civilisation, which was taken to be the state achieved by the modern Western society. What these first anthropologists sought were then the following :

1.  Determining this universal trajectory of human social development by
    comparing various societies

2.  Identifying and characterising the various stages and processes in
    this evolution

3.  Mapping and placing each modern and ancient society at these stages
    and processes

4.  Identifying the causal agents that enable the change from one phase
    to another in this evolution

# The Ladder of Savagery : The Victorian Dissection of Human Societies

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

### Tylor : Religion as Survival

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

The first person who held the chair of anthropology at a university was **Edward B Tylor** at Oxford and in 1871, published a work called “***Primitive Culture***” most of which was taken up with a theory of religion based on the evidence coming in from all over the world. In his book, Tylor provided the first scientific definition of "Culture," describing it as "***that complex whole which includes knowledge, belief, art, law, morals, custom, and any other capabilities and habits acquired by man as a member of society*****"**. Tylor believed that the fundamental task of the human mind was a cognitive one as humans had to engage with the world for survival. The history of humanity from the primitive tribes of the ancient times to the modern industrialised European man was hence, according to Tylor, enabled by the growth of knowledge. He also thought that the advances that took place among ancient pre-historic man was a result of single individual geniuses who happened to discover or invent something new that made a giant leap in the development of humanity ; like the discovery of fire or invention of wheel. Tylor referred to these pre-historic geniuses by the term “**savage philosopher**”. This savage philosopher was a heroic old man who observed the world intensely and wondering deeply how to come to grips with it and understand what is going on there. Tylor believed that many of the insights of these ancient geniuses were religious in nature.

Tylor’s theory of the origins of religion lay in what is called as “**animism**”, which he regarded as the most primitive stage of human society after savagery. He argued that animism was also first developed by a savage philosopher in each society sometime in the distant past. Animism was a worldview of nature that posited that spirits dwelt within both living beings and inanimate objects. But, why would such a notion come about? According to Tylor, the rationale for animism went as something like this :

1.  The early man observed people dying and wondered what exactly
    happened when a living man died and his body rotted away out of
    existence.

2.  But dead people could appear in the dreams of living persons. It was
    then reasonable for the early man to assume that the dead person who
    appeared in his dream was same as the dead person when he was alive.

3.  This means that there was something (let’s call it “**spirit**”)
    that animated the person’s body when he was alive and left his body
    when he died and it is this spirit of his that appeared in dreams.

4.  But people appeared in other people’s dreams even if they were
    alive. So, this means that this spirit was in some sense, detachable
    from the person’s body even before death. So, these spirits must be
    self sufficient that can exist on their own.

5.  Seeing that spirits can exist outside of physical bodies, then were
    there spirits that exist out there, that never inhabited any bodies,
    and were in some sense, always free floating?

6.  Spirits are hence there everywhere and hence can exist in
    hierarchies too. Apart from spirits of individual deer, there could
    be spirit of the species of deer as a whole with whom men could
    communicate with for guaranteeing a good hunt.

7.  Careless or offensive actions towards the things that the spirits
    represented could offend them in the same way humans get offended
    when acted inappropriately towards things they possess or inhabit.
    So, this gave rise to “taboos”.

Tylor says basically that religion is belief in spiritual beings and he bases his entire theory of religion upon this notion of spirit animism that was described above. Tylor says basically that religion is "belief in spiritual beings" **(**this is his famous **"minimum definition of religion")** and he bases his entire theory upon this notion of spirit animism as the origin of everything religious. Animism, for instance, gives rise to mythology because one could name these spirits and tell stories about them in the same way he could do for fellow human beings. One can look at the sun rising and make up stories of Apollo riding his chariot or observe a lighting flash and make up stories of Zeus striking his thunderbolt. Animism also gives rise to rituals and prayers that involve communicating with these spirits and try propitiating them to get some favour done in the same way one could give gifts to fellow human beings and ask favours from them. From animism, the society moves through various advanced stages like polytheism, monotheism, and then finally western modernism.

Another important notion in Tylor’s notion of religion is his **theory of survivals** : this refers to ideas or customs that were once created to satisfy a need or solve a problem or accomplish something but it still survives out of habit even though the original problem or need or motivation for doing that is gone. An example would be the sport of archery which was useful to ancient peoples to genuinely fight off enemies and feed your tribe but now survives as a mere sport even though our weapons and methods of warfare have improved and no longer require archery to fight enemies. Another example is the saying "*God bless you*" after a sneeze in the English society of his day; Tylor argued this was a survival of the animistic fear that a sneeze might allow the soul to escape the body.

In Tylor’s view, religion as a whole is a survival in the modern era. It means that religion made sense and was meaningful in primitive times to explain the world and inform about it but now still survives as a habit even though science has taken over the function for explaining how the world works.

> Tylor hence believed that the study of religion as survival allowed
> the anthropologist to act as a “***historical detective***” who could
> reconstruct the lost stages of human history from the “vestiges” left
> behind in modern folklore and rituals that are its survivals.

But, while Tylor would have been okay with the continuation of the sport of archery as survival in modern times, he thought that religion was something that is better eliminated in modern society following which they would get better.

One of the most important things that Tylor did was to turn the traditional picture of human evolution as a fall or degeneration from grace and perfection to savagery upside down because of sin. Tylor demonstrated from accumulating archaeological evidences that this garden of Eden moment was never being found and that anatomically, all primitive humans are indeed distinguishable from modern humans. So, he instead proposed that the original starting state of humanity itself is low, primitive, and savage level, out of which some (modern Westerners) could gradually climb out of to a very high level due to intelligence, experimentation, and persistence. He compared this to the growth and development of a single human individual who starts in a very ignorant state as a child, making up stories, talking to his dolls, believing in what everyone tells them, but then gradually matures to become more realistic and develops the capacity to act more effectively in the world.

Tylor proposed that that is exactly how the human race has developed as a whole but not all human societies have developed at the same pace. This is why we see modern industrial societies on one hand and modern savage tribes on the other hand at the same time. He said that modern savages are not anatomically different from modern civilised people but they are at a lower stage of growth and development and are yet to catch up. Tylor posits that basic human nature is the same everywhere and all times because this means that in order to study the pre-historic savage past, one can instead study modern savage tribes. Tylor has defined the notion of what is technically called as the **"psychic unity of mankind"** which is the idea that human nature is the same and it develops along the same processes everywhere. This means that in order to study pre-historic savage tribes, one can instead study modern savage tribes, who provide a "time travel portal" as they are in the same stage of development as their pre-historic counterparts of modern developed societies.

This is very important because if we assume that pre-historic tribes have a radically different human than modern humans, then there is no point in studying modern savage tribes to infer about pre-modern savage tribes. Thus, the pre-historic societies of savage tribes and the modern societies of savage tribes are fundamentally the same and are related to modern civilised societies in the same way a grown up man is related to his own childhood. Thus, since all human societies evolved from savagery in the same way, studying modern savage tribes can shed light on the origins of currently advanced societies and help in determining how modern technologically superior societies looked like in the past.

Also to be noted, because Tylor believed minds were the same everywhere (psychic unity of mankind), he argued that a person in London and a person in a remote forest would arrive at the same (mistaken) animistic conclusions if given the same (primitive) data. So, not only all human societies go through the same stages of social and cultural evolution but also same kind of processes were at play to enable the change from one stage to another.

### Frazer and the Golden Bough

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Next, we proceed to discuss another important thinker **James George Frazer** whose famous work “***The Golden Bough***” (published in 12 volumes in its final edition) who expanded and refined Tylor’s theories. Frazer was a classicist and studied myths deeply. Like Tylor, Frazer was an “*armchair anthropologist*”, meaning he almost never traveled to see the cultures he described, relying instead on the reports sent back by colonial administrators, travellers, and missionaries.

Like Freud was obsessed with the Oedipus myth, Frazer was obsessed with one particular Roman myth recollected by an Ancient Greek traveller **Pausanias** about a strange custom about a golden bough and begins his magnum opus work by recounting that story and also naming his book after it. There was a peculiar priestly institution at the woods of **Nemi** in ancient Rome. There was a priest who went by the title “the king of the wood” who had earned that title by having a sword in hand and seeking the tree upon which there grew a golden bough. He plucked the golden branch and with the branch in one hand and sword in another, found the previous king of the wood and killed him after engaging with him in a single combat. After killing him, he also became the male consort to the goddess Diana, the queen of the woods and through their union, the fertility of nature was renewed.

Frazer sees this myth to be anachronistic because Roman civilisation by the time of Pausanias had went beyond such barbaric practices and rites and hence he postulated that Pausanias was recording some survival ritual (survival in a Tylorian sense) and hence he thought that by looking at this myth, he could get a glimpse of a much deeper primitive past. After this, he proceeds to examine all myths of all cultures that he can get his hands on, and collect such parallel stories resembling the golden bough to capture this stage of human development in as many ancient and modern primitive societies as possible. For example, he finds myths from Northern Europe which involve trees having a golden bough which is actually based on the actual mistletoe tree which indeed turns golden in the depths of winter. He collects myths where people believe that mistletoe which grows on oak trees remains alive even as the oak tree loses its leaves and goes dormant which leads them to infer that there is a principle of life and fertility in the mistletoe and that a soul from another being can be put inside the mistletoe for preservation. He finds the legend of the Norse god **Baldur** who puts his soul in the mistletoe who hence cannot be killed except when somebody plucks the mistletoe, makes an arrow of it, and shoots him. Like this, he also finds parallels to other episodes in the golden bough myth like analogs of the figure of “the king of the woods” and so on. The assumption that Frazer shares with Tylor is that the basis for all myths and rituals have a fundamentally cognitive (to explain the natural world) and technological application (to manipulate the natural world for one’s own benefit) and hence completely ignores the social dimension of religion (which had been a British tradition in religious studies for a long time). For Frazer, magic was “bastard science” by which he elaborated that it was a logical but mistaken attempt to control the laws of nature. He identified two laws of magic:

1.  **The Law of Similarity / Sympathetic Magic** : Like produces like
    (e.g., sticking pins in a doll to hurt a real person).

2.  **The Law of Contact / Contagious Magic** : Things once in contact
    continue to affect each other (e.g., using a person’s hair to cast a
    spell on them).

This is perhaps Frazer's most famous and controversial contribution. What is the earliest form of religion, according to him? He argued that many (if not all) of the most ancient among myths (e.g. Adonis, Attis, Osiris, and even the "King of the Wood") are centred on a "Dying and Rising God" who represents the annual cycle of vegetation. The god must die in the winter (harvest) so that he can be reborn in the spring (planting). Frazer concluded that the earliest forms of many religions were, at their core, fertility cults designed to ensure a bountiful harvest through the ritualistic sacrifice of a "sacred king" who represented these deities. Frazer thus reduced the ancient origins of most profound religious mysteries to a mere agricultural calendar, suggesting that humans invented these "High Gods" simply to ensure their crops would grow**.** Applying this notion of magic to winter solstice myths and rituals, he finds fire rituals from winter solstice to be motivated from keeping the sun into sinking and stimulate it to grow again after the winter solstice. By reducing the sacred in these myths and rituals of the winter solstice to the “vegetation spirit,” Frazer implied that the origins of even the highest religions, including Christianity, were rooted in the simple desire of primitive farmers to ensure their crops would grow.

Like this, he identifies other parallel elements common to all pre-civilised human societies by studying all their ancient myths and myths of modern living savage tribes. Another important examples of this are now explained. A huge portion of Frazer’s work is dedicated to what he thought of was the concept of what is technically called the “**scapegoat”**. Frazer observed that in many societies, there is a periodic ritual to "transfer" the collective sins, diseases, and bad luck of the community onto a physical object or a living being (the scapegoat), which is then driven out or killed. He argued that this was a purely "technological" use of magic which means it was a way of "cleaning" the society of its invisible spiritual filth so the new year could start fresh and prosperous. The word “scapegoat” comes from the Jewish tradition. Jews used to do rituals annually that transferred the sins of their community to a goat or lamb and then used to sacrifice that animal (that now carries the sins of their community) as an atonement for the sins of their entire community. This used to happen every year at Yomkippur (the holiest day in the Jewish calendar) in the temple at Jerusalem. This Jewish ritual well-documented and is called the Scapegoat (Hebrew: ʿăzāzēl) ceremony and took place annually on the holiest day of the Jewish calendar Yom Kippur (the Day of Atonement) when the Temple in Jerusalem stood (i.e., until 70 CE). The procedure for this ritual is described explicitly in the Bible in Leviticus 16. Two male goats were selected. One was sacrificed as a sin offering to God (its blood was brought into the Holy of Holies). The High Priest laid hands on the second goat, confessed “all the iniquities and transgressions of the Israelites, whatever their sins,” thereby symbolically transferring the sins of the entire people onto the goat. This “scapegoat” was then led into the wilderness “for Azazel” (released, or in later practice, pushed off a cliff, carrying the sins away). This was an annual communal atonement ritual in Temple Judaism. Christianity too inherits this same theology of temple Judaism but claims Jesus (literally referred to as the lamb of God) dying as a sacrifice for atonement of sins. Early Christianity explicitly connects Jesus’ death to this theology and to the entire temple sacrificial system. Christian texts repeatedly present Jesus as fulfilling and replacing the Temple sacrifices, including this Yom Kippur imagery. Jesus is literally called “the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world” (John 1: 29, 36), thus directly echoing both Passover and the sin-removing function of the scapegoat. The Letter to the Hebrews in the New Testament (written ~60–90 CE) is the most explicit. Chapter 9–10 compares Jesus’ death to the Yom Kippur ritual in detail. Jesus is both the High Priest and the victim. His blood is brought (metaphorically) into the heavenly Holy of Holies “once for all” (Heb 9:11–14, 9:24–28, 10:12). The only difference is that the Yom Kippur goats and bulls had to be offered and renewed “year after year,” but Christ’s single sacrifice justifies believers with God “for all time” (Heb 10:1–14). Paul also uses this atonement type sacrificial language: Christ as “our Passover lamb” (1 Cor 5:7) and as a “sin offering” in Romans 8:3, using the Greek term “peri amartias” that literally translates the Hebrew “ḥaṭṭā’t” which is the very term for the Yom Kippur sin offering). Christian theology makes this death of Jesus as the final, perfect, and unrepeatable fulfilment that makes the atoning animal sacrifice at the Temple obsolete. Frazer’s inclusion of these Christian parallels was deeply shocking to the Victorian public, as it suggested that the deep soteriology of the crucifixion of Jesus was simply a sophisticated version of a "savage" scapegoat ritual.

Frazer (like Tylor) believes that all human societies start from savagery and go through various stages of growth that can be identified and described. The three broad stages of human development were :

1.  **The Stage of Magic** (rituals for practical purposes) : In this
    stage, humans believe they can directly control nature through
    spells and magic.

2.  **The Stage of Religion** (the world of deities and spirits) :
    Humans realise that magic doesn't work always (or most of the time)
    and instead try to propitiate higher spirits/gods to do the work for
    them.

3.  **The Stage of Science** (a return to magic but consists of rituals
    based on evidences that actually work!) : Humans return to the
    "logic" of magic (that there are fixed laws of nature) but base it
    on evidence and experimentation that actually work.

Frazer himself admitted that these three stages are not clean and one can find the other two present even in the stage where the third one dominates. There are traces of science and religion in societies that practise primitive magic and there are also traces of religion and magic in modern European scientific societies.

### Victorian Anthropology in Indian Context

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Tylor and Frazer’s theories had a massive impact on how the British administration and Western scholars viewed Indian society, particularly the diverse traditions outside of the Sanskritic mainstream.

**The Invention of “Animism” as a Census Category** : In the late 19th and early 20th-century British Censuses of India (led by officials like **Herbert Risley**), “animism” was used as a formal category to classify millions of people, particularly the tribal populations. Based on Tylor’s theory, British officials believed that these groups were stuck in the most primitive stage of human development. By labeling tribal traditions as “animism” rather than “Hinduism,” the British created a sharp and artificial divide between forest-dwelling communities and the rest of Indian society, which was foreign to the Indian society itself. This classification is documented in the *Census of India, 1901, Volume 1, Report*, where Risley argued that "animism" was a stage of "crude spirit-worship" distinct from the more "evolved" religion of the Hindus. This classification ignored the deep and organic overlaps and connections between Sanskritic Hinduism, folk Hinduism of the villages, and tribal practices, and instead framed the tribals as “living fossils” of a pre-historic past, rather than active participants in a living pan-Indian culture.

**Magic vs Religion in Village India** : Frazer’s distinction between “magic” and “religion” was used to condescend Indian village rituals. Practices like **bhuta aradhana** (spirit worship) or the use of **kavaca** (amulets) were reduced to “primitive magic” or “bastard science.” Western scholars argued that while the “high” philosophy of the Upanishads might count as “religion,” the daily practices of the masses were merely “magic” ; means it is just a failed attempt to manipulate nature. This caused immense harm by delegitimizing local traditions as “irrational”, “superstitious” or “fraudulent,” ignoring their profoundness and their connections and integration with the mainstream philosophies of Hinduism. This perspective is exemplified in the work of colonial ethnographers like **William Crooke** in *The Popular Religion and Folk-lore of Northern India* (1896), where village practices are cataloged as "survivals" of the magical stage.

**The “Survival” Bias in Indology** : Tylor’s “theory of survivals” was used to frame much of Hindu practice as an outdated habit. When Western scholars saw modern Hindus performing Vedic fire rituals or temple ceremonies, they viewed them through the Tylorian lens: as “survivals” of a primitive age that should have ended with the arrival of science. This led to a “museum mentality” in Indology, where Indian culture was studied as a collection of ancient remnants rather than a dynamic, evolving civilisation. It led to the stereotype that India was a land where “time stood still,” and thus reinforcing the colonial narrative that Indians were incapable of true progress (growing up in societal terms) without Western scientific intervention. This "museum mentality" is criticised by modern scholars like **Nicholas Dirks** in *Castes of Mind* (2001), who argues that colonial anthropology essentially made Indian culture to look "frozen in time" in order to justify British rule as a necessary "modernising" force.

**The “Superstition” Debate** : Today, the ghost of Frazer still haunts Indian public life in the “anti-superstition” laws and movements. While intended to prevent fraud, these movements often use a strictly Frazerian definition of “science” to attack any ritual that doesn’t fit a materialist worldview. By reducing complex spiritual practices to “failed technology,” they continue the colonial habit of treating the practitioner as a “child” in need of “scientific” maturity.

**Sympathetic Magic in India:** Western scholars often cited the **Holi bonfire** as an example of homeopathic magic. Frazer himself, in *The Golden Bough, Volume IX: The Scapegoat*, explicitly compared Indian Holi rituals to European fire festivals, reducing the Puranic legend of Holika and Prahalada to a simple magical attempt to "stimulate" the heat of the sun**.** They argued that by lighting a massive fire, the “savage” mind was trying to “imitate” the heat of the sun to ensure it returned with enough strength for the upcoming season.

**The Scapegoat in India:** Colonial anthropologists often classified rituals where a **sacrificial goat** or a specific “sin-offering” was led out of a village during a plague as a classic “Frazerian Scapegoat.” They failed to see the deeper puranic or tantric symbolism, reducing the ritual to a “primitive sanitation project.”

**The Dying God & the Puranas:** Frazerian Indologists tried to force Hindu deities into the “Dying and Rising God” mold. They struggled with figures like **Vishnu or Shiva**, who are eternal and cyclical, often trying to reduce the **Churning of the Ocean (Samudra Manthana)** or the **death of Sati** into mere agricultural metaphors for the seasons.

# From Origins to Operations : British Functionalism

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

The new generation of British anthropologists in Britain, starting from 1920s, grew suspicious of the previous generations of Victorian anthropologists like Tylor and Frazer who basically used data collected from various peoples and theorise grand narratives without consideration of the context in which those data were collected. They thought this theorisation was premature and followed highly questionable methods. For example, Frazer would take a mid-winter fire festival in Scandinavia and compare it with a spring equinox fire festival in Ireland and try to derive some conclusions about universal notions and functions of fire festivals. This older method is often called the "**diachronic**" approach because it looked at how things changed across time. The new school preferred a "**synchronic**" approach which means looking at how a society functions at a single moment in time, like a snapshot.

The new anthropologists like **Bronislaw Malinowski** and **A. R. Radcliffe-Brown** thought that one could not just compare two things by separating them out of context in their own culture and society and worldview. For example, Malinowski writes :

> **Cultures are not collections of isolated facts, but are
> interconnected wholes.**

It was better to go into a local society and live there and get to know it competently and see the big picture of what is happening in the society as a whole, before starting collecting them for comparison rather than relying on ethnographies generated from hearsay data on cultures observed by European travellers and missionaries, who did not even speak the local language of the tribes that they sparingly observed without any big picture of what was going on. Malinowski revolutionised the discipline by introducing "**participant observation**". This required the anthropologist to get off his veranda and live with the people, eat their food, and participate in their daily life for at least a year to truly grasp what he called "the native's point of view”.

The whole idea of looking at the origin of religion as bad science was a dead end according to this new school and Brown writes :

> The usual way of looking at religion is to regard all of them (or all
> except one) as bodies of erroneous beliefs and practices.

When the religions of other peoples are considered from the outset as systems of erroneous beliefs, then comes the problem of how these beliefs came to be be formed and accepted in the society. This method of approach, which was felt as the most direct, natural, or even the most natural for every Western scholar so far, is not the one most likely to lead to a real understanding of the nature of religions, according to the functionalists. They also disagreed that the question of the origin of religion is impossible to determine (it is mostly speculation based on sparingly available evidence) and also not that important to understand religion. What instead should be done is observation of religion as it is practised in living societies in their full context and the role it plays for its own practitioners. Their final source of dissatisfaction lay with the concern with the truth and falsity of religion and also the value judgements regarding religion that the armchair anthropologists had. The functionalists believed that in order to truly study religion neutrally, one should personally be agnostic to the truth claims of the religion one is studying, instead of assuming from the outset that it is false or bad. No matter whether the beliefs of the religion are true or not, and no matter whether the practises of the religion are useful or good or not, they are indeed doing something to the practitioners and shaping them in a foundational manner and that is what should be the real concern of religious studies from the anthropological viewpoint, according to functionalists. That is why these anthropologists are called functionalists because they focused on how religion functioned rather than how it originated or whether it is true or necessary.

The functionalists then first became their own ethnographers, travel to the tribe that they wished to study, observed it themselves, learn the local language, and get familiar with the culture before formulating their own questions to probe from native informants. They treated culture as an organism which is each parts forming an interconnected whole and function dependently to ensure the harmonious survival of the organism. Their task was to identify and elucidate the function of each component. They set aside their own reason or rationale to find their own reason within the culture as to why a society did certain things in a certain way.

### Malinowski : Bio-psychological Functionalism

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Bronislaw Malinowski is generally regarded to be the founder of functionalism. Like an organism, he first wanted to identify the functions of society that are necessary to keep its individuals surviving and thriving (nutrition, reproduction, bodily comforts, safety, relationship maintenance, movement, and growth) and then map what part of culture fulfilled what aspect. This is often called "**bio-psychological functionalism**" because it argues that all social institutions (including religion) exist primarily to satisfy basic human biological and psychological needs.

As an example, some native American tribes when planting corn in spring, put some minnows (small freshwater fishes) in it. The tribes themselves believed that the minnows were offering to the corn god so that the corn would prosper but what it actually doing was that the fish rotting in the ground was scientifically acting as a fertiliser for the corn. So, here the meaning of the myths and the ritual of putting minnows with corn seeds could not be explained in any reasonable way without the economic (in this case agricultural aspect) too. This was Malinowski’s overall integrated approach and his main study “*Argonauts of the Western Pacific*” was based on the study he undertook that was focused on the economics and commerce among the **Trobriand Islanders** in Papua New Guinea and became a foundational work for what later developed into the field of **economic anthropology**. He famously described the "**Kula ring**", a complex system of ceremonial gift exchange between islanders. To an outsider, the exchange of useless shell necklaces seemed irrational, but Malinowski showed it functioned to build political alliances, maintain peace, and establish lifelong social bonds.

He identified the way in which the religious practices provided precedence for the economic relationships between tribes that were reinforced using myths which cannot be understood when reading the myth and the ritual alone in isolation. He tells of the creation myth among the islanders where the first human beings emerged from the underworld through holes in the ground who then saw the light of the day and gradually established societies. In one particular village, there is a myth wherein the animal ancestors of four different clans all emerged from the same hole there ; among the four clans, two were the precursors to the Dog clan and the Pig clan. As the Dog and Pig emerge out of the hole, they have a weird conversation where the Dog ridicules the Pig because it searched for food in the ground and hence considered it inferior while the Dog considered itself superior as it hunted for food. Thus, the Dog finally asserts that it will have superiority over the Pig.

Malinowski pointed out that in the previous view, you would have simply had the text of the myth written down and stripped off from its context and an armchair scholar studying the myth would read it and find symbols in it and relate it to the symbols of other myths and use it to build a castle of abstract ideas that did not take into account what actually was going on with the society telling these myths. But, when one actually saw how this myth functions in culture, one sees that in this village, there is an active competition for status between the four clans and the Dog clan is at the top in society. When one reads this myth without context, one would assume that this myth is a reflection of a pre-established stable social order with the Dog clan at the top of the hierarchy but once we get on the scene, we see that status is always being contested and the myth is not fixed in time but a current reflection of the social status of the Dog clan in society and that the myth will change when another clan becomes dominant. Malinowski famously described myths as a "**social charter**": a document that validates the current social arrangement and provides a precedent for why certain people hold power. Also, this myth was told to anyone coming new to the village to acquaint him with the local social hierarchy. The Victorians considered myths as fixed universal narratives while the functionalists saw myths as moving reflections of current social functions.

Another function that myth and religion provided to the society to enable it to thrive is by providing hope in uncertainty. To explain this, Malinowski provided one of the most famous analogies in the history of religious studies, based on his observations of Trobriand fishermen. He noticed a striking difference in how the islanders used rituals depending on where they were fishing:

- **The Lagoon:** When the islanders fished in the calm, inner lagoon,
  they were safe and their catch was predictable. In this environment,
  where they had full control and no danger, they performed **no rituals
  or magic at all**.

- **The Open Sea:** However, when they had to go beyond the reef into
  the dangerous, unpredictable open ocean—where a storm could kill them
  or they might return empty-handed—they performed elaborate, intense
  religious and magical rituals before setting sail.

He argued that whenever humans reach the “*limit of their practical knowledge*” ; when hard work and logic are no longer enough to guarantee safety or success ; they turn to religion to fill the gap. Religion provides a “***mythological big brother***” or a ritualized sense of control that allows a person to act with confidence in the face of terrifying uncertainty. Malinowski argued that the most “terrible” uncertainty of all is **death**. Just as the open sea creates anxiety for the fisherman, the inevitability of death creates a massive psychological crisis for every human being. Religion “functions” to solve this crisis by providing a belief in immortality or a life after death. Without this functional belief, Malinowski argued, the individual would be paralyzed by fear, and the social fabric would eventually collapse under the weight of despair.

From this, Malinowski concluded that religion is not just “primitive science” (as Fontenelle thought) nor just “social identity” (as Durkheim thought). Instead, he reduced religion to a combination of many interconnected factors that ensure that a society survives and thrives that include **scientific functions** as well as **psychological coping mechanism**.

> In the Indian context, Bronisław Malinowski’s reductionism of coping
> is frequently applied to the study of **Grihya Sutras** (domestic
> rituals) and **Vastu Shastra**. Scholars trained in this school look
> at rituals like the *Grah Shanti* (peace-making for the planets) or
> the lighting of a *diya* during a crisis and reduce them to mere
> “anxiety-management” tools. They argue that a Hindu family performs a
> *puja* before a major surgery or a business venture for the exact same
> reason the Trobriand islanders performed rituals before entering the
> open sea: because they are scared and feel helpless. While this
> approach seems sympathetic on the surface, it causes significant harm
> by stripping the ritual of its efficacy and its cosmological
> grounding. It implies that the *mantra* has no inherent power and the
> *yajna* has no real effect on the environment or the subtle body; they
> are treated merely as “placebos” to keep the practitioner calm.
>
> In contemporary academic debates, this legacy is seen when Western
> sociologists study the **Ganga Aarti** or **Kumbh Mela** as “mass
> psychological therapy” for a developing nation. They suggest that as
> India gains more “control” through modern insurance, medicine, and
> technology, these “functional” religious behaviours will or should
> naturally wither away because the “anxiety” they treat will be solved
> by science. This ignores the Hindu understanding of ritual as a
> sophisticated science of the vibration and the soul, a way of aligning
> the individual with the *rta* (cosmic order). By reducing the *pujari*
> to a makeshift “psychotherapist,” Malinowski-style reductionism fails
> to see that Hindu ritual is often not about escaping fear, but about
> performing one’s *dharma* in harmony with a living, conscious
> universe. It treats the seeker not as a scientist of the spirit, but
> as a patient looking for a “safety valve” to vent their stress.

### Radcliffe-Brown : Structural Functionalism

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Now, let us proceed to the work of another functionalist Radcliffe-Brown who differed from Malinowski in one key aspect despite agreeing with him on other aspects. While Malinowski saw religion and other aspects of society (economics, culture, art, etc..) from the point of view of its function to each individual in its society, Brown saw society itself as the unit that had to function and not the individuals. This is known as "**structural functionalism**". For Brown, society was like a building; individuals were like bricks that could be replaced, but the "structure" of the walls had to remain stable for the building to survive.

The needs of individuals did not matter and what one had to look for was instead the practices and beliefs that enabled the society on the whole to preserve, legitimise, and extend its structure and thrive. The individual was only integrating himself to this society as a whole. For e.g. in a soldier going to war, it definitely did not benefit the individual in any way and puts his life at risk and what the war essentially did was benefiting the society as a whole. That is why Brown puts an even greater stress on rituals because it is in performing its rituals that an individual came together with others, renews his connection to the society and maintain its structure. Brown viewed religion’s primary function as regulating the sentiments of individuals (moods, motivations, emotions, feelings, propensities) that lead people to act in certain ways that are beneficial to the society). He writes that the primary focus of religion was rituals that aim to cultivate a feeling of dependence on a power and in his own words :

> *I venture to suggest as a general formula that religion is
> everywhere, an expression, in one form or another, of a sense of
> dependence on a power outside of ourselves, a power which we may speak
> of as a spiritual or a moral power.*

He agrees with Durkheim (Part 1) that the objects of rituals are basically a society’s way of representing itself to itself and binding its people into a viable social unit that would move forward together.

Functional anthropology

The problems that cropped up with this functional view are :

1.  **The Erasure of the Native Voice** : While functionalism critiqued
    armchair anthropology and made it into an on-field study that
    required the anthropologist to go out there and observe the natives
    first hand by himself, it still does not provide any room for the
    native voice itself when it comes to how to interpret them. Despite
    taking into account the full context of the religion, the functional
    anthropologist still theorised about the religion he observed on his
    own without giving any weightage to the native’s explanation of what
    he was doing. For example, if you go back to a Native American
    ritual of planting corn with minnows, Malinowski summarily rejected
    the native tribals’ own view of the ritual being motivated to
    appease a god. Instead, Malinowski supersedes that indigenous
    perspective with his own scientific-economic view which he thinks is
    the real explanation of how the ritual functions, that the performer
    himself is ignorant of. Also, to give another example already
    discussed, Brown does not ask the Trobriand islanders themselves
    what they taught was the meaning of their myths and rituals).  
      
    *Imagine that some foreigner has been observing you frequently for a
    few years. He is then commenting to you regarding something dear
    that you do regularly in your life (religious or otherwise). He
    comments that it cannot be understood or motivated in the terms in
    which you yourself describe it, but rather has to be understood in
    reference to functions that you yourself had not thought of. Would
    that seem satisfactory to you? Would you feel you had gained a new
    insight into your life, or would you instead feel that the foreigner
    has not understood you? Does the notion of an anthropologist that a
    religious doctrine or practice has a scientific function that aids
    in the working and survival of you or your community, (and that this
    function is something other than what the you yourself say it is),
    lead you to think that the anthropologist has understood that
    teaching or practice better than you? Or alternatively, does it seem
    to you that he has missed the entire point by omitting your own
    perspective on what you do and why you do it?*

2.  **Static Societies** : It tended to overly reify local societies and
    was static and did not take into account the dynamics of the society
    with time also didn't tame into account the influence from other
    societies.

3.  **The Colonial Presence** : It also didn’t take into account the
    studying foreign anthropologist himself who was living with them and
    collection data from them. The natives were aware that the
    anthropologists (Malinowski himself for example) who studied them
    had huge army support and lived near colonial garrisons and hence
    what information that the natives told the anthropologist and what
    actions that the natives showed in front of the anthropologist
    (which served as his data), that itself would certainly be
    influenced by the native perception of the colonial.

4.  **"Just-So" Stories** : It is prone to a developing a mythology of
    its own wherein one is already disposed to believe that any doctrine
    or ritual is only meant to serve a social function and that
    influences and clouds the observations that the functional
    anthropologist himself undertakes and also biases his
    interpretation. It had the potential to make anthropology into a
    spinner of “**just-so stories**” according to **E. E.
    Evans-Pritchard**, who caricatured this functional study as “*If I
    were a horse….*” approach wherein an anthropologist saw what the
    horse did and then starts thinking “*If I were a horse, why would I
    do this*?” without taking into account the internal physiology of
    the horse itself.

As two examples to demonstrate this drawback, consider the anthropologist **Marvin Harris** (who founded another related school of anthropology called “**cultural materialism**” about which we have no space to discuss unfortunately). He explains the Aztec ritual of human sacrifice followed by cannibalistic feasting as simply being motivated from a protein deficiency in their diet and pays no attention to how the Aztecs themselves understood why they were doing this. This also ignores the fact that it is anyway only the upper class of Aztecs (who were anyway well off) who participated in this sacrifice and the commoners who should be more protein deficient did not participate in this. The other example that he considers is the forbidding of pork in the Jewish Levitican dietary code which is also explained away as pure economics. Unlike cattle, pigs are not useful to humans in any other way (milk / wool / etc..) until they die and pigs compete with humans for the same kind of food (unlike cattle that mainly eat grass that humans do not) and hence he sees the religious code of banning pork as only serving the purpose of motivating the people to not get tempted to eat pork in the arid climate of Israel where pigs are economically unviable to cultivate. It ignores anything of what Jewish theology itself and the Jews themselves have to say on why they do not eat pork and also ignores that other non-Jewish cultures in the same region did not have any such taboos related to pork.

### Functionalism and the Indian Village

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

British Functionalism, particularly structural-functionalism, became a dominant “microscope” through which post-World-War-II Indian society was studied in the 1940s and 50s and continued well up to the 60s. While it moved away from the “savage” labels of the Victorian era, it introduced a new set of biases.

**1. M.N. Srinivas and the “Coorg” Study** : The most famous bridge between British Functionalism and Indian sociology was **M.N. Srinivas**. A student of Radcliffe-Brown, Srinivas applied the structural-functionalist method to his study of the Coorgs. He famously identified how Hindu rituals functioned to maintain social order and solidarity within the caste hierarchy **(Srinivas, M.N.,** ***Religion and Society among the Coorgs of South India*****, 1952)**. While this was a massive leap forward in fieldwork, critics argued it looked at Hinduism only as a mechanism for “social harmony”, often ignoring the theological depth of the rituals themselves.

**2. The Reification of the “self-sufficient” Village** : Functionalism led to a wave of “village studies” by scholars like **S.C. Dube**, who viewed the Indian village as a “closed, harmonious organism” **(Dube, S.C.,** ***Indian Village*****, 1955)**. This approach tended to frame the Indian village as a static, isolated unit where every caste and ritual had a “fixed function.” By treating the village as a self-contained biological system, functionalists often missed the large-scale historical movements, the influence of pan-national structures and movements, and the ways in which Hindu traditions were connected across vast geographical distances.

**3. Functionalism and the Defense of Caste** : Because functionalism seeks the “utility” in everything, it often fell into the trap of justifying the caste system by purely economic means. If a ritual or a caste restriction existed, the functionalist assumed it must serve a useful purpose for social stability. This led to a “conservative bias” where Western-trained scholars viewed the jati system as a mere “functional division of labor” that prevented social chaos, thereby ignoring the lived reality, the social and theological complexity, and social change across historical timescales, within those communities.

**4. Contemporary Legacy - The “Management” of Hinduism :** Today, this functionalist ghost survives in the way modern states and NGOs approach Hindu traditions. They often value a temple or a festival only for its “social utility” ; such as its ability to mobilise people for health awareness or its role in “environmental conservation” (e.g., sacred groves). While these are beneficial, it is a continuation of the Malinowski logic: reducing the spiritual act to a “functional” byproduct, essentially telling the devotee that their prayer is “actually” just a way to manage stress or protect a forest.

# Objects Conform to the Mind : The Philosophy of Immanuel Kant

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

In the previous sections, we saw the **evolutionists** (searching for origins) and the **functionalists** (searching for utility). Both schools differed in several ways but assumed that the world "out there" is objective and that humans are just reacting to it. Both schools explain religion based on how it relates to this external objective world, and hence conclude it to be delusional in the strictest sense. To understand further developments in anthropology in the 1960s, and also the phenomenological approaches to religious studies (that respect religion as a phenomenon in its own right) that I will explain in my next article, it is important to go back in time to digress and discuss about the philosophy of Immanuel Kant which revolutionised Western philosophy and influenced the later generation of anthropologists. Long before anthropology arrived on the scene, the philosopher Immanuel Kant, in the 18th century, changed the entire course of Western philosophy by developing his philosophy of “***transcendental idealism***” that implied that the so called "world" that we experience is not an objective external reality out there but actively constructed by our own minds. Let us now study him in detail. Kant’s philosophy essentially claims that we can only know the world as it appears to our human minds, not as it is in absolute reality. This was a radical departure from the common-sense view that we see the world exactly as it is.

### The Great Stalemate : Empiricism vs. Rationalism

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

To understand Kant, we have to understand a great intellectual warfare between two factions in Western philosophy that was raging hard before Kant arrived at the scene, and goes all the way back to disagreements between Plato and Aristotle. In the last article, we saw about David Hume and his philosophical view called **empiricism** (represented by thinkers like Hume, Locke, Berkeley, etc…) which was at one side of the camp in this war. Empiricism postulated that perception is the only source of knowledge and an object exists only if it is perceived by any of our senses. On the other side lay rationalism (represented by thinkers like Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz etc…) that postulated that reason is the source of real and solid knowledge. The empiricists argued that the human mind starts as a *tabula rasa* (a blank slate) and that all knowledge must enter through the five senses. For them, if you haven’t seen, felt, smelt, tasted, or heard something, it doesn’t exist. The rationalists believed that the senses could be deceptive and that true, certain knowledge could only be reached through pure reason and “innate ideas” that we are born with the capacity to reason about. This conflict created a crisis for the study of religion: since God could not be sensually perceived, the empiricists tended to reduce God to a “mistaken observation” of nature, while the rationalists tried to “prove” or “disprove” the existence/non-existence of God like a mathematical equation. Neither side could explain how we could have a meaningful, lived experience of things we couldn’t technically touch - like justice, the soul, or the sacred. This stalemate meant that Western thought was stuck: one side made religion impossible because you couldn't "touch" God, while the other made it a dry logic puzzle. Kant’s goal was to find a third way that accounted for how we know things without falling into either trap.

### The Problem of Universals

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

This conflict extended to abstract concepts, that are technically called as “**universals**.” Take the number 3: we have seen 3 horses or 3 books, but we have never seen the "concept of 3" itself. Empiricists like Hume argued that 3 was just a shorthand for what 3 of any kinds of perceived objects shared in common. Similarly, take the concept of **causation**. We don’t see “causes”; we only see one event followed by another. Hume argued that causation is just a "habit of the mind" born from seeing a correlation repeated often enough. In Hume's world, we can never be *certain* the sun will rise tomorrow; we just have a strong hunch because it always has, based on previously observed sequences of events. Rationalists, following the tradition of Plato, argued that these concepts (like 3, causation, etc…) have their own objective, eternal reality in a realm beyond space and time, which the mind accesses through reason. Empiricists followed the Aristotelian tradition, believing these ideas only exist within the perceived objects themselves as their properties.

Despite their differences, both the empiricist and rationalist side shared a common feature : they assumed the mind was a "passive mirror". They believed that the external world simply imprinted itself onto the mind, like a mould getting imprinted with the impression of the facsimile pressed on to it ; and the mind's only job when it comes to perception, was to record the accurate impression of external objects and also ideas (for rationalists). By calling the mind a "passive mirror," Kant meant that both sides so far, treated the perception of an object by the mind like a camera on the world ; simply recording what was in front of it. Kant’s revolutionary insight was that the mind is not a camera, but more like a filmmaker, not just blindly capturing but actively editing and producing the movie that we think of as “perceived reality”.

### The Kantian Turn: The Active Mind

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Immanuel Kant turned the table and proposed that the human mind isn’t a blank slate (tabula rasa) where the sense organs simply stamp the perceived external world on the mind. He argued that your mind is active ; it pre-digests your perceptions, organises them and presents them to you. So what you think of as “merely seeing or perceiving” is actually not the external world but the perception of the external world as organised and processed by your mind. He famously claimed he was effecting a "**Copernican Revolution**" in philosophy: just as Copernicus showed the earth revolves around the sun and not vice versa, Kant showed that "objects conform to the mind," rather than the mind conforming to objects.  
  
Kant argued that the universals (abstract concepts) are neither in the perceived objects themselves nor in a separate Platonic reality; they are the **“hardwiring” of the mind.** He called these the “**categories of understanding**. They do not come from the external objects themselves, they do not come from another abstract world either ; the mind organises raw, chaotic sensory data through these built-in filters to give us a coherent experience. These categories include:

- **The Intuitions of Space and Time:** We don’t “learn” space and time;
  they are the “glasses” or categories through which we see everything.

- **Causality:** The mind insists on seeing cause-and-effect; it is the
  software the mind uses to process events.

- **Quantity and Quality:** The notion of “one” or “many,” or “discrete
  objects” (this is an apple, that is a table).

These abstract concepts are all, Kant says, are neither out there in the world nor out there somewhere beyond this world, but are there in our mind as categories imposed by the mind on the raw perceptual data from the senses to give us a ready-made experience. If I say that I saw two balls hitting each other and hence bouncing off each other after that, Kant says that the very notion that I see two kinds of objects called balls (categorisation into discrete objects), the very notion that they come to same place (notion of space), and their coming together resulting in bouncing off each other (the notion of causation) - all of these are my own constructs from the mind - and aren’t intrinsic to the external world itself. Kant’s genius was in realising that if you take away the mind’s "software," the world becomes what William James later called a "*blooming, buzzing confusion***".** Consider, what at surface, appears to be the most rudimentary of perceived sights ; say that I see “***two balls hitting each other and bouncing off***”. Note in this phrase :

- the very notion of balls (discrete objects),

- the very notion of place (space),

- the notion of impact (causation)

- the notion that a certain phenomenon falls into the category ( which
  is called “bouncing”)

All these are all constructs the mind imposes to make the event intelligible and without them, it is all just a mess of unorganised sense impressions, a mess of colours, shapes, and sounds. Since science is based on abstract concepts like space, time, numbers, shapes, and causation , he says the concepts of science itself that we use to study the world is not out there but is my mind’s own construct that the mind imposes on my perceptions to give me what I feel as my experience. So he says that science is just a construct of my mind and not a part of the external world in itself.

This is the crucial "bridge" to modern anthropology. If even the concepts of "science" are mental constructs, then we can no longer judge other cultures simply for being "unscientific." Instead, we have to look at how their minds and cultures provide different sets of categories or software to organise their world.

### Noumena vs. Phenomena: The Limits of Knowledge

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

This leads to Kant’s most famous distinction:

1.  **The Noumenal World:** The “Thing-in-Itself” (*Ding an sich*). This
    is the world as it exists independent of our senses.

2.  **The Phenomenal World:** The world as it appears to us during
    perception to our minds after being filtered through our mental
    categories.

Kant says that we can never know “anything-in-itself”, in the noumenon, independent of our mind and senses because the moment we perceive something, we aren’t seeing that thing-in-itself but we see a reconstructed version of that thing by my mind after it imposes its categories on the raw perception. The external world by itself, he calls it the **noumenal** **world** and the world as we experience it, he calls it the **phenomenal** **world**. Kant says we can never know anything about the noumenal world since the only way we have access to it is through our senses and mind and they transfer the noumenal world into the phenmoneal one, when I try to experience it. He says :

> How things may be in themselves, without regard to the representations
> through which they affect us, is utterly beyond the sphere of our
> cognition

Kant’s radical conclusion was that we can *never* know the noumenal world**.** The moment we perceive something, it is already "processed." We are like people born wearing permanent blue-tinted goggles; we can talk about the blue world all day, but we can never know what the world looks like without the goggles. The mind through its categories filter our perception that we finally get like the blue goggles taints everything blue after stuff is seen through it. But unlike the goggles case, we can never know what the world out there actually is, independent of our fundamental categorisations of our perception.

This limitation is precisely why later anthropologists (like those we will see in the next article) stopped trying to figure out "*What does religion correspond to, in the world of objects and humans*?" and instead started asking "*How does the human 'goggles' of language and culture create the experience of the sacred?*"

# The Study of Signs : Ferdinand de Saussure

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Beginning in the 1950s and into the 1960s, a new generation of anthropologists rebelled against the functionalist and related schools because of its limitations, (especially in ignoring the native voice in assessing and explaining their religion), and a new generation of anthropologists came about which is called “**symbolic anthropology**”. We will be again studying two figures in this school as representatives but before going to them, we will study another French linguist named **Ferdinand de Saussure** who laid down the basic framework for this approach.

### The Rejection of Nomenclature

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Saussure wanted linguistics to be a part of a broader science that studied not just words and grammar but also signs of all kinds : gestures, symbols, logos, and literally anything that signified or represented something else ; and he called this field as **semiology** (now called **semiotics**). Let us start with the simple question :

> How do words signify the things that they signify?

If I say the word “tree” and point out to a tree , this pointing finger seems to link the word that I am using (t-r-e-e) and the thing that I am referring to when I use that word (the actual tree outside). So, the common sense notion seems to be that the word “tree” refers to the external object in the world that I call as “tree”. So, words refer to external objects that actually exist out there like a tree and has an external referent. This common-sense view is called "**nomenclature**" which is the idea that the world is a finished room full of objects and language just puts "name tags" on them.

But Saussure drew on Kantian thought, and he said that words do not point to things out there because the external world does not come categorised into objects like “trees” or “bushes” or “sky” or “mountain” or “house” and it is our mind that invents these categories. Remember what Kant says : whenever we see anything, it is all just a bunch of sensory impressions (colours, shapes, smells, touches, tastes) and it is our mind that takes a particular group of colours and shapes, determines that it is different from all other colours and shapes around it, and then decides this is a separate object and decides to use the word “tree” to refer to it. Saussure has added the linguistic component to Kant. Kant said that the mind has already taken this bunch of shapes and colours and decided that it is a separate object and different from the rest of the shapes and colours surrounding it, which Saussure calls as an “image”.

### Signifier and Signified: The Anatomy of a Sign

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

According to Saussure, language just associates these images to words (a string of sounds) and hence when we use the word “tree” , we are not referring to an external object out there but an internal image in our mind made up by selecting some groups of these impressions and unifying them by comprehending them as a single object. It is these mental images formed by the fragmentation of our impressions that we perceive ; i.e. what we perceive is itself a mentally pre-digested and pre-organised images of the world.

> Words of a language, according to Saussure, hence relate two mental
> things : one mental thing (a bunch of sounds = word) to another mental
> thing (bunch of impressions = image ) ; and not one mental thing to an
> external object out there, as understood by common sense.

Now, the word for Saussure, was called as a “sound image”. The “sound image” refers to the potential sound because the sound image “tree” does not actually become sound until we open our mouth and speak it but we can still think of this word in our mind which he calls it as a “sound image”. Language relates sound images to visual images. The sound image is called the “**signifier**” (the *form*) and the image/concept is called the “**signified**” (the *idea*). Together, they form the system called "sign."

What happens when I point to a tree and say “tree” to you is that the signifier or the sound image that I say (“tree”) and that you hear will correlate with the visual image of the tree that is formed in your mind when you are looking at the tree.

### Meaning through Difference: The Conceptual Fence

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Another aspect that Saussure focused was that was differentiation. The moment our mind groups together some of the sense impressions (colours and shapes) out there and creates a mental image of a tree as a separate object, what I am also doing is that I am fragmenting and determining that this group of colours and shapes (tree) is different from all the other bunches and shapes surrounding it. I might categorise some groups of colours and shapes that I observe into the category “tree” and group another groups of colours and shapes that I observe in the same time into another category “bush”. In the external world, there is nothing intrinsic to it that says that this group is a tree and this group is a bush. Everything just exists on their own and what we see is a bunch of gazillions of colours and shapes. In our mind, we take one groups of perception and form it into a concept and relate it with a sound image and form a word for it and decide that this is not the same as another group of perception.

So, the mind and language not just refer to images but also differentiate each image from others. When we look out there and say “this is a tree” and “that is a bush”, we are putting a conceptual fence between two groups of colours and shapes in our perception and say this belongs to one box called “tree” and that belongs to another box called “bush” and keep them separate in our minds. Saussure’s radical claim is that "*in language, there are only differences*". A word doesn't have a meaning "on its own"; it only has meaning because it is NOT another word. We only know what "tree" means because the language differentiates the impressions that it groups with the terms “tree” it from others that are not trees.

The very act of our perception hence fragments the sense impressions from our external world and arranges them into classes and form hierarchies based on these classes. For example, both “tree” and “bush” are contained in a more general category called “plant” and hence what we are doing when we observe the world, according to Kant, is that we are grouping and dividing a unitary mess of sense impressions into various hierarchies of categories at different levels, and Saussure simply adds to Kant that language just gives words to refer to these categories like “tree” or “bush” or “plant”. So, **what the mind does when it perceives anything is a complex interpretive act** :

1.  Decides some groups of colours and shapes that we perceive now and
    have perceived before, fence them from everything else, and forms a
    single and separate category of object in its own right (e.g.
    “tree”). The mind, thus decides that the group of perceived shapes
    and colours that it puts into the category “tree” is different from
    everything else that is seen and has been seen. Words then address
    and refer to these fences or a group of fences.

2.  Relate these groups to another (“tree” and “bush” both are contained
    in “plant” ; and both “olive-tree” and “pine-tree” are contained in
    the category “tree”). That is, there is a larger fence of
    categorisation of sense impressions called “plant” that contains
    within it, smaller categorisations and fencing of sense impressions
    like “tree” and “bush".

So, the mind forms a mesh of senses impressions and fences them at various levels and uses words to refer to these fragments of sense impressions. Saussure notes that when you study different languages of the world, this fencing takes place in slightly different ways and this is the reason why translating words from one language to another becomes a tricky affair. For example, he takes the following words from English and French that deal with sheep. He pointed out that English has two words that both refer to sheep, which are : “sheep” (when it is grazing) and “mutton” (when it is cooked and served). But, the French on the other hand have only one word to refer to sheep which is “mutton” always. What that meant for Saussure is that the French and English are organising the world in two slightly different ways. The English people put sheep into two different fences depending on whether they are moving around or served on the table whereas the French are putting both of them (sheep on the lawn and sheep on the table) into a single category without any fence within. This illustrates that "signs" are arbitrary and conventional.

Different cultures chop up the "unitary mess" of reality into different conceptual boxes.So, the French and the English are interpreting the world in two slightly different ways. This might be a trivial example but if we look at other examples, it might be better. In the Chinese language, there is a word called “guā” which when used as parts of compounds refers to either watermelons, papayas, sweet potatoes, or cantaloupes. This makes us wonder what the heck this word means because English definitely does not consider all these things in one category and it turns out Chinese organises food based on their shape and hence any edible thing from a plant that is long and rounded is lumped into this category “guā”. So, the Chinese person organises his world based on a slightly different set of relationships than an Englishman. According to Kant, neither the English nor the French nor the Chinese is more correct than the other, because the external world itself does not come lumped into any categories and this fragmentation and lumping of perceptions of the world into hierarchies of concepts are all purely the products of the mind and the Chinese mind seems to do it little differently from the English mind. The world out there is just there and does not care what we make of it and how we divide and describe it (at least we can never know the external world as it is, independent of our perception). So, the words of a language does not reflect how the world itself is organised but how its speakers organise the world as perceived by them.

### The Arbitrariness of the Sign : Language as both Immutable and Mutable

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Another important concept of Saussure’s thought is his idea of “**arbitrariness of the sign**”. There is no reason why a tree should be called a tree and any other sound image can be equally applied to refer to this mental image that I have in my mind and hence different languages do indeed use different words (sound images) for trees. The Spanish call it “arbol”, the Chinese call it “mù”, Tamil people call it “maram” and Sanskrit speakers call it “vṛkṣa”. All of these sound image are arbitrary and have nothing to do with the category tree itself which refers to an image in the mind. What does this arbitrariness of the relationship between words and images imply for language? This means that this relationship can change with time. Because there is only an arbitrary connection between the word “tree” and and the image “tree”, the connections can change with time and it is true that words do change their meanings with time, in the course of the history of a language. The same word can come to signify a different image in a language as it evolves. For e.g., if you read the King James Bible, you will see a verse in the Psalms where someone says “I *will prevent the dawn*”. This sounds weird but it turns out that back in 1611, the word “prevent” in the English language meant coming before or preceding and hence what this psalmist is saying is that he will be up before the dawn to make his prayers. Now, the word “prevent” means to impede or obstruct in the English language. Neither of these are correct and both of them are arbitrary connections between words and images, because both connections are arbitrary. So, due to the arbitrariness of the connection between word (signifier) and image (signified), this connection does indeed change with time. Saussure calls the study of language through time "**diachronic**", but he argues that to understand a system, we must study it "**synchronically**"; as a complete, frozen system of relationships at one specific moment.

But there is some other parameter with respect to which this connection is immutable and cannot change with. Saussure tells that language subsists at the level of the community and hence no single one of us can arbitrarily or unilaterally change the meanings of words because a language is a communal phenomenon and to enable communication between its speakers, all the speakers have to share the same connection between word and meaning. If somebody suddenly today starts using the word “prevent” to mean lifting or raising up something, then he can never communicate with other speakers of English who will force him to confirm to the shared meaning of the word. This is a hard pill to swallow for individualistic Westerners to think of something that can only be understood as happening within a community and not as a result of individual choice but this is what happens with language. When we grow up, we are not learning a language that we invented on our own or consented to, but are just learning something that is passed down to us to enable communication with others. But as time goes on, this language can change but only with the consensus of the entire community of speakers. When the word “prevent” ceases to mean preceding and starts to mean obstructing, it did not happen under the decree of a single person (no matter how powerful he might otherwise be) but it happened because the community shifted on the whole in its conventional usage of the word. So, language like living organisms are mutable and evolve with time but cannot change at the level of the individual and is understood only at the level of the community.

### Langue and Parole: The System and its Expressions

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Another aspect of Saussure’s philosophy of language is the distinction between two ideas. The notion between “the language as such” on the one hand and “the actual deployment of the language” on the other hand. To designate these, let us use the compact terms that Saussure himself used to refer to these two things :

1.  **Langue** : The language as a whole

2.  **Parole** : (literally) Speaking of the language (also can mean
    other manifestations like writing)

Langue is language as such and what is meant when we use phrases like “the English language" or “the Hindi language”. Where does this English language or Hindi language exist? In dictionaries? In grammar books? In people’s minds? Look as you may, you will never actually find the English language or Hindi language anywhere in the world. It is not contained in the totality in any one person’s mind or anywhere else in the world. The langue is hence a pure abstraction that does not exist anywhere in the world and we cannot point anywhere and say “That is the English language” or “That is the Hindi language”. Parole, on the other hand, speaking or writing, are concrete actions that instantiate the language. The words that you are reading right now are the parole of the English language ( I am using the grammar, lexicon, and syntax of the English language to write and you are using them to understand what I write). The parole then temporally and locally expresses this grand abstraction called the English language or the langue. To understand this, think of ‘the game of chess”. The rules of the game (how the knight moves, what a checkmate is) and the totality of the squares, characters, shapes of the characters, and strategies are its langue. The actual game you play with a friend on a Tuesday afternoon is a parole of chess. You can't play the game without the langue or referring to it, but the langue of the game only expresses itself when you play.

The parole has a concrete existence and you can point to this article and say “this is a parole of the English language”. The parole is the only kind of concrete existence that any language has and the langue exists nowhere in its totality. Yet, you cannot dismiss the langue as a vague abstraction that does not exist and is not good for anything because without the langue, there is no parole. I use a set of words in a set of ways when I speak or write English and they all must have some source logicality and that is the langue, despite the fact that I cannot locate it anywhere. Hence, we have to give importance to both langue and parole.

How does this all relate to anthropology? The ideas of langue and parole are evocative. Since Saussure himself says that his theories of language could be applied to any system of signs and meanings, anthropologists quickly saw that this method could be easily applied to culture. The relationship between langue and parole can be carried forward to the relationship between culture and cultural expressions and religion and religious beliefs-acts ; for example between Christianity as a system and a Sunday mass on a church in a village in Venice ; between Chinese culture as a system and a Chinese painter painting something. All other aspects of Saussure too can be mapped to culture and religion. The world is not an inherent set of pre-digested meanings and like language, culture and religion are some of the ways in which the mind takes the totality of all the sensory impressions coming into our sense organs, and organises them into such a way that we understand where we are and what we should do, and so on, and can create a whole web of significations over them. Like language, culture and religion too change with time but only at the level of a community and not individuals. The idea of the arbitrariness of the sign is also applicable to culture and religion too. Just as "tree" is an arbitrary sound for a concept, a "sacred Cow" or a "sacred Cross" is an arbitrary symbol that a specific community has "fenced off" and imbued with deep meaning. Anthropology, therefore, becomes the study of these "fences"; the structures of meaning that allow a community to live in a shared phenomenal world. There are cultures that categorise things differently like there are languages that categorise things differently. Religion like language and culture can change with time and also as it interacts with other religious societies and cultures, not because a single individual wills it so, but the community as a whole, experiences an evolution in the way it construes the world and creates the relationship between things and people. So, in taking the Kantian phenomenological approach to religion, we have moved away from the world out there, and moved into the world constructed within by a language or religion.

# Structuralism : Claude Lévi-Strauss

Building on Saussure’s linguistic revolution, the French anthropologist **Claude Lévi-Strauss** took these abstract theories of signs and applied them to the entire landscape of human culture. If Saussure showed us that language is a self-contained system of "fences" of “categories”, Lévi-Strauss argued that the human mind itself is hardwired or constrained by the hardware of human physiology so that it builds these fences in specific, universal ways. He believed that whether we are looking at the marriage laws of a tribe in the Amazon or the complex mythologies of Ancient Greece, we are actually looking at the "parole" (the specific expression) of a much deeper, universal "langue" of human thought. For Lévi-Strauss, culture was not just a collection of traditions; it was a sophisticated **logical machine**, called it a "science of the concrete" ; that uses the objects of the physical world to solve the deepest contradictions of human existence. I am going to admit, Strauss is a tough read and even graduate anthropology students struggle to decode selections of what Strauss wrote (which was voluminous) and hence what I am saying here barely scratches the surface of my limited understanding of my limited readings of his works, let alone his entire corpus.

### The Search for the "Pristine"

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Strauss accepted a position in Brazil as a lecturer in sociology as much of Brazil at that time (1930s) was still wild and relatively unexplored so that he could do field work from scratch. Between 1934 and 1937, he made a series of expeditions up the Amazon but due to his academic position, he could not stay there for extended periods and could go only for a few months at a time. What he was looking for was every anthropologist’s dream which was a completely undiscovered tribe, one that was completely uncontaminated by what he calls as “*western filth*”. They would be pristine and exhibit their own culture in the purest manner imaginable and Strauss look a long time to find such a tribe and had to sail farther up the Amazon. On the border near Paraguay and Bolivia, near the mouth of the Amazon river, he finally found the **Tupi-Kawahib**, a completely unknown, undiscovered, primitive, and uncontaminated tribe, who had never seen a white man. He writes :

> *I had wanted to pursue the primitive to its furthest point. Surely,
> my wish has been gratified by these delightful people whom no white
> man has seen before me and none would ever see again. I had come upon
> “my savages”. But alas, they were all “too savage”. There, they were,
> all ready to teach me their customs and beliefs and I knew nothing of
> their language*.

At this point, a functionalist anthropologist would want Strauss to interact with them, stay with them, observe them, learn their language, and get to know their culture as a whole. But Strauss decided that this mere presence of the western anthropologist risked contamination and in any attempts to study them, he would end up contaminating them if he interacted with them. But this creates a problem that one cannot get any deep or insider or contextual information or know the language of a people without interacting with them? This created a conundrum.

### Structuralism : The Mind as a Universal Machine

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

> How do you, as a foreigner, get at the pristine core of a culture’s
> being without also contaminating it by your presence?

Non-disturbance demands distance but insider understanding demands close interaction. The solution that Strauss came up with which makes his approach very controversial was that Strauss chose to simply not be there at all among them and just watch them at a distance. Strauss then began to piece together a way of approaching the “savage” mind that did not depend on mastering their language or talking directly to them. How can one study a culture from a distance? This is where Strauss borrowed Saussure’s linguistics.

He again looked to language ; it is possible to study another language deeply and its lexical and grammatical structures thoroughly without being able to speak a single sentence in it at native proficiency. In fact, that is what linguists do. Studying how language is structured on the whole can only be done comparatively and we all know that grammatical knowledge of languages has got nothing to do with competency in using the language. There are poets who don’t know any grammar and there are comparative grammarians who can’t speak a single sentence in the language they describe. Strauss argued that the people living within a culture are like the poets: they use the "language" of their myths and rituals beautifully, but they are often unaware of the underlying rules that govern them. The anthropologist’s job is to be the “grammarian" who uncovers these rules. And since all cultural acts were paroles of a universal language of cultural thought, he must be able to do this mapping very easily.

> *Just as speech was produced through the transformation of deep
> grammatical structures in language, Strauss thought that cultural acts
> took place through transformations of another kind of mental
> structure. This structure could be mapped out by the tabulation of
> comparative elements in such categories as myths and rituals.*

Strauss believed that myths were not just "stories," but "logical tools" that the brain uses to solve problems. To understand a myth, he argued we must break it down into its smallest units ; which he called **"mythemes"** (inspired by the notions of ***phonemes*** in language); and see how they are arranged**.** In this regard, the Victorians have been in the right track in that they meticulously catalogued various myths from various cultures. But, they messed up in the comparison. What exactly is a mytheme?

**Level 1 : Phonemes → Mythemes (The Atoms)**

In linguistics, a **phoneme** is the smallest unit of sound (like the “b” in *bat* or the “p” in *pat*). Crucially, a phoneme has no meaning on its own. The sound “b” doesn’t mean anything until it is placed in a system where it contrasts with “p”. Lévi-Strauss argued that myths are built from similar atoms which he called **mythemes**. These are the shortest possible units of information which are events in a story, for example :

- *“The hero kills the dragon.”*

- *“The sister is kidnapped.”*

- *“The father is sacrificed.”*

Just as a phoneme is a “meaningless” sound, a mytheme is a “meaningless” event when viewed in isolation. Killing a dragon doesn’t “mean” anything until we see how that event relates to the other events in the system. Take the example from the Hindu myth of Ramayana. **Level 2: Words → Bundles of Relations (The Molecules)**

In language, we combine phonemes to create a **word**. A word is a stable unit that carries a specific concept, and it is a word that carries meaning in a language and not phonemes. Although a word like “cat” is decomposable into shorter units of phonemes like “c” , “a”, “t”, they do not carry meaning individually. The word is the smallest unit of language that carries meaning. Similarly, a mytheme that corresponds to a single event does not carry meaning. Lévi-Strauss argued that we don’t just read a myth from start to finish like a novel (horizontal reading). Instead, we should group similar mythemes together into what he called **“bundles of relations**”. A bundle of relation is not just a story as a whole (consists of several sequential events) but also more than that and a thematic collection.

**Level 3: Syntax/Grammar → The Structural Grid (The Language)**

**Syntax** or **Grammar** is the set of invisible rules that tell us how to arrange words to make a meaningful sentence. You can have all the words in the world, but without syntax, you just have a list; you don’t have a language. For Lévi-Strauss, it is this “grammar” of myth is what needs to be compared, which he argues is shared universally between all cultures, with only the parole being particular and varying. Just as English grammar or syntax dictates that a subject must come before a verb, the “myth-grammar” dictates how a culture resolves its paradigms. We will see this soon in detail in another subsection.

Thus, what needs to be compared are not the individual ‘mythemes’ themselves, but the **underlying grammar** which refers to the way in which these mythemes are structured and put together. In other words, while the Victorians compared the contents (the ‘words’ of the stories), Strauss wanted to compare the logic and connections (syntax) of these myths. The Victorians simply juxtaposed similar things side by side like two two fire rituals from two cultures while Strauss wanted to ask something deeper :

> What is it about the way the human mind structures and fences the
> perceived world and puts them together in fences of signification,
> which leads to the emergence of these rituals as logical solutions to
> universal problems?

Saussure had asserted the arbitrariness of the sign and this is certainly plausible and there is no necessary reason why a tree should be called a “tree” and there should be no necessary reason as to not lump watermelons, papayas, and sweet potatoes not be grouped together in a single class (the Chinese language does it through the word guā). But, a new theory of linguistics called **transformative grammar** had emerged and what it added to Saussure was the idea that there may be some level at which the manipulation or connection of these signs and symbols may not be completely arbitrary and may be constrained in certain ways.

> This is the leap from "software" to "hardware." If Saussure's *langue*
> is the software of a specific culture, Lévi-Strauss was looking for
> the universal "operating system" of the human species whose
> universality constrains the kinds of software that the operating
> system can support.

In other words, it is like this : since humans all share the same brain physiology and observe the same overall natural categories, this external operating system might place constraints on the softwares of perception and place limitations on the way in which the mind perceives the world. You cannot simply put together these things in any way that you want but have to be consistent in the manner in which our shared human physiology and psychology demands. This is crucial for Strauss because he saw in this connection of mind and brain, a way of connecting culture to nature itself (both physical and human) and the way nature constrains us to manipulate and deploy symbols. (If this sounds abstract, the next paragraph gives a concrete example through a traffic light). Thus was born **structuralism**, a mode of cultural analysis that quickly took hold of the Western imagination and escaped Lévi-Strauss’s own control, spreading out into art, philosophy, and literature.

### The Traffic Light: A Lesson in Meaning as Relationship

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

If this still sounds abstract, consider a simple, everyday example: the traffic light. In our modern global culture, we know in traffic that :

- **Red** means “Stop”

- **Yellow** means “Beware”

- **Green** means “Go”

Following only Saussure, we could argue that the colours red, yellow, and green have no meaning on their own and we could use any three colours to indicate these three functions in traffic lights. Strauss would however argue that it is a calculated use of natural physics to solve a cultural problem, not an arbitrary convention. To see how the “operating system” of our biology and physics constrains the “software” of our culture, we can look at the triangular structure of a traffic light. While we might think the choice of Red, Green, and Yellow is random, Lévi-Strauss would argue it is a calculated use of natural physics and physiology to solve a cultural problem.

- **The Natural Constraint:** Assigning a colour to a message about
  stopping or starting a car is not entirely arbitrary. In the spectrum
  of human vision, **Yellow** lies at the centre of the visible spectrum
  which is the range of light frequencies that we can perceive. Because
  of this, it has the highest intensity and is the most “visible” colour
  to the human eye. Red and Green, being at the edges, are less intense
  and seen less distinctly. Also, according to Rayleigh scattering law,
  scattering is inversely proportional to the fourth power of the
  wavelength and hence light of the lowest wavelength gets scattered the
  most while light of the highest wavelength gets scattered the lowest.
  As a consequence of this, red light (which is of the highest
  wavelength) travels the farthest through fog and dust without
  scattering while green light gets scattered the most.

- **The Logic of the Transition & Scattering :** Since the “transitional
  state” (moving from going to stopping) is the most dangerous and
  important one to communicate, **Yellow** becomes the most “natural”
  choice for the warning light because it is the most intense and
  hardest to miss, thanks to our sensitivity to yellow light. Also,
  since red light is the next most important and stopping sign requires
  visibility from longer distances (you need enough time to stop your
  vehicle), it makes sense makes sense to use red light for stopping
  because its highest wavelength and lowest scattering ensures it is the
  first thing a driver sees from a distance.

To Strauss, religion is simply a massive, ancient traffic light designed to navigate the chaos of the human soul. Now how does Strauss apply this to religion?

What makes this a system?

Not the colours themselves.  
Not physics alone.  
Not convention alone.

It is the **relations between positions**.

There are three key structural features:

1.  Binary opposition (Go vs Stop)

2.  Mediation (Yellow between them that acts as a transition)

3.  Constraint from natural perception (visibility spectrum, scattering)
    determines what colour should function as what

The traffic light works because:

- Nature provides oppositions (differences in wavelengths, retinal
  sensitivity, scattering variation)

- The mind organises them and assigns social rules to those oppositions.

This is exactly the template Strauss applies to religion. Structuralism starts with just one premise which is :

> Humans everywhere think according to similar deep patterns, even when
> their cultures look wildly different on the surface.

But, what is the universal grammar of the langue of culture or the deep pattern that is determined by the constraints on the shared physiology of humanity? Our shared nature forces us to exhibit what kinds of cultural patterns ? Something happened to Strauss in Brazil that books most of the time compress into a neat sentence like : “*Strauss discovered the universality of mental structures*”. But, this was actually a gradual revelation for him when he noticed patterns of organisation repeating from one tribal group to another. As said earlier, Strauss was not cataloguing cultural expressions but the patters and structures that organise those expressions. Think of it like this

> COOKING :   
> On the surface - thousands of varieties of cuisines  
> Beneath - same principles of cooking and nutrition governed by same
> categories (e.g. boiling, roasting, frying, grains, greens, fruits,
> etc..)

> MUSIC :   
> On the surface - thousands of varieties of compositions and melodies
>   
> Beneath - same principles of octave based note, harmony, and scale,
> and rhythm

Strauss now says :

> MYTH :   
> On the surface - thousands of different stories   
> Beneath - a handful of recurring conceptual oppositions (life vs
> death, order vs chaos, nature vs culture, etc…)

Cultures across the world are all expressions (paroles) of this universal cognitive architecture which serves as the langue. A myth’s universal core lay not in its plot but in how its events reflect binary tensions. Structuralism at its core has four basic assumptions

1.  Human thought has universal patterns based on universally shared
    mental structure

2.  These structures reveal through oppositions or binaries

3.  Surface differences conceal this deep similarity

4.  To understand culture, analyse relationships and not isolated facts

Every custom, belief, and story is not random but a tile in a mosaic whose patterns can be seen only from above. What are they?

### Binary Oppositions

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

To understand why Lévi-Strauss believed that the human mind is a “logical machine,” we have to go deeper into the “source code” he borrowed from linguistics. While his predecessors looked at culture as a collection of stories, he looked at it as a system of **contrasts**. To do this, he relied on the work of the linguist he met in New York named **Roman Jakobson**, who argued that human thought is built on the smallest possible unit of logic: the **binary opposition**. In linguistics, a binary opposition is not just a “pair”; it is a **constraint** of our biological hardware.

1.  **At the Level of Phonemes (The Atoms) :** Our ears cannot
    distinguish a sound in isolation. We only hear the “p” in *pat*
    because it is “not-b.” A “p” is voiceless; a “b” is voiced. This is
    a binary switch. Jakobson argued that our brain is a
    “difference-detecting machine.” Without the binary contrast of
    voiced “b” versus voiceless “p”, there is no signal, only noise.

2.  **At the Level of Words (The Categories) :** Once we have sounds,
    the mind uses that same “binary switch” to create categories of
    meaning. As we noted with Saussure in the “tree” vs. “non-Tree”
    example, we define the world by what things are *not*. Our mind
    “fences” off a section of the world and label it “male” by
    contrasting it with “female,” or “high” by contrasting it with
    “low”, or “tree” by contrasting it with “non-tree” (This view is
    just Kant refracted through Saussure).

Strauss realised that if language could be studied as a structured system where structures are created by the networks of binary fences that categorise, why not other elements of culture like myth, ritual, social structure, or taboo? It is these oppositional categories that help us to carve order out of chaos. These oppositional categorisations (hot/cold, big/small, tree/non-tree) helps us to :

- simplify complexity

- recognise patterns

- assign values

- make decisions

Strauss noted some binary oppositions that are at the heart of myth making and religion. The human mind functions like a compulsive organizer, incapable of perceiving the world without sorting it into tidy, opposing pairs. To Lévi-Strauss, this **binary opposition** is the beating heart of structuralism. Just as language relies on “minimal pairs”—where the sharp difference between a “c” and a “b” is the only thing creating the distinction between *cat* and *bat*, culture relies on conceptual extremes to carve order out of chaos. Without “dark,” the concept of “light” has no meaning; without “chaos,” “order” is just a transparent, empty word. This isn’t merely a lack of imagination; it is a cognitive necessity. The mind requires these contrasts to produce meaning, ensuring that the overwhelming sensory input of reality doesn’t blend into an unrecognisable, meaningless soup.

This is where Lévi-Strauss made a revolutionary distinction. In everyday language, these binaries are **“cold.”** They are just organizational tools. Calling a tree a “tree” and not a “rock” doesn’t cause you any emotional distress; it just helps you navigate the forest. However, in myths and religion, these binaries become **“hot.”** Myths deal with the “big binaries” : the profound ones that involve our survival, our origins, and our inevitable end. When the human mind applies its binary hardware to things like

- **life vs death**

- **nature vs culture**

- s**acred** vs **profane**

- **man vs wild**

- **man vs god**

- **city vs forest**

- **lust vs dispassion**

- **instinct vs morality**

- **family vs duty**

- **self vs other**

- **man vs woman**

- **etc**.. ,

it hits a massive “logical bug.” Because the human mind cannot logically resolve the fact that we are "Culture" (alive/moral) yet also "Nature" (doomed to die/decay), it generates myths as a way to "patch" the code.

Among these countless pairs, the “master opposition” that governs almost every human society is the divide between **nature** and **culture**. Nature represents everything that exists without human intervention, while culture is anything humans modify, organise, or control. This fundamental “fence” explains the hidden logic behind our most basic rituals: cooking transforms raw food (nature) into a meal (culture), and marriage transforms biological reproduction (nature) into a social institution (culture). We are biologically driven to impose symbolism and rules onto the raw material of life, obsessively defining our humanity by how we distance ourselves from the wild. Practically every human act involves policing this boundary, turning the “raw” material of existence into the “cooked” products of civilization.

Myths serve as the grand stage where these binary tensions are dramatised and negotiated. Rather than being random tales of gods behaving badly, myths function as “rehearsal spaces” for a society’s deepest anxieties, such as the conflict between the earth and the sky or the human and the animal. When a culture is torn between two values, say between freedom and community, its myths personify and explore that tension, allowing the group to “think through” a contradiction. A structuralist investigator ignores the “moral” of the story to look at the underlying architecture, asking not what a myth explains about lightning or weather, but how it manages the psychological friction between two impossible extremes.

Because living in a world of pure, black-and-white extremes would be brittle and unsustainable, the human mind also invents **mediators**. These are the “boundary-crossers”—like Trickster figures who are half-human and half-animal, or Shamans who bridge the world of the living and the dead. These mediators occupy the messy middle, softening the harsh edges of binary oppositions so that life can continue. While our mental “hardware” starts with dualities, it uses these mythic transformations and symbols to build bridges between them. These pairs form the mental scaffolding on which all societies construct meaning; once you begin to spot these cultural oppositions, the invisible rules of the human “operating system” finally start to click into place.

Lévi-Strauss revolutionized the study of myth by treating it not as a “primitive” fairy tale or a failed attempt at science, but as a sophisticated **“thinking machine.”** He argued that myths are the brain’s oldest software, used by societies to process contradictions too frightening or complex to express in plain language. Instead of asking what a myth “means” in a moral sense, a structuralist breaks the story down into its smallest conceptual molecules, called **mythemes**. Just as a single word only gains full meaning within a sentence, a single myth is incomplete until it is compared to its variations across different cultures. By mapping how these mythemes transform (for instance, comparing a story where fire is stolen from gods to one where it is a gift from animals) we can decode the underlying “myth-code” that regulates how a culture views the boundaries between the human, the divine, and the wild.

To analyse a myth structurally, one must ignore the “plot” (the mere surface story) and look at the hidden network of relations beneath it. Lévi-Strauss believed that myths endure because they remain useful tools for managing existential tensions, such as the conflict between life and death or between instinct and social rules. By reading hundreds of stories back-to-back, he demonstrated that the human mind constantly recycles a handful of structural solutions to the same mental puzzles, regardless of the continent. This makes the supernatural elements of myths—talking jaguars, shape-shifting birds, or jealous gods—mere costumes for ordinary human anxieties. This “structural X-ray vision” reveals that the cultural code being written today in superhero sagas or science fiction is the same universal software that humans have used for millennia to make the unbearable contradictions of life finally bearable.

**Step 2: Identify the Governing Binary Oppositions**

The central structural oppositional axis of the Ramayana is Palace vs Forest. This is not merely geographical or moral but is semiotic according to Strauss.

Palace signifies:

- Centrality

- Visibility

- Legitimacy

- Political structure

Forest signifies:

- Marginality

- Liminality

- Ambiguity

- Suspension of hierarchy

Movement between palace and forest is not psychological transformation but displacement within a signifying structure. The hero’s exile repositions him within the system; it does not reveal hidden moral depth. From the bundles we made, the epic is structured around the following recurring tensions:

1.  **Kinship vs Social Order**

2.  **Marriage Bond vs Public Legitimacy**

3.  **Nature vs Culture (Forest vs City)**

4.  **Desire vs Moral Law**

5.  **Blood Loyalty vs Ethical Allegiance**

These are not moral lessons but unresolved contradictions.

**Kinship vs Social Order**

Some events **overvalue blood** (red):  
A mother privileges her biological son; vengeance is taken for a sister; loyalty overrides stability.

Other events **undervalue blood** (green):  
A father sacrifices his son to keep a vow; a king abandons his wife to preserve reputation.

The epic oscillates between these extremes. If kinship dominates, order collapses. If law dominates, intimacy is sacrificed. The contradiction cannot be erased but only redistributed. The **undervaluing of blood** is the structural compensation for the **overvaluing of blood**. In a Straussian grid, if the system "overheats" with too much intimacy, it must "cool down" with a cold, structural sacrifice to prevent total collapse. Overvaluation of blood (mother → son) transforms into undervaluation of blood (father → son exile).

**Marriage vs Public Legitimacy**

The fire ordeal and later abandonment reveal a deeper tension: Is truth private or public?

The wife’s fidelity is not enough as a personal fact. It must become socially legible. Finally, with the exile of Sita back to the forest, marital life is not dissolved because it is false but because it is politically fragile. Legitimacy requires visible proof.

**Nature vs Culture**

The forest initially appears as punishment and as an exile from civilisation. But structurally it becomes a mediating zone. In the forest:

- Blood loyalties are tested.

- Desire destabilizes order.

- Cross-species alliances form.

Nature is not chaos; it is a laboratory. When city logic fails, mediation emerges from the wilderness. The alliance with animals and the defection of an enemy brother are structural bridges (blue): solutions come from outside the rigid system of royal blood. For Strauss, the forest is the "**raw**" and the city is the "**cooked**." When the "cooked" logic of the palace becomes too rigid and brittle, the hero must return to the "raw" of the forest to forge **cross-species alliances** (the animal mediators). These alliances allow the hero to bring back a "refined" order that the palace could not generate on its own.

**Desire vs Moral Law**

Natural impulse repeatedly appears only to be suppressed:

- Illicit desire leads to humiliation.

- Ambition is refused.

- Passion is subordinated to duty.

The narrative transforms natural impulse into social law through mediating operations. Yet suppressing instinct generates tragedy. The tension remains active.

**Blood Loyalty vs Ethical Allegiance**

One brother wages war for family honor.  
Another brother defects for righteousness.

The same kinship principle produces opposite results. Blood can destabilize or stabilize. The epic does not condemn blood ties; it demonstrates their ambivalence.

**Transformations Across the Narrative**

Structural analysis tracks transformations, not chronology:

- A private promise becomes public crisis.

- A domestic manipulation becomes cosmic war.

- Exile becomes alliance.

- Purification becomes abandonment.

Each crisis shifts the site of contradiction rather than eliminating it.

**The Structural Pattern**

If blood dominates → political disorder.  
If law dominates → personal tragedy.  
If desire dominates → violence.  
If suppression dominates → sorrow.

So the epic inserts mediators:

- Duty without desire.

- Alliance beyond blood.

- Fire ordeal converting private truth into public legitimacy.

But no final harmony appears. Resolution does require sacrifice.

**What the Epic Is Doing Structurally**

Seen this way, Strauss would say that the *Ramayana* is not preaching ideal behaviour. It is staging a recurring problem:

> How can a ruler be simultaneously son, husband, brother, and king ;
> when each role demands mutually incompatible loyalties?

The epic’s answer is not “choose one.” It is: oscillate, sacrifice, mediate ; and accept loss. The story restores order, but only by renewing rupture. The system survives, but intimacy pays the cost. That is why the epic feels both triumphant and tragic. Structurally, this is not inconsistency. It is design. The Ramayana expresses, through transformations, the logical structure of oppositions present in the society’s conceptual system and provides a liveable example for people to navigate around them.

> Strauss argued that myths are **"logical patches"** for "logical
> bugs." You cannot "solve" the fact that being a king and being a
> husband are not always compatible; you can only use a myth to
> **mediate** the friction between them so that society can continue to
> function despite the tragedy.

To summarise the school of structuralism as a whole then taken from the book on cultural anthropology by A.R.N. Srivastava, we have that :

> **The task of structuralism is to break myths into their constituent
> mythemes, uncover the unconscious meanings found in the binary
> relationships between them. Uncovering this hidden structural code
> will reveal the essential and universal elements (the langue) of human
> thought.**

In the Ramayana, take one particular theme : the opposition between **palace and forest** (culture vs nature, **ordered centre** vs **wild periphery**) is not eliminated but mediated. Exile creates movement across the boundary, and alliances with vanaras, and defectors convert wilderness into a partner of civilisation. Claude Lévi-Strauss would argue that this is not unique. **Across mythic systems,** **the boundary between centre and periphery is repeatedly crossed through exile, initiation, alliance, or liminal figures who are neither fully inside nor outside**. The forest is never just forest ; it is a structural term in a binary system that requires mediation.

Greek myth provides parallel mechanisms. In the story of Oedipus, exposure in the wilderness precedes kingship; abandonment in nature mediates accession to the city. Theseus travels from rural margins to Athens, defeating monstrous embodiments of wild chaos before entering civic space. In the cult of Dionysus, forest ecstasy temporarily invades the polis, dissolving order only to reconstitute it ritually.

In Mesopotamian myth of the epic of Gilgamesh, a priest’s daughter civilises a man Enkidu who was abandoned in the forest and brought up among animals ; and Enkidu comes to the city to have an intense fight with the king Gilgamesh, after which he befriends the wild man Enkidu, who now moves from animal existence into urban life, mediating between nature and civilisation; even their cedar forest expedition ritualises the encounter with wildness rather than abolishing it. Finally after Enkidu dies, Gilgamesh adventures to far off places seeking immortality in vain, when it dawns upon him finally that the only way humans could become immortal is by creating a legacy, and then comes back to become a very good king.

The Hebrew Bible shows a similar structure in the opposition between promised land and desert. Abraham leaves homeland into wilderness; Israel wanders before settlement; Joseph is exiled to Egypt before preserving his family; and Moses encounters revelation outside the political center. Wilderness (the wandering in the desert, and the estrangement of the patriarchs) becomes preparation space rather than mere absence of order.

In Norse myth, Midgard (human realm) is encircled by giant lands; Thor and Loki repeatedly cross the boundary, while Yggdrasil connects cosmic levels vertically.

Among many Indigenous American myths analyzed by Lévi-Strauss, village/forest and raw/cooked oppositions are mediated through tricksters and culture heroes who obtain fire, food, or marriage rules from animals where the wilderness of nature itself becomes source of culture.

Across these traditions, removal from the centre (whether through exile, abandonment, wandering, or initiation) often functions as a mediating operation : separation from the social centre =\> contact with liminal beings =\> acquisition or transformation of power or knowledge =\> reintegration into structured order. The specific narrative device varies from myth to myth; what recurs is not a universal motif but a structural necessity.

> Binary oppositions such as centre/periphery, culture/nature, or
> order/wildness generate tensions that myths symbolically negotiate.
> Because neither pole can be eliminated without collapsing the system
> of differences that gives it meaning, myths repeatedly produce
> mediating figures, spaces, and transformations.

The opposition of palace and forest, therefore, is not a simple antagonism but a relational pair within a system that continually reworks the contradiction it encodes. This example provides a foundation for understanding how Lévi-Strauss derives structures through comparison: not by identifying identical themes, but by tracing transformations of oppositions across different mythic corpora according to the principles of structural analysis.

There are many more diverse examples of binaries and their recurring and universal methods of resolution that Strauss gives but this example has already given you the foundation for understanding all the other examples that he mentions in his work and also enable you to follow how he derives the various universal structures from the principles of his school of thought.

### Critics of Structuralism

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Despite its intellectual brilliance, the primary criticism against Strauss centres on his unapologetic **reductionism**. By treating culture as a “logical machine” designed to solve binary contradictions, he effectively abstracts out the vibrant, messy, meaningful, and emotional reality of human religion to reduce it into a series of cold mathematical equations. In his quest to find the universal “hardware” of the human mind, the actual human being (the “subject”) and his agency disappears entirely. Critics call this the “**death of the subject**”: in a structuralist world, people are not creative agents making their own meaning; they are merely puppets whose every thought, myth, and ritual is a pre-determined “parole” dictated by the invisible, rigid rules of the “langue”.

> Imagine being told that your most inspired and unique meaningful
> culinary creation is not a product of your own creativity, but merely
> a mathematical necessity. To a structuralist, your cooking is nothing
> more than a mechanical mediation of binary oppositions : sweet vs.
> salt, liquid vs. solid, raw vs. cooked, hot vs cold, etc. In this
> view, distinct cuisines and recipes are distractions that do not
> matter; everyone who is cooking is simply a biological machine running
> a universal program that would produce essentially the same phenomenon
> in a kitchen, whether in Chennai or in Paris or a village in the
> Amazon.
>
> This is the primary frustration with structuralism that many people
> share : it provides a perfect description of the universally shared
> basic categories and processes of cooking (determined from a shared
> biology for all humans) but loses the flavour of the chef and the
> taste of the food. By reducing the vibrant, diverse, and emotional
> reality of human experience to a series of cold equations, it gives us
> the "syntax" of the human spirit while leaving us wondering where have
> the food and the chef gone away from this picture.

Furthermore, his approach is famously **ahistorical**. By taking a “synchronic” or frozen snapshot of a culture, he ignores how religions change, adapt, or are forged in the fires of history. For many, his “**science of the concrete**” is simply too cold; it provides a perfect map of the brain’s wiring but leaves us with a religion that has no heart, no history, and no room for the unique, lived experience of the believer. He gives us the “syntax” of the human spirit but leaves us wondering if anyone is actually speaking the language.

### Structuralism in the Hindu Context

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Here are three famous ways Indologists have used Strauss’s “grammarian” approach :

1.  **The “Erotic Ascetic”: Wendy Doniger**

In her work ***Siva: The Erotic Ascetic*** **(1973)**, Wendy Doniger applied structuralist logic to the figure of Shiva. Shiva is the ultimate “mediator.” He is the great yogi sitting in icy meditation (asceticism/tapas), yet he is also the passionate lover of Parvati (desire/kama). Doniger argued that the human mind needs Shiva because we struggle to reconcile spiritual power with physical desire. Shiva is the “bridge” that proves you can be both. By comparing his role in various myths like his interaction with the love god Kama, his marriage to Parvati, and other Puranik tales, she concludes that Shiva is the mediator between lust and abstinence. But as usual, many traditional scholars argue that this approach is **reductionist**. By stripping Shiva down to a “logical category” of A vs. B, Doniger often ignores the *bhakti* (devotion) and the lived spiritual experience of the devotee. Critics also argue her focus on the “erotic” is a Western projection that over-sexualises the structural logic. You can buy her book by clicking [here](https://amzn.in/d/0bql7Gnj).

2.  **The Hierarchy of Purity: Louis Dumont**

**Louis Dumont** used a structuralist methodology in ***Homo Hierarchicus*** **(1966)** to explain the jati (caste) system in Hinduism. He argued that Indian society is built on a single binary: **the pure vs. the impure**. Just as “red” only means “stop” in relation to “Green,” the brahmin (pure) only exists in relation to the untouchable (impure). For Dumont, caste isn’t about “power” or “money”; it’s an “ideology of purity” that fences the entire social world. Scholars counter that Dumont’s view is **ahistorical**. By treating “purity” as a timeless mental grammar, Dumont ignored how British colonial laws actually hardened and changed these categories. His “structure” makes a historical oppression look like an inevitable law of the mind.

3.  **The Meaningless Ritual: Frits Staal**

In ***Agni*** **(1983)**, **Frits Staal** argued that Vedic rituals are structured exactly like a language’s syntax. He claimed that the “meaning” of a ritual (what god it’s for) is secondary to the **rules of its performance**. Like a sentence, a ritual must follow a precise “grammar” to be correct. He famously claimed rituals are “meaningless” in the sense that they don’t point to anything outside themselves; they are self-contained logical machines. His critics find this **overly formalistic** and by claiming that the ritual is “empty”, Staal effectively silences the actual practitioners who believe their rituals have profound symbolic and spiritual meaning. You can purchase the book [here](https://amzn.in/d/0iYQWsfy).

# The Symbolic Anthropology of Clifford Geertz

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

If Claude Lévi-Strauss claimed to give the “X-ray” of the human mind, showing us the cold, universal skeleton of logic that holds all cultures together, we are left with a haunting question: **Where is the life in the bones?** Structuralism claims to have succeeded in mapping the “hardware” of humanity, but in doing so, it effectively silenced the individual. By reducing the Ramayana or the Vedas to a series of mathematical equations, it ignores the tears of the devotee, the specific history of the land, the agency of people, and the unique flavour that a culture brings to its own story. If Strauss viewed culture as a logical machine to be decoded, our next thinker, **Clifford Geertz**, viewed it as a “*manuscript to be read*”.

Geertz’s departure from structuralism was fundamentally a shift from the search for universal "laws" to the search for local "meaning". He famously argued that man is an animal suspended in webs of significance that he himself has spun, and that the analysis of these webs is not an experimental science in search of a "myth-code," but an interpretive one in search of significance. Geertz argued that the “**science of the concrete**” was too distant and too cold. He believed that the job of an anthropologist is not to find universal laws like a physicist, but to find meaning like a literary critic. He wanted to move from the “thin description” of Straussian a structuralist grid to what he famously called **“thick description”** which is an approach that puts the chef back in the kitchen and the heart back into the ritual. He wanted to bring back the native voice instead of an external account or abstract universal rules, divorced from the lived experiences of the people. He criticised this external approach in his writing as :

> *If you only attend to external actions, you would have no way to
> distinguish between a simple twitch in the eye and the wink.*

A twitch would we be a random involuntary movement of the eyelid, a wink is a voluntary action that is a pattern communicating something to somebody. As a way of recommending a procedure for field observation, he used the term ‘thick description’ to mean going beyond a simple description involving surface details and make notes of linkages that would form a cultural web of significances. For example, not just nothing that X winked at Y but also what X meant by that and why X winked at Y. This is all Straussian but Geertz wanted to determine that using fieldwork. Also, he wanted to describe evolution and change rather than focusing on static structures. For Geertz, the "thick description" of a wink involves understanding the layers of irony, conspiracy, or rehearsal that give the physical movement its social life. He believed that without these layers of context, the anthropologist is merely observing biology, not culture.

This distinction can be summarized:

- **Lévi-Strauss:** What logical relations organize myths beneath the
  surface?

- **Geertz:** What meanings do cultural actors attribute to symbols in
  lived contexts?

The difference is epistemological.

> Structuralism seeks formal relations independent of actors’ awareness.
> Interpretive anthropology seeks understanding of actors’ own symbolic
> worlds.

Yet both share a commitment to pattern. The divergence lies in where pattern is located: in **deep structure** or in **symbolic interpretation.**

Geertz studied lot about Islam in Morocco and Indonesia as a part of his fieldwork. Once, he writes that he witnessed a funeral in a Balinese village that disoriented into chaos. In the 1960s, there were Muslim groups in Indonesia which were pushing its Muslims in observing a stricter form of Islam rather than the syncretised animistic water-downed variety of Islam that had been dominant on the island. When a death happened in a village, it was normally the task of the village head man to make arrangements for the funeral and officiate it. Once in a village where Geertz was working, a ten year old boy had died in the middle of the night and the parents were called over and people set right to work as the custom in the village was that the body had to be buried within 24 hours of death. The village headman however decided to use this funeral to push his Islamisation agenda and set a condition that he would perform the funeral only if those assembled would convert to a stricter form of Islam and that the funeral would be done according to strict Islamic rules. This created a crisis because as the boy’s body lay in the sun, people squabbled with each other and the situation was dissolving into tears and screams as people wanted to just get it over with so that they could grieve in the normal way and get closure. At this, they were paralysed emotionally and were staring into space, not knowing what to do.

### Religion as Worldview and Ethos

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Geertz said that the structuralist could not study this situation as it is static and could not handle transitions as competing religious sects fought to win over each other. So, he decided that what is needed was a pragmatic approach that left the langue and look deeply at the parole. In his essay “Religion as a cultural system”, he proposes to begin the study by proposing a definition of religion. He says that this is not a definition set in stone to contain religion definitively but only as a paradigm and hence his definition of religion is :

> Religion, an aspect of culture, is a **system of symbols** which act
> to establish powerful, pervasive, and long lasting moods and
> motivations in men by formulating conceptions of a general order of
> existence and clothing these conceptions with such an aura of
> factuality that the moods and motivations seem uniquely realistic.

What this system does? He points out that religion is a system of symbols that model reality and creates an order of existence. But. he points out that the word “model” can be used in two different senses :

1.  Models of reality (what-is) : **Worldview :** The way you look at
    the world and structure it and get a picture of the world as you see
    it

2.  Models for behaviour (what-to-do) : **Ethos :** The model by which
    you can go out into the world and do things which is a code of
    behaviour or conduct

In this framework, religion serves as a bridge that ensures the "ethos" (the way we act and feel) is grounded in a "worldview" (the way the universe actually is). Religion makes it seem as though our moral rules are not just arbitrary inventions, but are built into the fabric of the cosmos. Like Kant, he agrees that we do not get the world as it is but it comes to us pre-digested my mediating mental symbols and meanings. The way in which we create this symbolic and meaningful model of the world is quite important for humans as we do not have strong instincts like animals. Geertz agreed with another earlier thinker called Peter Berger that to have this model threatened in any way constitutes a grave fundamental crisis of being, called **anomy** (dissolution of order). He tells us that there are three different ways in which our worldview can reach its limits and get threatened.

- **Cognitive crisis** : Encountering something that can no longer be
  adequately explained by the worldview. Everyone wants to feel
  reassured that his worldview can deal with anything that can be
  explained it.

-  **Pointless suffering** : Geertz believed that humans could endure
  any amount of suffering as long as they thought it was meaningful but
  when suffering seems random, it threatens their worldview and in these
  situations, the worldview must rise the occasion and provide a
  plausible account failing which things fall apart.

- **Evil** : It is often noted that the Holocaust was the cause of
  massive irreligion in the West. When one cannot fit extreme evil into
  the worldview, again this threatens the religion.

To Geertz, religion should act and indeed does as a psychological "template" that prevents humans from becoming "mental basket cases" in the face of these three threats. It provides the aura of factuality needed to make sense of the senseless. Assuming that we have a worldview that has sufficiently withstood all these crises, the next thing that follows from it is ethos. The relationship that ethos has with worldview is that if you believe the world is a certain way, then it makes sense to act within it in certain ways. If you are a Christian and its worldview tells you that humanity is aliened from God by sin, then its ethos makes us do things to rectify this relationship. He uses the terms “moods” and “motivations” to describe two aspects of ethos.

- **Moods** : This emotional or effective aspect is a way of feeling
  about things and tells us what is appropriate to feel when and where.
  They have intensity but not direction.

- **Motivations** : This is a propensity it act in a certain way in a
  certain direction established by habit.

Not just religion but cultural also is essential for worldview and ethos. He says that rituals are the events when both the worldview and ethos intersect which both enact and reinforce the worldview and ethos. Geertz uses the description of a dramatic ritual in Bali in which the witch character **Rangda** and the monster-buffoon **Barong** come into combat as a reflection of this interaction. The basic plot reinforces the point that there is no pure, unambiguous good or evil in the world. The strife between opposing forces is never resolved, only quelled for the time being until the defeated force regroups. He noted that the audience in the drama is never merely passive; the emotional effect of the ritual leaves them convinced of the power of their world view and he even notes that quite a bit of people started fighting and lacerating themselves after going into a trance and identifying themselves with one of the two characters. Having had their world view reinforced in the ritual, they then carry its power and influence even into their daily lives. Unlike Strauss, Geertz spent no time trying to systematise the symbols and describe the “web” they form, but instead described how they lead to certain moods and motivations.

### Culture as Text

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Geertz often speaks of culture as if it were a text. Not because it is written, but because it is interpretable. Symbols are public; their meanings are socially transmitted. Anthropologists, therefore, engage in a kind of hermeneutics (textual interpretation). This textual metaphor distinguishes him from Lévi-Strauss. Structuralism seeks an underlying grammar of interpretation for all texts in all traditions. Geertz seeks interpretation of particular passages in a tradition.

In his work “***The Interpretation of Cultures**”*, he writes that anthropologists do not aim to produce law-like generalisations akin to physics. Instead, they aim to produce convincing interpretations of meaning. The goal is not universality but plausibility within context. Applied to the palace/forest theme, Geertz would ask:

- How do participants narrate exile?

- What moral emotions are attached to departure and return?

- How are forest beings imagined?

- What political authority is symbolically constructed through the act
  of return?

Rather than deriving a formula (Center : Periphery :: Culture : Nature), he would reconstruct the cultural logic internal to the narrative tradition.

### Negara: Theatre State

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Geertz’s historical ethnography “***Negara***” illustrates his interpretive approach to political order. Studying nineteenth-century Bali, he argues that the state was not primarily a bureaucratic apparatus but a “theatre state.” Power was enacted through ritual spectacle. Authority was aesthetic and ceremonial before it was administrative. This is crucial. For Lévi-Strauss, myth expresses structural logic. For Geertz, ritual enacts symbolic meaning. In a theatre state, palace and forest are not abstract structural poles. The palace becomes a stage upon which cosmic order is dramatized. Ritual processions, cremation ceremonies, and calendrical rites enact a vision of the cosmos. The periphery does not merely oppose the centre; it is incorporated into ceremonial display. Thus, political order itself is symbolic performance.

Geertz was often critical of grand universal theories. Structuralism, in his view, risked over-formalization. By abstracting myths into diagrams of opposition and mediation, it risked flattening historical specificity. He once suggested that the anthropologist’s task is not to uncover hidden structures but to make sense of meaning in context. Culture is not a code waiting to be deciphered through algebraic transformation; it is a lived symbolic environment. In contrast to Lévi-Strauss’s “***Mythologiques**”*, which compares hundreds of myths across the Americas, Geertz typically focuses intensely on specific locales : Bali, Morocco, Java. His comparisons are more modest, more historically grounded.

### Shared Commitments and Divergences

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Despite differences, Geertz and Lévi-Strauss share important commitments:

1.  Both reject reduction of culture to economics or biology.

2.  Both treat symbolic systems as central to social life.

3.  Both emphasize pattern rather than randomness.

Their divergence lies in analytic ambition.

- Lévi-Strauss seeks formal, cross-cultural structural invariants.

- Geertz seeks interpretive understanding within particular traditions.

In this sense, Geertz can be read as humanising structuralism. He retains attention to pattern but embeds it in thick description.

Returning to the palace/forest theme: how would Geertz reinterpret it? He would begin not with a structural formula but with lived meanings:

- What emotions surround exile?

- How is kingship imagined?

- What moral ideals are dramatized in withdrawal from power?

- How do audiences respond?

Exile might be a metaphor for spiritual testing. It might symbolize renunciation. It might encode political commentary. Its significance depends on the symbolic universe of the society. Geertz would resist saying that exile universally mediates centre and periphery. Instead, he would argue that exile, in each case, is a culturally specific symbol that communicates a conception of authority, virtue, and cosmic order.

### Geertzian dissection of Ramayana

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

A Geertzian interpretation of the Ramayana would begin not by extracting a universal structure but by situating the epic within the symbolic world of South Asian civilisation. For Clifford Geertz, culture is a “web of significance” spun by human beings, and the task of interpretation is to render those webs intelligible. The *Ramayana* would therefore be approached as a text-in-action: recited, performed, ritualised, staged in festivals, embodied in ethical discourse, and invoked in political rhetoric. Rather than asking what binary oppositions underlie the epic, a Geertzian reading asks what conceptions of kingship, duty, kinship, and cosmic order are made meaningful through its narrative. The epic becomes not merely a story but a model of how the world is and how it ought to be.

Central to such an interpretation would be the concept of dharma. Rama’s exile is not only a narrative device but a thickly meaningful act embedded in a moral universe where obedience to paternal command, fidelity to truth, and preservation of cosmic order outweigh personal interest. A Geertzian analysis would explore how audiences understand Rama’s compliance with exile: as an enactment of righteous kingship, as a dramatization of self-mastery, and as an affirmation that sovereignty rests on moral integrity. The forest, in this reading, is not simply “nature” opposed to “culture” but a charged moral landscape and a site of testing, ascetic discipline, and divine encounter. Its significance derives from how it participates in a wider symbolic system linking kingship, renunciation, and sacred geography.

The relationship between Rama and Sita would likewise be interpreted as a cultural text. Sita’s abduction and ordeal are not reducible to a structural opposition between purity and danger; they are embedded in social understandings of marriage, honor, gendered virtue, and public legitimacy. The fire ordeal, for example, can be read as a ritualized assertion of purity that speaks to anxieties about female chastity and royal authority. A Geertzian account would examine how this episode has been historically received, debated, and performed, showing that its meaning is not fixed but continually renegotiated within public discourse. The epic thus sustains “moods and motivations” concerning loyalty, sacrifice, and social order.

The figure of Ravana would also be treated not merely as chaos opposed to order but as a symbol whose power lies in cultural ambiguity. Ravana is learned, devoted, and kingly, yet transgressive. His characterization allows the epic to dramatize the dangers of power unrestrained by dharma. In performance traditions such as Ramlila, his defeat is staged as a public reaffirmation of moral order. From a Geertzian perspective, this ritual dramatization is crucial: the burning of Ravana’s effigy is not symbolic in an abstract sense but constitutive of collective sentiment. It renders visible and emotionally compelling a shared conception of good governance and cosmic justice.

Finally, a Geertzian interpretation would stress the epic’s role in shaping political imagination. The idea of “Rama Rajya” has historically functioned as an idiom for just rule, invoked in devotional, reformist, and nationalist contexts. The Ramayana thereby operates as a cultural charter: a narrative through which authority is evaluated and idealised. Rather than seeking universal structures beneath the text, a Geertzian analysis shows how the epic acts as a reservoir of symbols through which communities interpret their world. It is a story that not only reflects social values but helps produce them, weaving together ethics, emotion, and power into a dense and enduring web of meaning.

### Geertz in the Hindu Context

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

From the perspective of a practicing Hindu rooted in sampradāya (lineage), temple life, and lived ritual, the interpretive anthropology of Clifford Geertz raises a serious concern, once again because it is reductionistic. It treats Hindu traditions primarily as symbolic systems ; as “webs of meaning” to be interpreted ; rather than as living revelations, sacred obligations, and transformative spiritual paths. What for the practitioner is *satya* (truth) becomes, in this framework, a cultural text. What is experienced as divine presence becomes “symbol.” What is worship becomes “performance.” Here are three major areas where a traditional Hindu might see the Geertzian method as inadequate or distorting.

**Ritual as “Cultural Performance” vs. Ritual as Sacred Efficacy**

A Geertzian reading of temple pūjā, yajña, or Ramlila would emphasize symbolism, public mood, and collective meaning. Ritual becomes a dramatization of cosmic order. It generates emotional dispositions and reinforces social identity. But for a traditional practitioner, ritual is not merely expressive ; it is efficacious. When a priest performs a Vedic fire ritual, it is not a symbolic enactment of cosmic order; it is participation in cosmic order. The mantras are not cultural poetry; they are śruti ; revealed sound. The deity invoked is not a representation of moral ideals; the deity is present. To call ritual a “performance” (even respectfully) subtly relocates its centre of gravity. The meaning shifts from divine transaction to social communication. From a traditional standpoint, this is not a neutral description; it is a reduction. It replaces transcendence with interpretation. The practitioner does not attend temple to express cultural identity. He or she attends to receive darśana ; the reciprocal seeing between devotee and deity. This ontological claim disappears when ritual is framed as a symbolic text.

**Dharma as Symbolic System vs. Dharma as Binding Reality**

Geertzian anthropology would interpret concepts like dharma, karma, or moksha as components of a symbolic worldview that shapes “moods and motivations.” Dharma becomes a cultural model for ethical action. But for the traditional Hindu, dharma is not merely a moral vocabulary that sustains social order. It is a binding cosmic law ; ṛta extended into human life. It does not function because people collectively believe in it; rather, people flourish or suffer depending on their alignment with it.

When exile in the Ramayana is interpreted as a “symbolic dramatisation of kingship”, something essential is lost. Rama does not enact exile to communicate meaning. He obeys dharma because it is real. The moral authority of the story does not arise from shared cultural interpretation; it arises from alignment with cosmic truth. From within tradition, dharma precedes society. It is not constructed by it.

**The Deity as Symbol vs. The Deity as Living Presence**

In a Geertzian analysis, Rama, Shiva, or Devi are symbols that sustain powerful, pervasive moods and motivations. They render a worldview emotionally compelling. But a practicing Hindu does not approach Shiva as a symbol of asceticism and desire. Shiva is Mahadeva ; a living Lord who responds to prayer, grants grace, and manifests in murti form. Bhakti is not aesthetic appreciation of a cultural symbol; it is relationship.

When scholars describe Hindu gods as embodiments of cultural values, practitioners often experience this as flattening. It suggests that divinity is produced by social meaning rather than revealing itself through it. A murti in a temple is not a “representation” in the ordinary sense. After consecration (prāṇa pratiṣṭhā), it is treated as inhabited. Food is offered not metaphorically but literally. The deity is awakened, bathed, clothed, and put to rest. To interpret this solely as symbolic dramatization is, from the devotee’s standpoint, to misunderstand its ontology.

**The Deeper Concern: Translation Without Transformation**

At its most subtle, the traditional critique is not that interpretive anthropology is hostile. It is that it translates everything into its own vocabulary.

Grace becomes “charisma.”  
Darśana becomes “mutual gaze.”  
Dharma becomes “ethical ideology.”  
Temple becomes “ritual theatre.”

The vocabulary is elegant, even sympathetic. But the metaphysical claims are bracketed. What remains is meaning without metaphysics, symbolism without transcendence. For the practitioner, however, the metaphysical claim is central. The sacred is not a cultural construction; culture is an expression of the sacred.

**Experience vs. Description**

A final concern is experiential. Hindu practice is transformative. Through japa, pilgrimage, fasting, meditation, and devotion, the practitioner seeks inner change purification of mind, realisation of self, union with the divine. Interpretive anthropology describes how such practices generate shared meaning. But it does not account for the practitioner’s claim that these practices change consciousness in ways that transcend culture. When a yogi enters samādhi, this is not experienced as participating in a symbolic system. It is experienced as direct realization (anubhava). The practitioner may argue that anthropology can describe the external frame but cannot grasp the interior state.

From within the traditional Hindu standpoint, once again, the Geertzian approach is refined but incomplete. It honors meaning but sidelines truth claims. It respects symbolism but suspends ontology. It appreciates devotion but reframes it as cultural mood. If structuralism reduces Hinduism to logical oppositions, Geertz risks reducing it to symbolic expression. The practitioner would insist on something further: that ritual works, that dharma binds, that the deity is present, and that spiritual realisation is real. Any account that translates these into metaphor may be intellectually sophisticated ; but from within the tradition, it remains externally descriptive rather than internally faithful.

Thus, so far, we have seen many reductionist theories of religion, which is religion, seen through other disciplines. In these theories, you cannot talk about religion in religious terms and employ non-religious terminology. You might wonder as a lay practitioner

> What the heck happened to my religion?

Looks like we were simply talking about sociology or psychology, or anthropology, or semiotics, or culture. This is not surprising because when we use a non-religious worldview to study religion, we cannot expect to see anything religious. Sociological tools only give sociological information. That is not to say that all these reductionists are all wrong about all their conclusions. For instance :

- It is plausible that some religious rituals do indeed scientifically
  perform some beneficial function (like the Native American ritual of
  putting minnows in fields)

- It is plausible that myths do indeed possess universal structures that
  involve mediating between binary oppositions

- It is plausible that myths bind society together and renew its
  structures

- It is plausible that myths do provide psychological comfort

But, saying that myths and rituals only perform sociological functions or psychological functions or scientific based economic functions is where reductionisms goes over the board. It is true that religion indeed has a psychological, social, cultural, and economic dimension to it because religion is practised by humans who indeed have psychological, social, cultural, and economic dimension to them. But, the problem arises when one says that religion is just that and nothing more.

In the next article, we will see an entirely new class of approaches to religion which is the phenomenological approaches. These approaches recognise that one of the vital claims of religion is the existence to a reality of the sacred realm, which cannot be dismissed or understood or captured by any other means. It is about these theories that we will go to next.
