In this article, I give a detailed book review of the book “Chatuh Shloki Manusmriti” by **Nithin Sridhar** that can be purchased [here](https://amzn.in/d/09PtY0UP). I had the privilege of interacting with the author, first through social media (his twitter page [here](https://x.com/nkgrock?s=20)) and later personally in a conference. I felt at home talking to him and found that we had very similar tastes and I purchased his amazing book on Manusmṛti. This is the first book review I am writing and hence I hope I can do enough justice to the author. Given that multiple celebrities from both the traditional and modern intellectual sphere have written reviews, I am glad that the author still asked me to write mine and I have put my best efforts in doing it here.

You should not read this book review article to get a TLDR version of his book because I do not write about everything the book says (I want you to buy the book). In between, I also add some of my own excursive thoughts on the subject matter of the text that would enhance what the author is trying to say or provide a wider perspective on it, given my background in comparative religion. The flow of this article is not exactly the same as the flow of the book. I initially go chapter-wise and then go theme-wise.

# Place and function of Dharmaśāstras in Hinduism

After an introductory chapter on the origins and transmission of the Manusmṛti and his justifications for unitary authorship of the text, it is in Chapter 2 of his book, that gets right to the heart of the subject matter. The author starts this chapter with the commonly held view by modern Hindus which denigrate the importance of śāstras for Hinduism and instead understand Hinduism to be an exact anti-thesis of the rigid Abrahamic monotheisms where everything goes and no scripture is binding. Some of them are :

- Hinduism is not a book-based religion and Hindus are not bound to
  texts (unlike Christianity or Islam)

- Hinduism is completely flexible and is always in flux without any
  binding principles (unlike Christianity or Islam that demand
  orthodoxy)

- Texts like Manusmṛti are outdated now and the genre of smṛti is
  anyways not central to Hinduism (unlike Christianity or Islam with
  their texts)

- No Hindu keeps the Manusmṛti at home (unlike Christians and Muslims
  who keep their texts at home and read them regularly) and hence it has
  no relevance

- The problematic verses in the text of Manusmṛti are all later-day
  interpolations and we have to remove these verses

- Manu and his text were not important except for a narrow section of
  bookish brahminical class and it was the British who were looking for
  a Mosaic like text for Hinduism and thereby colluded with the brahmins
  and ended up putting these texts on central stage by making them the
  basis of Hindu law

The author gives multiple examples (both historical and textual) to demonstrate how important śāstras and smṛtis were to the Hindu tradition and also gives a basic primer into Hindu epistemology that is good enough for one to understand his next section on his arguments regarding how smṛtis were indeed justified and accepted as valid sources of knowledge regarding what constitutes dharma.

Most importantly, he goes into the well known popular issue today as to how smṛtis have no authority in comparison with the śruti. He says that in case of an apparent contradiction between the śruti and the smṛti on a given issue, the following three options in sequential order should be adopted.

1.  The contradictory purports apply in different contexts or for
    different peoples

2.  Both are valid alternatives

3.  (Only if both 1 and 2 are impossible) The śruti overrides the smṛti

He also pauses to explain that the third option in no way denies the authority of the smṛti text but just that :

> **The overriding of smṛti by a śruti** (due to irreconcilable
> contradiction) **is a matter of preference and that the contradictory
> smṛti purport can still be used as a valid alternative if the purport
> of the śruti is infeasible.**

Next, he has an important section on the nature of authority of the smṛti. He verily points out and accepts that :

> **Yes, the smṛtis are not law-books or commandments to be
> hegemonically imposed by a ruling authority or a clergy**

But he also points out that that this does not in any way mean that they were irrelevant or were of only marginal interest. They were very influential in the sense that :

> **The smṛtis did form the knowledge bank of Hindu society and served
> as a toolkit or a repository from which society could draw from to
> apply unto their own contexts**

But, if the smṛtis are not binding in a legal sense, in what sense are they exactly binding? The author also answers this question succinctly that :

> **The tenets of the smṛti are binding in the karmic sense : that an
> injunction like “The performance of so and so act leads bindingly to a
> so and so karmic result (karmaphala)**” **is bindingly true**

The author closes this chapter with an exhortation to the reader that whether he chooses to follow the dharmaśāstras or not, the texts themselves stand valid eternally and are binding karmically, despite not being binding in the legal sense.

I would also like to add my own perspectives from comparative religion to shed light on this aspect. Yes, many people say that Hinduism and other pagan religions do not have a Bible or a Quran. This is not exactly true at least for richly intellectual traditions like Hinduism, Chinese religions, and Hellenism where there were rich varieties of texts that were being produced. Those texts could not have been produced for no reason and definitely they would have been influential in some way, right?

The thing is that while Hinduism does not have a Bible or a Quran, it does have many scriptures, each of which take up some function of the Bible in Western society or of the Quran in Islamic society. In Western Christendom, the Bible served all the following functions simultaneously :

1.  **Divine Inspiration :** It was divinely (at least supernaturally)
    inspired and revealed to a special class of people called prophets

2.  **Doctrine :** It was doctrinally binding for all matters of belief

3.  **Legal/Ethical Prescript :** Its commandments were legally and
    theologically binding for all matters of practice

4.  **Common Knowledge :** It has been widely read and understood by all
    practitioners of the religion and has been widely enjoyed and
    translated into many languages

5.  **Narrative Glue :** It provided the foundational narrative glue for
    Christian society, especially on matters of ultimate origins and
    teleology - who are we? where do we all come from? who is the God
    that created us? where are we all headed to?

6.  **Knowledge Bank :** It served as the knowledge bank for all
    cultural elements that the West drew from along with other secular
    texts - premodern Western art, literature, law, philosophy, and
    inspiration for social behaviour - are all incomprehensible without
    the Bible

In Hinduism, we have no single text that performs some of these six functions but we do have many texts that when taken together perform all these six functions.

1.  **Divine Inspiration :** In Hinduism, the Vedas are
    uncontroversially accepted as divinely or supernaturally revealed
    texts to a special class of ancient seers. But, the Vedas were not
    widely read or understood in the sense of 3 ; the mantric and
    ritualistic part of the Vedic canon are regarded as powerful and
    sacred not for their content and meaning but for their sounds itself
    ; the Upaniṣadic part of the Vedic canon was regarded sacred and
    ultimate truth but they were not directly understood and their
    mostly metaphorical content came to be differently interpreted by
    different sects of philosophical schools.

2.  **Doctrine :** As for doctrinal issues, each Hindu sect considered
    its own important sectarian text as the binding authority in matters
    of right belief ; again this does not mean that anything goes as
    valid belief in Hinduism but just that there was a well-defined
    sectarian diversity regarding matters of doctrine.

3.  **Legal/Ethical Prescript :** It is in this sense that Hinduism has
    no text whose commandments exactly serve this function and it is
    here when we say that Hinduism is a contextual religion where local
    kings and brahmins of their own time drew from the śāstras and
    applied it as they deemed fit for their context, time, and
    situation. The matters of the dharmaśāstras were hence, while being
    karmically binding, and provided the starting point for Hindu law,
    it was not being directly considered legally binding and the actual
    legal prescript depended on the particular local and temporal
    circumstances.

4.  **Common Knowledge :** In Hinduism, the last three functions are
    performed undoubtedly by the itihāsas and purāṇas and especially in
    the sense that they were common knowledge for all Hindus - that are
    widely read, understood, and enjoyed by all and find diverse
    translations and regional expressions in many languages.

5.  **Narrative Glue :** In Hinduism, the itihāsas and purāṇas are also
    the major source of texts that provide the ultimate narrative glue
    for Hindu society regarding their origins, the nature and acts of
    their gods, and their ultimate teleology. In this regard, they are
    supplemented by other regional narratives such as well.

6.  **Knowledge Bank :** These itihāsas and purāṇas also served as
    knowledge bank in the sense that all cultural elements of Hindu
    society drew from. Other regional texts also were embedded into
    these larger itihāsaic and purāṇic narrative to supplement them and
    provide a foundation for art, metaphors, literature, architecture,
    law, folk philosophy, and inspiration for social behaviour.

> **Thus, while it is true that Hinduism does not have a Bible in the
> sense of a single text performing all its types of functions for all
> practitioners, it did have many diverse varieties of religious texts,
> that when taken together, indeed served many of the functions that the
> Bible did in pre-modern Western society.**

# Decoding & making sense of Manusmṛti

The author begins this chapter by reflecting on the contemporary Hindu society’s extreme discomfort (even by the so called conservatives) with the dharmaśāstras which is at being odds with the classical framework of Hinduism that views dharma as that which upholds, sustains, nurtures, stabilises, and harmonises at all levels - individual, society, and the cosmos.

He also provides an important properly sourced justification found in Hindu philosophy that while other sources of knowledge like perception, inference, etc.. can provide only the information about “what is” (facts of the world), they cannot provide any instruction for dharma which concerns itself with the question “what should we do?”. Hence, for matters of dharma, it is mainly the testimony of sacred texts (śabdapramāṇa) in the forms of śruti and smṛti that can inform us about dharma. Contrary to the popular perception that the content of the smṛtis are not eternal truths, he argues that they do reveal eternal relationship of the karmic binding (between a karma and a karmaphala) which is a universal relation and hence makes these tenets universally true. The particularity comes only when one infers how to apply them in particular contexts.

In the next section, he then proceeds to demonstrate why **śraddhā** (trust in the words of śāstras) are required before one approaches to study these texts because as already stated, these texts do not state empirical facts but about performance of duties. He contrasts this with the ***hermeneutics of suspicion*** employed by Western scholars where one reads a text with scepticism or other viewpoints to expose their purported or repressed or hidden meanings motivated by contemporary ideologies.

Then, he proceeds to explain how the text of Manusmṛti locates the manuṣya-dharma at the cosmic level, and how it shifts the perspective from individualistic humanism (that emboldens ego of human expectionalism) to cosmic metaphysicalism (that leads to humility and rootedness) ; this therefore locates dharma as part of a cosmic scheme. This contrast between the theistic worldview and the humanistic worldview as a whole is discussed in detail by the author and I also discuss it in detail in my another article on the Bhagavad-Gītā that can be read by clicking below.

He then proceeds to elucidate the general subject matter and the organisation of the book of Manusmṛti, which parts are generically applicable and which parts are specifically applicable and so on. He takes the example of two controversial verses (5.148, 9.3) regarding women that are generally considered anti-women which restricts women’s freedom and he uses several linguistic and hermeneutical arguments to dismantle such widely held contemporary views of these verses. There is equality of benefit (when it comes to the fruits of dharma) between everyone and just that ritual pathways are different between different types and genders of people.

# Varṇa as Ritual State & the Hindu view of social justice

The author is not shy to admit that in Hinduism, birth is indeed one of the three factors deciding varṇa. He discusses about it both in the commentary to the second verse and more extensively in a dedicated appendix chapter. He quotes several traditional commentaries and authorities to establish that traditionally, birth (janma) is indeed the necessary condition for someone to be born into a particular varṇa and be eligible for the ritual qualifications that come along with it. The author also gives several other commentators that tell when a birth-brāhmaṇa loses his qualification.

This birth ordained varṇa is also understood traditionally to be divinely and cosmically ordained and the author supports it by viewing its definition and characterisation through various Hindu texts (Appendix I). But two other factors - guṇa-svabhāva (inner tendencies) and svadharma-karma (performance of one’s dharmic duties) are needed for one born into an appropriate family to take up the duties and manifest the ritual qualifications that come with it. He supports this by quoting several traditional commentaries despite the fact that a birth-based eligibility system looks extremely inegalitarian for us now. But in Hindu metaphysics, one’s birth is not arbitrary but a result of one’s karmic accumulations in his previous life. The author explains this carefully despite the fact that whether we may choose to accept it or not for our present times. He writes :

An important aspect to note here is that Manusmṛti does not posit an egalitarian sociopolitical order where everybody is equal, where everyone has equal rights, where there are no gaps between the haves and have-nots, or where everyone is happy. That is a utopian idea which has no basis in human reality. Human reality is that no two persons are same. They are not same in their temperaments, in their life situations, in their competencies, in their ethical compass, and most importantly, they are not same in the karmic burden they are born with. As a result, there is huge diversity in human actions, their waste of life, and their pursuits of life. This diversity is very core to how nature functions.

Hindu philosophy recognises this reality of the world and also recognises the fact that it is not possible to overcome the diversity, differences, and conflict at the sociopolitical level. This can be transcended only at the ethical and spiritual level by developing equanimity and other ethical qualities which would eventually lead to attainment of Moksha or liberation through the knowledge of oneness and non-duality.

Stated differently, the concept of karma and reincarnation is what differentiate the Hindu view from the modern view. What is regarded as inequity in the contemporary world influenced by modern Western worldview is understood in the dharmic traditions as a working of karma from past lives which cannot be wished away. For a Hindu, the work of the society is to provide an environment that is conducive to all for practising their Dharma and pursuing the puruṣārthas undisturbed so that they may quickly progress towards loka-saṅgraha and mokṣa.

# Ritual Eligibility vs Moral Fallibility

One of the most important themes that this book touches on (AI.5) is the clear elucidation of the distinction between ritual eligibility or purity and moral eligibility. The author emphasises there that varṇa signifies ritual state and is not an indicator of one’s moral state or intelligence or any other secular state with hierarchical notion of superiority and inferiority.

We see some questions asked about Hinduism like :

- Are women and non-dvija men inferior, which is why, they can’t have
  access to Vedic chanting and other ritual functions?

- Aren’t there morally bad and incompetent brāhmaṇas and morally
  excellent śūdras too? How is it that an otherwise morally infallible
  and technically competent śūdra or a woman, cannot perform a Vedic
  yajña?

- How come rapists are allowed in temples but not menstruating women?
  Are menstruating women more morally deranged than rapists?

At this point, I would like to provide my own comparative insights here. These kind of arguments are commonly directed against Hinduism and this is the subject of this section. This type of questioning rests on a fundamental category mistake that quietly imports the Christian concept of “sin” as moral guilt into traditions that actually operate with a very different framework of “ritual purity/eligibility”.   
  
Christianity represents a radical break from its parent religion Judaism. One of its central innovations in the New Testament was precisely to abolish Jewish ritual purity laws : declaring all foods ritually clean, doing away with notions of rituals and ritual cleanliness, and shifting the entire focus from ritual states to moral disposition and faith. Hence, in this post Christian age, many people unconsciously assume that every religious restriction in other traditions too, must always be about morality, or personal worthiness. They then read all other traditions through exactly the same lens.  
  
But ritual purity (or ritual eligibility/adhikāra) is orthogonal and completely independent from both moral purity and hygiene. It is a technical state, like being qualified to perform a specific function, enter a particular space, or handle a sacred object. It has nothing to do with whether someone is a good person, whether they are “dirty” in the modern hygienic sense, or whether they deserve divine love.   
  
In Hinduism, a menstruating woman is ritually ineligible (aśauca) to enter temples or perform certain pūjās. The author elaborates more on this in his another book which can be read [here](https://amzn.in/d/0a18lgF5). In traditional sects of Hinduism, a woman or a non-dvija man is ritually ineligible to chant the Veda. This has never been about their moral character or intellectual capacity. The Sabarimala Ayyappan temple restricts entry to women of menstruating age not because of hygiene concerns or moral judgment, but because of the deity’s specific vrata and the ritual field it creates. It is like this ; one can ask “*An innocent person is denied entry into railway stations without a travel ticket or platform ticket but a rapist is allowed entry into railway stations if he has the necessary ticket ; does this mean that railway stations as institutions condone rape or that railway stations should do away with ticket-eligibility altogether because it cannot weed out morally corrupt individuals?*”. That is how the questions posed in the beginning look like.

> **One can indeed debate on whether ritual restrictions are needed or
> not but definitely morality does not need to enter the picture at all.
> At least while understanding the past, we should sympathise with our
> ancestors and think in their own terms, despite the fact that we may
> find that unattractive today. Importing from another tradition and
> conflating between different categories cannot be the way to initiate
> these debates.**

In Judaism, a menstruating woman is ritually impure, and in a state of tumah (Leviticus 15). This is a ritual category, not a moral condemnation. Shinto shrines in Japan have historically restricted menstruating women from certain sacred spaces or roles (particularly near the kami), rooted in notions of kegare (spiritual pollution) that are ritual, not ethical. Among the Yazidis, strict rules exist around menstruation and ritual purity that limit participation in certain ceremonies. In the Yazidi community, only some hereditary castes have rights to perform religious rituals and all other castes have to depend on these hereditary priestly castes to satisfy their ritual needs, thus creating ties of responsibility and dependence between the priestly and non-priestly lineages. Even in native Mongolian religion, some sacred mountain peaks associated with war deities carry restrictions on women entering these high ritual spaces, reflecting the same logic of ritual field integrity.

> These are not isolated quirks. **Almost every indigenous religious
> system worldwide has operated with some form of birth-based ritual
> purity/eligibility** (from Greco-Roman, Zoroastrian, ancient Near
> Eastern traditions to many African and Native American practices).
> Christianity is the great exception that did away with it.

When we project modern notions of equality as sameness of access and social justice as the elimination of every distinction onto these systems, they naturally appear unfair or discriminatory. But despite the fact that whether we find them currently appealing or not , that is an anachronistic reading when it comes to the past. The deeper traditional view actually contains its own form of equality: **Everyone is eligible for the highest spiritual goods: mokṣa, grace, wisdom, and devotion**. In the Vedic tradition, every man can be a yajamāna of a homa (half of the good phala that a husband receives by performing a sacrifice is transferred to the wife automatically), receive the blessings of the gods, study itihāsa-purāṇa, perform bhakti, or undertake tantric/sādhana paths that do not depend on Vedic ritual or chanting eligibility. **The benefits are universal; only certain ritual pathways are technically constrained.**  
  
To collapse ritual eligibility into moral worthiness is to misunderstand both the system and the spiritual architecture behind it. So, the presence of a rapist in a temple does not prove that a menstruating woman should be allowed in. The two categories simply don’t speak to each other. One concerns moral standing; the other concerns eligibility for a particular ritual context. Conflating them is a modern post-Christian misunderstanding of an ancient widespread religious logic.

# Conclusion

Overall, I think this is a well written book and irrespective of whether you will agree with it or not, it is a must read if you are serious to start explore Hindu theology from its own lens, rather than anachronistic modern lenses. You also will get exposed to how traditional commentaries function and gain lots of insights into traditional Hindu viewpoints.
