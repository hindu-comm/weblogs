Often, traditional religious scholars and lay practitioners criticise western scholarly studies of their religious texts on an individual case-by-case basis which is futile. When both the academic scholar and the religious practitioner reads the same sacred texts, how can the scholar and the practitioner who both read them can come up with entirely different conclusions? For that, one needs to know the approaches through which modern western scholars approach religion and what are the foundations of religious studies in the humanities departments of universities. This article is the first of a four-part series that starts with the history of religious studies in western universities that began to arise during the Reformation and Enlightenment. This first article summarises reductionist approaches to the study of religion till the 20th century. So, what is religious studies?

# Reductionism : Is Religious Studies a Discipline?

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

We have seen lots of religious studies departments in several universities. You might also have seen departments or subdivisions that study a particular religion or a group of religions like Buddhist Studies Department, Hindu Studies Department, Islamic Studies Department, and so on. But, what exactly is this western discipline called “religious studies”? Do they teach religion? No. Western scholars of religious studies often repeat either of these two answers when they are asked by laymen (both religious and non-religious) what they study and teach. They are :

> *I do not teach religion. I teach **about** religion.*
>
> *I teach about religion from a **non-religious perspective**.*

In the beginning, it might look very weird. Have you ever seen a mathematician who says “*I do not teach mathematics. I teach about mathematics*” or a historian who says “*I teach history from a non-historical perspective*”? Then why do we see religious studies scholars distancing themselves from the field that they are studying? The answer to this question lies in the history of the discipline itself that takes us back to the two key events that defined European modernity :

1.  The Protestant Reformation

2.  The Scientific Revolution

Prior to the sixteenth century, there was no such thing as religious studies in the West where one tried to approach the study of a religion (whether one’s own or not) from a non-religious perspective. When one studies one’s own religion from the perspective of one’s own religion itself, the discipline is called **theology** which is the discourse of the intellectuals of a religious group about their own religion and aimed at the consumption of others within the same religious community to help them understand and apply their religion better. While religion itself is as old as humanity and theology is found in all sophisticated intellectual civilisations, religious studies is a very recent discipline that emerged in the West in the 16th century as a reaction to the Protestant Reformation and the Scientific Revolution.

After the **Protestant Reformation** and the ensuing wars of religion, it was clear that a certain amount of religious diversity was going to be a permanent state of affairs in Western Europe since neither Catholics nor Protestants (Lutherans and Calvinists initially but many to emerge later) could claim any clear victory in the Thirty Years War. People also knew very well that there were conflicts with Muslims immediately to their East and Jews within their own territories. So, people began to wonder if there could be an approach to studying religion that would be generic and encompass all these religions (Judaism, Catholicism, Protestant sects, Islam) that did not take the side of any of these religions and instead adopt a non-biased distant perspective. That is, they stopped doing theology within the mould of their own religion and adopt a broader approach for studying all these religions and hence which could bring together these competing religious factions and prevent further wars.

In the 17th and 18th centuries moreover during the **Enlightenment**, a new method of scientific and rational thinking arose that was entirely independent of any religious concepts and assumptions that could explain a wide range of phenomena observed in nature. Often, these scientific methods (which better explained and predicted natural phenomena), viewed nature as a purely material phenomenon to be rationally explained, and were at odds with the teachings and the explanations of the Church. Thus, this made religion look false and unnecessary to explain the world to the Enlightenment scholars. Motivated by the success of this scientific approach, scholars of the Enlightenment then wondered if they could explain away religion scientifically using the same kind of approaches that worked well for the natural sciences. They expected that as humanity progressed, more and more of religion could be explained away as remnants of past superstition and all of the cosmos could be explained scientifically by natural and mechanistic laws.

It is from these two crucial developments that western religious studies takes root. The distance from religion that the West has taken and still takes, to a large extent, in studying religion, started off with two motivations - to settle religious diversity that arose from the Reformation and the Enlightenment’s sceptical attitudes to religion itself.

Is religious studies a discipline? Until the twentieth century and even after that (barring a few scholars like Eliade for whom we plan to dedicate a future article), religious studies scholars were convinced that their field was not a disciple on its own with its own methods like physics or historiography. Scholarship until the 20th century, taking its distance from religion seriously, has studied religion and explained it away by using methods from other disciplines like philosophy, history, psychology, sociology, anthropology, or economics. Each of these approaches brings its own assumptions and tools when used to study religion and are designed to handle something much broader than religion. For examples, anthropology studies culture, sociology studies social structures and their interactions, history studies the interactions between past events, and psychology studies the human mind. When they are hence used to explain religion, they are **reductionist** and hence reduce religion to their own field. For the anthropologist then, religion is thus nothing more than a part of culture that he studies and for a psychologist, religion is just a part of the manifestation of human psychological functioning that should be studied like anything else that the human mind does and feels (like falling in love). So, reductionism suffers from the drawback that it assumes religion is entirely just X where X is some other discipline. This discards theological terms and assumptions and brings in other assumptions from its own field.

# Theology & Religious Studies Separate

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

### The Reformation & The Search for an Inclusive Theology

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

In this section, we start with the beginnings of the subject of religious studies by going back to the 16th century, prior to which there was only theology. In fact, the very word “religion” itself referred to Catholic Christianity which was considered as the only true religion. But after the bloody wars that ravaged Europe following the Protestant Reformation, things began to change drastically. The separation of Christian theology from religious studies was not a sudden process but a gradual one enabled by several individuals but in this section, we just take two of those people as representatives of those who initiated the separation.

At this time, a French diplomat named **Jean Bodin** saw clearly that religion lay at the root of this conflict. He also realised that theological discourse could not help solve this problem at all and in fact, it was fuelling the conflict and setting the terms of it. So, as a diplomat, he thought hard about what would put an end to this warfare. He wanted to think of a new religion that would unite these warring camps together and also the Jews and Muslims. He published “***The Colloquium of the Seven About Secrets of the Sublime***” which was a fictitious religious dialogue involving seven people from all the religious denominations that were known to Bodin which included a Jew, a Muslim, a Lutheran, a Calvinist, a Roman Catholic, a Natural Philosopher (Epicurean/skeptic), and a Deist/Proponent of natural religion, where the last two members are sceptics and pose questions to the rest. The unprecedented move that is made in this book is that all these seven people discuss and debate about their religions as equals where in they attempt to find the things that would hold in common and remedy the chronic conflict of religions. The dangerous move that Bodin makes here is that the book ends amicably with no declared winner and all agree to continue the discussion some other time (maybe the plan of a sequel that Bodin could never manage to write?).

Even though there is no winner in this colloquium, there is a clear position that Bodin takes that is reflected through this book. In order to find a common religion that all the five religious people could agree on for uniting together, Bodin uses the following criterion :

> The one true religion for mankind is the oldest religion which is the
> religion of Adam and Eve.

Note that Bodin believed in the Biblical view of history starting with Adam and Eve with whom God walked in the garden of Eden. This viewpoint was also shared with Judaism and Islam and hence this was not surprising. So, he thinks that the religion followed by Adam and Eve is the oldest religion and since Adam and Eve directly walked with God and all of us are descended from them, that religion had to be the universally true religion as well. But how does one recover this oldest and hence true religion of Adam and Eve?

Bodin argues that the oldest religious traditions among all the religions would be the closest religion to Adam and Eve and hence believes in the notion that religion originates in a pure form and later gets corrupted (note that this is still just a more inclusive extension of Protestant Theology that views the religion of Jesus and his apostles as the true Christianity with corruptions accumulating over time). So, if we might summarise Bodin’s thesis, it would be :

> The oldest religion is the true religion.
>
> Hence, the oldest attested texts or traditions of a religion are the
> closest to the true form of the religion.

Bodin’s book was never popular and hence never made a great impact and the wars of religion became more intense as the 16th century progressed. Some kind of temporary truce was made at the end of the century between the warring Lutherans and Catholics but the arrival of another Calvinist sect upset the balance and resumed the warfare well into the 17th century all over Europe, pitting the Protestant Union against the Catholic League.

It is at this time that our next thinker who is also a diplomat named **Edward Herbert of Cherbury** who introduced another procedure for deciding on what would be the true religion that would unite all the known religions of his time. While Bodin assumed that the oldest religion is the true religion, Herbert systematically compared the doctrines of the religions known to him and proposed that the true religion is one whose doctrines were the most widespread and found in as many religions as possible. That is, if one found the propositions that were accepted by all religions, then one could fashion a religion that would be universally accepted and hence end religious conflict. That is :

> The true religion is one that all (or many) religions agree with

The result of this comparative analysis was his five notions of religion that he proposed were universal :

1.  There is only one God

2.  This God must be worshipped

3.  Virtue is the chief end of religion

4.  People ought to repent of their sins

5.  There is a life after death after which eternal rewards and
    punishment would be meted out to people based on their deeds

Even though Herbert was more widely read, and his ideas began to be accepted by a small group of people who would eventually become the **Deist**s, it did not have the impact that he expected. Also note that these five notions that he gave (which are supposed to hold true for all religions) still is within the Abrahamic religious framework only because he did not know about any other non-Abrahamic religion in his day.

Both Bodin and Herbert represent a break from theology. While they have not yet taken a naturalist scientific approach to religion that later intellectuals would take, and they are still doing theology in some sense (albeit more universal), they have broken from the mould of a theology of a particular religion or sect.

After the Enlightenment, we find thinkers who come with an agenda. Also by the time of the Enlightenment, more data on non-abrahamic religions from Asia and the newly discovered continents of America and Australia, began to reach Europe. So, the views of Herbert and Bodin began to look outdated although their two broad approaches (the oldest religion is the truest religion / the universal religion is the truest religion) will still continue to inform some thinkers.

### The Enlightenment : Pagan Religions as Primitive Science

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

As in the previous section, we will take two individuals as representatives of the Enlightenment approach to religion. **Bernard Fontenelle**, with his interest in the Greco-Roman Classics (which were older than Christianity) took a more sympathetic approach to religion. While others thought that religion was just superstition now rendered outdated by science, Fontenelle wanted to explain as to why would people still follow a religion and wondered if religion were just superstition or if it was something that intellectually reasonable people of the past would still engage in, given the kind of evidences that they had.

Fontenelle noticed that the earliest prominent gods of pagan classics were gods of nature at its scariest like the lightning or the storm or the sea and so on. So, Fontenelle argued that religion was not something antithetical to science but as some sort of primitive science. He put himself on the shoes of people who lived during Homer’s lifetime who were impressed by the vast display of power by natural forces like storm or the lightning or the sea, which could bring immense devastation, if not properly accounted for. But since the ancients did not have much data on these natural forces, they did the best they could using whatever tools of thinking were available to them in their own day. The ancients observed that many things happened around them because someone willed it to happen and caused it. For example, I moved the stone because I willed it and caused it by pushing it. So, since causation though human will and agency was most familiar to the ancients, Fontenelle argued that these natural forces also behaved the way they could because someone (god) who had will exhibited agency. So, gods were just the best way of explanation that the ancients could have to explain natural phenomena. Once there were stories of the gods, there blossomed art, literature, rituals, and other sophisticated phenomena. So, Fontenelle reasoned that while science now rendered the gods useless as agents to explain natural phenomena, the arts that they motivated and inspired could still remain with humanity. He was firm that anyone who still believed literally in the gods, was just holding on to outdated modes of thought. But Fontenelle was careful to not offend Christianity and had to concede by saying that his reasoning above applied only to man made religions like European paganism or Hindu idolatry or Chinese ancestral spirits, and not to Christianity which was a revealed religion and hence immune from this line of reasoning and hence was true.

Another thinker called **Giambattista Vico** who surveyed the same material as Fontenelle differed with him. While Fontenelle said that ancient gods and myths were just bad but still precursors of modern science, Vico concluded that the ancients literally thought in symbols and stories and had a literary cast of mind where things were endowed with spirits and the people actually believed in them (not just as explainers of nature). Vico’s central argument was that the first humans were “*stupid, insensate, and terrible beasts*” who lacked abstract reasoning. They were stupid in the sense that they were entirely immersed in their bodies and senses and hence couldn't think abstractly about "cause and effect”. They were insensate in the sense that they could only understand the world through strong physical feelings. In Vico’s vocabulary, "terrible" carries its older, more literal Latin meaning: inducing terror**.** When he describes the early ancestors as "terrible beasts," he isn't just saying they were "bad" or "scary"—he is describing a specific psychological and social state that mankind lived in constant terror of natural forces.

Unlike Fontenelle, who thought the ancients were like modern scientists with bad data, Vico believed they possessed a “***poetic wisdom***.” Because they could not form an abstract concept like “the power of the sky,” they used their imagination to create a concrete symbol: the character of Jove (Jupiter). Vico argued that if Fontenelle were right, then different people could have their own gods and their own myths but this was not the case and hence ancient religions were not individual quests for explanations but a collective mode of thinking by a society. For Vico, these symbols were not just “stories”; they were the very “imaginative universals” through which a society functioned. When the first thunder rolled, the humans of the earth did not seek a physical cause; they felt a physical fear and imagined a giant body in the sky. This “Jove” became the first symbol around which society organised. Such gods gave rise to the first burials, the first marriages, and the first laws. In this sense, religion was not a “precursor” to science, but the very womb of civilisation itself. While Fontenelle thought that art was a result of the gods and myths, Vico viewed them as fundamental modes of thinking by societies. The relationship between religion and science for Vico is one of historical succession rather than simple error and correction. Religion is the mode of the “Age of Gods” and the “Age of Heroes,” where truth is felt through the body and the senses. Science belongs to the “Age of Men,” where truth is grasped through abstract reason. Vico believed that as reason grows (science), the poetic imagination (religion) inevitably withers, because we can no longer “feel” the symbols as our ancestors did. But still, Vico, like Fontenelle, thought that religions were a product of societies and hence man-made unlike something that was inherited like language or body anatomy. Also both Vico and Fontenelle claimed that Christianity was exempt from the analysis that other religions were subject to because it was not a natural religion and was revealed.

> In the Indian context, Jean Bodin’s search for the "oldest, purest
> religion" became the blueprint for both 19th-century Indology and
> subsequent Hindu reform movements. Western scholars like William Jones
> and later reformers like Swami Dayananda Saraswati of the **Arya
> Samaj** adopted this exact reductionist logic: they identified the

> doing so, they reduced the vast, multi-layered forest of Hindu

> bias where the lived experience of millions of Hindus, such as *Murti
> Puja* (deity worship) or *Tantra*, was dismissed as "medieval
> corruption" or "superstition" simply because it was not in the

> fuels academic debates where "Brahmanical" texts are treated as the

> non-textual indigenous traditions that have flourished for millennia,

> Western-defined box of "Scriptural Religion in its Original Form".

# David Hume : Religion is False

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Unlike Fontenelle and Vico, the Scottish philosopher **David Hume** never gave any special privilege to Christianity for being exempt from scientific analysis and attacks it vehemently. But before his views on religion, it is important to get an overview of his overall philosophical orientation wherein Hume is described popularly as a radical **empiricist**. Empiricism is the philosophical position that all of our knowledge of everything that exists comes from the sense perceptions ultimately. Whatever is not perceived, does not exist. Knowledge of nothing is implanted in our heads innately and the human mind is a *tabula rasa* (blank slate) when it just comes into being at birth. Everything that we know comes from sense experience by seeing / hearing / smelling / touching / tasting it. Hume will now apply this position in the field of religion and follow it into its logical conclusion in some of his works like “***Of Miracles***” (which is a part of a larger work named “***An inquiry concerning human understanding***”), “***The*** ***dialogues concerning natural religion***”, and “***A natural history of religion***”.

Given that claims of miracles are central claims in all religions, how do we know that a miracle has occurred? Normally, people have never experienced a miracle through on their own and have heard it through testimonies and end up believing in them. In the Enlightenment spirit, most people thought that their knowledge of the laws of nature (like Newtonian mechanics) are certain but Hume being a radical empiricist, says that we do not directly perceive the laws themselves. We do not directly see Newton’s laws or the law that planets move around the sun in ellipses. All we have are empirical data of natural observations and we have seen that so far whatever we have observed, does obey these laws and hence we postulate a generic law. Scientific laws are not absolute truths but provisional according to Hume. So far, we have not observed any violation of the scientific laws that we claim are fundamental. Hence, from our evidences gathered so far, we see that the laws are obeyed for all our instances of observation and are violated never. So, seeing our overwhelming evidence of our observations for the law and zero evidence against the law, we conclude that the law holds true in general for all future and potential observations as well. From tomorrow onwards, if we start finding evidences where the law is not obeyed, our judgement will shift and we will not hold the law to be true. For example, consider the following natural law :

> Law : All humans die

Even this obvious natural law, Hume says, is not directly perceived. What we actually perceive is as follows :

> Observations : All humans that have been observed so far die

Making the leap from the evidential observations to the generic law is called the problem of induction in philosophy. Hume says that we cannot justify the problem of induction by any observation and we just have to take it as an unproven assumption. We observe consistent patterns (e.g., fire observed so far is hot) and infer they will hold in general in the future and unobserved instance as well (all fire is hot).

In this epistemological context, Hume argues that miracles are by definition, violations of the law of nature. If they are not, they would not be surprising. But Hume just said that the laws of nature are not matters of hard empirical facts. So, what we are really saying is that a miracle runs contrary to whatever patterns we have observed so far and given Hume’s stance on the unprovability of the laws and patterns of nature, one expects that he accommodates the possibility of miracles and take that miracles are counter-evidences for the laws and patterns ; but he does the opposite !

As an example, Hume takes the miracle of the resurrection of Jesus Christ to illustrate his argument. We have a natural law that dead people do not rise to life again based on so many observations of so many people. For judging this miracle of Christ’s resurrection, what therefore, are the evidences and counter evidences? The only evidence that we have for the resurrection is that of the testimony (which is already very weak) of the apostles in the Bible. On the other hand, the counter evidence that we have is enormous given the enormous number of dead people that everyone has seen with their own eyes who do not resurrect to life again. In this scenario, the counter evidence weighs much higher and hence we can’t believe in the miracle. What should happen for the credibility of the miracle to increase? For that, our weight of counter-evidences to the natural law that “dead people do not rise to life again” should increase, which means that we should see more people resurrecting from the dead ourselves. But, if more people resurrected like Christ did, then Christ’s resurrection will no longer be a miracle given that many Tom, Dick, and Harry have resurrected. So, by definition, a miracle is super unlikely to occur and hence Hume concludes that it is not rational to believe in miracles.

In his work “***Natural History of Religion**”*, Hume attempted to account for the origin and nature of religion as a solely human phenomenon, with no reference to divine or supernatural beings or miracles. Religion springs from the confrontation of humans with vast natural forces and tendency to anthropomorphise them as personalities with will and agency , as proposed by Fontenelle. But, unlike many others, Hume believes that no religion is better than the other and there is no progression from ancient religions to modern monotheistic religion. Other thinkers thought that there was a progression in religious evolution as follows :

1.  **Fetishism** : the most primitive religion where everything has a
    spirit

2.  **Polytheism** : a systematic pantheon of gods arranged in a
    hierarchy which is little more sophisticated than fetishism

3.  **Monotheism** : one god to explain everything makes monotheism more
    sophisticated than polytheism

4.  **Scientific Metaphysics** : replacing one god my abstract natural
    laws that could most successfully explain everything is the pinnacle
    of human evolution

While many others argued that monotheism was more sophisticated than polytheism (easy to go to zero god from one god than many gods), Hume argued that it was not the case. While monotheism may be more sophisticated than polytheism in terms of metaphysics, it is the most dogmatic and intolerant of all systems while polytheism was open to newer bodies of systems albeit being intellectually haphazard and chaotic. So, Hume says that there is no progression as we move from one religion to another and all are in a tug of war against empiricism.

What you might have already observed is that Hume tends to focus on religious ideas and beliefs and ignores the ritual and social aspect of religion completely and sets this precedent for all British religious studies scholars to come after him.

# Max Müller: Religion as a “Disease of Language”

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

As more data from the East reached Europe in the 19th century, scholars began to look beyond the “primitive science” of the Greeks and Romans. Leading this charge was the German-British scholar **Max Müller**. Müller was a titan of his era; he was the primary editor of the fifty-volume “***Sacred Books of the East**”* and held the first chair of Comparative Philology at Oxford. While David Hume focused on how the mind perceives the world, Müller focused on how the mind *names* it. He believed that the key to religion lay not in psychology or sociology, but in the history of words.

Müller’s central thesis was a unique, and quite literal, form of reductionism:

> **Religion is a “Disease of Language.”**

To understand what he meant by this, we have to look at his work with **Sanskrit**. Müller noticed that in the oldest layers of the Vedas, the names of the gods were almost identical to the names of natural objects. *Dyaus Pitr* was “Father Sky,” *Agni* was “Fire,” and *Ushas* was “Dawn.” He argued that early humans did not have the abstract, “dry” language of modern science. If an ancient person wanted to describe the sun rising and “defeating” the darkness of the night, they didn’t speak of “photons” or “rotation”; they spoke in vibrant, active metaphors. They might say, “The sun chases the dawn as a lover chases a maiden.”

Müller’s theory of reductionism follows a three-step process he called **“Nomina became Numina”** (Names became Deities):

1.  **The Poetic Stage:** Humans use metaphors to describe nature (e.g.,
    “The Sun is a golden-haired warrior”).

2.  **The Linguistic Slip (The Disease):** Over generations, as language
    evolves, the original metaphorical intent is forgotten. The word
    remains, but the context vanishes.

3.  **The Mythological Stage:** People take the “fossilized metaphor”
    literally. They no longer think of a “golden-haired sun,” but of a
    literal god named Apollo or Surya who has golden hair.

Müller believed that religion was a grand misunderstanding. He argued that the human mind had “suffered” a linguistic error where a figure of speech was mistakenly taken for a literal, supernatural reality. To Müller, **mythology was a parasitic growth on language.**

To explain this, imagine a modern example. Suppose a poet today writes, “The stock market is a volatile beast that devours the savings of the poor.” Thousands of years from now, if all our history is lost including the context in which the word “stock market” is used, except for that one sentence, a future “religion” might emerge that literally worships a “Beast of the Market,” offering it sacrifices to keep it from “devouring” them. For Müller, this is exactly how Zeus, Indra, and Thor were born. They were nothing but nouns that had “gone wrong.”

Müller’s reductionism is powerful because it allows the scholar to “dissolve” a god back into a dictionary entry. By using the tools of etymology, Müller claimed he could take any complex religious figure and reduce them back to a simple natural phenomenon like a thunderstorm or the morning mist. This gave scholars a feeling of immense control over religion; it was no longer a mysterious “revelation,” but a puzzle to be solved by looking at grammar.

However, like the other thinkers in this article, Müller’s approach is strictly reductionist. He assumes that **religion is entirely just a linguistic mistake.** This creates a significant blind spot. By focusing so heavily on the *origin* of a name in a text, he ignores what religion actually *does* for people. A devotee of Agni is not simply “misunderstanding a Sanskrit noun for fire”; they are engaging in a ritual that provides social cohesion, personal comfort, and a sense of the sacred—things that a dictionary cannot explain away. For Müller, religion was a problem to be corrected by better linguistics, rather than a fundamental human experience. While his work was instrumental in bringing Sanskrit literature to the Western university, he treated the “sacred” as if it were a typo that needed to be edited out of the human story.

> Max Müller’s "Disease of Language" was applied directly to the **Rig
> Veda**, transforming a living liturgy into a primitive weather report.
> By reducing *Indra* to a mere thunderstorm, *Agni* to a physical fire,
> and *Ushas* to the literal dawn, Western-trained philologists stripped
> these deities of their conscious, metaphysical reality. They argued
> that the Rishis were simply "confused poets" who lacked the scientific
> vocabulary to describe nature, and thus leading to "invented" gods by
> mistake. This caused immense harm by delegitimizing the internal yogic
> and meditative realizations of the Vedic seers, treating their deep
> insights as linguistic errors rather than spiritual truths. In
> contemporary debates, this legacy survives in "Naturalist"
> interpretations of Hindu myths that insist on finding a physical or
> astronomical event behind every sacred story, refusing to acknowledge
> the symbolic or transcendent dimensions of the *Devas*. It reduces the
> *Vedas* from a source of eternal wisdom (*Sanatana*) to a historical
> curiosity—a collection of "fossilized metaphors" for natural or
> historical events that a modern dictionary can explain better than a
> living Guru.

# Auguste Comte on Religion : False but a Social Necessity

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Auguste Comte, contrary to Hume focuses on the sociological aspects of religion and lays the foundations of this French approach to studying religion. Comte was living at a turbulent time where the following were happening :

- Scientific progress and Industrial Revolution were rapidly changing
  the social and cultural landscape of France and the rest of Europe

- Irreligion was sweeping rapidly from the intelligentsia to other
  classes

- The French revolution which had removed the power of the church and
  the clergy left people to think about human life and society in
  non-religious terms

Comte himself was born into a pious Catholic family and apostatised from the faith as a teenager. His genius quickly brought him to the attention of the elite but he also experienced episodes of insanity as well, which would serve as data points for his own theory of religion. Comte was the founder of a philosophical movement called **positivism** which stood against metaphysics. Positivism was purely empirical and inductive (like Hume’s theory) and avoided abstract metaphysical questions like the nature of an invisible reality (which metaphysics deals with). Positivism attends only to that which can be experienced, observed, and described, without asking about the nature of its existence.

Comte believed that sociology was the queen of all sciences and could be made into a hard science and his insightful and central thesis was :

> Society is not just a mere collection of autonomous individuals

One cannot extrapolate and deduce collective social behaviour from the behaviour of individuals constituting it and a society is a level of reality in its own right. Once people interacted to form relations and institutions, the society takes a life of its own that can only be studied at its own level. ***Comte was looking for laws that could describe and direct the evolution of human societies and thus enable control of human development.*** If natural sciences could have laws that describe natural phenomena accurately, the same would be possible for the social sciences also, according to Comte.

Comte, unlike Hume, believed that human societies progressed as they evolved in history. The historical forces of humanity was pushing humanity in a certain direction that could be mapped into orderly stages of development. The three broad stages with which he grouped the religious evolution of human societies are :

1.  **Theological** : People believed in gods who were needed to explain
    and control natural phenomena (e.g. lightning was due to the
    striking of Zeus’ thunderbolt and sacrifices to Zeus hence could
    control the weather). This theological stage itself could be grouped
    into three substages in the increasing order of sophistication -
    **fetishism**, **polytheism**, **monotheism**. In the most primitive
    stage of fetishism, men in tribes worship idols and totems believed
    to be endowed with local spirits. The next stage was polytheism
    wherein people looked beyond and looked at larger processes of
    nature and began to imagine broader gods of various cosmic and
    abstract categories (like gods of natural forces, human forces, and
    so on) and arrange them in a complex hierarchy reminiscent of their
    own societies that have evolved from primitive tribal to a more
    complex culture. The last stage was monotheism where one believed in
    only one god which is the most abstract stage that one can get to in
    the theological stage. But as societies evolved further, Comte said
    that they have to let go even of this monotheism and adopt the next
    stage.

2.  **Metaphysical** : This is the stage in which humanity let go of
    gods and began to think in terms of more abstract principles that
    led to philosophy. But this too is not the ultimate stage as it just
    replaces anthropomorphic gods by other abstract principles that lay
    beyond the world of phenomena (experience).

3.  **Positivism** : In this final stage, the human gaze finally breaks
    free of any fictitious transcendental realm and knowledge was gained
    by the application of scientific principles that were derived from
    direct experimentation.

Thus, in this final stage of positivism, Comte believes that religion, from the point of view of its function in giving explanation of nature, is now unnecessary, as it is superseded by science is no longer necessary to explain nature and has to die. So far, Comte’s views are not that different from that of Hume’s, except that he has added a progressive evolution to human history. But, if religion is no longer needed to explain natural phenomena, does that mean it deserves to die? This is where Comte disagrees as he identifies another function of religion that the science of his era was unable to perform.

> Religion according to Comte, had an important social role in society
> of serving as the glue that binds people together.

He could see that in the village, every Sunday morning, people gathered in church and performed rituals that brought them face to face, reinforced their social roles and connections, and supported themselves. The sermon on the church pulpit instilled a common set of values that enabled society to function smoothly. Comte therefore saw a huge problem churning in European society of his time. Religion was dying in Europe in his day and many intellectuals were celebrating it as they thought it would birth rationalism and progressivism. But, Comte was worried that if religion dies, then the social function served by it also goes away and thus create a vacuum where people would no longer be able to make connections and share a common identity and a set of values. He hence thought that something had to be done to fix this and he himself wanted to create a new functional equivalent of religion that would take over the social function of religion while not venturing into the metaphysical purpose that it held normally. The requirements of this new “religion” were :

1.  Compatibility with positive science (not disprovable by science)

2.  Compelling enough to command ascent (science itself cannot do it -
    e.g. Newton’s law cannot be loved or be an object of devotion)

3.  Provide moral consensus (science cannot do this as well ; it only
    tells how things happen in this world and not how people should
    behave, organise, and cooperate among themselves)

4.  Provide institutions that do all these things smoothly (like church,
    priests, saints, Sunday schools, rituals officiating birth,
    marriage, and death, festivals and so on).

Comte hence wanted something that would resemble traditional religion in many ways but also that which would not be dogmatic and hence be compatible with science. Comte had ideas for such a religion. He envisioned saint days to be replaced by the heroes of the scientific revolution - scientists whose discoveries led to to the progress of humanity and hence would be commemorated in this new religion by various rituals throughout the year. But, the central problem for Comte was to find the object of devotion ; who would replace God in this new religion?

> Comte thought that the most suitable object of devotion is the
> collective humanity itself.

He invented his own religion and called it as “**the religion of great being**” and the great being was humanity itself. This would be a perfect object of devotion because humans would worship their own collective and thus be self reliant and confident of their own abilities and not be reliant on some illusionary god in the sky. Collective humanity would also draw humans’ attention away from their own narrow selves and would thus install a sense of selflessness when they worship it. Since human development and progress was paramount according to Comte, it was necessary that they worship their collective humanity itself. And also if you remember, according to Comte, society itself was greater than any individual and more than the sum of all individuals and hence the great being would not be an individualistic religion where people worship themselves but they worship a larger reality that emerged only when they came together to form society. Comte himself declared himself as “the high priest of humanity” and declared himself as the pope of his new religion and his deceased lover became his highest patron saint, an equivalent of the Virgin Mary. Comte himself evangelised and attracted many converts to his new religion and took confessions from people. He also imposed a strict moral code for his religion, even stricter than the church (e.g. he outlawed divorce completely). This church of positive science was viable and today too, it flourishes in Brazil (to view its website, click [here](http://positivists.org/blog/brazil)). Comte is the fountainhead of a French tradition of sociology that will be applied in religious studies. Marx, Freud, and Durkheim - all of them read and appreciated Comte.

Comte suffered episodes of madness and used this too in his social theory. Comte claimed that when he descended to madness, he could see himself regressing and witness what happened to the human mind. He said that as he descended further into madness, he regressed down into the stages of human development that he had postulated (from positivist to metaphysical to theological). Don't mad people see and believe in things that are not there and develop weird fetishes? He thus came to see that the progress of humanity although normally was ascending, previous stages of human development remained latent in the human mind and also in the human society, and thus was always ready to reassert itself and hence human society would regress under the right conditions.

> brahmins should fulfill the role of Comte’s 'scientific priesthood' to

> secularisation of the Bengali intelligentsia, popularising the idea
> that the primary purpose of religion was not the salvation of the
> soul, but the 'order and progress' of the society.
>
> Interestingly, the Comtean urge to manufacture a 'rational religion'
> found echoes in 19th-century and 20th-century India, most notably in
> the later work of **Keshab Chandra Sen**. His **Naba Bidhan (New
> Dispensation)** was an attempt to create a universal, 'scientific'
> religion that moved away from sectarian dogma toward a collective
> spiritual identity. Similarly, in South India, the **Self-Respect
> Movement** led by **Periyar** mirrored Comte’s third stage of
> 'Positivism.' Periyar sought to replace traditional religious life
> with a purely rationalist social order where people would worship
> their own collective rationalism, even introducing secular rituals
> like 'Self-respect marriages' to fulfil the social function of ritual
> without its theological baggage. Both movements demonstrate how the
> Western 'Positivist' impulse - the desire to move from God-centered to
> Society-centered devotion to its own progress - was experimented with
> on Indian soil, often with the goal of social engineering and
> modernisation.

# Karl Marx : Religion as Oppression

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Karl Marx’s quote “***religion is the opium of the masses***” (from his essay “*Toward a critic of Hegel’s philosophy of right*”), is very famous and most people are familiar with it. The full quote reads as follows :

Religion is the sigh of the oppressed creature ; the heart of a heartless world ; just as it is, the spirit of an unspiritual situation. It is the opium of the masses.

Marx was a founding of a German school of sociology and despite his extreme hostility towards religion, he never developed a full blown theory of religion. Because he felt that religion was not important enough to bother about separately, as he believed that religion rested on a shaky foundation. Hence, if that foundation was removed, religion would come crashing down all by itself and no offensive assault was necessary.

In idealism, ideas or spirit or consciousness or some non material reality existed over the actual matter and energy that constituted things and determined processes in the empirical world. Hegel thought that human history was governed by a world spirit, a non material reality that acted through material things. Marx on the other hand, believed on radical materialism where only materials are real and hence was the starting point of his hostility with religion as it said that material reality was not important in front of a higher and more important spiritual reality that was also responsible for consciousness.

On the other hand, social structures were real and took care of people’s material needs. So, Marx looked at the economy and looked at who was making stuff and who was selling and buying what. He saw the terrible conditions under which workers worked in factories owned by rich owners. He saw that the labor of workers was the most fundamental things in the world as it produced important materials that kept societies alive and comfortable. But this valuable labour according to Marx, was not compensated properly. Marx believed that there was a theft of value of labour in any factory. The worker poured his heart and soul into making a cloth that would sell for a certain price in the marker but he himself would only be getting wages that is a small fraction of that value. The rest of the value was simply pocketed by the owner who never worked but simply took most of the value of the labour of a worker for himself and raised his profits.

Marx split social phenomena into two basic levels :

1.  **Substructure** : Foundational things that took care of the
    essential needs of physical human beings (production of food,
    medicine, housing, clothes, …)

2.  **Superstructure** : Things like art, philosophy, music, culture,
    and religion that were unnecessary (according to him) but still was
    built upon the foundations of the substructure

Marx thought that people were so far hoodwinked into believing that the superstructure was more real, more important, and more valuable than the substructure when it was the opposite. This was not just an innocent mistaken belief but resulted in oppression of people and trapping them in social structures that make them live in abject poverty. Marx sees abstractions like nationalism and religion as illusions cast upon the working class by the owning class so that the workers do not unite and rise against them. According to Marx, a rich factory owner in Germany has more in common with the rich factory owner in France than with his own workers in Germany. German or French nationalism hence is an illusion cast upon the working class masses to make the German working class somehow feel a patriotic connection to their German owning class than with their fellow working class compatriots in France. So, this creates an obstacle for the working class of various nations to unite against the owning class. So while the rich wealthy elite owning class travel around a lot between nations and enjoy together and share similar tastes, the working class is kept divided on the basis of nationalism. Think of a war between nations; only the poor soldiers fight and the rich stay away from it and are least affected. Religion too is viewed in the same manner by Marx as a set of beliefs and institutions to coerce the working class and disable them from rising against their owning class in the name of morality and assurance of a good afterlife. So, both religion and nationalism are obstacles for creating a class consciousness among the proletariat.

Marx thought that the superstructure was a sinister tool used by the owners to oppress the workers. The whole history of humanity was a history of class struggle in a Marxist viewpoint. There was initially a kind of a garden of Eden like paradise where in people were independent and freely made stuff for themselves and enjoyed it for themselves. But the institution of a private property, was the original sin, when people began to view things as private property that were not products of their own labour and extension but as stuff to be brought and sold by exchanging it with other people, there came division of humanity into two classes - workers (who made stuff) and owners (who owned stuff). Thus, the class struggle began and the superstructure became a tool for oppression for owners. This superstructure brainwashed the workers to believe that their oppression was normal and that it did not matter in front of some fictitious higher realities. For e.g, the divine right of kind justified the oppression of king and all the superstructures like coronation rituals and church pulpits, reinforced this and kept workers compliant and docile, in promise of a heaven. If belief in God was not natural, where did it come from according to Marx?

Marx accepted the theory put forward by an earlier thinker named **Ludwig Feuerbach** which proposed that religious realities (including God) were projections of human qualities. If the Bible said humanity was made the image of God, Feuerbach argued that it was the opposite and that God was made in the image of humanity in his own reflection. God was simply a human conception of an idealised humanity and embodied qualities (like wisdom, justice, mercy) that humans ought properly to embody on their own. But in doing so, he argued that man had alienated from himself, those good qualities for which he deserved credit. So God gets all the credit for being all knowing, all merciful, and all just and humanity, on the other hand became sinful, ignorant, and unjust (at least this was the Christian religious worldview).

Marx believed that Feuerbach was in right track but had not gone far enough and was just content with the psychology of this. Marx said that this projection of humanity into God was moulded in a certain way by a ruling class that would give God a certain shape and character, which would enable them to keep the workers in line. The economic situation of the workers that kept them trapped, had also created this type of God according to Marx. Thus, if this economic order was overturned and a workers dictatorship was established, Marx believed that religion would automatically vanish.

> Marxist reductionism has dominated Indian history departments for
> decades, particularly through the works of scholars like D.D. Kosambi
> and the **Aligarh School**. This approach reduces the complex
> spiritual and ethical framework of **Dharma** to a mere
> "superstructure" designed to protect the "substructure" of class and

> the actual heart of Indian civilisation.

# Emile Durkheim : Religion as the Mirror of Society

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

While Comte invented a new religion wherein humanity worships its own collective, Durkheim turned the tables by proposing that if we look deeper, that is what traditional religions had been doing all along!

Durkheim paid lot of attention to how social phenomena related to and impacted the life of an individual and showed that even the most private of acts had social dimensions to it. For example, in a book called “***The study of suicide***” (wherein people normally think suicide to be a private affair and a manifestation of personal psychological issues), Durkheim noted statistically that suicide was more common in Protestant Europe than in Catholic Europe. He then explained that the fierce individualism of Protestantism gave individuals less community support and made them more disconnected, which resulted in higher suicide rates. His magnum opus “***The elementary forms of religious life***” was motivated by ethnographic data of the aboriginals that was beginning to come from Australia. He considered these as a laboratory for study of religion among primitive societies.

The two leading theories of the origin of religion that were in force against which Durkheim argued against were **naturism** and **animism**.

> **Naturism** was the view that religion originated in the
> personification of the forces of nature (like Fontenelle said).
>
> **Animism** is the view that religion originated in the dreaming of
> primitive men about ancestors who had passed away (which gave rise to
> the notion of spirits that continued to be alive without a body)

Durkheim argued that religion had nothing to do with lone individuals sitting around and thinking of ideas (as naturism implied). If these theories were true, religion would only focus on objects of devotion as large, imposing and frightening forces or spirits whereas aboriginals were worshipping a small ordinary object (like a stone or a rock or a mineral or a painting of an animal) as totems. Animism explained it away by saying that people worshipped these totems to gain control over the spirit inside things that the totem represented (e.g. A totem of a deer could represent the deer spirit whom people worshipped to have a successful hunt of deer). This also had to be false because many times, aboriginals worshipped pictures of animals that they never hunted and ate, and many totemic objects had no relation to anything useful to seek control of. These two theories assumed that aboriginal religions were stupid and the people following them were doing primitive science. But Durkheim argued that all people are basically very rational and intelligent and that no form of religion could survive very long if its fundamentals were fully false, flawed, or foolish. To find the rationale behind aboriginal practices, Durkheim wanted to look at the communal ritual actions that gathered people and focused their attention on those particular objects.

For him, the primary force behind religious worship was not a thing but a quality which he called as “**the sacred**” which stood in opposition with the concept of “**the profane**”. Durkheim saw from the data on the aboriginals that they would consider a certain rock with a picture of a cockatoo on it to be sacred but after some time, discard it, and move over to another place and start worshipping another object as a totem. So, the sacrality of a totem was not a fixed forever and its property of sacredness did not come from the object itself. The object imbibed sacredness by the rituals that the people displayed towards the object. He hence postulated that sacredness was a social behaviour. Durkheim noted that large tribal federations in Australia were split into smaller subgroups (like clans) and that whenever a new subgrouping happened, the members of the clan would come together and adopted an object as an emblem and treated it as a sacred totem and behave in such a way that showed distinct respect towards it. He called this phenomenon as **clan totemism**.

Durkheim believed that it was a society’s distinct behaviour towards an object that made it a sacred totem (not a property of the object itself). Then, the next question to ask is what kind of behaviours could imbibe an object with sacredness. Three things are noted in this regard by Durkheim :

- **Ritual Taboos** : Whenever an object is taken as sacred, there came
  taboos instituted around it ; there were many things which was allowed
  to do to profane objects that become impermissible to do to sacred
  objects or in the vicinity of sacred objects. Eg. You may be forbidden
  to say the name of the cockatoo if you adopted its picture in the
  totem, you might be forbidden to insult it, menstruating women could
  be forbidden to come close to it, you might prevent the sacred totem
  from touching those objects that would defile it.

- **Focus for Rituals** : The sacred object serves as a focal point for
  the entire clan and all religious rituals involve it in some way or
  the other. E.g. during festivals, the sacred object might be taken
  away from its natural resting place and carried as a procession around
  the residences of the clan members.

- **Identity for clan & individuals** : The sacred object serves as an
  identity marker for the clan as a whole and also for individuals
  inside it. E.g. If you are a member of a clan that has a rock with a
  picture of cockatoo as its totem, then you are a member of the
  cockatoo clan.

Durkheim concluded that the property of sacredness was not intrinsic to the object but as a product of social belief and attitudes above shown towards it. The same rock was an ordinary piece until it was painted with a picture of a cockatoo and was consecrated as sacred. Similarly, after this rock wears out, it could be thrown away another rock might be consecrated with a cockatoo picture on it to serve as the totem. Neither the cockatoo had anything to do with sacredness because it was not a particular cockatoo but the species as a whole that was considered sacred and the cockatoo clan were okay with members of another clan treating the cockatoo species as ordinary profane ones.

So, if the sacredness of the totem is not a property of an object, Durkheim thought that they were symbolic of something and that the totem object just served as a vessel to capture it and represent it. Durkheim noted that the aboriginals had a word in their language that captured this concept and idea (sacred, the great spirit, divine presence) and if asked what was it and where was it, they would just say that they could just feel it when they were in the presence of it and it was not exactly a material object sitting somewhere. Durkheim named it as the “**totemic principle**” that made these symbolic representations on objects sacred after consecration, by infusing these symbols on objects with this principle.

Now, we will come to the main argument of Durkheim.

> **Society** == *makes a totem* ==\> **Sacred** (consecration and
> ritual codes)
>
> **Sacred totem** == *represents* ==\> the **Society** (serves as an
> identity marker)
>
> Conclusion : Society makes itself sacred

So far, he has separately shown that the sacredness was imputed into this totemic emblem by the society and once consecrated, the sacred totem represented the clan group itself. So, what was sacred was the society itself, according to Durkheim. The clan makes the rock with a painting of a cockatoo sacred and this cockatoo symbol represents the clan itself. If we remove the middle term of the object, we conclude that :

> **The sacred is the society**.

So, *the only thing that the totemic emblem does is that it provides a symbolic point of focus which allows the social group a way of representing itself to itself*.

This may not be so strange if you think about it. Take the Indian national flag for example. We do rituals around the flag (e.g. flag hoisting and salute on national holidays) and any object with the symbol of the national flag on it is considered consecrated in the sense that it deserves to be respected and treated with some dignity (eg. a cloth with the national flag, unlike an ordinary profane cloth, cannot be worn as an undergarment or be used for wiping noses or be thrown away casually if it is worn out). But do we actually honour that saffron, white, and green with the blue wheel in the middle? No, it just represents our entire nation as a whole. So, when we are saluting the flag, we are just saluting ourselves in the collective who have formed a nation. The cloth with a national flag is respected by Indians neither because of some special property of the cloth nor because of some special property of the symbols (the layout of the tricolour and the wheel) ; there is nothing in these things and symbols itself that make it worthy of salute and honour. It is the decision of our collective nation and our code of behaviour that we have constituted towards it that makes it worthy of honour. Also, the Indian flag is sacred only for Indians and it is recognised that other nations and the people of other nations are not obligated to honour it in the same way as Indians do. Durkheim basically argues that all of religion is like this only where a sacred object of worship represents the collective itself that honours it. As the society ultimately honours the collective (nation state) it has formed by itself when honouring the totem representing the collective (national flag), Durkheim’s theory is not strange. But the problem of Durkheim like all the other people we have seen so far is that they are reductionist. Durkheim says that through religion (at least in its most primitive form), is entirely just this thing where in a religious community worships the collective it has formed by itself. Durkheim would probably argue that the family deity and ancestral spirits that your family worships ultimately represents the collective that is your family. But is that all I am doing when I participate in a family deity worship? That is the problem of reductionism. Also, it is not clear how deities like Vishnu or Shiva that are worshipped throughout India are basically just Indians worshipping themselves. There seems to be some individualistic soteriological aspect to the worship of such national deities. But Durkheim being a confident reductionist would say that ultimately that too is some form of a collective worshipping itself.

Comte thought that his religion of the great being that he invented where humanity worships literally itself in the collective, was where religion needed to go, if it had to survive and continue serving its social function. But, Durkheim was saying to Comte that he had basically gotten it right but that he was mistaking for a future goal, something that was already happening in place all along. The simplest manifestations of religion according to Durkheim was basically the worship of society by society. Comte had recognised that society was a larger reality that transcended its individual constituents, he forgot to answer how an individual relates to the society and how religion served an individual in the society. Durkheim now tries to answer this by noting that religion provided to its individual practitioners, what he called as “**piacular rites**”. It means ways that society or individuals could re-dedicate themselves to the good of the whole collective. At the group level, this may be periodic mass congregations or mass internal reflections (e.g. the Christian season of lent or ad hoc ceremonies needed when the society has gone astray and lost track of its goals and virtues ( e.g. in the Bible, the prophets of Israel exhort and call upon the Israelites to let go of idolatry and come back to the covenant they made with their god Yahweh after doing rituals of collective repentance). At the individual level, if a particular person who has strayed from the goals and ideals too has rituals to rededicate himself (e.g. confession of sins).

Durkheim also stated that the notion of a soul is a social phenomenon as well and is nothing but the social conscience. It is the image and ideals of the society interjected into the individual and adopted by him as his most important identity. When the person has successfully integrated himself into the religious community, they take the image of the group (its virtues, ideals, goals, missions, fears) and brings it on board as a part of their own fundamental being as their soul. This soul submerges an individual’s antisocial impulses and drives and puts the image of this society itself as a fundamental part of his identity.

Durkheim’s theory of religion was very influential in his own day but has been subject to a lot of critiques, apart from it being reductionist in saying that social dimension is all there is to religion. If you assume from the onset that religion is a purely social phenomenon, then it is not surprising to conclude that religion is nothing but society worshipping itself. Look at the hermit or monk or renouncer in any religious traditions who give up their place in society in order to do something that looks religious. Marx reduces religion to just economics, Durkheim reduces religion to just sociology, the Enlightenment thinkers reduce religion to just primitive science, Freud reduces it to just psychology. That it not to say that they are wrong or they are not valuable and they can indeed shed light on important aspects of religion. But reducing one aspect of the religion to say that it is all that a religion is about, is what anti-reductionist scholars argue.

> Durkheim’s approach to religion as a “collective mirror” of society is
> frequently used by modern sociologists to study the **Kumbh Mela** or
> the **Jagannath Rath Yatra**. These scholars argue that the millions
> of devotees are not actually encountering a deity, but are merely
> experiencing “collective effervescence”—essentially a social high from
> being in a crowd. In this view, Lord Jagannath is reduced to a “social
> totem” that represents the Odia identity, and the worship is nothing
> more than a society celebrating its own solidarity. The harm here is
> the total erasure of the **bhakti** (devotional) experience; it treats
> the internal relationship between the *bhakta* and *bhagavan* as a
> social illusion. In contemporary academic debates, this is often used
> to “secularise” Hindu spaces, arguing that temples should be managed
> as public social utilities rather than sacred abodes. By saying that
> “the sacred is the Society,” they justify the state’s intervention in
> religious traditions, as they view the temple not as a dwelling of the
> divine, but as a community centre that belongs to the “Social
> Collective.” Also secularisation of Hindu festivals like Pongal by
> stripping it of its worship of the sun is facilitated by this theory
> where in religion is just ultimately treated as a society worshipping
> itself and hence the actual nature and even the presence of a sacred
> totem does not matter.

# Sigmund Freud : Religion as Neurosis

![](data:image/svg+xml;base64,PHN2ZyBjbGFzcz0ibHVjaWRlIGx1Y2lkZS1saW5rIiBmaWxsPSJub25lIiBoZWlnaHQ9IjE4IiBzdHJva2U9ImN1cnJlbnRDb2xvciIgc3Ryb2tlLWxpbmVjYXA9InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2UtbGluZWpvaW49InJvdW5kIiBzdHJva2Utd2lkdGg9IjIiIHZpZXdib3g9IjAgMCAyNCAyNCIgd2lkdGg9IjE4IiB4bWxucz0iaHR0cDovL3d3dy53My5vcmcvMjAwMC9zdmciPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xMCAxM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy41NC41NGwzLTNhNSA1IDAgMCAwLTcuMDctNy4wN2wtMS43MiAxLjcxIiAvPjxwYXRoIGQ9Ik0xNCAxMWE1IDUgMCAwIDAtNy41NC0uNTRsLTMgM2E1IDUgMCAwIDAgNy4wNyA3LjA3bDEuNzEtMS43MSIgLz48L3N2Zz4=)

Sigmund Freud is considered as the founder of psychoanalysis. Like Marx, Freud too is a severe critique of religion but his basis of criticism is not economical but psychological. But before let me go into his approach to psychoanalysis, let me tell a story first, that is based on a real incident in the case of Dr M. Scott Peck.

*There was a woman who was dead afraid of spiders and this was spoiling her entire life and bringing it down. She could not even dare to enter into a room even at the mere thought that there could be spiders inside it. So, she came to Dr Peck for help who spent several years taking to her about her life and trying to extract as much information from her that could help overcome her problem. After this, he learnt that the following was her family background. Her father was passive and was not present much in her life but her mother was a real nightmare for her who had no sense of personal boundaries with her daughter. Even after her daughter moved out of her home, the mother showed up there every day and was insanely curious about everything her daughter did ; even the details of her love life and sex life. Even worse, the mother was also having affairs behind her father’s back and kept sharing intimate personal details of that too with her daughter. Dr Peck saw that there was some connection between his patient’s fear of spiders and her messed up mother but was not quite sure how and wanted her to arrive at the connection on her own. The breakthrough came one fine day when the woman suddenly shouted out something like “my mother is disgusting and scary like a spider who has me caught in her web and I cannot break free at all!”. Then, the patient realised and it dawned upon her that she had not been able to admit to herself that her mother was the problem because she was conditioned by society and culture about how much mothers love their daughters and how much daughters ought to love their mothers. It was threatening and even guilt inducing for the daughter to confront the fact of how much anger she had towards her mother subconsciously. Not being able to face those feelings of intense anger and fear towards her mother consciously, she pushed it to the subconscious level and then redirected those same feelings towards some vaguely analogous entity of spiders. This provided a guilt-free socially-approved safety valve to vent out her angers and fears on what she had towards her mother. Once she realised that connection, she was able to face the issue directly and immediately got cured of her fears and was then bold enough to confront her mother and estranged her. The fear of spiders which was a makeshift solution to her real psychological problem could now be averted and she then led a normal healthy life after solving her actual problem. The condition that this girl has suffered is technically called as **neurosis**.*

Sigmund Freud was the founder of the psychoanalytic technique that solved this woman's problem and was the first in the west to postulate the existence of a hidden subconscious realm. Before that, people thought that whatever happened in your conscious mind represented your total personality and there was nothing below the thoughts and feelings that we had and which we knew to have had. If you behaved weirdly, the traditional explanations were that either a demon had taken possession of you or it was your failure as a person to not behave normally. Freud postulated the existence of thoughts and feelings that we never even knew were there which is the subconscious ; the dark garage in the basement of our minds into which thoughts and feeling were pushed down and shoved into when we did not want to confront them, which then regurgitated up and manifesting as strange behaviours which are otherwise inexplicable to the person himself. Psychoanalysis was the process by which a doctor (called a therapist) talks to the person through these problems and leading them to realise for themselves the contents of their own subconscious mind that were raising up to consciousness so that they can face them directly and rationally, which was good for Freud.

Freud was a hardcore atheist and hence looked at religion as a psychological problem to be solved or cured. He addressed the problem of religion in three books - “***Moses and monotheism***” , “***Totem and taboo***” , “***The future of an illusion***”. In the second book, Freud goes into history to look for the origins of religion. His origin story of religion sounds like a disgusting fantasy novel (almost all of his theories are!!) but here it is. In some distant past, this episode repeated many number of times wherein there was a family group controlled by a dominating patriarchal figure who was kind of like the alpha male. This figure monopolised access for all the women in the area to himself, denying conjugal access to even his sons. The sons being shut off from this lack of sexual access were frustrated, branded together, conspired in secret to murder this alpha male father and then took the harem of his women for themselves. The sons regretted that they had killed his father and also felt the guilt of incest as the women their father had were their mothers. He says that religion springs from this and many feel shocked that this single traumatic event was the origin of the vast and diverse experiences of religion. Freud does not even attempt to produce any piece of historical evidence for this wild claim and it is just a retrojection of his theory of Oedipus complex into primeval history. But, this is not entirely fantastic as evolutionary biologists see that gorillas and chimpanzees indeed exhibit this alpha male behaviour and Darwinian theory holds that with the development of language in humans, this ended as now with the use of language, the other males could band together and coordinate to conspire to eliminate the alpha male. So this type of event must have happened in the origin of species. But, it is a great leap to link that scenario causally with the development of the sky-father god.

In the third book, Freud tackles the inner mechanisms of the workings of religion wherein he says that religion is an illusion. Freud takes the approach of **methodological atheism** where the scholar studying religion does not assume the truth of any religious claim. The foundation of religion according to Freud is an illusionary wish-fulfilment mentality. What exactly is it? Freud identifies there fears or dissatisfactions

1.  **Subconscious repressed urges** : Every man has a set of needs,
    wishes, and urges but in a civilised society, he controls most of
    them. An average man might want to act like the alpha male and
    dominate over everybody, have access to all women in his tribe, and
    get all the wealth in the world by even fighting and stealing if
    possible. But most of us have these urges controlled for the sake of
    the peace and security that civilised society offers. But Freud
    thinks that these unfulfilled urges go into the subconscious and is
    regurgitated differently as religion wherein we believe that there
    is a heaven after death where we will get all our wishes fulfilled.
    Freud argues that even babies have latent sexual feelings towards
    their opposite-sex parents, like the Oedipus complex.

2.  **Protection from forces of nature** : In this aspect, he sounds
    like Fontenelle and Hume. Humans formed civilisation to escape from
    the raw terror of nature that makes a lonely human feel utterly
    powerless. Thus, in order to protect themselves by working together
    to protect everyone from the forces of nature. When we were kids,
    then too we had this feeling of helplessness and depended on our
    parents for everything and we felt nice to have the loving
    protection of our parents. Hence, as adults, when we feel helpless,
    we believe in fatherly and motherly deities who would protect us
    from the terror of natural forces. A pastor himself said in a church
    that Christians pray to God by kneeling down and having their arms
    up because that is the position a child makes to signal his father
    or mother to pick them.

3.  **Fear of death and uncertainty** : The fear of death and
    uncertainty in the grand scheme of things is another terror that we
    face. Hence, the feeling that there is somebody in charge with a
    plan who will make this all right, we feel much better off.

Like the girl in the story whose fear of her mother and repressed feelings of maternal hatred manifested as fear of spiders, Freud says that our repressed animalistic urges, fear of nature, fate and death are pushed into the subconscious mind and is regurgitated as religious belief and behaviour. So, basically, religion is a feel-good illusion and a mass illusion at that because most people would not even be willing to acknowledge these fears and repressed feelings that Freud talks about. So we believe in God not because he is necessarily there but because he is necessary to be there for us.

Thus, religion according to Freud is technically a mass societal level of **neurosis**. Note that neurosis is distinct from another phenomenon called **psychosis** where the patient has completely lost touch with reality and is imagining stuff that is not there and hallucinating. But a neurotic person is still well functional and well in touch with reality, but there are still things operating in their subconscious mind (that they are not aware about consciously) that makes them act and do certain things in particular ways (like the woman fearing spiders). These fears that makes us be religious are by and large unconscious because most people would not openly admit that they have repressed urges and are scared of nature, death, and fate, but they indeed are and these feelings are shoved into their subconscious minds.

One of the most common symptoms of neurosis that Freud observed was the tendency for ritualistic behaviour (like OCD) where a person demands that they do the exact same thing over and over again periodically (everyday or every hour or every week) without which they become extremely anxious and stressed. Freud noted that one of the main features of religion was performing repetitive rituals without which an anxiety ensues ; Freud hence he connected this religious behaviour to the symptom of neurosis. So, religion is a neurosis and a group neurosis at that one. Whatever neurosis is to an individual, religion is, to an entire group of people in a society. Freud called religion technically as a **universal obsessional neurosis**.

If you remember the section on Karl Marx, we saw that Feuerbach too postulated that religion was a human projection. The difference with Freud is that for Feuerbcach, God was the projection of everything that was good and ideal about humanity whereas for Freud, God was the projection of everything that was bad and scary about humanity. Based on this, he concluded that religion is bad and is a dysfunctional coping mechanism that needs to be rejected and gotten rid of.

If religion is a neurosis, what is the cure? They need to admit their repressed urges in society and their fear of nature, fate, and their morality and talk about it openly and face those fears in a health way, following which religion will automatically vanish from society.

Freud was obviously not without criticism and his disciple Jung split with him on these and other views. For Jung, religion could be a good thing if after all it helps to cope up with fears because the fear of nature and death cannot be solved anyways. But he not only disagreed with the conclusion, he also viewed the entire origins and mechanisms of religion positively. Subsequent psychiatrics have questioned the need for methodological atheism and negative attitudes towards religion when they discovered through their patients that religion could be a useful tool to solve psychological issue and sometimes it was just enough to redirect the religious practices of a person from unhealthy ones to healthy ones to solve his problem more effectively, rather than killing his religious impulse altogether. Many thinkers have pointed out that Freud’s theory of religion is not scientific. In *“**The Future of an Illusion**”*, Freud offers no evidence at all, even from his own clinical experience. Charles Elder tells that Freud argued by analogy, which is always only heuristic, rather than by inference. Despite these criticisms, once one encounters Freud’s ideas, it is difficult to see religion in the same way as before. Freud did succeed in getting many people into believing him or atleast getting influenced by him.

- **Marx** says religion is nothing but an economic tool of oppression.

- **Durkheim** says religion is nothing but society worshipping its own

- **Freud** says religion is nothing but a collective neurosis and hence
  a regurgitation of repressed urges and fears.

The great achievement of these reductionist approaches was their ability to bring the study of religion into the university. By treating religion as a “natural phenomenon” like any other, they opened it up to scientific and historical scrutiny. They moved us away from the narrow sectarianism of the wars of religion and allowed us to compare different traditions using a single, objective yardstick. However, (as we will soon see the scholar Eliade saying this), something was lost in this process. By looking at the “elephant” only through the microscope of sociology or psychology, these scholars arguably missed the elephant itself—the **Sacred**.

By the middle of the 20th century, a new generation of scholars began to feel that reductionism had gone too far. They asked: if religion is just sociology, why not just study sociology? If it is just psychology, why not just study psychology? If there is nothing unique or “irreducible” about religion, then “Religious Studies” as a discipline has no reason to exist.

In the third article in this series, we will leave the world of the reductionists behind and look at the **phenomenologists**. We will then explore how 20th century thinkers like Mircea Eliade and Rudolf Otto attempted to rescue the “sacred” from the hands of the sociologists and psychologists, arguing that religion must be understood on its own terms : as a fundamental and irreducible category of human experience. They recognised that religion is a legit phenomenon to be studied in its own right using its own terms and that distance from a particular religious tradition in approaching religion in general, need not mean total distance from religion in general. A famous proponent of this approach is the Romanian scholar **Mircea Eliade** who writes the following in his book “***Patterns in Comparative Religion***” :

Modern science has restored a principle which, was seriously endangered by some of the confusions of the nineteenth century: “**It is the scale that makes the phenomenon**.” Henri Poincare queried with some irony whether “**a naturalist who had studied elephants only under the microscope would think he knew enough about those animals?**” The microscope shows the structure and mechanism of the cells, a structure and mechanism which are the same in all multicellular organisms. But is that all there is to know? At the microscopic level, one can’t be certain. At the level of human eyesight, which does at least recognize the elephant as a phenomenon of zoology, all uncertainty departs.   
  
In the same way, **a religious phenomenon will only be recognized as such if it is grasped at its own level, that is to say, if it is studied as something religious**. To try to grasp the essence of such a phenomenon by means of physiology, psychology, sociology, economics, linguistics, art or any other study is false; **it misses the one unique and irreducible element in it—the element of the sacred.**

Obviously, there are no purely religious phenomena; no phenomenon can be solely and exclusively religious. Because religion is human, it must for that very reason be something social, something linguistic, something economic—you cannot think of man apart from language and society. But **it would be hopeless to try and explain religion in terms of any one of those basic functions, which are really no more than another way of saying what man is.**

I do not mean to deny the usefulness of approaching the religious phenomenon from various different angles; but it must be looked at first of all in itself, in that which belongs to it alone and can be explained in no other terms. It is no easy task. It is a matter, if not of giving an exact definition of the religious phenomenon, at least of seeing its limits and setting it in its true relation to the other things of the mind.
