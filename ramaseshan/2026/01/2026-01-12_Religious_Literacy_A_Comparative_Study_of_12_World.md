# All Religions are not the Same

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!_Ejh!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc0a53b60-a257-4460-b195-eab61fedbc9d_1024x1024.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!_Ejh!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc0a53b60-a257-4460-b195-eab61fedbc9d_1024x1024.heic)

I have never seen anyone in a political discussion claim that democracy, oligarchy, and monarchy are all essentially the same systems of government and that differences between them do not matter. I have never seen any economist of any orientation claim that communism and capitalism are both essentially the same and that the differences between them are non-substantive. Yet, in religious discourse, the biggest lie that has been told and retold as a new orthodoxy in the name of politeness and political correctness is “all religions are essentially the same” and that differences between them are just distractions.   
  
At its best, this claim of all religions being fundamentally the same, is just a desperate utopian hope. At its worst, this stupid claim is not just untrue but also extremely delusional, dangerous, and disrespectful for the religions themselves. The notion that all religions being essentially the same has never contributed anything at all in advancing our understanding about the various religions and also regarding what happens in the real world. Surely, this belief has also not contributed in making the world any safer. Au contraire, this naive thinking has caused only more harm by making us blind to the fundamental differences between religions that matter a lot. No Muslim will ever say that his hajj pilgrimage is an inessential aspect of his religion (in fact it is one of the 5 pillars of Islam) and no Christian will say that baptism is a superficial part of Christianity (in fact it is one of the 7 sacraments of Christianity) ; then how can anyone boldly claim that both religions are essentially the same in some sense, when they differ in such essential aspects?   
  
Note that this does not mean there cannot be religious tolerance. Acknowledging that there are fundamental differences between various religions is not a sign of religious bigotry anymore than the fact that acknowledging fundamental differences between various games is. But one can’t delude himself into pretending that there are no differences between religions in order to enable religious tolerance and interfaith dialogue. One can’t stop inter-religious conflict by pretending there is no difference at all that serves as a potential substance for conflict.
  
  
One reason I think we have become coaxed to the notion of all religions are the same is to avoid argument and debate - we have been taught to never talk about religious differences and keep it within ourselves. But this again is just avoiding the problem and instead we should encourage respectful debates and arguments regarding the differences of religions if we really are to foster inter-faith harmony. Argument per se is not ill-mannered as long as it is respectful. Pretending that there are no differences or that they are trivial won’t make us any safer and doesn’t make us more moral in anyway. Ignorance makes our world only more dangerous and tolerance and respect are empty shells if we are ignorant about who our rival religionists are.  
  
I recently came across the works of the 20th century Catholic theologian **Karl Rainer** who claimed that Buddhists, Hindus, and Jews are “***anonymous Christians***” who will make it to heaven in the world to come. This statement was made in the spirit of inclusivity and was appreciated by many because what more can signal more inclusivity than saying that people of other religions will be able to partake in heaven with you (instead of suffering eternal damnation in hell)? But as a Hindu, I felt insulted (rather than complemented) that my entire religious identity is reduced to just being an anonymous Christian because Hindus don’t even seek heaven at all. Many Catholics themselves opposed Rainer for contradicting the core Christian doctrine that there is no salvation outside the Church but the more fundamental problem is that heaven is not any goal for Hinduism.  
  
The popular metaphor given is that all religions being different paths to the same mountain peak. But in actuality, one religion is mountain trekking, another is like scuba-diving, and another is like skiing. They all have entirely different goals, and use different toolkits & metrics to reach that goal. Christian missionaries saying to me “*Jesus is the only way to salvation*” and Muslims saying “*Allah is the only road to Jannat* (paradise)” to evangelise Hindus and Buddhists is like cricket players saying “Bat & ball are the only road to scoring runs” to football players. Hindus are playing a different game with completely different metrics, tools, organisation, & rules. They don’t seek salvation or jannat anymore than a football player seeks runs & wickets. Hindus seek mokṣa, & they have developed sophisticated techniques for it. It is high time we seek and have honest and realistic conversations about religions if we really care.

Another important mistake that historians make is that they tell the history of the world as if religion did not play any role at all. One can routinely people claiming things like the Spanish conquered the Americas only for gold, the British came to New England just to catch fish, the Turks destroyed temples and churches only for looting the wealth, the French Revolution had nothing to do with Catholicism, the U.S. Civil Rights movement was a purely humanitarian endeavour, and also that the current contestation of Jerusalem and the Israel-Palestine conflict is just economic and political. But even if religion makes no sense to you, you need to make sense of religion in order to make sense of the people in the world whose behaviours are deeply motivated by religion.

Any meaningful inter-faith dialogue has to start only by acknowledging these fundamental differences. In this article, I summarise the twelve major world religions and also point out their fundamental contrasts that have to be acknowledged.

1.  Judaism

2.  Christianity

3.  Islam

4.  Hinduism

5.  Buddhism

6.  Jainism

7.  Confucianism

8.  Daoism

9.  Shintoism

10. Yoruba Religion

11. Communism

12. Atheism

The choice of the religions that I listed obviously is not random and was dependent on both popularly and personal bias. I have included all world religions that command huge number of followers and hence I have to include Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, Buddhism, Confucianism, and Daoism. I also cannot leave out Judaism because of its historical influence on Christianity and Islam. I have included Shintoism and Yoruba religion for some novelty. I have excluded many other religions either because they are minor or because I have not studied them in detail. I have included communism and atheism too in this list which might be surprising but I will convince you that these two ideologies too (especially communism) too are functionally religious despite their claims of being anti-religious.

# Koyaanisqatsi : Common Starting Point

The popular metaphor given is that all religions being different paths to the same mountain peak. But in actuality, I think it is the opposite. What religions share is not the end point but the starting point.

In the **Hopi** language, the word **Koyaanisqatsi** refers to the fact that life is out of balance.

> **All religions start with the presumption that there is something
> that is fundamentally wrong with human existence and the world in
> general.**

As Shakespeare says through his character Hamlet that there is something rotten, not just in the state of Denmark, but in the state of human existence itself, all religions (and communism as well) agrees that there is something fundamentally bad or wrong that characterises human existence. But it is on the details from here on that they part ways :

1.  **Problem / Diagnosis** : What is wrong with the human existence?

2.  **Solution** : What remedies the problem?

3.  **Technique / Path** : How to reach the goal?

4.  **Exemplar** : Who are the previous exemplars that have charted out
    the path for us?

In this article, I will summarise all the religions that I write about in this four-question answer format. Obviously this approach is simplistic and is just a starting point and there are lots of nuances that I am going to miss by summarising thousands of years of histories of about a dozen religions but one of the advantages of this approach is that this format exposes clearly and succinctly, the differences between world religions in an easily comparable form.

Another system with which we will also be comparing religions in this article are the seven dimensions. For example, functionally religious people tend to :

- perform rituals

- tell stories about the origins of the cosmos, early history, and so on
  and write them down in scriptures

- cultivate techniques of ecstasy and devotion

- organise themselves into institutions

- instruct human beings on how to act toward one another

- profess belief about the gods and the supernatural

- invest objects and places with sacred import.

Philosopher of religion **Ninian Smart** has referred to these aspects as the seven “dimensions” of religion -

1.  the ritual

2.  the narrative

3.  the experiential

4.  the institutional

5.  the ethical

6.  the doctrinal

7.  the material

Some religions emphasis one of these things more than the other. For example, the experiential dimension is central in Buddhism and Daoism, the ethical dimension is crucial in Confucianism, while Hinduism and Judaism emphasise the narrative dimension and Christianity and Islam give a heavy importance to doctrine. But all religions do not share the same ultimate goals, do not prescribe same rituals, and do not teach the same doctrines.

This article is addressed to both religious and non-religious people alike. You don’t have to believe in God yourself or be religious to realise that how religions have transformed individuals and societies from biblical Israel to contemporary India. You do not need to be a follower of a certain religion to understand the immense effects that religious rituals have on the people who undergo them and also on those who administer them. As Max Muller said about religion - “***he who knows (just) one, knows none***”, contrasting your religion with other religions will help you to illumine aspects of your own religion.

# Judaism : A Story of Exile and Return

To the outsider, religion is often assumed to be a matter of what you believe: Do you believe in God? Do you believe in an afterlife? Do you believe in sin? Judaism, however, challenges these assumptions from the start. It is not primarily a religion of belief (orthodoxy); it is a religion of practice (orthopraxy). It is not a system of abstract doctrines to be accepted, but a story to be entered into—a narrative of a specific people and their tumultuous, intimate, and often argumentative relationship with their God.

Judaism is the smallest of the great world religions, claiming only about 14 million adherents worldwide—roughly 0.2 percent of the human population. Yet, its influence is staggering. It gave birth to both Christianity and Islam, the two largest religions on Earth. Without Judaism, there would be no Jesus and no Muhammad. But to understand Judaism, one must look past its famous offspring and understand it on its own terms. It is a religion of memory, a religion of law, and above all, a religion of a people who have spent millennia wrestling with the experience of being far from home.

### The Problem: Exile

Every religion begins with a diagnosis of the human condition—a fundamental problem that needs solving. In Christianity, that problem is sin. In Buddhism, it is suffering. In Judaism, although it sometimes focuses on the problems of the human condition in general, but the central theme is on the the defining problem for its own community - which is **exile**.

Ever since the conquest of the kingdom of Judah and the exile of many of the Judeans by the Babylonian empire in 586 BCE, the Jews have always been a community displaced from their origins and have always been on the move. Even today, with the state of Israel re-established as a Jewish nation, more Jews live outside of Israel than inside and this has always been the case for two and a half millennia. In fact, the very word “diaspora” which means “dispersal” in Greek was used for the Jewish people. Most people who were exiled in antiquity lost their distinct identity and merged with their surrounding peoples. But the Jews did not and have managed to survive as a distinct people and remembering their origins for millennia. Judaism, as a religion, is the glue that enables this cohesion and their survival as a united distinct nation despite being in diaspora. Thus, the fundamental problem in Judaism is exile and displacement ; separation from their geography, separation from their God, and separation from their Jerusalem temple (which has still not been rebuilt).

This is not exile in a purely geographical sense, though the physical displacement of the Jewish people from their holy ancestral land is central to their history. It is also transferred to a deep, existential sense of distance : distance from God, distance from the community, and distance from where one ought to be and how things ought to be. The motivating tragedy of Judaism is not a single event like the death of a savior, but a chronic condition of being scattered and separated.

The Jewish narrative is a chronicle of this recurring trauma. It begins in the Book of Genesis with the very first humans, Adam and Eve, being exiled from the Garden of Eden for disobeying God. It continues with the first patriarch, Abraham, who is commanded to leave his home in Mesopotamia and become a wanderer for God. The motif repeats when the Israelites are enslaved in Egypt, living as strangers in a strange land. Even after they settle in their Promised Land, they are conquered by the Babylonians in 586 B.C.E., their holy Temple is destroyed, and they are marched into captivity.

For centuries, Jews have lived in the Diaspora - dispersed among the nations, often persecuted, always aware that they were not “home.” This physical exile mirrors a spiritual reality: the world itself is broken. Things are not as they should be. The Jewish God is a “God of movement,” and His people are a people on the move, forever wrestling with the distance between the world as it is and the world as it should be.

### The Solution: Return (*Tikkun Olam*)

If the fundamental problem that characterises Jewish existence is estrangement and brokenness, the solution is **Return**. The ultimate goal of Judaism is to bridge the gap between their God and humanity and to return to a state of wholeness.

In Hebrew, this concept is often tied to *tikkun olam*, meaning “to repair the world”. Unlike religions that focus on escaping this world for a heavenly paradise, Judaism is resolutely focused on the here and now. The goal is not to leave the world behind but to fix it ; to make this world a place where God can dwell.

This solution has taken different forms throughout history. In biblical times, the hope was often for a physical return to the land of Israel and the rebuilding of the Temple in Jerusalem. Over time, this evolved into a messianic hope : the belief that a **Messiah** (an anointed king) would come, defeat the enemies of their nation and usher in an era of peace and gather the exiles home. However, Judaism does not demand passive waiting. The work of return is an active duty. Jews are partners with God in the ongoing work of creation. By living righteously and following God’s laws, the community actively works to end the exile. They live in the tension between the reality of a broken world and the hope of a restored one, using their lives to bridge that gap.

### The Technique: Story and Law

How does a group of people survive exile? How do they keep their identity when scattered across the globe without a king, a state, a temple, or a country? The Jewish answer is twofold : they tell their **story** and they follow the **Law**.

#### 1. Story (Haggadah)

Judaism is a religion of memory. The word “religion” itself implies binding together, and Jews bind themselves to one another and to God by retelling their history. This is not just history for history’s sake; it is a sacred act of recollection.

The core of this storytelling is the **Tanakh**, the Hebrew Bible (which Christians call the Old Testament). It is an anthology of law, prophecy, poetry, and history that spans a millennium. But the story doesn’t end with the Bible. It continues in the **Talmud**, a massive collection of rabbinic debates and folklore, and in the **Midrash**, which fills in the gaps of biblical narratives.

To be a Jew is to step into this story. When Jews celebrate Passover, they do not merely recall that their ancestors were slaves in Egypt; they say, “We were slaves in Egypt.” They eat unleavened bread to physically taste the haste of their flight to freedom (commemorating the wandering in the desert for forty years after escaping Egypt) and eat bitter herbs to taste the bitterness of slavery. By internalising this narrative, they refuse to let the exile erase their identity.

#### 2. Law (Halakha)

If the story provides the identity, the Law provides the behavior. The Hebrew word for Jewish law is ***Halakhah***, which translates literally as “the way” or “the path” to walk.

For a non-Western audience, it is crucial to understand that ***Halakhah*** is not just a set of criminal or civil laws; it is a total way of life. It creates a sacred rhythm to existence. There are **613 commandments** (**miṣvot**) in the Torah covering every aspect of life, from how to worship God to how to treat a neighbor.

- **Ritual and Ethics:** The laws are divided into those between humans
  and God (ritual) and those between humans and other humans (ethical).
  Judaism insists these are inseparable. You cannot be holy before God
  if you are unjust to your fellow man.

- **The Sabbath (Shabbat):** Perhaps the most distinctive Jewish
  practice is the Sabbath. From sunset on Friday to sunset on Saturday,
  all creative work ceases. This is not just a day off; it is a
  sanctuary in time. By refraining from manipulating the world (no
  cooking, no writing, no driving), Jews acknowledge that they are not
  the masters of the universe—God is. It is a weekly rehearsal of the
  “return” to a state of peace.

- **Dietary Laws (Kosher):** Jews observe strict dietary laws known as
  keeping *kosher*. They do not eat pork or shellfish, nor do they mix
  meat and dairy products. These laws turn the biological act of eating
  into a religious act of discipline and identity, reminding the eater
  with every bite that they belong to a specific people set apart by
  God.

### The Exemplar: The Rabbi

In many religions, the key figure is a priest who mediates between the people and God, performing sacrifices and rituals. Ancient Judaism was like this, centered on the Temple in Jerusalem. But when the Roman Empire destroyed the Second Temple in 70 C.E., the religion had to transform to survive.

The sacrifices stopped, and the priests were replaced by a new exemplar: the **Rabbi**.

A rabbi is not a prophet or a savior. The word literally means “teacher”. The rabbi’s authority comes not from a divine bloodline or mystical power, but from knowledge of the texts. The rabbi is a scholar who studies the Torah and the Talmud and teaches the community how to apply the Law to their daily lives.

This shift changed Judaism from a religion of the temple to a religion of the book. The centre of religious life moved to the **synagogue** (a place of assembly) and the **yeshiva** (a school for study). In the yeshiva, students engage in *havruta*—study in pairs—where they debate the meaning of sacred texts. This culture of argumentation is holy in Judaism. To argue with God or with one’s fellows is not a sign of disrespect; it is a form of engagement. As the Nobel Laureate Elie Wiesel said: *A Jew who has no one to argue with ; quarrels with God*.

### The Diversity of Jewish Life

Just as there are many ways to tell a story, there are many ways to be Jewish. Modern Judaism is divided into several major movements, each addressing the problem of how to live as a Jew in the modern world.

1.  **Orthodox Judaism:** This branch maintains that the law (Torah) is
    eternal and divine. Orthodox Jews strictly observe the traditional
    dietary laws, the Sabbath, and gender roles (such as separate
    seating for men and women in synagogues). They focus on preserving
    the tradition against the changes of the modern world.

2.  **Reformed Judaism:** Emerging in 18th-century Europe, this movement
    sought to adapt Judaism to modern life. Reformed Jews view the laws
    as adaptable rather than absolute. They often conduct services in
    the local language (vernacular) rather than purely in Hebrew and
    emphasize the ethical teachings of the prophets over the ritual
    laws. They were the first to ordain women as rabbis.

3.  **Conservative Judaism:** This is a middle path. Conservative Jews
    believe that Jewish law is binding but that it can evolve and adapt
    to new circumstances through careful deliberation. They seek to
    conserve the tradition while engaging with the modern world.

4.  **Zionism:** While not a religious denomination, Zionism is a
    crucial political movement that sought to solve the problem of exile
    by returning Jews to their historic homeland. This movement
    culminated in the creation of the State of Israel in 1948, following
    the horrors of the Holocaust, where six million Jews were murdered
    by the Nazis.

5.  **Kabbalah (Mysticism):** For those seeking a more direct, spiritual
    experience, there is Kabbalah. This mystical tradition seeks to
    repair the brokenness of the cosmos itself. Kabbalists speak of
    ***Shekhina***, the divine feminine presence of God that dwells
    among the people in exile. By performing commandments, mystics
    believe they help reunite God with His Shekhina, healing the divine
    realm.

# The Way of Salvation: Christianity

If you ask a stranger in the West about their religion, they will likely tell you what they *believe*. This is a distinctively Christian way of thinking. While other religions prioritize rhythm, ritual, or law, or ethics - how you act - Christianity is arguably the most dogmatic of the great religions. It focuses intensely on **orthodoxy** (right belief) rather than **orthopraxy** (right practice).

Christianity is the largest religion in the world, claiming roughly one-third of the human population. To an outsider, it may appear confusingly diverse. It encompasses the incense-filled rituals of the Eastern Orthodox in Russia, the silent meetings of Quakers in England, and the ecstatic, drum-filled worship of Pentecostals in Brazil. Yet, despite this diversity, the tradition is held together by a shared narrative and a shared “soft monotheism.”

Unlike the “hard” monotheism of Islam or Judaism, which strictly forbids associating God with human forms, Christianity centers on a God who became human. This is the doctrine of the **Incarnation**: the belief that the one God took on flesh in the person of **Jesus of Nazareth**. This event is the fulcrum of history for Christians, fusing the sacred world and the secular world. To understand how this vast tradition operates, we must look at the specific diagnosis it makes of the human condition and the cure it offers.

### The Problem: Sin

Every religion identifies a fundamental defect in human existence. In Buddhism, it is suffering; in Islam, it is pride or self-sufficiency. In Christianity, the problem is **Sin**. For a non-Western audience, it is vital to distinguish the Christian “Sin” (capital S) from eastern notions of sins that arise from crime or performing morally bad actions (bad karma). In the Christian view, Sin is not merely a list of bad deeds like stealing or lying; it is a state of being. It is a fundamental disconnection or estrangement from God.

The Christian story explains this through the narrative of Adam and Eve. In the beginning, human beings lived in harmony with God in the Garden of Eden. However, they disobeyed God by eating forbidden fruit. Whereas in Judaism, this story prefigures the theme of exile, in Christianity, this act, often called “The Fall,” introduced Sin into the world. It is not just that humans commit sins; it is that humans *are* sinners.

This condition is often described as “**Original Sin**” It implies that human beings are born with a propensity toward wrongdoing. As the text notes, “*the wages of Sin is death*” (Romans 6:23). This death is not just physical mortality but a spiritual separation from the divine. Humans are unable to bridge this gap on their own. No amount of meditation, ritual washing, or ethical behaviour, or keeping of divine commandments, can fully erase the stain of sin. The human condition is one of helplessness; humanity is like a person drowning who just cannot swim.

### The Solution: Salvation

If the problem is a desperate situation of drowning in sin, the solution is a rescue operation. Christianity is, at its heart, a **rescue religion** for those who believe that Sin is the fundamental problem concerning human existence. The goal is **Salvation**. Salvation comes from a Latin word that literally means being “saved.” But saved from what? Christians seek to be saved from the consequences of Sin (death and hell) and to be granted “eternal life” in the presence of God.

This rescue was accomplished, according to Christian teaching, by God himself. Because humans could not climb up to God, God came down to humans. This is the story of Jesus. Christians believe Jesus was the “Son of God”—fully divine and fully human. He lived a sinless life and was executed by crucifixion by the Roman Empire.

For Christians, this death was not a tragedy but a transaction. On the cross, Jesus took the penalty of human sin upon himself. This is known as **Atonement**. Three days after his execution, Jesus rose from the dead (the **Resurrection**), defeating the power of death.

The good news (***Gospel***) of Christianity is that the price has been paid. The gap between the divine and the human has been bridged. The goal of the Christian life, therefore, is to accept this rescue and to secure a place in heaven and a relationship with God that lasts forever.

It is kind of like this. We humans are all drowning in an ocean of sin and we just cannot swim. So, God incarnated himself as his own son Jesus, who despite being fully divine, also became fully human, to rescue humans from Sin. Now, Jesus’ death on the cross was actually an atoning sacrifice for all the sins of mankind according to Christian theology. Jesus, who was human but also God, paid the penalties for Sin and defeated death for all of us, and through the resurrection, he got ascended and pulled up to unite with God. By believing in Jesus and accepting him as our only Lord and saviour, we participate in that atonement of Sin. Therefore, we also defeat death like Jesus and are raised up along with him to reunite with God. You are drowning in an ocean and you cannot swim according to Christianity. So, God incarnated himself as his Son amongst us on the ocean, for a rescue operation wherein God the Father stretches out his hand and pulls up his Son Jesus from the ocean of sin to himself through the resurrection. Our only hope is to hold firmly to Jesus so that we also will get pulled up along with him and get saved and rescued from the ocean of Sin and be eternally re-united with God. This is what salvation entails in Christianity. The key necessary condition for salvation is **faith** ; believing in Jesus as the only Lord and Saviour.

### The Technique: Faith and Works

If salvation is the goal, how does one achieve it? This question has caused deep divisions within Christianity, splitting the religion into rival camps that disagree on the proper technique for accessing divine power. Broadly, the technique involves a combination of **faith** (belief / trust) and **works** (rituals/ethics).

#### The Catholic Path: Faith and Sacraments

For Roman Catholics, who make up half of the world’s Christians, the technique involves cooperation with the church. Salvation is a process. It is accessed through **faith** but mediated through **sacraments**—rituals that convey God’s grace.

There are seven sacraments in Catholicism, but the two most central are:

- **Baptism:** A cleansing ritual using water, usually performed on
  infants, which washes away Original Sin and initiates the person into
  the church.

- **The Eucharist (Holy Communion):** A ritual meal of bread and wine.
  Catholics believe that during their worship service (the Mass), this
  bread and wine are supernaturally transformed (transubstantiated) into
  the actual body and blood of Jesus. By eating it, the believer
  physically takes the divine into their own body.

#### The Protestant Path: Faith Alone

In the 16th century, a movement called the Reformation split Christianity. The Reformers (such as Martin Luther) argued that rituals, priests, and institutions (like the Roman Catholic Church) were unnecessary barriers between the individual and God. Protestants argued that human beings are so sinful that they cannot possibly do anything to “earn” salvation. Therefore, the technique is **faith Alone** (*sola fides*). You are saved not by doing rituals, but by simply trusting that Jesus has saved you. For Protestants, the central religious technology is not the altar but the **Bible**. They encourage every believer to read the scriptures in their own language. Consequently, while Catholics emphasise the holiness of the Church as an institution, Protestants emphasise the authority of the Text and every person directly accessing God through reading and interpreting it without any mediating institutions (like the Church) or individuals (priests).

#### The Orthodox Path: Mystical Union

The Eastern Orthodox Church (dominant in places like Greece and Russia) offers a third technique. They focus less on legalistic questions of “paying for sin” and more on **theosis** (deification). Their goal is mystical union with God: “becoming by grace what God is by nature”. Their technique involves heavy use of **icons** (sacred images) and sensory-rich rituals to connect with the divine mystery.

### The Exemplar: Saints and Believers

Who shows the way for salvation? Because Christianity is so diverse, its exemplars also vary by tradition.

#### The Saint (Catholic/Orthodox)

In Catholic and Orthodox Christianity, the cosmos is crowded with spiritual heroes called **saints**. These are humans who lived with such abundant holiness that they can now intercede with God on behalf of the living. Some examples are :

- **The Virgin Mary:** The mother of Jesus is the supreme saint, revered
  as the “Mother of God.”

- **St. Francis of Assisi**: A medieval figure who abandoned wealth to
  find God in poverty and nature.

For a non-Western audience, the role of saints is similar to the role of ancestors or minor deities or personal spirits in other traditions. They are accessible intermediaries who help bridge the gap to the supreme God.

#### The Knight of Faith (Protestant)

Protestants rejected the cult of saints, viewing it as superstitious and bordering on idolatry. They emptied the cosmos of these intermediaries. For them, the exemplar is the **ordinary Believer**—the “knight of Faith” who trusts God completely without needing miracles or priestly magic. Protestant exemplars are often figures of conscience who rely on the Bible, such as Martin Luther King Jr. or the German theologian Dietrich Bonhoeffer.

### The Diversity of the Tradition

To understand Christianity, one must recognize that it is not a monolith. It has evolved into three major branches, and a fourth distinct “force” that is currently reshaping the world.

#### Roman Catholicism: The Institution

Catholicism is the oldest continuous international institution in the world. It is characterized by hierarchy. It is led by the **Pope** in the Vatican, who has supreme authority. It stresses the communal and visible nature of the church. To be Catholic is to belong to the institution that holds the “keys” to the Kingdom of God.

#### Protestantism: The Word

Protestantism is not a single institution but a collection of thousands of denominations (**Baptists, Methodists, Lutherans**, etc.). It is defined by what it rejects: the authority of the Pope. It emphasizes the **individual’s** relationship with God. If Catholicism is like a vast empire, Protestantism is like a marketplace of entrepreneurs, each reading the Bible and establishing their own communities.

#### Eastern Orthodoxy: The Mystery

Orthodoxy split from Catholicism roughly a thousand years ago. It has no Pope; instead, it is a collection of national churches (Greek Orthodox, Russian Orthodox) led by Patriarchs. It is deeply conservative, preserving ancient rituals and resisting change. It focuses on the **mystery** of God, using incense, music, and art to create a “heaven on earth” during worship.

#### Pentecostalism: The Spirit

The most significant development in modern Christianity is **Pentecostalism**. Emerging in the early 20th century, this movement focuses not on doctrine (like Protestants) or ritual (like Catholics), but on **experience**. Pentecostals believe the “Holy Spirit” (the third person of the Trinity) can possess the believer, granting miraculous powers such as “speaking in tongues” (ecstatic languages), prophecy, and faith healing. This is the fastest-growing form of Christianity, especially in the “Global South” (Africa, Asia, Latin America). It appeals to the poor and dispossessed by offering direct access to divine power and promising hope and healing in the here and now. All the miracle healings you see on the news or in TV which take place under charismatic healer pastors are all in Pentecostal churches.

### Christianity in the Modern World: The Shift to the Global South

A common misconception is that Christianity is a “white” or “Western” religion. While it was historically dominant in Europe, the center of gravity has shifted dramatically. Today, the “average” Christian is not a European man, but a Latin American or African woman.

- **The Browning of Christianity:** In 1900, 80% of Christians lived in
  Europe or North America. Today, over 60% live in Asia, Africa, and
  Latin America.

- **Explosive Growth:** In Africa alone, the Christian population
  exploded from 9 million in 1900 to over 350 million by the year 2000.

- **Diversity:** Today, there are more Catholics in the Philippines than
  in Poland and Germany combined. There are likely more Christians
  worshipping on a Sunday in China than in all of Europe.

This shift has changed the character of the faith. Southern Christianity is often more conservative, more supernatural, and more focused on miracles and spiritual warfare than its Western counterparts.

# Islam : The Way of Submission

While Christianity is often described as a religion of *belief* (focusing on correct doctrine), and Judaism is primarily a religion of practice, Islam focuses on *faith* and *practice* (focusing on correct action). It is a tradition of structure, rhythm, and discipline.

Islam is the second-largest religion in the world and the fastest-growing. Although the word ***Islam*** in Arabic is etymologically related to the Arabic word for peace ***salām***, which means “peace”, note that this is not a peace arrived at through negotiation or compromise. The word *Islam* literally means **“submission”** or **“surrender”.** A Muslim is one who submits. The path to peace, in this tradition, is a path of surrendering one’s individual will to the will of the one true God, Allah.

### The Problem: Self-Sufficiency (Hubris)

If the Christian problem is Sin (a moral corruption of the nature of the soul), the Islamic diagnostic problem for human existence is **self-Sufficiency**. According to the **Quran** (the holy scripture of Islam), human beings are not born sinful. Islam rejects the Christian concept of Original Sin. Humans, in fact, are born with an inclination towards Allah and the good, according to Islam. The problem is that we forget this. We drift into a state of pride and arrogance. We begin to act as if we can get along without God, as if we are the masters of our own universe. This leads to **shirk** which is not just idolatry in the literal sense but also making an idol out of our own self - the dangerous delusion that a human being is self-sustaining. This illusion that created humans are sustained without the creator leads to shirk which is associating partners with God. It covers idolatry because when worshipping a created idol, one mistakes the idol as providing sustenance which is only due to Allah. But, shirk is a much broader term and covers any form of hubris where we place anything equal to Allah. When we are proud, then we think that we are our own sustainer and we place ourselves equal to Allah. Whether it is an idol or the self, shirk is a very big deal and the symptom of the most fundamental problem characterising humanity which is hubris. This hubris is what separates humanity from Allah and leads to the “forgetfulness” of our true nature as servants of the Allah.

### The Solution: Submission (*Islam*)

If the problem is the arrogance of thinking we are independent, the solution is **submission**. The goal of Islamic life is to return to one’s natural state of dependence on God. This surrender results in a “soul at peace” in this life and the reward of **Paradise** in the next. Unlike religions that speak of salvation (being rescued from a fallen state), Islam speaks of success and stability. The imagery is often architectural: building a life that can stand firm. The ultimate destination is the afterlife. The Quran is filled with vivid descriptions of the “Garden” (Paradise)—a place of cool shade, running rivers, and peace—and the “Fire” (Hell), which awaits those who refuse to submit.

### The Technique: The Five Pillars

How does one conquer the idol of the self and achieve submission? The technique is a rigorous system of ritual practices known as the **five pillars**. These are not merely good deeds; they are the structural supports of the Muslim life.

#### Shahadah (Profession of Faith)

The central pillar is a simple declaration: “I testify that there is no god but God, and Muhammad is the messenger of God”. To become a Muslim, one need only say this phrase with sincere intent. It affirms Islam’s radical monotheism (the oneness of God) and the authority of Muhammad as His final prophet.

#### Salat (Prayer)

Five times a day—dawn, noon, mid-afternoon, sunset, and night—Muslims stop whatever they are doing to pray. This is not a casual conversation with God; it is a physical act of surrender. Worshippers face Mecca, the holiest city, and perform a choreographed set of movements: standing, bowing, and prostrating themselves with foreheads touching the ground. This physical posture of “kneeling before the King of the Universe” repeatedly and daily is designed to break the pride of the human ego.

#### Zakat (Charity)

Islam requires that believers give a portion of their wealth to the poor—typically 2.5% of their total assets (not just income). This is not viewed as a voluntary gift but as a duty. The word zakat connotes purification; by giving away wealth, Muslims “purify” the rest of their property and acknowledge that everything they own actually belongs to God.

#### Sawm (Fasting)

During the month of Ramadan, Muslims abstain from eating, drinking, smoking, and sexual activity from dawn until sunset. This month commemorates the time when the Quran was first revealed to Muhammad. By controlling basic bodily instincts, Muslims assert that they are not slaves to their appetites but servants of God. It is a time for discipline, recitation of scripture, and empathy for the hungry.

#### Hajj (Pilgrimage)

Once in a lifetime, every Muslim who is physically and financially able is obliged to travel to Mecca. During this pilgrimage, millions of Muslims from every race and nation dress in simple white sheets (symbolizing equality and purity) and perform rituals that reenact the stories of Abraham and his family. The highlight is circumambulating the **Kaaba**, the black cube shrine in the center of the Grand Mosque, believed to have been built by Adam and rebuilt by Abraham.

### Jihad

No term in the world’s religions is as controversial as *jihad*. It is also sometimes called the sixth pillar of Islam. While often understood as “holy war,” the word literally means **“struggle”**. Traditionally, Islamic theology has understood this concept to encompass two distinct types of exertion:

1.  **The Spiritual Struggle:** This is the internal battle against
    pride and self-sufficiency. It is the fight against the “idol of the
    self” to maintain submission to Allah.

2.  **The Physical Struggle:** This is the external battle against the
    “house of war” (enemies of Islam). This struggle utilises various
    tactics and also includes war.

Apologists try to insulate Islam from extremism by claiming jihad is *only* a spiritual concept. However, the Quran mentions war, and repeatedly so.

- **2:190-91:** Commands believers to “fight,” “slay,” and “expel”.

- **2:216:** States that fighting is “prescribed for you, though it be
  hateful to you”.

- **9:5 (The “Sword Verse”):** Commands Muslims to “slay the idolaters
  wherever you find them”.

- **9:123:** “O believers, fight the unbelievers who are near to you”.

- **4:76:** “Fight you therefore against the friends of Satan”.

While translating *jihad* as “holy war” may be technically incorrect, the “plain sense” of the term in scripture and practice includes a strong military dimension to it. While the Christian New Testament largely ignores the topic of war (leaving Christians to invent “just war” theories later), the Quran explicitly promotes and regulates it. It provides a code of conduct for warfare.

A [survey](https://www.pewresearch.org/religion/2007/05/22/muslim-americans-2007-event-transcript/?_legacy_redirect=483) conducted by the Pew Research among American Muslims found a greater propensity for younger Muslims to self-identify as Muslims rather than as Americans, more religious observance, and – this is all relative to older Muslims – also a much-higher-than-average expression of support for suicide bombing. Fifteen percent of the Muslims under 30 years of age said that suicide bombing could be justified “often” or “sometimes.” That compares to less than 5 percent among Muslims over 30 years of age. This pattern also repeats in Europe. While many Muslims outside the West do reject suicide bombing as a violation of Islamic law, significant minorities in quite a bit of countries, ranging from 25% to 75% ( 42% in Nigeria, 34% in Lebanon) believe it is justifiable in defence of Islam. In the Palestinian territories, support is found to be as high as 70% . It is high time that the Muslim community addresses this issue of radicalisation rather than washing off terrorism and pretending that Islam has got nothing to do with the Muslim terrorists who do commit suicide bombings. Christianity too had a lot of theological churn to give up its traditional hatred of the Jews and treatment of heretics and Islam too badly needs one soon.

### The Exemplar: Muhammad

In Christianity, Jesus is worshipped as God. In Islam, God (Allah) has no partners, no children, and no human form. Therefore, the exemplar of Islam, **Muhammad**, is emphatically *not* worshipped. He is considered a human being albeit the ideal human being. Muhammad (570–632 C.E.) is viewed as the “Seal of the Prophets,” the final figure in a long line of prophets that includes Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, and Jesus. While previous prophets were sent to specific peoples, Muslims believe Muhammad was the final one and sent to all of humanity. He is the exemplar because he was successful in both the religious and secular realms. Muhammad was a merchant, a husband, a father, a general, and a head of state. He established a community (***ummah***) that governed according to God’s law. When Muslims ask how to live, they look to his ***Sunnah*** (his example) and ***Hadith*** (his sayings) for guidance on everything from how to pray to even how to brush their teeth.

### The Scripture: The Quran

The holy book of Islam is the **Quran** (literal meaning : “the recitation”). For a non-Western audience, it is crucial to understand that the Quran is not like the Bible; it is like Jesus. In Christianity, the Word of God became flesh (Jesus); in Islam, the Word of God became a book (the Quran). Muslims believe the Quran is the perfect, untranslatable speech of God, revealed in Arabic to Muhammad by the angel Gabriel. Because it is the direct sound of the Divine, the recitation of the Quran is a sacred art. Many Muslims, even those who do not speak Arabic, memorise the entire book to carry the word of God within them.

### Diversity: Sunni, Shia, and Sufi

Like all major religions, Islam is not a monolith.

1.  **Sunni:** The vast majority (about 85%) of Muslims are Sunni. They
    believe that after Muhammad died, authority was passed to the
    *Caliphs* (leaders chosen by the community). They emphasize the
    consensus of the community and the law (*Shariah*).

2.  **Shia:** The minority (about 15%) are Shia. They believe authority
    should have stayed within Muhammad’s family, specifically with his
    son-in-law **Ali**. They are led by *Imams*, figures believed to
    have special spiritual authority. The Shia tradition emphasizes the
    suffering and martyrdom of their early leaders, particularly
    Hussein, Muhammad’s grandson.

3.  **Sufi:** This is the mystical tradition of Islam. Sufis seek not
    just to follow the law but to experience God directly in the “here
    and now.” They are often compared to the “drunk” in their ecstatic
    love for God, contrasting with the “sober” legalism of mainstream
    scholars. Sufis use poetry, music, and dance (like the Whirling
    Dervishes) to remember God and purify the heart.

# Confucianism : The Way of Propriety

To the modern observer, Confucianism can seem puzzling. It has no priests, no weekly services, and its temples are dedicated to mere mortals rather than gods. It is often described less as a religion and more as a philosophy or a system of ethics. Yet, for over two millennia, it has been the dominant force in East Asian life, shaping the minds and morals of nearly a quarter of the world’s population.

While religions like Christianity or Islam look to the heavens for salvation, Confucianism looks resolutely at the earth. It is arguably the most “this-worldly” of the great religious traditions. It does not care at a fundamental level about the afterlife, gods, or spirits. Instead, it cares passionately about *this* life—how to navigate the complex web of human relationships, how to govern a state, and how to become a fully realised human being. To understand Confucianism, one must abandon the Western distinction between the sacred and the secular. For Confucians, the secular *is* the sacred. The way to heaven is found not in escaping the world, but in engaging with it—through family, school, and society.

### The Problem: Chaos

Many religions emerge as a response to a crisis which then affects its diagnosis of the fundamental problem of human existence. For Confucianism, that crisis was the disintegration of society. Confucius lived during a period in China known as the “Age of Warring States,” a time of bloody conflict when the social order of the Zhou dynasty was crumbling into feudal violence. He was a rough contemporary of the Buddha. Therefore, the fundamental problem Confucianism identifies is social **chaos** (*disorder*). The world is broken not because of sin (as in Christianity) or suffering (as in Buddhism), but because society has fallen apart. People do not know their places; rulers are oppressive; children are rebellious; and basic human decency has been forgotten. The Confucian diagnosis is that civilization itself is fragile and prone to decay. Without a deliberate effort to maintain structure, human life degenerates into a state of “nasty, brutish, and short” anarchy.

### The Solution: Order (Social Harmony)

If the problem is chaos, the solution is **Order** or **Social Harmony**. The goal of the Confucian life is to restore the structure of society and, in doing so, align human beings with the “Way of Heaven” (*Tian*). This solution rests on a profound optimism about human nature. Confucians believe that human beings can improve. We are not born static ; we are malleable. Through education and effort, any ordinary person can become a **junzi**—an “exemplary person” or “superior man”. A *junzi* is a person of noble character who radiates virtue. When a society is filled with such people, harmony naturally follows. The Confucian dream is not a paradise in the afterlife, but a harmonious society here on earth, where everyone accepts their role and treats others with respect.

### The Technique: Ethics and Ritual

How does one turn a chaotic society into a harmonious one? How does an ordinary person become a *junzi*? Confucians reject the use of force or law to compel behavior. Instead, they rely on **education** and the twin techniques of **Ren** and **Li**.

#### 1. Ren (Human-heartedness)

Ren is the core inner virtue of Confucianism. It is often translated as “human-heartedness,” “benevolence,” or “compassion”. It refers to the feeling of empathy and kinship between people. To possess ren is to treat others as human beings. Confucius formulated an early version of the Golden Rule : “Do not impose on others what you yourself do not desire”. This inner virtue is the source of all right action.

#### 2. Li (Ritual/Propriety)

If ren is the inner seed, Li is the outer flower. Li literally means “ritual,” but it extends to encompass all forms of propriety, etiquette, and manners. For a non-Western audience, it is crucial to understand that “ritual” here does not just mean lighting incense in a temple. It means doing the proper thing in every social interaction.

- It is *Li* to bow to an elder.

- It is *Li* to take off your shoes before entering a home.

- It is *Li* to pour tea for a guest.

Confucians believe that these small, daily rituals are the glue that holds society together. By practicing good manners, we “tame” our raw impulses and show respect for others. *Li* transforms biological life into social life. It makes us human.

#### 3. The Five Relationships

The practice of *Ren* and *Li* takes place within a specific social structure known as the **Five Relationships**:

1.  **Ruler and Subject:** Loyalty and duty.

2.  **Parent and Child:** Filial piety (*xiao*) and deep love.

3.  **Husband and Wife:** Mutual obligation.

4.  **Elder Brother and Younger Brother:** Respect for age.

5.  **Friend and Friend:** Trust and equality.

Notice that most of these are hierarchical. Confucians believe that equality is a myth; in reality, we all have different stations in life. What we should seek should not be equality but harmony which comes when we accept our roles. The most important of these is the relationship between parent and child. **Filial Piety** (respect for one’s parents/ancestors) is the root of all virtue. If you cannot respect the parents who gave you life, you cannot possibly respect a stranger or a ruler.

### The Exemplar: Confucius

The exemplar of this tradition is **Confucius** (551–479 B.C.E.), known in China as *Kong Fuzi* (”Master Kong”). Confucius was not a messiah or a god. He did not perform miracles, and he did not claim to receive revelations from heaven. He was a teacher. Born in humble circumstances, he sought to solve the political chaos of his time by advising rulers to govern by virtue rather than force.

Although he failed in his political career—he never found a ruler who would fully implement his ideas—he succeeded as a “transmitter” of wisdom. He gathered the ancient classics of Chinese culture and taught them to his students, emphasizing that the path to peace lay in reviving the values of the past. Confucius viewed himself as a failure when he died, yet he established a model of the “sage” or “teacher” that would dominate East Asian civilization for centuries. He is the ultimate *junzi*: a man who cultivated his character until, as he famously said, by the age of seventy he could “follow his heart’s desire without overstepping the line”.

# Daoism : The Way of Flourishing

If Confucianism is the East Asian religion of the office, the school, and the family reunion, Daoism is the religion of the mountains, the artist’s studio, and the solitary wanderer. These two traditions have functioned as the yin and yang of Chinese culture for over two millennia. While Confucianism emphasizes social hierarchy, duty, and rigid etiquette, Daoism emphasizes freedom, nature, and spontaneity. For an audience unfamiliar with East Asian traditions, it is helpful to think of Daoism as the “counter-culture” to the “establishment” of Confucianism. Where Confucians value the structured life of the city, Daoists value the wild chaos of nature. Where Confucians worry about how to be good citizens, Daoists worry about how to be fully alive. Daoism is arguably the least “religious” of the great religions in the Western sense : it has no creed, and no desire to save the world from sin. Instead, it offers a path to physical and spiritual vitality.

### The Problem: Lifelessness (Artificiality)

Daoism identifies the fundamental human problem as **Lifelessness**. This state of being drained or “dead inside” is caused by civilisation itself. According to Daoists, rules, etiquette, social duties, and moral codes do not make us better people; they suffocate us. They are artificial structures that cut us off from the natural flow of the universe. The Daoist critique is that we are born soft and flexible (like a baby) but become hard and brittle (like a corpse) as we are molded by society. We exhaust our vital energy (*qi*) worrying about money, status, and what the neighbors think. We live lives of “galloping by sitting”—busy, anxious, and disconnected from our true nature. The problem is that we have forgotten how to be natural. We have become foreigners in our own bodies.

### The Solution: Flourishing (Returning to the Dao)

If the problem is a life constricted by artificial rules, the solution is **flourishing**. This is often described as a “return”—returning to the root, returning to nature, or returning to the uncarved block (*pu*) of our original simplicity. The goal is to live life to the fullest, to be physically healthy and spiritually vibrant.

For some Daoists, this flourishing is metaphorical: living a long, happy life in harmony with the world. For others, particularly in “religious” Daoism, the goal is quite literal: **immortality**. These Daoists seek to overcome death entirely, transforming the physical body into something that lasts forever, allowing them to ascend to the heavens as “Immortals” (*xian*).

Whether the goal is a long life or eternal life, the objective is to align oneself with the **Dao** (the Way). The Dao is the mysterious, impersonal force that flows through all things. It is the rhythm of nature—the source of the galaxies and the changing of the seasons. To flourish is to stop fighting this current and to float along with it.

### The Technique: *Wu Wei* (Natural Action)

How does one return to the Dao and achieve flourishing? The primary technique is **Wu Wei**. This is often translated as “non-action” or “inaction,” but a better translation for a non-Western audience might be “natural action” or “effortless action”. It does not mean doing nothing; it means doing nothing *unnatural*.

- **The Water Metaphor:** The supreme symbol of *wu wei* is water. Water
  is soft and yielding, yet it wears down the hardest rock. It does not
  strive to reach the ocean; it simply flows downhill, moving around
  obstacles rather than smashing into them. Daoists try to live like
  water: flexible, adaptable, and powerful through softness.

- **Wandering (*****Yu*****):** Another key technique is “wandering.”
  Unlike a traveler who has a destination and a schedule, a wanderer
  moves without a fixed purpose, open to the surprises of the road. By
  wandering through life without rigid goals, one remains open to the
  Dao.

- **Preserving Vital Energy (*****Qi*****):** On a more practical level,
  Daoists use techniques to preserve their *qi* (vital energy). This
  includes **qigong** (breath exercises), dietary regimes (avoiding
  grains), and even sexual practices designed to retain energy rather
  than expend it. The body is viewed as a laboratory where one cooks the
  “elixir of immortality”.

### The Exemplar: The Sage and The Immortal

Daoism offers two main types of exemplars: the philosophical **Sage** and the religious **Immortal**.

#### 1. The Sage (*Zhenren*)

The Sage, or “Genuine Person,” is the ideal figure in philosophical Daoism. This person acts with total spontaneity. They are not burdened by social expectations. The ultimate prototype is **Laozi**, the reputed author of the foundational Daoist text which is the **Daodejing**. Legend says he was a government archivist who, weary of the corruption of society, decided to leave civilization. He rode a water buffalo toward the western mountains. At the border, a guard asked him to write down his wisdom before he left. He dashed off 81 short poems—which became the Daodejing—and then vanished into the wild. Another famous sage is Zhuangzi, who refused a high government post because he preferred to “drag his tail in the mud” like a happy turtle rather than be a venerated, dead tortoise in a palace.

#### 2. The Immortal (*Xian*)

In religious Daoism, the exemplar is the Immortal—a figure who has supercharged their body with *qi* to the point where they possess supernatural powers. They can fly on clouds, heal the sick, and appear in multiple places at once. The “Eight Immortals” are beloved figures in Chinese folklore, representing ordinary people (a soldier, a merchant, a woman, an old man) who achieved transcendence through Daoist practice.

### The Scripture: The Daodejing

The core text of Daoism is the Daodejing (The Classic of the Way and Its Power). It is the second most translated book in the world (after the Bible). It is a short, poetic, and enigmatic text. It glories in paradox. It tells us that “the soft overcomes the hard,” that “the last shall be first,” and that “those who know do not speak; those who speak do not know.” It advises rulers to govern lightly, cooking a small fish—which creates a mess if you poke it too much.

# Hinduism : The Way of Moksha

If Islam and Christianity are religions of structure and history, Hinduism is a religion of geology. It is not a single building but a vast landscape composed of layer upon layer of history, belief, and practice. Hinduism is the oldest of the great religions of the world and the third largest. It is arguably the most diverse and least dogmatic religion on Earth. It has no single founder, no single scripture, and no single leader. It includes those who worship one God, and also those who worship thousands. To an outsider, this diversity can be dizzying. Yet, there is a shared logic underneath the cornucopia of colours and gods. While many Westerners associate Hinduism with the physical postures of yoga or the peaceful philosophy of nonviolence, its core is a fierce wrestling match with the cycles of time and the nature of existence itself. Many Hindus refer to their faith not as “Hinduism” (a term invented by outsiders) but as **Sanatana Dharma**, meaning “Eternal Law”.

### The Problem: Samsara (Reincarnation)

The Hindu diagnosis of the human condition stands in stark contrast to the modern Western view. In the West, reincarnation is often viewed as a romantic opportunity—a “second chance” to return to earth and get things right. For Hindus, however, reincarnation is the problem. They call it **samsara**, which literally means “wandering on” or “flowing by” referring to the soul repeatedly wandering across different bodies and states. It refers to the vicious, endless cycle of life, death, and rebirth. In this view, the world is a place of transience and suffering. We are born, we suffer, we die, and then we are born again to suffer and die again. This cycle is fuelled by **karma** (our own action). Every action we take creates a ripple effect that binds us to the world. As long as we are acting out of desire and ignorance, we are trapped in this loop, unable to find true peace. Even the heavens are part of this cycle; gods, too, are trapped in time, destined to eventually fade.

### The Solution: Moksha (Release)

If the problem is being trapped in an endless cycle of rebirth, the solution is to get out. The goal of Hinduism is **moksha**, which means “release” or “liberation”. Moksha is not “salvation” in the Christian sense (being saved from sin), because Hindus do not believe in Sin. Instead, it is an escape from the laws of karma and samsara. It is the moment when the wandering of the soul stops. What happens after this release? Opinions vary.

- For some, it is a loving union of the individual soul with a personal
  God.

- For others, it is an impersonal merging into the divine essence, like
  a drop of water returning to the ocean.

- For still others, it is a state where one enjoys eternal bliss with
  the god(s).

Regardless of the destination, the goal is freedom :freedom from the burden of life, death, and rebirth.

### The Technique: The Yogas (Disciplines)

How does one move from the trap of samsara to the freedom of moksha? Hinduism offers not one path, but many. These paths are called **yogas** (disciplines). While the West thinks of yoga as stretching exercises, in Hinduism, it is a spiritual technology designed to “yoke” the human spirit to the divine. There are three primary yogas, each complementary to another or suiting different personalities (opinions on this too vary):

#### 1. Karma Yoga ( The Discipline of Action)

This is the path for the active. Originally, this referred to the complex Vedic fire sacrifices performed by priests to maintain cosmic order. Over time, it evolved into the path of selfless service. By performing one’s sacred duty (dharma) without attachment to the results ; for example by doing good not for a reward, but simply because it is right ; a person can act without accumulating the “karma” that binds them to rebirth.

#### 2. Jnana Yoga (The Discipline of Wisdom)

This is the path for the philosopher. It is a steep and difficult path of study and meditation. The goal here is to use the mind to cut through the illusions of the world (**maya**). The central insight of this path is that the individual human soul (**atman**) is actually identical to the divine essence of the universe (**brahman**). The classic teaching is “Tat Tvam Asi”—”You Are That”. When a person realizes that they are not their small, temporary ego but the eternal, infinite spirit, they achieve liberation.

#### 3. Bhakti Yoga (The Discipline of Devotion)

This is the path for the emotional. It is the “way of love.” This is by far the most popular form of Hinduism. Practitioners of bhakti yoga do not need to perform complex rituals or understand high philosophy; they only need to love their god. By surrendering themselves completely to a deity ; whether it be Shiva, Vishnu, Krishna, or the Goddess ; they believe the god will use grace to carry them out of samsara.

- **Puja:** The central ritual of devotion is *puja*, an act of worship
  involving food offerings, incense, and light.

- **Darshana :** The climax of worship is *darshana*, or “seeing.”
  Hindus go to temples not just to pray, but to see the god (in the form
  of an icon) and to be seen by the god.

### The Exemplar: The Renouncer (Sannyasin)

Because Hinduism has layers of history, it has different exemplars.

- In ancient times, the exemplar was the **priest**, who kept the world
  spinning through fire sacrifices.

- In the Bhakti tradition, the exemplar is the **devotee,** the ordinary
  person whose heart is filled with love.

- However, the most distinctive and enduring exemplar of the Hindu
  tradition is the renouncer (or Sannyasin). This is the “holy man”
  often seen in images of India: the ascetic who has left behind family,
  job, and possessions to wander in search of moksha. The renouncer is a
  living symbol of the solution to samsara. He has “died” to the social
  world—often performing his own funeral rites—to seek the eternal self.
  While most Hindus remain “householders” (raising families and working
  jobs), they revere the renouncer as the one who has taken the direct
  path to the ultimate goal.

### The Diversity of the Divine

Hinduism is famous for its “mathematics of divinity.” Does it have 330 million gods? One God? No God? The answer is “yes” to all.

- **Many Gods:** Hindus worship a vast pantheon. **Ganesha**, the
  elephant-headed remover of obstacles, is honoured before any new
  venture. **Shiva** is the destroyer and the great ascetic. **Vishnu**
  is the preserver who incarnates (as **Rama** or **Krishna**) to
  restore justice. The **Mahadevi** (Great Goddess) appears as a warrior
  or a mother.

- **One God:** Many Hindus believe these thousands of deities are just
  different “masks” or manifestations of one supreme reality
  (*Brahman*). As the ancient Rig Veda says, “Truth is one, the wise
  call it by many names.”

# Buddhism : The Way of Awakening

Buddhism is often the favourite religion of modern Westerners, even those who do not practice it. It is frequently romanticized as a rational philosophy rather than a religion ; a peaceful, godless science of the mind that fits perfectly with modern psychology. However, to view Buddhism merely as an stress-relief technique is to miss its radical core. It is a religion that begins with a devastating diagnosis of the world. While Christianity says the world is fallen and Judaism says it is broken, Buddhism says the world is burning. It is a tradition that seeks to wake us up from the nightmare of our lives. Claiming roughly 7% of the world’s population, Buddhism is the fourth largest religion. It originated in India but is now a pan-Asian (and increasingly global) faith. It is a religion not of “being saved” by a god, but of “waking up” through one’s own effort.

### The Problem: Suffering (*Dukkha*)

Every religion starts by telling us what is wrong. For Buddhism, the problem is **Suffering** (in Pali, ***dukkha***). For a new audience, it is crucial to understand that *dukkha* means more than just physical pain or sadness. It refers to a pervasive sense of **unsatisfactoriness**. It covers everything from the agony of losing a loved one to the subtle anxiety that “something isn’t quite right” even when things are going well. The root of this suffering is **Impermanence** (*anicca*). Buddhists observe that everything changes. Flowers wilt, buildings crumble, loved ones die, and feelings fade. Because we crave permanence in a world that cannot provide it, we suffer. We cling to things that are slipping through our fingers. Furthermore, Buddhism argues that there is no “self” (Pali : ***anatta***). We think we are permanent, unchanging souls, but we are actually just a collection of shifting thoughts and body parts. Suffering arises because we cling to the illusion of “I” and “mine.”

### The Solution: Nirvana (Extinguishing)

If the problem is the suffering caused by clinging, the solution is **nirvana**. The word *nirvana* literally means “blowing out,” like extinguishing a candle. But what is being blown out? It is the extinguishing of suffering, and specifically the extinguishing of the “Three Poisons” that fuel it: greed, anger, and ignorance. Nirvana is not a place like Heaven where you go after you die. It is a state of being that can be achieved in this life. It is the moment when the burning stops. When you stop clinging to the world and to your own ego, you achieve liberation. You are released from *samsara*—the endless, weary cycle of birth, death, and rebirth.

### The Technique: The Eightfold Path

How does one move from suffering to nirvana? The Buddha was very practical. He described himself as a doctor prescribing a cure. That cure is the **Noble Eightfold Path**.

This is not a set of commandments to be obeyed, but a regimen to be practiced. It is often grouped into three disciplines:

1.  **Wisdom (Prajna):**

    - **Right View:** Understanding the reality of suffering and
      impermanence.

    - **Right Intention:** Committing oneself to the path of mental
      cultivation.

2.  **Ethics (Sila):**

    - **Right Speech:** No lying, slander, or harsh language.

    - **Right Action:** No killing, stealing, or sexual misconduct.

    - **Right Livelihood:** Not working in jobs that cause harm (like
      selling weapons or intoxicants).

3.  **Meditation (Samadhi):**

    - **Right Effort:** Cultivating positive states of mind.

    - **Right Mindfulness:** Being totally aware of the present moment
      (a technique now famous globally).

    - **Right Concentration:** developing deep mental focus.

Through **meditation**, Buddhists train the mind to see reality as it is, breaking the habits of clinging and desire.

### The Exemplar: The Buddha, The Arhat, and The Bodhisattva

The primary exemplar is, of course, the **Buddha** (the historical prince Siddhartha Gautama). Born a prince in roughly the 6th century B.C.E., he lived a life of luxury until he saw the reality of old age, sickness, and death. He renounced his throne to become a wandering ascetic. After years of failure, he sat under a Bodhi tree and vowed not to rise until he found the answer to suffering. After a long night of spiritual struggle, he “woke up” (the word *Buddha* means “The Awakened One”).

However, as Buddhism evolved, different exemplars emerged:

- **Arhat (Theravada Buddhism):** In the oldest school of Buddhism
  (Theravada, dominant in Sri Lanka and Thailand), the ideal is the
  *arhat*. This is a monk who, like the Buddha, has achieved
  enlightenment and will not be reborn. It is a solitary path of
  self-discipline.

- **Bodhisattva (Mahayana Buddhism):** In the later, larger school
  (Mahayana, dominant in China, Japan, and Korea), the ideal is the
  *Bodhisattva*. This is a figure who is on the brink of nirvana but
  **turns back** to help others. Motivated by compassion (*karuna*), the
  Bodhisattva vows not to enter final nirvana until every single grass
  blade and sentient being is saved. This introduced a pantheon of
  “celestial bodhisattvas”, like **Avalokiteshvara** (Chinese : Guanyin)
  who function much like Catholic saints or Hindu gods ; powerful beings
  who can answer prayers and offer grace.

- **Lama** (Tibetan Buddhism) : The Lamas are the exemplars in Tibetan
  Buddhism. The head of the community who functions like the Pope of
  Catholicism is the Dalai Lama.

### The Diversity of the Tradition

Buddhism is not one thing; it is a family of traditions.

1.  **Theravada (“Way of the Elders”):** The conservative branch. It
    focuses on the historical Buddha as a human teacher and emphasizes
    monastic life and individual effort. (Key countries: Thailand,
    Myanmar, Sri Lanka).

2.  **Mahayana (“Great Vehicle”):** The expansive branch. It views the
    Buddha as a cosmic, eternal principle. It emphasizes compassion and
    the power of Bodhisattvas to assist laypeople in achieving nirvana.
    (Key countries: China, Japan, Vietnam).

3.  **Vajrayana (“Diamond Vehicle”) :** The mystical branch, often known
    as Tibetan Buddhism. It uses intense rituals, chanting, and
    visualization to achieve enlightenment quickly ; in a single
    lifetime. Its leader, the **Dalai Lama**, is believed to be the
    reincarnation of the Bodhisattva of Compassion.

# Jainism : The Way of Ahimsa

To the casual observer, Jainism might look like an extreme form of Hinduism or Buddhism. Its monks wander naked or wear white robes, sweep the ground before them to avoid stepping on bugs, and wear masks to avoid inhaling microscopic life. However, Jainism is a distinct, ancient tradition with a unique view of the universe. It is perhaps the only major religion that is strictly **atheistic** (it denies a creator god) yet deeply **spiritual** (believing in the eternal soul). For Jains, no god can help you. You are the master of your own destiny, and the universe is a giant cosmic mechanism that you must navigate alone. It is a religion of “heroism.” In fact, the word **Jain** comes from the Sanskrit *Jina*, meaning “Conqueror” or “Victor.” But this is not a conquest of other lands; it is a conquest of the self.

### The Problem: The Weight of Karma

The Jain diagnosis of the human condition is unique. In Hinduism, karma is often viewed as a cosmic law of cause and effect. In Jainism, **Karma** is strictly **material**. Jains view karma as a subtle form of matter—like sticky, invisible dust or sludge. The universe is filled with these floating karmic particles. Whenever we act with passion, anger, greed, or ego, our soul (Jiva) becomes moist and sticky. This causes karmic matter to stick to the soul. Therefore, the problem is weight. The soul is naturally light, luminous, and omniscient. It wants to float to the top of the universe. However, because it is encrusted with layers of heavy karmic matter, it is weighed down and trapped in the cycle of rebirth (samsara). It sinks into physical bodies (human, animal, or even microscopic) and suffers.

### The Solution: Kaivalya (Omniscience)

If the problem is being weighed down by karmic dust, the solution is to scrub it off and become weightless. The goal of Jainism is Kevala (or Kaivalya), which means “isolation” or “aloneness.” This is the state of supreme omniscience and bliss. When a person successfully sheds all their karma, their soul is no longer heavy. It instantly floats up to the very ceiling of the universe, a place called the **Siddhashila** (Realm of the Liberated Ones). There, the soul dwells forever in a state of crystal-clear consciousness, isolated from matter and suffering. It does not merge with God (as in Hinduism) or dissolve into nothingness (as in Buddhism). It remains a distinct, individual, perfect star in a galaxy of other perfect stars.

### The Technique: Ahimsa and Asceticism

How does one scrub the soul clean? The Jain technique is the most rigorous ethical code in the world. It involves two steps: stopping the influx of new karma (*samvara*) and burning off old karma (*nirjara*). This is achieved through the **three jewels**: Right Faith, Right Knowledge, and Right Conduct. The core of Right Conduct is the Great Vows (*mahavratas*):

#### 1. Ahimsa (Radical Non-Violence)

This is the supreme principle of Jainism: Ahimsa Parmo Dharma (”Non-violence is the highest duty”). Jains believe that everything—humans, animals, plants, water, fire, and rocks—has a soul (Jiva). Therefore, to hurt any being is to accumulate heavy, dark karma.

- **Diet:** Jains are strict vegetarians. They also avoid root
  vegetables (like potatoes and onions) because pulling them out kills
  the plant and disturbs soil organisms.

- **Practice:** Monks and nuns take this to the extreme, straining their
  water and walking only by daylight to avoid accidentally harming
  insects.

#### 2. Anekantavada (Many-Sidedness)

To avoid the violence of thought, Jains practice Anekantavada—the doctrine that truth has many sides. They use the famous parable of the blind men and the elephant: one feels the trunk and says it’s a snake; another feels the leg and says it’s a tree. Both are partially right, but neither sees the whole truth. This technique promotes intellectual humility and tolerance. A Jain will rarely say, “You are wrong.” They will say, “From your perspective, that is true.”

#### 3. Tapas (Asceticism)

To “burn off” the old karma that is already stuck to the soul, Jains practice intense tapas (literal meaning : heat). This includes fasting (Jains are famous for their long fasts), meditation, and voluntary poverty. By denying the body’s desires, the Jain creates the spiritual heat necessary to melt away the karmic crust.

### The Exemplar: The Tirthankaras

Because there is no God to help us, Jains look to human guides who have successfully crossed the river of rebirth. These are the **Tirthankaras** (“Ford-Makers”). In every cosmic cycle, 24 Tirthankaras appear to teach the path of liberation. They are not gods, but human beings who achieved perfection. They are worshipped not to ask for favors, but as idealized models of what the soul can become.

- **Mahavira (The Great Hero):** The 24th and final Tirthankara of our
  era (contemporary with the Buddha, c. 500 B.C.E.). Born a prince, he
  renounced the world at age 30. He spent 12 years in deep silence and
  meditation, enduring abuse and physical hardship, until he achieved
  *kaivalya*. He is the primary exemplar for modern Jains.

- **Bahubali:** A revered figure who stood in meditation for so long
  that vines grew up his legs. His massive statue in India is a major
  pilgrimage site, representing total detachment from the body.

# Yoruba Religion : The Way of Connection

If you pick up a standard textbook on world religions, you might find a chapter at the very back labeled “primal,” “tribal,” or “primitive” religions. Lumping together traditions from Africa, Australia, and the Americas, these chapters often treat these faiths as relics of the past. This is a mistake. The Yoruba religion of West Africa is not a museum piece; it is a vibrant, global faith with perhaps 100 million practitioners ; more than Judaism, Sikhism, or Jainism. It is a “world religion” in every sense of the term. Originating in Yorubaland (modern-day Nigeria and Benin), it was carried across the ocean on slave ships to the Americas, where it evolved into vibrant new forms like **Santería** (in Cuba) and **Candomblé** (in Brazil). Unlike Christianity or Islam, which are “religions of the Book” centered on belief, Yoruba religion is a “religion of the hand” and body. It is pragmatic and earthy. It is not about saving your soul for the afterlife; it is about gathering the power to thrive in *this* life.

### The Problem: Disconnection

The Yoruba diagnosis of the human condition is not sin (as in Christianity) or suffering (as in Buddhism). The problem is **Disconnection**². According to Yoruba cosmology, before we are born, each of us chooses a destiny (*ayanmo*) at the feet of the High God, **Olodumare**. We choose our character, our occupation, and our measure of good or bad luck. This destiny is essentially our “inner head” or **Ori**.

The tragedy of human life is that at the moment of birth, we forget. We arrive in the world with amnesia, disconnected from the destiny we chose. We wander through life aimlessly, or we struggle against the current of our own fate. We become disconnected not just from ourselves, but from the spiritual entities (***orishas***), our ancestors, and our community. This disconnection leads to a life of friction, illness, and bad luck.

### The Solution: Connection

If the problem is wandering in a state of forgetfulness and disconnection, the solution is **Connection**. The goal of the Yoruba religious life is to reconnect with one’s destiny and with the spiritual powers that can help us fulfil it. This is not a religion of “salvation” where one seeks to escape the world. It is a religion of flourishing like Daoism. The aim is to live a long, healthy, wealthy, and meaningful life here and now, and eventually to become a revered ancestor.

Central to this solution is the concept of **Ashe**. *Ashe* is the power to make things happen. It is the sacred energy that animates the universe. It flows through gods, humans, animals, and plants. To flourish is to gather *ashe*, to stop wasting it, and to use it to drive your life forward.

### The Technique: Divination, Sacrifice, and Possession

How does one recover a forgotten destiny and gather *ashe*? The Yoruba tradition offers a “technological” suite of rituals designed to repair connections.

#### 1. Divination (Ifa)

Because we have forgotten our destiny, we need help remembering it. This is the role of Ifa Divination. When a person faces a crisis (sickness, a business failure, a romantic problem, etc..) they visit a priest known as a **Babalawo** (”Father of Secrets”) or **Iyalawo** (”Mother of Secrets”).

The priest does not give his own opinion. Using a divining chain or palm nuts, he accesses the memory of **Orunmila** (the god of wisdom). The random pattern of the cast points to a specific verse from a vast oral library of poetry (the **Odu**). The priest recites the story, and the client finds their own life reflected in the ancient myth. It is a way of retrieving the “script” of one’s life.

#### 2. Sacrifice (Ebo)

Once the problem is identified, it must be fixed through Sacrifice (ebo). This is a transaction. Reciprocity is the rule of the universe; you have to give to get. Sacrifices can range from a few cowrie shells or a piece of fruit to an animal (like a chicken). These offerings feed the spirits, engaging them in the solution of human problems. By feeding the gods, humans gain the power (ashe) needed to change their circumstances.

#### 3. Spirit Possession

In the Diaspora (especially in the Americas), the most dramatic technique is possession. In a drumming ceremony, a practitioner does not just pray to a god; they become the god. The deity “mounts” the devotee like a rider mounts a horse. The person’s personality is temporarily displaced, and they dance, speak, and act with the distinct mannerisms of the god. This collapses the distance between the divine and the human, bringing the power of heaven literally down to earth.

### The Exemplars: The Orishas

The Yoruba cosmos is populated by a High God, **Olodumare**, who is the remote source of all being. But Olodumare is distant and not worshipped directly. The active figures in religious life are the **Orishas**. The Orishas are superhuman beings who are “larger than life, but not other than life”. They have distinct personalities, favorite foods, and profound flaws. They are not distant saints; they are powerful, complicated personalities with whom humans form intense relationships.

Some of the key Orishas include:

- **Eshu:** The Messenger and Trickster. He stands at the crossroads. He
  delivers sacrifices from humans to the gods. If you don’t feed Eshu
  first, your message never gets through. He introduces chaos to test
  our character.

- **Orunmila:** The god of wisdom and destiny. He knows the secret of
  every human life and speaks through divination.

- **Ogun:** The god of iron and war. He is the patron of anyone who
  works with metal—blacksmiths, soldiers, and today, truck drivers and
  surgeons. He is creative and destructive, violent and protective.

- **Oshun:** The goddess of rivers, love, and fertility. She represents
  the sweet power of women. In a famous story, the male gods tried to
  fix the world without her and failed miserably; they had to beg for
  her return to restore the waters of life.

- **Shango:** The god of thunder, lightning, and male virility. He was a
  former human king who became a god, representing raw power and
  justice.

- **Obatala:** The god of creativity and coolness. He shapes the human
  form in the womb.

### Elasticity and Survival

One of the most remarkable traits of Yoruba religion is its elasticity. When Yoruba slaves were taken to the Americas, they were forbidden from worshipping their gods. Instead of abandoning their faith, they adapted it. They recognized their Orishas in the Catholic saints.

- **Ogun**, the warrior, was worshipped as **St. Peter** or **St.
  George**.

- **Shango**, the thunder god, became **St. Barbara** (whose father was
  struck by lightning).

- **Oshun**, the goddess of love, became **Our Lady of Charity**.

This was not just a mask; it was a fusion. This ability to absorb and adapt allowed the religion to survive slavery and thrive in the modern world. Today, a practitioner might go to Mass on Sunday and consult a Babalawo on Monday, seeing no contradiction. It is a system of “both/and” rather than “either/or”.

# Shintoism : The Way of Purification

If Judaism is the religion of *story* and Hinduism is the religion of *devotion*, Shinto is the religion of **presence**. Shinto (literally “The Way of the Kami”) is the indigenous faith of Japan. It is one of the few major religions that has remained strictly ethnic like Judaism ; it has no desire to convert the world. It has no founder, no sacred scriptures (like the Bible or Quran), and no formal theology. Instead, it is a religion of “awe” directed at the natural world. For the Japanese, the sacred is not found in a distant heaven, but in the landscape itself—in twisted pine trees, towering waterfalls, and mountain peaks. Shinto is arguably the religion most focused on the concept of *national* identity, binding the Japanese people to their land and their past.

### The Problem: Pollution (*Tsumi*)

Every religion identifies a fundamental defect in the human condition. In Shinto, the problem is not sin (moral guilt) or suffering (existential pain). The problem is **pollution** (in Japanese, *Tsumi*). This is a physical and spiritual heaviness. *Tsumi* can be caused by unethical behavior, but it can also be caused by contact with things that are “unclean,” such as death, blood, or disease. In the Shinto worldview, human beings are born pure. We are not “fallen” creatures. However, as we move through the world, we accumulate dust, grime, and bad luck, much like a mirror that gathers dust over time. This pollution cuts us off from the *kami* (the spirits) and causes misfortune, sickness, and unhappiness.

### The Solution: Purity (*Kiyome*)

If the problem is getting dirty, the solution is getting clean. The goal of Shinto is **purity** (*Kiyome*) or **harmony** (*Wa*). The objective is to return to one’s natural state of brightness and clarity (known as *akaki kokoro* or “bright heart”). When a person is pure, they are in harmony with the natural world and the community. Shinto does not focus on the afterlife. It is resolutely focused on *this* life : on fertility, crops, health, and family safety. The “solution” is to maintain a harmonious relationship with the *kami* so that life can flourish.

### The Technique: Ritual Purification (*Harae* and *Misogi*)

How does one wipe the dust off the mirror? The Shinto technique is entirely ritualistic. There is no emphasis on “believing” the right things; the emphasis is on **doing** the right things to restore purity.

#### 1. Misogi (Purification by Water)

This is the most dramatic technique. It involves cleansing the body with water.

- In its most intense form, practitioners stand under freezing cold
  waterfalls or bathe in the ocean during winter to shock the spirit
  into clarity.

- In daily life, this is seen at every Shinto shrine. Before entering,
  visitors stop at a water basin (*temizuya*) to wash their hands and
  rinse their mouths. You cannot approach the gods while physically or
  spiritually dirty.

#### 2. Harae (Priestly Purification)

This is a formal ritual performed by a Shinto priest. The priest waves a wand adorned with paper streamers (*onusa*) over the person, object, or new car (yes, cars are purified for safety). This act sweeps away impurities and bad spirits.

#### 3. Matsuri (Festivals)

Shinto is famous for its loud, vibrant festivals. These are not solemn church services but raucous community parties. The *Kami* are carried through the streets in portable shrines (*mikoshi*). The noise, music, and sweating bodies are believed to entertain the gods and revitalize the community’s energy.

#### The Exemplar: The Kami

Shintoism does not have a human exemplar like Jesus or the Buddha. The “model” figures are the **Kami** themselves. What are the kami? They are often translated as “gods,” but “spirits” or “phenomena” is better. Kami is anything that inspires awe.

- **Nature as Kami:** The sun, the moon, Mt. Fuji, old trees, and oddly
  shaped rocks are all *Kami*.

- **Ancestors as Kami:** Great humans—emperors, warriors, or beloved
  ancestors—become *Kami* after death.

- **The Imperial Line:** The most important figure in Shinto mythology
  is **Amaterasu** (the Sun Goddess). The Emperor of Japan is
  traditionally believed to be her direct descendant.

The “ideal” in Shinto is not to *be* a Kami (which usually happens only after death), but to live in **Kannagara**—”the way of the Kami.” This means living with sincerity (*makoto*) and gratitude, aligned with the natural rhythms of the world.

# Communism : The Way of Revolution

To the Marxist, calling communism a religion is an insult. Karl Marx famously called religion the “opiate of the masses”; a painkiller administered by the ruling class to keep the poor too sedated to revolt. Communism claims to be a science (dialectical materialism), not a faith. However, if we look at how it actually functions in practice, communism ticks every religious box functionally and it resembles more like Christianity and Islam in many respects. It has its own holy scriptures (*The Communist Manifesto*), its own prophets (Marx and Engels), its own saints (Lenin, Mao, Che), its own heretics (Trotskyites), and its own vision of the End Times (The Revolution). It is a “secular salvation myth” that demands total devotion from its converts.

### The Problem: Class Struggle (Alienation)

Every religion identifies a fundamental flaw in the world. For Communism, the problem is **class struggle**, driven by the institution of **private property**. In the communist worldview, the “Original Sin” was the invention of private ownership. This created a divide between the “haves” (the **bourgeoisie**) and the “have-nots” (the **proletariat**). This structure leads to **alienation**. Just as Christians believe sin separates humans from God, Marxists believe capitalism separates humans from the fruits of their labor. The worker makes a chair, but the chair belongs to the boss. The worker is reduced to a cog in a machine, dehumanized and exploited. The world is a place of suffering, not because of karma or devils, but because of the material conditions of capitalism.

### The Solution: The Classless Society (Communism)

If the problem is the hell of capitalism, the solution is the heaven of **communism**. This is the **Utopia**. It is a prophesied time in the future where history stops. In this state:

- Private property is abolished.

- The State withers away (no police, no armies, no borders).

- There is no hunger or poverty.

- The guiding principle is: *“From each according to his ability, to
  each according to his needs.”*

This functions exactly like the “Kingdom of God”. It is the perfect state of being where the contradictions of history are finally resolved, and humanity lives in total harmony.

### The Technique: Revolution and Party Discipline

How do we get from the hell of capitalism to the heaven of Communism? We cannot pray it into existence. The technique is **revolution** followed by the **dictatorship of the proletariat**.

- **Violent Struggle:** Just as Muslims might struggle against the self
  (Jihad), Communists believe in the struggle against the oppressor.
  Violence is often viewed as a purifying force—the “midwife” of the new
  society.

- **The Party (The Church):** The Communist Party functions as the
  “priesthood.” They are the “vanguard,” the elite few who understand
  the truth of communism and guide the masses.

- **Self-Criticism (Confession):** In Maoist China and Soviet Russia,
  party members engaged in “struggle sessions” or “self-criticism.” They
  would publicly confess their ideological impurities (wrong thoughts)
  and beg for forgiveness from the group. This is structurally identical
  to the Christian practice of confession and penance.

### The Exemplar: The Revolutionary Leader

Communism has a clear pantheon of exemplars who show the way.

- **Karl Marx (The Prophet):** He is the lawgiver, the Moses figure. He
  did not lead the people to the promised land himself, but he received
  the “revelation” (the theory) and wrote the “scriptures” (*Das
  Kapital* and *The Manifesto*).

- **Vladimir Lenin (The Messiah):** He is the one who turned the word
  into flesh. He actualized the theory by leading the Bolshevik
  Revolution. After his death, his body was embalmed and placed in a
  mausoleum in Moscow. Pilgrims queue for hours to view his
  incorruptible body, a tradition directly borrowed from the veneration
  of Catholic and Orthodox saints.

- **Mao Zedong / Stalin (The God-Kings):** In their respective
  countries, these leaders were elevated to god-like status. Their
  images were hung in every home (like icons), their words were
  memorized in the “Little Red Book” (like catechisms), and to question
  them was blasphemy.

# Atheism : The Way of Reason

While atheism is “not a great religion” in the traditional sense (it has no creeds, rituals, or scriptures), it functions as a fierce rival to the great religions. It is a belief system that answers the same “big questions” about human existence, often with more passion and conviction than the religions it opposes. For an audience unfamiliar with the specifics of Western secular thought, it is helpful to frame atheism not just as a “lack” of belief, but as a positive assertion of a specific worldview '- one that values logic, evidence, and the tangible world over the supernatural.

### The Problem: Religion

For the “New Atheists” (the modern, aggressive form of this tradition), the fundamental problem with the human condition is **religion** itself. Atheists argue that religion is not a harmless error; it is a dangerous delusion. They see it as the root cause of much of the world’s ignorance, violence, and bigotry. From the Crusades to 9/11, they argue that faith (belief without evidence) allows human beings to commit atrocities while feeling righteous. Religion is viewed as a “virus of the mind” or a poison that infects politics, education, and science. The problem is that humanity is stuck in the childhood of our species, clinging to imaginary friends (gods) instead of facing reality.

### The Solution: Rationality (Reason)

If the problem is the delusion of faith, the solution is **rationality** or **reason**. The goal of the atheist life is to grow up. It is to clear away the “rubbish” of superstition and to rely on the human mind to solve human problems. The solution is a world where decisions are made based on evidence, logic, and scientific inquiry rather than ancient scriptures or the commands of invisible deities. This is not a hope for an afterlife (which atheists deny exists), but a hope for a better life *here and now*. By discarding religion, atheists believe humanity can finally focus on making this world a paradise, rather than waiting for one after death.

### The Technique: Critical Inquiry

How does one move from the darkness of superstition to the light of reason? The technique is **Critical Inquiry** (or Skepticism). This involves a rigorous application of the scientific method and logical debate. Atheists encourage people to question authority, to demand evidence for extraordinary claims, and to refuse to accept things on faith. The “New Atheists” often use the technique of **ridicule** and sharp argumentation to shake believers out of their complacency, acting as a “disinfectant” to wipe away the germs of religious dogma.

### The Exemplar: The Free Thinker

The exemplar of this tradition is the **free thinker**—the courageous individual who dares to stand alone against the herd. There are also kind of modern “patron saints” of this movement, such as the “Four Horsemen” of the New Atheism which are : **Richard Dawkins**, **Christopher Hitchens**, **Sam Harris**, and **Daniel Dennett**. These figures are admired not for their holiness, but for their intellect and their bravery in speaking “inconvenient truths” to power. They represent the ideal of the autonomous, rational individual who needs no god to be good or to find meaning in life.

# The Road Ahead

Historically, interfaith dialogue was often restricted to "religious liberals" who were willing to set aside their doctrines to get along. But, I think that genuine dialogue must be open to everyone, including traditional believers and nonbelievers, and must acknowledge the fundamental boundaries that separate different traditions. Rather than forcing participants to "bargain to agreement" on theology, one should rather learn to "agree to disagree" by first understanding exactly where and why we differ. This religious literacy is not just a personal pursuit but a civic necessity for navigating a world run by diverse religions.
