# What was the world’s first religion?

Before diving into the distinctions in religion between monotheism and polytheism, what is religion? We all intuitively recognise a religious phenomenon but it becomes hard to define it. The English word “*religion*” now carries a lot of baggage especially in a Christian context but the Latin word “**religio**” from which we get the English word did not carry any specific connotations. The Latin “**religio**” comes from the verb root “**lig**” that means “to bind” that signified a bondage between a group of people and their gods. The same root verb gives the word “*ligament*” which is the kind of tissue that binds bones together.

The next question to ask is how old are religions and what was or were the first religion(s)? Practitioners of ancient polytheistic religions did not put a date to the origin of their own religions and traced the origins of their religion all the way back to the creation of the world and considered it as an eternal part of the cosmic order. But, modern historians, with the point of view of evolution of humans, have asked what are the first religions or religious practices. Since most of humanity lived in pre-history, it is difficult to trace the origin of religion in homo sapiens (or other hominids) because by the time we have historical records, religion already appears as a matured part of all civilisations and cultures. Hence, any origins of religion is highly biased by surviving archaeological evidences and their methods of interpretations. The earliest archaeological evidences of religious practices in pre-historic humans fall into three types :

1.  **Ritual burials** : In the Upper Palaeolithic age (roughly between
    40,000 to 10,000 years ago), we see first appearances of clear
    archaeological evidences of ritual burials where excavations show
    human bones buried along with objects ranging from red ochre, animal
    bones, items of adornment, tools, etc. Obviously, burial with grave
    goods, reflect a sort of belief in an afterlife, veneration of the
    deceased by caring for them, and the power of the deceased to bless
    their living descendants.

2.  **Fertility goddess sculptures** : Another sign of religious
    practice in early pre-history relates to a class of carved art
    objects technically called “**Venus figurines**” which have been
    found across many pre-historic cultures and the earliest of them
    date back to as far as 35,000 BCE although most of them are from
    around 30,000 BCE to 20,000 BCE. These Venus figurines are carved or
    moulded female figurines (of clay / stone / bone / ivory) with
    prominent breasts, hips, and belly. It is assumed that they had
    religious significance related to deification of fertility and
    worship of prehistoric fertility goddess.

3.  **Cave art of natural objects or animals** : The third main source
    of evidence of religious practices is Upper Palaeolithic cave or
    rock art, such as the world famous examples from Lascaux and Chauvet
    in France. The earliest among them again have been dated to around
    30,000 BCE and depict animals (like bison, deer, horses, birds, and
    others that were hunted for food).

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3-6I!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2464a783-1be9-4933-97d0-02db44955344_2118x948.heic)

These three main sources of archaeological evidences has generated three candidates for the earliest religions among humans - which are :

1.  **Ancestor worship**

2.  **Mother Goddess worship**

3.  **Animism** / **Totemism** / **Shamanism** (where representations of
    animals or objects are considered as depicting their presiding
    spirits which can be communicated with by expert shamans)

Of course, these three candidates need not be mutually exclusive and elements of all these three can be found in all religions of the historic civilisations. What distinguishes history from pre-history is the presence of writing or texts that preserve the beliefs, ideas, and practices of the religions. The earliest literary evidences of religions in the form of extensive texts are to be found in the cuneiform tablets of Mesopotamia, the hieroglyphs of Egypt, the Vedic texts of India, Homeric epics of Greece, Chinese oracle bones, Mayan and Aztec hieroglyphs.

All this does not mean that there was no religion in *Homo sapiens* before the appearance of these evidences. There are strong reasons to believe that religion is hardwired into human psyche in form - the recognition of a sacred realm. The famous Romanian scholar **Mercia Eliade** proposed that we should call human beings as “***Homo religiosus***” (Latin : religious man).

# Monotheism vs Polytheism

**Religious intolerance was inevitably born with the belief in one God.**

—Sigmund Freud, in “Moses and Monotheism”

While the worship of gods can be argued to be a part of human nature itself, on the contrary, the notion that there is only one God who can be known and worshipped only in one way, is definitely not a part of human nature and emerged very late in the history of our species. Men and women throughout the world have worshipped thousands of gods and goddesses in various forms before the advent of monotheistic religions. Monotheism has always in history, been imposed top down, by a king or a prophet and when it emerged, its very idea was appalling to the polytheistic populations. In fact, as we will see, the very first recorded experiment with monotheism by an Egyptian pharaoh in the 14th century BCE was an abject failure and completely reversed after his death. But fatefully, monotheism turned out to inspire a kind of “ferocity” and sometimes even a “fanaticism” that was typically not present in polytheism. Hence, monotheism did win and take over large parts of the world especially with the aid of the power of highly centralised empires.

Before going on though, there are some important technical terms that we need to have in mind.

1.  **Polytheism** - from two Greek words that mean “many” and “divine”
    respectively ; hence, it is the belief in and the worship of many
    gods.

2.  **Henotheism** - from two Greek words that mean “one” and “divine”
    respectively ; it is the belief in one god who is supreme and the
    highest among a pantheon of many other gods. Although many gods are
    recognised and worshipped, one god among them is consistently
    recognised as the most powerful.

3.  **Monolatry** - from the Latin word that means “one” and a Greek
    word that means “worship”. Here, people worship only one god
    exclusively but there is a recognition that there are other gods
    that exist, and other peoples who would worship them.

4.  **Monotheism** - from a Latin word for “one” and the Greek word for
    “divine” ; hence, it is the belief that there is just one god who
    exists and hence trivially, it is only he who deserve worship. All
    the other gods conceived by other peoples can be either regarded as
    imagined entities, or demonic (malevolent spirits).

You might think that monotheism gradually emerged from polytheism always through the bridge of henotheism and monolatry but that also has not always been the case too. While it is indeed true in the case for Judaism, the jump from polytheism to monotheism was relatively very abrupt in Pharaonic Egypt as we will see in the next section.

**The very fact that we are classifying religions based on the number of gods believed and worshipped is a specifically monotheistic concern**. Polytheists did and never do go about asking people how many gods they believed in and worshipped. In the polytheistic worldview, there are many gods and they fill up the entire universe. What set of gods a polytheist chooses to worship depends on what gods he knows about from his tradition and personal experience.

# Syncretism in Polytheism

One of the most important features of polytheism was syncretism. Syncretism is when two different peoples worshipping different set of gods come to know each other (by trade / conquest / migration), they harmonise their pantheons by identifying the gods of the other peoples. Whenever a polytheist from one culture meets a new god X from another group of peoples he has never met so far, he can do either of the following :

- He may regard the new god X as simply an alternative form of one of
  his own gods (say Y) to other peoples, and hence views that X deserves
  to be worshipped because it is a form of the god Y whom he is already
  worshipping. The myths of X are simply regarded as the tales of
  activities of the known god Y to other peoples.

- He may regard as a completely new god that was previously unknown to
  them and hence adds the god X into his pantheon and worships him or
  her for more support.

- Even if he did not worship X, the worship of X by a different set of
  people was not considered offensive in the eyes of a polytheist. This
  was because the worship of the new god X was not deemed blasphemous by
  the god Y. Polytheistic gods did not bother about what other gods
  apart from them, were worshipped by their devotees, as long as they
  got their due worship.

> Note that syncretism did not mean that there was no violence or
> conflict. The world even in modern times, has always been full of
> conflict and enmity between different peoples but the difference is
> that on the whole, the enmity was between peoples and not motivated
> based on religion. The mere fact that a set of people worshipped a
> different god did not automatically make them my enemy.

There are lots and lots of examples of syncretism in history but I present only some representative popular ones.

#### Sumero-Akkadadian synthesis

In Mesopotamia, the first civilisations to emerge were the Sumerian city states around 3000 BCE that spoke the Sumerian language and invented the cuneiform script for writing it.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!mwfl!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0a4bed20-4e00-4dbf-9a27-4f6d075cf9d1_1080x1003.png)

The language these city states spoke was **Sumerian**. Sumerian in linguistics is called a **language isolate** - which means that it is not related to any other languages that we know of, and hence is not a part of any language family. This Sumerian culture would serve as the nucleus for Mesopotamian civilization and as a center of attraction, like how the Vedic culture did for the Indian civilization. This would be the culture around which other groups would build and contribute to the pan Mesopotamian civilization. From here, civilization spread to other parts of the Middle East, and eventually trickled down further to even Syria and Turkey.

But as Sumerian culture spread northward, we also saw that eventually, the northern cities like Akkad captured the southern city states, and there was a huge migration of northern peoples to the south, starting from about 2300 BCE. The language or more precisely, the group of dialects spoken in the northern region which included **Akkadian** (spoken in Akkad), **Babylonian** (spoken in Babylon) and **Assyrian** (spoken in Ashur) - these all belong to a language family called the **Semitic language family**. The Semitic languages include other known languages like **Aramaic** and famous languages now known which include **Hebrew** and **Arabic**.

Due to the migration of Semitic speakers to the north, one finds that Sumerian died out as a spoken language around 1800 BCE. But even though Sumerian died out as a spoken language, one finds that it still served as an important language for writing and liturgy. One sees an extensive bilingualism amongst the literate class and the northern Akkadians continue to revere Sumerian as a sacred language and use it in parallel with Akkadian.

It should be noted that linguistically, Sumerian and Akkadian completely belong to two entirely different language families - the former is a language isolate and the latter is in the Semitic family related to Hebrew and Arabic.

But did the Mesopotamians view Sumerian and Akkadian as distinct competing languages and cultures? Actually due to the extensive bilingualism and since Sumerian was regarded and preserved as a sacred language even after it ceased to be spoken, the average Mesopotamian person thinks of Sumerian and Akkadian as twin languages and completely culturally equivalent! Every Sumerian word was thought to be exactly translatable to an Akkadian equivalent. (No, there was not a Dravidian-Aryan like separatist movement in this civilization!). There was a synthesis between their gods so that the Sumerian and Akkadian names of the syncretised divinities were considered equivalent both linguistically and theologically.

#### Athenians worship the Thracian moon goddess

In the 5th and 4th centuries BCE, the **Thracian** moon goddess **Bendis**, whom the Greeks identified with their goddess **Artemis**, began to gain a significant cult following in Athens, where she was called as **Artemis Bendis**. A prominent state festival, the **Bendideia**, was created in her honor, featuring processions and a torch race on horseback, a common feature in Thracian worship.

#### Greeks in Egypt : The cults of Isis & Serapis

When the Greeks conquered and ruled Egypt, they honoured the Egyptian goddesses by identifying them with Greek gods. They identified the Egyptian god of creation **Amun-Re** as their **Zeus**, associated the Egyptian god of learning **Thoth** with their **Hermes**. Sometimes, deities were adopted because they were both familiar and distinctive. The Egyptian goddess **Isis** (equated with Greek Demeter and Roman Ceres) became hugely popular across the Greco-Roman world with shrines and cults to her documented and seen across even the farthest territories of the Roman Empire, for e.g, all the way up in the Romano-British city of **Londinium** (now modern London) in Roman Britain.

A new Greco-Egyptian god named **Serapis** also emerged in Alexandria, the capital of Hellenic Egpyt, that combined aspects of the Egyptian god Osiris (along with the sacred bull **Apis)** with that of Greek gods like **Zeus** and **Hades**. Serapis was a local deity at Alexandria before the Greek conquest but was soon Hellenised and popularised by the Ptolemy dynasty after they ruled from Alexandria. A grand hill-top temple was added upon in the **Serapeum** **Mount** and this deity served as the national god of Hellenic Egypt. The temple was destroyed during the Christianisation of Egypt when it was a part of the Roman Empire.

Jan Assmann considers the polytheistic approach to internationalizing gods as a form of “intercultural translation”. He writes :

The great achievement of polytheism is the articulation of a common semantic universe. ... The meaning of a deity is his or her specific character as it unfolded in myths, hymns, rites, and so on. This character makes a deity comparable to other deities with similar traits. The similarity of gods makes their names mutually translatable. ... The practice of translating the names of the gods created a concept of similarity and produced the idea or conviction that the gods are international.

The ancient Roman historian **Pliny the Elder** expressed the “translatability” of deities as “**different names for different peoples**” *(*Latin *: **nomina alia aliis gentibus**).* This capacity made possible the religious syncretism of the Hellenistic era and the pre-Christian Roman Empire.

From the Roman point of view, it was natural to apply the above principle to the Jewish God. Various Romans equated the Jewish god to various divinities like Jupiter or Dionsysus and so on. However, the Jews, unlike other peoples living under Roman rule, rejected any such attempt out of hand, regarding such an identification as the worst of sacrilege. This complete divergence of views between Romans and the Jews was one of the factors contributing to the frequent friction between the Jews and the Roman Empire; for example, the Emperor Hadrian‘s decision to rebuild Jerusalem under the name of Aelia Capitolina, a city dedicated to Jupiter, precipitated the bloodbath of the Bar Kokhba revolt. **The Romans were puzzled that the Jews, who were otherwise a very civilised and reasonable people, refused to participate in this process of syncretism and take part in honouring the gods of other peoples and nations within the empire.**

> *It is said that anti-Semitism was not invented by Christians but
> perfected by Christians. In the ancient world, Jews were hated because
> they were monotheists, while in the Chriso-Islamic worlds, they were
> hated despite them worshipping the same God of the Jews, simply
> because the Jews refused to accept Jesus and Muhammad as prophets in
> continuity with their own religious traditions.*

#### Romano-Celtic syncretism

When the Romans conquered the Celts in **Gaul** (France), **Hispania** (Spain), and **Britain**, they encountered the various **Celtic** gods and local spirits of water springs and other water bodies. One can see lots of inscriptional evidences of Roman authorities making offerings to these Celtic gods and goddesses. For e.g, at a site of a thermal spring in **Bath** (England), the Roman soldiers and authorities there, seem to have recognised the presiding local goddess of the spring named **Sulis** as a variant of their own **Minerva**, and hence addressed her as **Sulis-Minerva** and made offerings to her.

#### Cybele : The Roman mother goddess

The story of how the goddess **Cybele** came to be worshipped is fascinating and hence I give some details. The name of this goddess was Cybele, but mostly, she was referred to by the title “***magna mater***” which in Latin means ‘great mother’. Cybele was the supreme Mother Goddess of Rome and was worshipped extensively when Rome was beginning to emerge as a dominant political power in the Mediterranean. The origins of Cybele go back to prehistoric Anatolia (Turkey) where a Proto-Cybele-like figurine, dated to about 6000 BCE, was excavated by archaeologists— known as the **Seated Woman of Çatalhöyük**.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rLBA!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F53b6e9f0-1163-430e-a69d-341d157d812b_834x1268.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rLBA!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F53b6e9f0-1163-430e-a69d-341d157d812b_834x1268.heic)

At this stage, she was a fertility goddess, since she is crafted in the pose of giving birth. During the time of Greek culture coming into Turkey, this goddess was adopted and adapted by the Greeks and identified with the Greek goddess **Gaea**—the Mother Earth and earlier supreme Mother Goddess in Greek culture. There does not seem to be a unanimous consensus about this identification of Cybele with Gaea—some accepted her and some rejected this identification. Because of this divisiveness, the shrines of Cybele in Greek culture were built and she was honoured outside the city walls—as a protector deity. The Athenians eventually built a dedicated structure to Cybele inside the city too, after they felt that she had sent a plague to the city for not honouring her. Soon, however, Cybele was gradually more accommodated into the Greek pantheon and stories involving her with the other Greek gods circulated widely.

But Cybele’s future would be the greatest in Rome. Rome fought three major wars (Punic Wars) with **Carthage**, a rival power, after which it was clear that it was the sole rising power in the Mediterranean. But in the second war, Rome was almost defeated and narrowly missed being destroyed by Carthage. It was at this time that Cybele entered Rome. According to historical records, during the Second Punic War in the late third century BCE, omens seemed to indicate that Rome was about to experience a catastrophic military defeat at the hands of its rival Carthage. Desperate for relief, the Roman senators consulted the oracle, who declared that Rome could defeat Carthage if the city offered worship to the Great Mother. Roman priests determined that this referred to the **Great Mother of Pergamum**— who worshipped Cybele as the Mother Goddess.

According to legend, the Great Mother came to Rome in the form of a black meteorite stone. On a sweltering day in 204 BCE, a barge (flat boat) sailed up the Tiber River, carrying the meteoric stone that the Romans viewed as the incarnation of Cybele, the Great Mother. Suddenly, the ship caught on a sandbar and for a moment, it looked like the goddess could not make it to Rome. But **Claudia Quinta**, a matriarch of the city, prayed to the Great Mother to release the ship. Miraculously, the barge pulled free and Claudia herself—miraculously—towed it to the shore. Cybele had freely chosen to come to Rome to save their city from defeat and this bolstered her status as a goddess. In addition, soon after the stone was brought to the city, Rome defeated Hannibal—Carthage’s supreme military leader. This sealed Rome’s faith in Cybele. A temple was built for the Great Mother on the Palatine Hill and an annual festival, the Megalesia, was established in her honour.

The story of Cybele’s dramatic barge ride into Rome was retold and cherished by all Roman families. In the epic poem Aeneid by Virgil (written 14 BCE), Cybele is said to be the mother of Jupiter himself—the king and the supreme among all Roman gods. The Romans hence, from then on, saw Cybele’s arrival in Rome not as as a coming of a new goddess, but as a return, after having been forgotten in Rome and exiled from it, for all these years. In short, Cybele had been fully domesticated by the Romans—they had seen her as the Supreme Mother ever since.

# Pharaoh Akhenaten : The First Iteration

The first recorded experiment in monotheism occurred in ancient Egypt in the fourteenth century BCE under the reign of an eccentric pharaoh named Akhenaten. Egypt might be surprising for monotheism to originate because it was a vibrant polytheistic civilisation with lots of gods, cults, and priesthoods. But it is not surprising when we think about it for a while. Ancient Egypt was one of the most centralised polity on earth at its time with the Pharaoh considered as an incarnation of Horus and possessed immense power.

#### The discovery of Tell-el-Amarna

Rarely we see an archaeological discovery challenging what we have been taught to believe in the history of religion. It was taught for a long time that monotheism was “***the gift of the Jews***” and the origins of worship of the one true God was traced back to Abraham, the founding prophet, acknowledged in all the three monotheistic religions - Judaism, Christianity, and Islam.

But when archaeologists began to excavate the ruins of a city at **Tell-el-Amarna** (a remote site near the Nile in Southern Egypt, about 200 miles south of Cairo), the inscriptions on the stones and shards that were dug out at Amarna revealed the life of a young pharaoh who originated the first monotheistic revolution in religious thought. Who was this pharaoh?

#### Historical Background : Egypt before Aten

Egypt was, on the whole, a politically unified entity from about 3000 BCE except for brief centuries of instability. In 1353 BCE, a pharaoh ascended to the throne of Egypt, during the period of **Dynasty XVIII** under the name **Amenhotep IV**, named after a god named **Amen** (his name literally means “Amen is pleased”). Amen was one of the many deities of Ancient Egypt, but around this time, he began to emerge as the prominent among gods and merged with the earlier sun god Re and began to be described as **Amen-Re**. The pharaohs who preceded Amenhotep IV to the throne of Egypt considered themselves to be the bearers of an age-old and fixed tradition, and they always devoutly consulted the priests of Amon and hundreds of other gods and goddesses before setting themselves upon any enterprise. They not only believed in the gods fervently but they were also convinced that their gods favoured Egypt above all other nations of the known world. Also, Egypt was at the time of the New Kingdom—what we would today call a superpower. It dominated the ancient Near East, not only militarily but also culturally and commercially. Egypt was on the whole, stable and prosperous during this time.

#### There is no God except Aten

The pharaoh **Amenhotep IV** was raised opulently in the traditions of Egypt. But a few years after ascending to the throne, something inspired him to reject this good status quo, and re-invent everything from scratch. He declared that all the gods and goddesses who were worshipped in Egypt for millennia were false and that there was only one God who deserved worship named **Aten**. Aten is a word in ancient Egyptian language that refers to the solar disk and the pharaoh renamed himself as **Akhenaten** (Meaning : *Splendour of Aten*). The solar disk implied that the new deity had an association with the sun like the older Amen. But Akhenaten claimed that this new deity Aten could or would not have depiction that might remind his worshippers of any living thing and was abstractly represented as an austere circle of gold that suggested the brightness of the sun. Another revolutionary claim by Aten is that just as the sun shines on everybody and there is only sun shared by all, this god Aten was the only god for all peoples; not just Egyptians. Egypt was no longer a special divinely favoured nation. This new god Aten would not tolerate the affection given to any other divinity and was jealous for sole worship.

And as you would expect, Akhenaten, having repudiated the gods of ancient Egypt, commanded all the shrines of all other deities to be closed and the suppressed all the rituals that were going on since time immemorial. He began to destroy the idols and chiseled away the hieroglyphic names of all other deities in temples and monuments; the most affected were popular gods of that time like Amen-Re. The high priest of Amen was put to work in a stone quarry like a common slave.

#### Founding a new city

To put himself beyond the sights of the old gods, Akhenaten abandoned the royal capital at **Thebes** where generations of pharaohs had reigned before him and were buried. He built a new capital on virgin soil on the banks of the Nile about 200 miles north of the old capital. He called the city as **Akhenaten** (also named after the god Aten). This new city that Akhenaten founded had temples dedicated to Aten alone.

#### Revolutions in Art & Architecture

While the city of Akhenaten had temples dedicated to Aten alone, the pharaoh Akhenaten also invented new styles of art and architecture along with a new royal entourage and began to institute a new government. In art, Akhenaten was shown as deformed: spindly neck and arms, breasts, wide hips, elongated face. We do not know whether Akhenaten actually looked like that or was depicted so. But, this had never been done before; the pharaoh was always traditionally depicted as well muscled and vigorous in Egypt. In architecture too, Akhenaten’s temples at Akhetaten show changes from a traditional closed massive style temples to one more open to the sun. One temple is named **Gem-Pa-Aten** (Meaning : Aten is found). His ***heb-sed*** festival (the traditional festival celebrated to renew a pharaoh) was celebrated, but we find that there are only representations of Akhenaten, not other gods, in the shrines. We cannot even surely say either that whether they really had that festival or if it is it just painted on the walls.

#### Neglect of State Affairs

Like other true believers who would follow his example in the centuries to come, Akhenaten convinced himself that the brave new world he had created would replace all that had come before and would last forever. The Egyptologist **Jan Assmann** writes :

The monotheistic revolution of Akhenaton was not only the first but also the most radical and violent eruption of a counter-religion in the history of humankind.

It was this capital city that was dug up by the archaeologists at **Tell-el-Amarna**. Akhenaten spent his time in his religious affairs of his own religion and neglected the internal and external affairs of Egypt completely, as plentifully evidenced by the Amarna letters. The boundary stelae around the city present lots of what we can now see as some kinds of “sermon on the mount” that Jesus gave : Akhenaten says to his followers that Aten had revealed the site to him; and that there should be no images of Aten fashioned. They also tell us of one of his other important revolutions - the abolition of military. Akhenaten also tells us that he would never leave the city; and that only Akhenaten knows the Aten. No wonder Egypt fell into chaos internally and externally. Imagine a pharaoh refusing to fight and leave his capital city. Diplomatic correspondences of his time too reveal these problems due to ignoring the military and diplomacy which made Egypt become isolated.

#### Preaching & Ministry

Having neglected his royal duties, he focused on his religion ; he wrote hymns to Aten and began creating institutions and preaching his new religion and invited the followers of his new religion to his city. One of his longest and popular hymns called “Hymn to the Aten” has commanded lot of scholarly attention because of its similarity to Psalm 104 from the Bible. In one of his hymns, he says : **“*****O You, only God, there is no other God than You***” which echoes the fundamental creed of Judaism as reflected in the Shema prayer that Jews say daily : “***Hear O Israel. the Lord our God, the Lord is One***” (Deuteronomy 6 : 4) and the Islamic creed “***There is no god other than Allah***”.

One thing is for sure though - Akhenaten failed to win the hearts and minds of the people of ancient Egypt. Living and ruling in his remote capital for nearly seventeen years, he was distrusted and detested by all classes of people for destroying all traditions that they had inherited and for neglecting the running of the nation for propagating his new heretic cult, having stayed confined in his city.

#### Restoration of Egypt after Akhenaten

As soon as he died after 17 years of reign, the monotheistic revolution that he imposed from top down ancient Egypt was wholly undone—the royal capital and the temples of Aten were abandoned, and the newfangled cult was forgotten. Just as Akhenaten had once erased the names of the old gods from the monuments of ancient Egypt, now the people erased Akhenaten’s name by having it chiseled out of the stone inscriptions and stricken from the lists of kings that the Egyptians maintained with such meticulous care. They recarved the erased names of the old gods that were removed by Akhenaten and reopened all the old temples, priesthood, and rituals. The entire city of Akhetaten and especially the temples that he built to Aten were dismantled and the stones there were used as fillers in later buildings. In modern times, the Akhenaten Temple Project attempted to put together a45,000-piece jigsaw puzzle to rebuild on paper several of these temples, from their remains in other buildings.

Akhenaten was succeeded on the throne by his (probably) son-(in-law), who moved the royal court back to Thebes, reopened the old shrines, changed his name from the initial “**Tut-ankh-aten**,” a name that honoured the god Aten, to “**Tut-ankh-amen,**” thus proclaiming his allegiance to the old and beloved god Amen—he is the celebrated King Tut (**Tutankhamen**) whose magnificent tomb was discovered in 1922 and made all the news.

# Atenism & Judaism

A highly debated topic in scholarly circles was if Judaism was somehow influenced by the cult of Akhenaten. People in support of this thesis point out some evidences. The Hebrew word for Lord is **Adon** (a title used by Jews today to refer to their God) which sounds similar to **Aten**, and hence there is a connection, speculates Jan Assmann. The Bible itself says that the Jewish people were enslaved in Egypt who were then freed by God through the prophet Moses. A more plausible piece of evidence comes from the similarities between Psalm 104 form the Bible and the Hymn to the Aten by Akhenaten.

But this evidence is not enough to postulate a connection between Judaism and Atenism. First of all, the timelines do not match. Also, Atenism was a failed experiment that lasted only for 17 years and was actively weeded out after the death of Akhenaten and hence it is unlikely that it influenced later Judaism.

Also, we will see in the next article in this series (second iteration) that the Bible itself shows the initial Jews as reluctant monotheists who keep slipping into idolatry time and again when God has to punish them and rebuke them. Solidified monotheism emerged among the Jews only during the time of Babylonian exile in the 6th century BCE around the time of the Biblical prophets. But whether there is any connection between Israelite Judaism and Atenism, it is striking to witness parallels between the emergence of the monotheistic impulse in two places and in two cultures.
