As the world awakens to the vibrant blossoms of spring, millions across the globe are marking the arrival of a “New Day.” From the festive tables of **Nowruz** in the Persian highlands to the ritual oil baths of **Ugadi** and **Gudi Padwa** on the Indian subcontinent, these diverse celebrations all share a single, profound heartbeat: the quest to align human life with the cycles of the heavens. But why does one neighbor celebrate the New Year in mid-April while another follows the shifting moon, and why is the global calendar we use for business so indifferent to both?

In this article, I will remove the curtain on the celestial mechanics, exploring how ancient civilisations solved the “impossible puzzle” of the mathematical incommensurability of the cycles of the sun, moon, and the earth. We will journey thorough the calendars of a wide variety of traditions from the mathematically simple and primitive **Gregorian** system and the purely lunar obsession of the **Islamic** calendar, to the high-resolution **Jewish**, and **Persian** calendars and finally dive into the self-adjusting brilliance of the natural and adaptive **Hindu calendar and the Chinese calendar**. Prepare to discover that a calendar is far more than a grid of numbers ; it is a living bridge between the rhythm of our lives and the pulse of the universe.

This cycle is caused because the earth revolves around the sun in a periodic orbit. This cycle is important in determining seasons and climate and also in astrology where the sun’s relative position among the constellations is thought to influence one’s personality and hence the solar cycle is important in determining that.

### Tropical vs Sidereal Solar Cycle

While we often speak of THE Solar Cycle, there is a fundamental incommensurability between slightly two different cycles hidden within it. Because the Earth’s axis “wobbles” like a spinning top (a phenomenon known as **axial precession**), two different ways of measuring the year arise:

- **The Tropical Cycle (Seasons):** The time it takes for the Earth to
  return to the same tilt relative to the sun (e.g., from one spring
  Equinox to the next). This governs our weather and agriculture.

- **The Sidereal Cycle (Stars):** The time it takes for the Earth to
  complete a true 360∘ orbit relative to the fixed stars. This governs
  the sun’s position in the zodiac.

Because of this wobble, these two “solar years” do not align. They drift apart by about **20 minutes every year** (or 1 day every 72 years). This creates a profound gap between our experience of the **seasons** and our observation of the **stars**. Over millennia, this means that the constellations associated with “spring” eventually shift into “winter,” proving that even the most fundamental cycles of nature are inherently “out of sync” with one another.

The enormous calendric diversity we see in the world cultures is based on the things they choose to prioritise and the things they ignore, given these constrains of incommensurability.

# Periodicity Mismatch & Calendric Diversity

Life would have been simple if these three periodic cycles were commensurate ; i.e. an integer number of days fit exactly in a single lunar cycle and an integer number of lunar cycles fit exactly in a single solar cycle. If that were the case, then the days could keep phase with the tides, and the tides would keep phase with the seasons. But, unfortunately, this is not the case. We have the following calculations

We have that approximately 365 days fit in a solar year, approximately 30 days fit in a lunar cycle, and approximately 12 lunar cycles (1 lunar year) fit in a solar cycle. But, these are only approximate and due to this incommensurability, the diurnal, lunar, and solar cycles drift from each other. The fundamental problem of any calendric system is to harmonise these three or a subset of these three cycles so that a periodic ritual life is created that is in sync with them, despite this incommensurability. Different calendar traditions prioritise different things and solve this problem differently which is why we have many calendar systems. In the rest of this article, we will first study some representative calendar systems (starting from simple to complex) and use them to map and classify all other calendar systems.

# The Gregorian Calendar

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!vszc!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5f281b70-fe33-44ab-a067-dd1d92d54249_901x1191.jpeg)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!vszc!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5f281b70-fe33-44ab-a067-dd1d92d54249_901x1191.jpeg)

Let us now start with the Gregorian calendar which is the simplest and most widely used secular calendar in the world. Although it has roots in ancient Republican Rome, the current standardisation was a long process started by **Julius Caesar** in the first century BCE and completed by **Pope Gregory XIII** in the sixteenth century CE.

The Gregorian calendar ignores the lunar cycle for the most part (except for setting the date for Easter). It only attempts to harmonise the diurnal (day-night) and solar cycle (tropical year). Before that, remember that 1 tropical year (solar cycle) corresponds to 365.24219 days.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!X6oe!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5c30075e-f5b2-45f5-9e1c-44300c8afdd7_1586x128.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!X6oe!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5c30075e-f5b2-45f5-9e1c-44300c8afdd7_1586x128.heic)

If each year has a fixed number like 365 days, this will cause an inaccuracy of approximately 1 day every four years and hence every century, it results in a lag of 25 days. This means that if every year had only 365 days always, then after a century, seasons would drift by about a month (summer after a century would start a month later than now). To fix this, as a first measure, leap years are introduced where one year every four years has an extra day which means that we have one out of every four years to have 366 days (this extra day is added at the end of February). But, this is not enough. If we have three years of length 365 days and 1 year of length 366 days, then the average length of a year would be 365.25 days which is close but still not close enough to the exact figure of 365.24219 days. It is in fact an overcorrection as it is slightly greater than the exact number. So, we need to trim some leap years off. So, there is another adjustment that a leap year is removed every hundred years so that in a century, we have 24 instead of 25 leap years. This gives the average length of the year as :

\\(24/100)\times 366 + (76/100) \times 365 = 365.24 \\

But, this is still 365.24 days and is now somewhat still slightly lower than the more exact 365.24219 days. So, we need to now add little more leap years in the long term. So, it was decided that this leap year omission every hundred years would be omitted every fourth century and hence this now gives the final average length of the Gregorian year as :

\\(3/4)\*365.24 + (1/4)\*365.25 = 365.2425\\

This is only slightly more than the more exact 365.24219 days. This system is quite accurate and causes an accumulated error of only one day every three thousand years which is not that bad. There have been more rules proposed to refine this and reduce the gap further but none of have been standardised as of yet. Let us summarise the calculations that we have done so far.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!9u_H!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F29057f69-f025-4bd6-bfa1-b42ef545adb9_1824x988.heic)

But do note that the Gregorian calendar has no intention to keep phase with the lunar cycle except in setting of the Easter date.

# Persian Calendar

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Py2Q!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9b531721-cbb7-476a-8944-d121ebb5a354_2536x1528.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Py2Q!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9b531721-cbb7-476a-8944-d121ebb5a354_2536x1528.heic)

An ancient relief at Persepolis for Nowruz: eternal combat between the bull, representing the Moon, and the lion, representing the Sun and spring.

The Persian calendar, specifically the **Jalali** or **Solar Hijri (SH)** calendar, is also a solar calendar (hence, ignores lunar cycles, like the Gregorian calendar) and is widely considered the most accurate solar calendar (in sync with tropical solar cycle) in the world. While the Gregorian calendar drifts by one day every 3000 years, the Persian calendar only drifts by one day every **110,000 years**.

The absolute zero of the Persian calendar is the **Vernal Equinox** (the exact moment Spring begins). The New Year, **Nowruz**, starts at this precise astronomical second the Sun crosses the celestial equator. This is why Nowruz can start at different times of the day (morning, afternoon, or even midnight) depending on the year. According to the Persian epic poem ***Shahnamah***, the festival of Nowruz was established by the legendary **King Jamshid** after he constructed a magnificent jewel-encrusted throne that rose into the heavens by divine glory (*farr*). When the sun’s rays struck the throne on the first day of the month of Farvardin, the world was illuminated with a new light, and the people celebrated this miraculous event as the “New Day,” marking the beginning of a golden age of peace and prosperity.

Unlike the Gregorian months (which have arbitrary lengths like 28 or 31 days), the Persian months follow the Earth’s actual speed in its elliptical orbit:

> **First 6 Months (Spring/Summer):** These are all **31 days** long.
> Because the Earth is further from the sun (aphelion), it moves slower,
> making these seasons slightly longer.
>
> **Next 5 Months (Autumn/Winter):** These are **30 days** long. The
> Earth is closer to the sun and moving faster.
>
> **The Final Month (Esfand):** This is **29 days**, but becomes **30
> days** in a leap year.

The Gregorian calendar uses a simple “every 4 years” rule with a “100/400” exception. The Persian calendar uses a far more complex **algorithmic cycle** (typically 33 years). Within a 33-year block, leap years occur every 4 years, but occasionally there is a **5-year gap** to perfectly “reset” the calendar against the sun. This ensures that the first day of the year (1 Farvardin) *always* lands on the astronomical equinox. Let me give more details.

In a 33-year block, the Persian calendar typically schedules **8 leap years**. If you had a leap year exactly every 4 years, you would have 8 leap years in 32 years (8×4=32). But the Persian cycle waits until the **33rd year** to finish the block. This creates a sequence of seven 4-year intervals followed by one 5-year interval. The pattern of leap years in a 33-year cycle usually looks like this: **4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24, 28, and then 33** ~~(not 32 as expected)~~**.** The gap between year 28 and year 33 is **5 years**. This 5-year leap interval is called a **Quinquennial** leap year, whereas the 4-year ones are **Quadrennial**. The “extra” time we are trying to account for every year is roughly **0.24219 days**. After 4 years, we have accumulated 4×0.24219=0.96876 days. We add 1 leap day. Now we have “over-corrected” by about **0.03 days**. Over several cycles of 4 years, that tiny 0.03 “over-correction” starts to add up. By the time we reach the end of the 28th year, those tiny over-corrections have accumulated enough that if we added a leap day on year 32, we would be too far ahead of the Sun. By **waiting until year 33** to add the leap day, the calendar lets the Sun “catch up.”

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!9U8k!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb2ab8363-e859-4d89-ae47-bd91da1b05f2_648x130.heic)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!9U8k!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb2ab8363-e859-4d89-ae47-bd91da1b05f2_648x130.heic)

This average (365.2424) is much closer to the true solar year (365.24219) than the Gregorian average (365.2425). While the 33-year cycle is the basic bread and butter of the calendar, it isn’t the only one. Persian astronomers (most famously **Omar Khayyam**) realized that even the 33-year cycle has a microscopic error. To fix this, they combine these blocks into a **Grand Cycle of 2,820 years**. This is why the calendar is so accurate that it won’t drift by a day for over 100,000 years ; it is constantly performing these “mini” and “macro” resets to stay glued to the precise moment of the Spring Equinox !

# The Islamic Calendar

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!IJt4!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd1224be5-aff0-4623-b10a-06e8adc15714_600x360.webp)

![Maome](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!IJt4!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd1224be5-aff0-4623-b10a-06e8adc15714_600x360.webp "Maome")

While the Gregorian calendar ignores the lunar cycle and is based on formulaic harmonisation of the diurnal and solar cycle (by the sophisticated placement of leap years appropriately), the Islamic calendar is its polar opposite. It ignores the solar cycle completely and only attempts to harmonise the diurnal and lunar cycle. Islamic months begin at the sighting of the new moon which means that each month straddles between consecutive new moons. Since each lunar cycle lasts 29.5306 days, 12 such lunar months which make a lunar year are :

This gives an average month length of 29.5 days which gives the length of the year to be 354 days. As I said, the Islamic calendar ignores the solar cycle and hence has no intention to keep this in phase with the solar year in the long term. But, even then, there is a mismatch between the diurnal cycle and the lunar year where a lunar year is not 354 days but 354.3671 days. How does Islam get around this problem? If every lunar year had a fixed 354 days, then this leads to an error of about one day every three years and approximately fifteen days about every half of a century. If this were the case, then, after half of a decade, then the tides would begin in the middle of a month than it does now. How does the Islamic calendar fix this to keep phase? It also uses a formulaic adjustment by adding leap years periodically. In a thirty-year cycle, Islamic calendar adds an extra day in the month of Hujja at years 2, 5, 7, 10, 13, 16, 18, 21, 24, 26, & 29. So, we have that in every thirty year cycle, we have 11 years of an extra 355 days and 19 years of 355 days. This gives the average length of the Islamic year as

\\(19/30)\*354 + (11/30)\*355 = 354.3667\\

which is quite close to the actual figure of 354.3671 and leads to an error only of one day every two thousand and five hundred years.

But, since the Islamic calendar does not attempt to keep pace with the solar cycle at all, it means that any ritual or festival based on Islamic dates drift between season every thirty-three years. This is why one sees the times of the Ramadan fasting month and the Hajj season shift through various seasons through a decade. Why does the Islamic calendar refuse to correct the lunar months to keep phase with the solar cycle? Traditions cite the Quranic verse 9:37 as a reason.

In pre-Islamic Arabia, this seasonal drift of the lunar calendar was indeed considered problematic for society. Arab culture was deeply tribal, and its economy depended on seasonal patterns (like every other society) — the timing of harvests, major trade fairs (like the *Sūq ʿUkāẓ*), and the pilgrimage to Mecca. These events needed to occur during stable, hospitable weather and often coincided with the four "sacred months" when warfare was forbidden. If these months changed seasons, this would create problems. To fix this, pre-Islamic Arabs practiced a system called ***Nasi’***, which translates roughly to “*postponement*” or “*intercalation*.” Under *Nasi’*, if the religious month of pilgrimage (*Dhū al-Ḥijjah*) started falling too early relative to the season, tribal leaders would choose to insert an extra, 13th month (like a leap month) into the year. This effectively “pushed back” the start of the next year, realigning the lunar months with the solar seasons. However, the prophet Muḥammad felt that this made tribal elders misuse the provision. For example, if a tribe wanted to continue a profitable or necessary war during what should have been a forbidden month (like *Muḥarram*), they could strategically insert a *Nasi'* month just before it, declaring that *Muḥarram* (and the peace that came with it) was delayed for another month. The Qur'an condemns this in [9:37](https://quran.com/at-tawbah/37) where sacred time is treated as a manipulable tool for political gain and it explicitly condemns this: *“...They make it lawful one year and unlawful another year, that they may make up the count of which Allah has forbidden...”* . # The Jewish Calendar

The Jewish calendar is neither purely lunar (like the Islamic calendar) nor a purely solar one (like the Gregorian calendar). It is based on the lunar cycle but adjusts to make it keep phase with the solar (tropical) cycle. Such a calendar which is based on the lunar cycle but has provisions to reset it to make it keep phase with the solar cycle is called a **lunisolar calendar**. Do you remember when I said in the Islamic calendar that the pagan Arabs used to add an extra lunar month periodically to keep the lunar months in pace with the solar cycle? This is the example of a lunisolar calendar and this is also how the Jewish calendar works. The Jewish calendar is a lunisolar calendar which means that it has 12 months based the sighting or estimation of the new moon. Jewish religious and ritual life and also the official calendar of the modern state of Israel works on the lunar months (adjusted periodically to keep in phase with the solar year) whose months and important events are summarised below.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ns-R!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9fb5d554-92dd-4a2d-be43-c6bc5360ca48_1000x1000.jpeg)

![The cycle of the Jewish religious year](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ns-R!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9fb5d554-92dd-4a2d-be43-c6bc5360ca48_1000x1000.jpeg "The cycle of the Jewish religious year")

Unlike the Muslims, the Jews have a strong theological reason to align their lunar calendar to the solar cycle. The primary reason for the solar connection is the **Passover (Pesach)** festival that commemorates an event in the Exodus in the Bible. The Torah of the Bible explicitly states that Passover must be celebrated in the spring month of **Nisan**. If there were no solar based adjustment of lunar months, the Passover would not fall on spring every year and will drift through various seasons over the long course of time.

The Jewish New Year called the **Rosh Hashanah** begins at the month of **Tishri** that happens in September. It consists usually of 12 alternating lunar months of 29 and 30 days each (except for Ḥeshvan and Kislev, which sometimes have either 29 or 30 days, more details to follow soon), and totals 354 days normally (which is close to the actual 354.3671 days). This however as we saw, creates about an eleven days of deficit compared to the solar year. Hence, extra months are added periodically to keep the lunar cycle in phase with the solar cycle. The Jewish system is based on an ancient astronomical discovery called the **Metonic Cycle** (named after the 5th-century BCE Greek astronomer **Meton** of Athens, though the principle was known earlier in Babylon, from which the Jews would have picked up). The discovery is that **19 solar years** align almost perfectly with **235 lunar months**. This means that if one establishes a cycle that is exactly 235 lunar months long, then it also corresponds very well to the end of 19 solar years, which means that after that time period, the moon's phase will have returned to almost the exact same day of the solar year as when you started (the error in such a 19 year period is very small and only about **2 hours)**. But, 235 lunar months is not divisible by 12. When we have only 12 months for all the 19 lunar years, we get only 19×12 = 228 months which is 7 months short of the required 235. Hence, to fit 235 lunar months into 19 solar years (while 19 typical lunar years have only 228 months), the system must add **7 extra months** over the course of the 19-year cycle. This means that seven out of every nineteen years have to have an extra month for harmonising with this Metonic cycle. These become the counterparts of leap years in the Jewish calendar that have these extra months (called in Hebrew as *: **Shanah Me’uberet***, literally "*impregnated year*"!!!). The Jewish calendar has a fixed pattern for adding the extra month 7 times within the 19-year cycle. Years **3, 6, 8, 11, 14, 17, and 19** of the cycle are designated as Leap Years.

- A **Common Year** has 12 months (approx. 354 days).

- A **Leap Year** has 13 months (approx. 384 days).

By following this more precise 19-year pattern, the calendar ensures that Passover and all other Jewish holidays cycle within a very stable window of dates on the Gregorian calendar, always remaining in their assigned seasons. The window during the leap year in which the extra days of an extra month are added, consists of an additional 30-day month called First Adar, which always precedes the month of (Second) Adar. Among the consequences of this lunisolar structure are these:

- The number of days in a year may vary considerably, from 353 to 385
  days.

- The first day of a month can fall on any day of the week, that day
  varying from year to year. Consequently, the days of the week upon
  which an annual Jewish festival falls vary from year to year despite
  the festival’s fixed position in the Jewish month.

While the Metonic cycle handles the large, year-to-year alignment, additional complex rules are necessary for the precision of the calendar. A real lunar month is closer to **29.5306 days**. But, with alternation between just 29 and 30 days for twelve months, we have only 29.5 days. This extra 0.0306 days (about 44 minutes) must be accounted for, or the new Moon will drift from the *actual* first of the month. To do this, the traditional alternating 30/29 month pattern is occasionally altered.

- Two specific months, **Cheshvan** and **Kislev** (the 8th and 9th
  months, respectively), can vary in length. They can both have 29 days,
  both have 30 days, or one of each.

- This creates three types of years: **deficient** (353/383 days),
  **regular** (354/384 days), or **complete** (355/385 days). These
  flexible months act as buffers to manage cumulative errors and align
  the month-to-month calculation.

The Jewish calendar has a highly complex set of rules called the **Four Postponements (Deḥiyot)** that prevent specific holidays from falling on certain days of the week, primarily to avoid conflicts with Sabbath (Shabbat) observances. The main example is: **Rosh Hashanah (The New Year) may not begin on a Sunday, Wednesday, or Friday.** The elaborate rules ensure important things like :

1.  The fast of Yom Kippur never falls on a Friday or Sunday (because
    Saturday which falls between a Friday and Sunday is the day of
    fasting called the Shabbat in Judaism where one is not allowed to
    cook).

2.  Hoshana Rabbah (the last day of Sukkot) never falls on Shabbat.

If a mathematical calculation (or moon-sighting in the ancient world) puts Rosh Hashanah on a forbidden day, the *entire year* is strategically delayed by one day, and the flexibility of Cheshvan and Kislev is used to absorb the day.

A larger source of error in the Jewish calendar is the inaccuracy of the **Metonic** **cycle** that we saw above, which is only an approximation. Nineteen Jewish years average 6939d 16h 33m 03¹⁄₃s, compared to the 6939d 14h 26m 15s of nineteen mean solar years. Thus, the Hebrew calendar drifts by just over 2 hours every 19 years, or approximately one day every 216 years. Due to accumulation of this discrepancy, the earliest date on which Passover can fall has drifted by roughly eight days since the 4th century, In the distant future, this drift is projected to move Passover much further in the year. If the calendar is not amended, then Passover will start to land on or after the summer solstice around approximately 12892 CE.

The origin of the Jewish calendar cannot be very accurately traced although it seems to have been adopted from the lunisolar calendar similar of ancient Babylonia. But we are at a much firmer ground when it comes to how the calendar was used by the time of the **Late Second Temple period** (i.e., 1st century BCE to 70 CE), calendrical matters were regulated by the **Sanhedrin**, or the **Council of Elders**, at Jerusalem. The testimony of two witnesses who had observed the New Moon was ordinarily required to proclaim a new month. Leap years were proclaimed by a council of three or more rabbis with the approval of the ***nasi***, or president, of the Sanhedrin (which was what Arab pagans did). With the decline of the Sanhedrin, calendrical matters were decided by the Palestinian patriarchate (the official heads of the Jewish community under Roman rule). Jewish persecution under the Roman Emperor **Constantius II** (r. 337–361 CE) and advances in astronomical science led to the gradual replacement of observation by calculation. # Hindu Calendars

Let us now come to the elaborate and sophisticated systems of Indian Hindu calendars. The first thing to note is that Hindu solar and lunisolar Calendars are not formulaic but self-adjusting every year based on natural cycles.

### The Solar Map

First, let us look at those Hindus who follow the solar calendar (which does not take into account the lunar cycle). This is not only useful in its own right but the Hindu lunisolar calendar also uses this solar calendar for its intercalation (correcting) mechanism to make the lunar cycles match the solar cycles. How does the Hindu calendar track the solar cycle and how does it differ from the Gregorian calendar? Hindu solar calendar uses the position of the sun relative to the zodiac to keep track of the solar cycle. This means that it tracks the sidereal cycles and not the tropical (seasonal) cycles (more on this later). As the earth revolves around the sun, the relative position of the sun w.r.t the 12 zodiac constellations change. This divides the solar cycle into 12 units which are the solar months for those Hindus who follow the purely solar calendar for other purposes. When the sun leaves one zodiac sign to enter another zodiac sign, it marks the end of one solar month and the beginning of the next. This transition is called in Sanskrit as the **saṅkrānti**. The name of a saṅkrānti is normally the name of the zodiac sign that the sun is entering into and is also the name of the solar month ; that is, if the sun exits the **Dhanu** (*Sagittarius*) zodiac to enter the zodiac sign of **Makara** (*Capricorn*), then the saṅkrānti is named as **Makara saṅkrānti** (a very famous occasion in India) and the new month that follows is called the Makara in the Hindu solar calendar.

The start of the new year for those Hindus who follow the solar calendar is defined as the day when the sun transitions to enter the zodiac sign of **Aries** (Sanskrit : **Meṣa Rāśi**). In other words, the new year for solar calendar following Hindus is the **Meṣa saṅkrānti.** Since this is only based on the solar cycle, this solar new year occurs around a fixed Gregorian date in Hinduism (which is around April 14).

But how many days in a month and what about leap year adjustments? There is absolutely no such adjustment required here. The numbers of days in a month are not fixed in this calendar. When the sun transitions from one zodiac sign to another zodiac sign, a new month begins. And when the sun has completed a periodic tour of all the twelve signs, a year is elapsed. There is no need to have any leap year correction because the months are not formulaically fixed but based on a purely natural event of the sun transitioning between consecutive zodiac signs. This is a self-adjusting mechanism and the number of days in a month adjusts automatically based on the time the sun spends in each zodiac zone. Also, note that there is another factor complicating the number of days per month in the solar calculation.

The earth’s orbit around the sun is not circular but elliptical and hence the earth moves around the sun at a lower speed when it is farther from the sun (aphelion) and faster when it is closer to the sun (perihelion). From a geocentric viewpoint, this means that on average, the months corresponding to perihelion will have a lower number of days (sun moves through faster there) and that the months corresponding to the aphelion will have a higher number of days. The text **Sūryasiddhānta** composed in the fifth century calculates the minimum possible length of the solar month as 29d 7h 8m and the maximum possible length of the solar month as 31d 15h 28m.

Thus, the *Sūryasiddhānta* shows its true mathematical depth. Unlike the Gregorian calendar, where month lengths are arbitrary 30 or 31 days and corrected formulaically for leap years for the sake of long term adjustment, the Hindu solar month length is determined by natural celestial events which is the **sun's true velocity** and its relative positions amidst the constellations.

### Five Limbs of the Hindu Lunisolar Calendar

Although Hinduism has solar calendars followed at certain places for various other secular purposes (like to determine the start of the new year), the religious calendar of all Hindus is a complex lunisolar one called the **Pañcāṅga** (Sanskrit : Five limbs). What are the five limbs of this calendar?

- **Vāra (Week):** A simple solar-based seven-day week

- **Nakṣatra (Lunar Mansion):** The specific constellation through which
  the moon is transiting on a given day. The sky is divided into 27
  unique Nakshatras.

- **Karaṇa (Half-Tithi):** Two karaṇas equal one Tithi. There are 60
  karaṇas in a full lunar cycle, used for refining specific
  calculations. To put in other words, one lunar cycle is divided into
  60 karaṇas or 30 tithis. Each karaṇa mathematically equals 6 degrees
  of the lunar cycle.

- **Yoga (Union):** A calculation based on the sum of the sun’s and
  moon’s positions, used mostly for defining auspicious or inauspicious
  times.

- **Tithi (Lunar Day):** This is the most critical unit for
  understanding the month. A Tithi is not a fixed 24-hour period.
  Instead, it is defined by the angular separation between the sun and
  the moon. There are 30 tithis in a lunar month but a lunar month is
  only approximately 30 days and hence 1 tithi is a lunar day and is
  hence not exactly equal to day.

There are two distinct traditions for when a new Hindu lunar month begins, dividing India into approximately a “North-South Split”:

1.  **Pūrṇimānta (North Indian):** In this convention, a month **ends on
    the Full Moon (Purnima)** and begins the day after. That is, a month
    is counted from one full moon to another full moon.

2.  **Amānta (South Indian/West Indian):** In this convention, a month
    **ends on the New Moon (Amavasya)** and begins the day after. That
    is, a month is counted from one new moon to another new moon.

> **Note**: The actual sequence of Tithis and holidays (like Diwali or
> Shivaratri) is the same in both traditions; only the official name and
> start date of the month vary.

But we see that we have directly went from Aśvin month to the Mārgaśīrṣa month, skipping Kārttika month completely because its saṅkrānti got jammed and had to coincide with another zodiac which got to name the month. So, we see that a month is skipped in a rare occasion. This is called a **kṣaya māsa** or a lost month in Sanskrit and occurs even rarer.

Statistically, since lunar months are on average shorter than solar months, the extra months are more frequent (once every three years approximately) than the lost months. The last time when we had a **kṣaya māsa** or a lost month was in 1983 and the next time we will have one is only in the 22nd century !

> Note (for those interested) : To add further, if you paid attention to
> the tropical vs sidereal cycles, the **Gregorian calendar** and many
> other calendars use the **tropical year**, which is timed to the
> seasons; it defines a year as the time between two vernal equinoxes.
> In contrast, the **Hindu solar calendar** (Siddhantic) uses the
> **sidereal year**, which measures the sun’s position against the fixed
> stars of the zodiac. Because of precession of the earth’s rotational
> axis, the Hindu zodiacal “New Year” (Mesha Sankranti) slowly drifts
> forward through the seasons by about one day every 72 years, whereas
> the Gregorian year stays fixed to the spring equinox. So, while the
> Hindu calendar drifts with respect to seasons, it is faithful when
> viewed w.r.t the heliocentric model and its zodiacal concerns (note
> that from the heliocentric perspective, it the starts that are fixed).
> Thus, even though the Hindu calendar becomes “less accurate” regarding
> tracking the seasons on earth, it is “more accurate” regarding
> tracking the Earth’s position in space astronomically. The drift
> between the tropical solar cycle (seasonal cycle) and the sidereal
> solar cycle (the earth’s position w.r.t the sun) is fundamentally
> irreconcilable. Any system designed for catching up with one of them
> drifts with respect to the other. You can either catch up accurately
> with the solar based seasons or the solar based zodiac cycle; not
> both. The Hindu civilisation is one of the few that tracks sidereal
> solar cycles.

The Chinese calendar is also a lunisolar calendar that uses lunar months compensated for solar cycles. In the Chinese calendar, a new month starts exactly on the day of the **new moon** (when the moon is completely dark). As usual, a normal lunar year (12 months) is only **354 days**. But a solar year (seasons) is **365 days**. If the Chinese only used the moon, it would mess up and for instance, their “Spring Festival” would eventually end up in the middle of a snowy winter! To fix it, every 2 or 3 years, they add an **entire extra month** (a 13th month) to the year. This “leap month” acts like a reset button that pushes the calendar back in line with the sun. So, it is like the Jewish approach. The traditional Chinese calendar also resembles the Hebrew calendar in having ordinary years with 12 months and 353, 354, or 355 days, and leap years with 13 months and 383, 384, or 385 days for precision adjustments. Days are measured from midnight to midnight (this aspect is like the Gregorian calendar). The first day of the month is the date of the new Moon (not the first visible crescent used in the Islamic and Hebrew calendars).

To know *when exactly* to add that extra month, they follow a complex approach. The Chinese divide the sun’s path into **24 small segments** (called Solar Terms) which are grouped into **12 pairs**. These 12 pairs of milestones are not based on zodiacs but are like seasonal milestones and are named intuitively (e.g., “Beginning of Spring,” “Insects Awaken,” “Winter Solstice”). Because these divisions are based on seasons, it means that it tracks the tropical solar cycle and not the sidereal solar cycle. These seasonal milestones in Chinese are also called as the **principal terms**. The lunar months of the Chinese calendar are numbered by the principal term that falls within it (like in the Hindu calendar where a lunar month inherits the name of the solar saṅkrānti that happens inside it). In the Chinese system, the **Winter Solstice (Dōngzhì)** is the absolute “North Star” of the calendar. It is **Principal Term 11**.

> The rule is strict: **The lunar month that contains the winter
> solstice MUST be numbered Month 11.** The calendar is adjusted to
> ensure that the principal term for winter solstice always falls in
> month 11.

This acts as a global reset point. Even if other months get slightly messy, the system forces a realignment every year to ensure the deepest point of winter stays in the 11th month. To see if a year needs a leap month, astronomers look at the gap between two consecutive Winter Solstices:

> **Normal Year:** There are **12 New Moons** between the start of one
> Month 11 and the next Month 11.
>
> **Leap Year:** There are **13 New Moons** in that gap.

If the "13-Moon" condition is met, the system knows a "leap month" *must* be inserted somewhere in that span to prevent the calendar from drifting. Where to insert the leap month? Once the system identifies a leap year by seeing that it contains 13 new moons in the inter-solstice period, it looks for the **first lunar month that does not contain a Principal Term (zhōngqì)** (like Hindu lunisolar calendar inserts an adhika/extra lunar month once every 3 years on average, when there is no solar saṅkrānti happening in the entire duration of that month)

> **The Leap Month Rule :** Every regular month *must* contain one of
> these major solar milestones. If a lunar month passes by and **no**
> major solar milestone happens during it, that month is declared a
> “Leap Month.” It’s basically a “waiting room” month until the sun
> catches up.

Because a solar month is about 30.4 days and a lunar month is only 29.5 days, the lunar months slowly “slide” ahead of the solar markers. Eventually, a lunar month will start and end entirely *between* two Principal Terms.

> **Normal Month:** New Moon → **Solar** **Milestone** → New Moon. (The
> month is anchored).
>
> **Leap Month:** New Moon → **(No Solar Milestone)** → New Moon.

The Chinese system is also a natural mechanism, like the Hindu calendar, except that it readjusts to the tropical solar cycle (rather than the sidereal solar cycle as in Hinduism). It uses the winter solstice to define the year, and the "missing milestone" rule based on natural solar markers to decide exactly where to tuck in the extra month so the seasons stay perfectly aligned.
