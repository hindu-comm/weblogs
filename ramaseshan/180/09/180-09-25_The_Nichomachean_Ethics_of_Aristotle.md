Have you always passionately wanted to explore the great classics of human civilisations, but, have you also been intimidated and felt lost while you were trying to read these ancient texts on your own? Have you wanted a sort of tour guide to explore the various classics of the world? If yes, this series of articles on the “great texts of the world” is exactly what you were looking for. Here, I try to act as that informative and engaging tour guide who introduces you to the overall structure of these ancient texts, what arguments stay put forth, and also provide some nice extracts to get a flavour of what the writer originally is saying so that you will gain context and acquire confidence to read the full texts on your own. In this article, we start by exploring the Nichomachean ethics attributed to Aristotle.

In the Western tradition, the mundane (worldly) and the transcendental (spiritual) concerns derive from two very different roots. The former derives from Ancient Greece while the latter derives from the Hebraic tradition. Post the classical period, Western philosophy is a union of the Greek focus on the mundane dimension of the meaning of life and the Hebraic focus on the transcendental vision of the universe from the Middle East. Unlike other famous ancient texts, the ***Nichomachean ethics*** that we are going to explore in this article, is not poetry with rich metaphors, but is a dry prose text, which are the lecture notes of **Nichomachus**, the son and student of **Aristotle**.

# Context and the Platonic background

**Aristotle** (384 - 322 BCE) was an aristocratic citizen of **Athens**, and was a student of **Plato** and after graduating from his **Academy**, became a tutor to Alexander the Great, who conquered the world, while teaching from his own academy called the **Lyceum**, also in Athens. Late in his life, he had to flee Athens because he was charged with impiety, like Socrates, the teacher of Plato. While his teacher Plato is famous for his philosophical texts in dialogues, Aristotle did write them too but unfortunately, none of his own texts survive. All the corpus of his texts that survive in his name are lecture notes from his students albeit being a much larger corpus.

Unlike other students, Aristotle disagreed with many of his teacher Plato’s views. While Plato focused on the abstract and the ideal, arguing that the material world is not perfect and not as real compared to a perfect, transcendental, and eternal world of forms and concepts, and that we have innate knowledge of these forms while the sensual knowledge of the material world is a corrupt reflection of these forms, Aristotle focused on the importance of sensory knowledge and argued that all of our knowledge is acquired from the senses - almost a 180 degree turn from his teacher’s views.

# Architecture of an Aristotlean soul

The Greek word **psyche** (translated into Latin as **anima**) that is used by Aristotle that is normally badly translated into English as “*soul*” deserves some special examination. Unlike the later Western conception of soul which refers to an immortal entity that serves as the basis of our action and travels between lives and suffers judgement for good and evil, Aristotle’s psyche or anima is not immortal and simply means the principle that animates and makes us alive and is the essence of life. He views human beings as a sophisticated animal unlike Plato who thought that humans are spiritual beings in communion with the world of forms. But since the translation to soul has been entrenched for a while in tradition, I will continue to use the word soul while assuming that you will keep this caveat in mind. Aristotle classified souls into various layers:

1.  **Vegetative anima** - enables growth and nutrition shared by us
    with plants and animals.

2.  **Appetitive anima** - gives us appetite, and desire shared by us
    with animals but not possessed by plants.

3.  **Sensory anima** - makes it possible for us to see, hear, smell,
    and so on (associated with the senses) and shared by us with
    advanced animals.

4.  **Locomotive anima** - makes it possible for us to move and control
    our movements and also shared by us with most animals.

5.  **Rational anima** - enables us to use language and reason that is
    unique to human beings.

# Eudaimonia : Aristotle’s overall vision of human moral life

The central value for Aristotle is the Greek word **eudaimonia** which is translated badly into English as happiness. I do not like this translation because the word happiness in English connotates more of a feeling and a transitory emotion whereas eudaimonia means living well on the whole (***eu*** = good, ***daimonia*** = spirit) which means flourishing well in the long term by leading a globally successful life that one can be proud of in the end of days. Eudaimonia is hence a property of life as a whole. In the Nichomachean ethics, Aristotle first asks what is eudaimonia, and then how to cultivate it, and then argues for it as the ultimate goal of life.

# Goodness tied to function

Another thesis of Aristotlean ethics is that goodness is closely related to function - i.e. ***to be a good thing of a particular kind X is to always perform the function that all members of the kind X perform well*** (faint echo of the idea of svadharma from Gītā). For example, a good knife cuts well, a good chef cooks well, a good doctor cures well, and so on. So, what is the function of a human being, as a whole? Not only kinds of human beings have specific functions, but a human being in general has a function too, and hence a good human being is one who performs that function well. What is the function?

# Dimensions of human virtue : Arete, Phronesis, Enkrateia

Aristotle views that flourishing of a life (eudaimonia) involves three dimensions :

1.  **Arete** (virtue / excellence) : the character of a thing that
    makes it a good thing of its kind - the enduring states of character
    that when cultivated, enables us to flourish.

2.  **Phronesis** (practical wisdom) : knowledge of how to do things -
    skill in making choices for our action and figuring our way to reach
    our goals.

3.  **Enkrateia** (moral strength) : the strength to free ourselves from
    the grip of the desire to have a genuine freedom to do the right
    thing even after knowing what it is and have figured out how to do
    it.

Aristotle argues that flourishing requires all three dimensions of character— virtue, practical wisdom, and moral strength—but it also requires a larger context that includes material goods and friendship.

# Searching for The Highest Good

Aristotle begins that every art or science or investigation is aimed at some good. But it is clear that there is a difference in the ends at which they aim - different goods for different activities. In some cases, the activity itself is the end (e.g. listening to music) and in most other cases, there is a product beyond the activity (e.g. cooking).

In these cases, the actions are grouped hierarchically where the goals of the superordinate actions are more important than the goals of the subordinate actions. For e.g, the goal of passing tomorrow’s semester exam is subordinate to the goal of getting the degree which itself is subordinate to the goal of getting a job that we like. The goal to master cavalry is subordinate to the goal to become expert in military strategy which itself is subordinate to diplomacy. Aristotle argued that if this hierarchy were to go on ad infinitum (means every good is only good for something else), then all of our actions would be pointless. There will be no answer to the question “Why are we doing all of these?”. So, there must be a highest good. So, what is this highest good that is the ultimate goal for all enterprises?

Aristotle then argues that we do everything to benefit us in some sense. But the collective good is better than the good of an individual. But the science of collective good is nothing but political science. So, he argues that politics is the most superordinate master science that governs all the sciences and takes us to the highest good.

Then, Aristotle cautions us that even though our ultimate goal is this collective good, it is difficult to be precise about it. Some things might be good for some people but bad for others, might be good for some times but not at other times (e.g. getting a good lump sum of money is good for most people but not good for a compulsive gambler). The other issue that makes collective good messy is that some answers might involve matters of subjective taste (e.g. what is the best color to paint this room?). So, Aristotle argues that knowing what is good for a collective group of human beings requires lots of experience and judgement - it is not for the amateur but for grown ups. It can’t be decided simply based on abstract, theoretical arguments like Plato did. He writes:

Each man can judge competently the things that he knows… \[Therefore\], a young man is not equipped to be a student of politics; for he has no experience in the actions which life demands… Moreover, since he follows his emotions, his study will be pointless and unprofitable. Whether he is young in years or immature, makes no difference.

Aristotle begins with an argument by elimination to dismiss candidates that are definitely not the highest good. Some examples are :

### Pleasure ruled out

The common run of people, and the most vulgar, identify it with pleasure; and for that reason, are satisfied by a life of enjoyment… They betray their utter slavishness in their preference for a life suitable to cattle.

The argument here is that pleasure is good even for animals but we are looking for something that is distinctive to human beings and hence must employ our rational anima in some form. Hence, pleasure can’t be the highest good.

### Honour ruled out

Cultivated and active men, on the other hand, believe the good to be honour; for honor, one might say, is the end of political life. But this too is clearly superifical to be an answer : **for honor seems to depend on those who confer it rather than on him who recieves it**. Furthermore, men seem to pursue honor to assure themselves of their own worth.. Obviously then, excellence, as far as they are concerned, is better than honour.

Here, he argues two ways to argue that honour is not the highest good - honour is dependent on who confers it, and that people seek who honor are doing so because they want to confirm of their excellence. So, it is not the highet good. Can excellence be? He rejects that too.

### Excellence ruled out

One might… consider excellence rather than honour to be the end of political life. However, even excellence proves to be imperfect as an end : for a man might possibly possess it while asleep or while being inactive all his life, and while, in addition, undergoing the greatest suffering and misfortune. Nobody would call the life of such a man happy (eudaimonia).

Here he again gives two reasons as to why excellence at something cannot be the highest good. First, let us say you have the potential to become the most excellent guitar player but you never practised and played and mainfested it. So, it cannot be the highest good. Second, even if you did manifest your excellence in guitar playing, let us say you become a drug addict, ruin your life in all possible ways and end up suffering - that too cannot be a perfect life. So, excellence cannot be the highest good. How about wealth?

### Wealth ruled out

As for the money-maker, his life is led under some kind of constraint : clearly, wealth is not the good which we are trying to find, for it is only useful ; it is a means to something else.

Wealth can’t be the highest good because clearly money is just an exchange value and one possesses money only to exchange it with something else one desires.

### Against Plato’s idea

Briefly, Aristole considers Plato’s view that “the Good” is an abstract universal concept and not necessarily what is “the Good” for human beings and then ask how can human beings realise it? But he dismisses this because there are different goods for different things and they are too diverse to be subsumed under a single overarching category. To know what is the highest good for a human being, one has to really probe into what a human being is and what is his function.

The good is evidently something different in different actions and in each art : it is one thing in medicine, another in strategy… What then, is the good of each? Is it not that for the sake of which everything else is done? That means it is health in the case of medicine, victory in the case of strategy, … Thus, if there is some one end for all that we do, this would be the good attainable by action.

### Characteristics of the highest good

About this highest good, he further continues by characterising it :

The highest good on the other hand, must be something **final**… We call that which is pursued as an end in itself more final than an end which is pursued for the sake of something else… What is always chosen as an end in itself and never as a means to something else is called final in an unqualified sense.

### Eudaimonia as the highest good

He finally argues that eudaimonia is that which fits what he is looking for.

This description appears to apply to eudaimonia above all else ; for we always choose eudaimonia as an end in itself; and never for the sake of something else.

Honour, pleasure, intelligence, and all the virtues that we choose partly for themselves - but we also choose them partly for the sake of eudaimonia ; because we assume that it is through them that we will have eudaimonia. On the other hand, no one chooses eudaimonia for the sake of honour, pleasure, and the like, nor as a means to anything at all

Aristotle also then argues that the highest good should be self-sufficient by itself and hence why eudaimonia fits as the right candidate for the highest good.

We define as **self-sufficient**, that which taken by itself, makes life something desirable and deficient in nothing. It is eudaimonia, in our opinion, which fits this description.

It is eudaimonia, in our opinion, which fits this description. Moreover, eudaimonia is of all things the one most desirable, and it is not counted as one good thing among many others…

\[If\] it were, it is obvious that the addition of even the least of goods would make it more dersirable.. We see that eudaimonia is something final and self-sufficient and is the end of our actions.

If something is self-sufficient, then it means you have need of nothing else to make it better. Everything else is for gaining happiness and happiness by itself is self-sufficient and hence it is the highest good. But then comes the next question.

# What is eudaimonia? The Function of man

To call eudaimonia as the highest good is perhaps a little trite, and a clearer account of what it is , is still required.

Next, Aristotle argues that it is known only by answering another question, that might seem surprising at first.

Perhaps this is best done by first ascertaining the proper function of man. For just as the goodness and performance of a flute player, a sculptor, or any other kind of expert, .. are thought to reside in his proper function; so the goodness and performance of man would seem to reside in whatever is his proper function.

He says here that since every kind of person has a function, a person as such too ought to have a certain function. And perhaps, the key to understanding what eudaimonia is, lies in unlocking what that function is. He also presents another parallel argument to reinforce this.

As an eye, hand, foot, and in general, each of the parts evidently has a function, may one lay it down, that man similarly has a function apart from all of these?

Again, Aristotle will give an elaborate set of possibilities and consider whether any of these can serve as the function of a human being as such.

Life seems to belong even to plants but we are seeking what is peculiar to man. Let us exclude therefore, the life of nutrition and growth. Next, there would be a life of perception, but it also seems to be shared even by the horse, the ox, and every other animal. There remains then, an active life, of the element that has a rational principle… The function of man is an activity of the soul which follows or implies a rational principle.

But this just narrows the range of possibilities down by telling us that the function of a human being should involve acting in accordance with the rational principle and does not tell us further what it specifically is. Aristotle continues :

If we say “so-and-so” and a “good so-and-so” have a function which is the same in kind, e.g. a lyre player and a good lyre player, … And if any action is well-performed, if it it is performed in accordance with the appropriate excellent … human good turns out to be the activity of the soul in accordance with virtue.

This was a slighty subtle point. A bad musician and a good musician both have the same function as playing music. It is not that the function of the bad musician is to play bad and that the function of the good musician is to play good music. Both have the same function ; just that the good musician is performing that function well and the bad musician is not. Similarly, if you want to know what the function of a thing is, look at what a good thing does. Since good human being is one who has virtue, then the function of a human being is to act rationally in accordance with virtue.

> **Eudaimonia is rational activity with virtue**

And to drive home the point how eudaimonia is different from happiness, he memorably says:

For one swallow does not make a summer nor does one day ; and so too one day…does not make a person blessed and eudaimonic (happy).

So, to judge whether a person’s life is eudaimonic, one has to have a long term judgement of their life. Atleast while they die, can we judge it? Aristotle says no! Because, some times, projects near and dear to somebody might be ongoing and taken up by the next generation - so he didn’t die eudaiomonic but died believing that his life was eudaimonic!

So, if eudaimonia is acting with virtue, we have two questions

1.  What is virtue?

2.  How does one act in accordance with virtue?

# Virtue attained by habit

Aristotle begins by arguing that virtue is acquired by habit. It is interesting to note that what is translated as “habit” here is the Greek word “**ēthikē**” that gives us the word “ethics”! This tells us that ethics, according to Aristotle, is best attained by habituation. He gives three arguments for it.

### The change argument

If the thing has a capacity by nature, then it can’t change its capacity by practise. The example he gives is that a stone falls down by nature and one can’t change it no matter what. But we see people who aren’t virtuous becoming one - e.g., a child growing up into an excellent person. So, it is not that virtue is something that comes out of a person’s nature.

### The acquisition argument

For natural qualities, we first have the capacity and then the action corresponding to it. The example that Aristotle mentions is perception - we have the capacity to perceive by having a pair of functioning eyes and then we see using that. We don’t start seeing and then develop a set of eyes. But for things that are acquired, we practise first, and then we develop the capacity. It is by practising building houses and then we become a good builder. Aristotle argues that virtue too is in that category.

### The same causes argument

It is a characteristic of things like arts and performance that the same kind of thing that produces excellence in a domain can also destroy it. By practising to build shoddy and shady houses, one becomes a bad builder. By practising to build good houses, we become a good builder. Virtue too, Aristotle says, belongs to that nature. To quote him:

In one word, states of character arise out of like activities. That is why the activities we exhibit must be of a certain kind; it is because the states of character correspond to the differences between these. It makes no small difference, then, whether we form habits of one kind or of another from our very youth; it makes a very great difference, or rather all the difference.

# How to detect virtue?

The next question then is; what is the difference between merely acting virtuously and being virtuous? Isn’t it circular? Just now we argue that a person becomes virtuous by habit - by doing virtuous actions. But now we want to imply that there is some difference between merely acting virtuously and being virtuous while we are asking this question. Aristotle initially tries to circumvent this by seeming to imply that there is a difference between the initial stage where the person is doing virtuous actions to cultivate it as a habit and the later stage when he becomes virtuous and does it spontaneously - at that latter stage, he regards virtuous acts as inherently pleasing to perform. He writes :

We must take as a sign of states of character the pleasure or pain that supervenes upon acts; for the man who abstains from bodily pleasures and delights in this very fact is temperate ; … He who stands his ground against things that are terrible and delights in this, or at least is not pained, is brave; while the man who is pained is a coward.

For moral excellence is concerned with pleasures and pains; it is on the account of pleasure that we do bad things, and on account of pain that we abstain from \[doing\] noble ones. Hence, we ought to have been brought up in a particular way from our very youth, as Plato says, so as both to delight in and be pained by the things that we ought; for this is the right education.

But Aristotle does recognise the chicken and the egg problem explicitly.

The question might be asked what we mean, by saying that we must become just by doing just acts, and temperate by doing temperate acts; for if men do just and temperate acts, they are already just and temperate, exactly as, if they do what is in accordance with the laws of grammar and music, they are grammarians and musicians.

How does a child become a grammarian? By producing grammatical sentences. But if one already produces grammatical sentences, is he not already a grammarian? Aristotle is asking this same question about virtue in the above passage. How can be perform virtuous acts in the first place to be virtuous, if we are not already virtuous? Aristotle resolves this.

Or is this not true even of the arts? Is it possible to do something that is in accordance with the laws of grammar, whither by chance or under the guidance of another. A man will be a grammarian, then, only when he has both done something grammatical and done it grammatically; and this means doing it in accordance with the grammatical knowledge in himself.

The difference that Aristotle makes here is that between just doing acts according to some set of rules (by chance or instruction) and internalizing them and making it come out spontaneously from ourselves. Thus, he concludes :

If acts that are in accordance with the virtues have themselves a certain character, it does not follow that they are done justly or temporarily. The agent must also be in a certain condition when he asked them; in the first place you must have knowledge, secondary, he must choose the act, and choose them for their own sakes, and thirdly, his action must proceed from a firm and unchangeable character.

# Virtue as a mean between vices

Finally, the most important question : what is virtue? Aristotle says that it is the mean between two extreme vices. That is why virtue is tough and requires lots of judgement. The mean can also be different for different people under different circumstances.

Hence it is no easy task to be good. For in everything, it is no easy task to find the middle, e.g. to find the middle of a circle is not for everyone but for him who knows; so, too, can anyone get angry - that is easy - or give or spend money; but to do this to the right person, to the right extent, at the right time, with the right motive, and in the right way, that is not for everyone, nor is it easy; wherefore goodness is both rare and laudable and noble.

# Phronesis and Enkrateia : Independent dimensions to cultivate virtue

Now that we have understood what is virtue, let us go to the next question - how does one act in accordance with virtue? Just because we know what is virtue, does it automatically mean that we will all act in accordance with virtue? Of course not!

Activity requires choice and choice requires wisdom. What is choice? Choice, here, does not mean just a wish, but deliberation in choosing the sequences of actions required to achieve virtue which requires practical wisdom (Greek : **phronesis**) . If I want to learn the Russian language, choice is not just a wish that I want to learn Russian but the exact sequence of steps that I need to take to gain a proficiency in Russian. This is where wisdom comes in, according to Aristotle. You might ask, is not wisdom, a part of virtue? Aristotle thinks it is not, and gives two reasons.

- Phronesis is not a mean - too much of it is good! (unlike virtue which
  Aristotle defined as the mean between two vices)

- Even a thief can have phronesis - it is a skill that can serve vice as
  easily as it can serve virtue.

So, phronesis is an independent dimension to cultivate virtue. So, phronesis is necessary but not sufficient for virtue. Without phronesis, one might become a person good at heart but will end up screwing up in his endeavours.

Is there anything else apart from practical wisdom that is required to act in accordance with virtue? It is moral strength (Greek : **enkrateia**), says Aristotle, that makes us actually do the right thing by resisting temptations and other exigencies. Moral strength, again, is independent of virtue itself; the same two arguments above apply. Too much of moral strength is good and even a terrorist has moral strength, fortitude, and practical wisdom to choose and do the right thing needed to carry his non-virtuous goals. Think of how scary a murderer must feel deep inside when he commits a murder but he resists it and does it anyway - that is moral strength in action but serving vice instead of virtue.

Also, moral strength is independent of both virtue as well as practical wisdom. Virtue is the “what” to do. Phronesis is the “how” to do. I can know what to do and how to do and still end up not doing it because I lack the moral strength to execute it. There are many who regret unable to act virtuously despite knowing what and how they should have done. Moral strength is not virtue and moral weakness is not vice.

Are these three enough? Aristotle says this is still half of the equation and that which comes from within. One has to know from within, what is virtue. One has to cultivate from within, the practical wisdom to make choices that serve virtue and possess moral strength from within to implement them. But there are still externalities that are necessary for the expression of eudaimonia which we will see in the next section.

# Friendship

Aristotle says that there are material and social factors from the outside as well that are required for the flowering of eudaimonia even if all the internal factors are gotten right. The material factors include material objects - one needs money to become generous, one needs good health to become courageous, one needs a functioning mouth to speak honestly. But more subtle are the social goods that are mentioned by Aristotle, and is a distinctive characterisation of Aristotlean philosophy. Elsewhere, Aristotle said that a distinguishing feature of man is that he is a **zoon politikon** (Greek for : *political animal*). By this, he does not mean that we have to take part in elections, campaigns, and so on, but that we need a society to flourish. The Greek word “polis” from which the word “politics” is derived actually refers to a city-state. In Ancient Greece, each city was politically independent and had radically different forms of governments. By saying that man is a political animal, Aristotle hence means that man is an animal that is a member of the polis - the city society that he forms. Men are not naturally isolated wanderers who sometimes happen to come together to form a society. To the contrary, the natural state of man is to be a member of his constituent society. Eudaimonia needs a society to realise itself. More specifically, Aristotle says that friendship (Greek : **Philia** ) is required for eudaimonia. But Aristotle uses the word “friend” in a much more specific way - not just for people who like to hang out with but to whom we connect to. He says :

No one would choose to love without friends, even if he had all the other goods. For in fact, rich people and holders of powerful positions, even more than other people, seem to need friends. For how would one benefit from such prosperity, if one had no opportunity for beneficience…… and how would one guard and protect prosperity without friends?…..in poverty also, and in the other misfortunes, people think friends are the only refuge…Moreover, the young need it to keep them from error. The old need it to care for them and support the actions that fail because of weakness. And those in their prime need it, to do fine actions.

Friendship provides a social cohesion and generates goodwill and provides the context for the expression of virtue. Aristotle distinguishes three types of friendship based on the thing over which they form a bonding over. I will let Aristotle himself describe the three types of friendship.

### Friendship of pleasure

The cause of friendship between young people seems to be pleasure. For their lives are guided by their feelings, and they pursue above all, what is pleasant for themselves, and what is near at hand. But as they grow up, what they find pleasant, changes too. Hence, they are quick to become friends and quick to stop…

### Friendship of utility

This sort of friendship seems to arise especially among older people, since at that age, they pursue what is advantageous, not what is pleasant, and also among those in their prime or youth who pursue what is expedient. Nor do such people live together very much. For sometimes, they do not even find each other pleasant. Hence, they have no further need to meet this way, if they are not advantageous to each other.

### Friendship of virtue

Complete friendship is the friendship of good people, similar in virtue; for they wish goods in the same way to each other in so far as they are, and they are good in themselves. Now, those who wish goods to their friend for the friend;s sake are, friends, most of all; for they have this attitude because of the friend himself, not coincidentally. Hence, these peoples friendship lasts as long as they are good, and virtue is enduring.

These kinds of friendship are likely to be rare, since such people are few. Moreover, they need time to grow accustomed to each other; for, as the proverb says, they cannot know each other before they have shared that traditional peck of salt.

Thus, friendship and especially virtue friendship, forms an important and necessary dimension of our lives and in cultivation of eudaimonia. So, a happy meaningful life according to to Aristotle is an active social life that is cultivated in a civic context.

Thankyou for reading this far and wishing you a life of eudaimonia.
