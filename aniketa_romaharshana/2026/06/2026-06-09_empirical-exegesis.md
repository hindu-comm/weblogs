Empirical Exegesis

``` wp-block-verse
 अप्रत्यक्षाणि शास्राणि विवादस्तत्र केवलम्‌। 
प्रत्यक्षं ज्योतिषं शास्त्रं चन्द्रार्कौ यत्र साक्षिणौ॥ 
```

– मौक्तिकम् (*२१६० [महासुभाषितसङ्ग्रहात्](https://archive.org/details/in.ernet.dli.2015.407928/page/482/mode/2up)*)

``` wp-block-verse All doctrines deal with the invisible, and in them lies only debate. But the Astronomical doctrine is visible, wherein the Sun and Moon stand witness. ```

– Anonymous ([*Mahāsubhāṣitasaṅgraha, 2160*](https://archive.org/details/in.ernet.dli.2015.407928/page/482/mode/2up))

The human experiences with faith, empiricism, and authority have long occupied philosophers across cultures and epochs. In the Indian intellectual tradition as well, these questions were neither incidental nor unanimous in answer. In fact, deciding which sources of knowledge are authoritative, and over what kinds of objects they hold sway, lay at the very root of the contemplations of Indian philosophers.

Occasionally, such contemplation (or the lack thereof) can lead to confusions where people end up applying the wrong mode of knowledge to make an inference in the wrong domain. Evidently, astronomy was no exception to such misconceptions, with some ostensibly coming to assert that the entire science was divinely revealed and hence should not be modified with time. It is with a view to clarify such confusions and establish the true epistemology of the science, that the [legendary](https://youtu.be/L3uOVlzAoZk?si=n02kztz_WhrBMpjM&t=3823) 15^(th) century CE astronomer [Nīlakaṇṭha Somayājin](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-981-95-4857-6) wrote his *Jyotirmīmāṁsā* — A one-of-its-kind text in the Indian tradition, for it deals primarily and in great detail with the qualitative principles of Astronomy rather than with mathematics and algorithms. The way that Nīlakaṇṭha dispels these misconceptions is rather skillful, for rather than *antagonizing* religion, as we find when similar scenarios appear in other cultures, he *cites* religion to support his defense of strict empiricism.

One of his expositions regarding the revealed nature of Āryabhaṭa’s constants has [already been brought to light](https://vriitrahan.wordpress.com/2021/07/21/twilight-of-hindu-science/) in recent years, which demonstrates quite well his staunch empiricism while also accepting without contradiction Āryabhaṭa’s own claim of divine revelation. In this article, we bring to light another of his expositions, where he cites Vedic authority to establish the primacy of empiricism in the physical sciences.

Nilakaṇṭha quotes from the first *prapāṭhaka* of the *[Taittirīyāraṇyaka](https://vedicheritage.gov.in/aranyakas/taittiriya-aranyaka/)*, also known as the *[Aruṇapraśna](https://youtu.be/-zlkjezNKXo?t=0)* for it comprises the formulae used in the *Āruṇaketukacayana* ritual. Let us first look at the verse in question, along with the (paraphrased) traditional exegeses of [BhaṭṭaBhāskara](https://archive.org/details/taittiriya/taittiriya_aranyaka_bhaskara_01/page/n12/mode/1up) and [Sāyaṇa](https://archive.org/details/taittiriya/taittiriya_aranyaka_sayana_01/page/n12/mode/1up) (both of whom agree on the central points, with the former mainly providing more grammatical details and supporting context):

“**Smṛ́tiḥ Pratyákṣam Aitihyàm. Ánumānaś catuṣṭayám.  
etáir á̄ditya-maṇḍalam. sárvair eva vídhāsyate..**“

–*[Taittirīyāraṇyaka 1.2.1](https://youtu.be/-zlkjezNKXo?t=126)*

**Smṛ́ti** refers to the *\[Dharma\]śāstra*s (moral codes) authored by Manu, etc., that are to be inferred from the Veda; **Pratyákṣa** refers to the Vedic sentences directly perceived by one’s ear; **Aitihyà** refers to the *Itihāsa*s and *Purāṇa*s (the traditional historical narratives including famously the *Rāmāyaṇa* and *Mahābhārata*); **Ánumāna** refers to the conduct of cultured people. With all these sources, the nature of the Solar Orb is to be determined – how this Orb is, how It impels, how It creates divisions of time such as the *manvantara*, etc., and how It generates everything through creation from water. With such words, the water-bricks employed in the *Āruṇaketuka* rite are extolled.

However, Nīlakaṇṭha Somayājin takes quite a different approach towards this verse. Following is our translation of [section 11 of the *Jyotirmīmāṁsā* per K.V. Sarma’s 1977 edition (pp 21-22)](https://archive.org/details/Jyotirmimansa/page/n59/mode/2up):

## Nīlakaṇṭha’s Exegesis of a Vedic hymn

> Now, the mode for obtaining the motions of the planets is shown. It is
> heard in the *Taittirīyopaniṣad (sic)*:
>
> “**Smṛ́tiḥ Pratyákṣam Aitihyàm. Ánumānaś catuṣṭayám.  
> etáir á̄ditya-maṇḍalam. sárvair eva vídhāsyate..**“
>
> –*[Taittirīyāraṇyaka 1.2.1](https://youtu.be/-zlkjezNKXo?t=126)*
>
> The meaning of this is [expanded on in the Holy
> *Bhāgavata*](https://www.wisdomlib.org/hinduism/book/the-bhagavata-purana/d/doc1127139.html).
> \[Vyāsa (the traditional author of the *BhāgavataPurāṇa*)\] says, as
> an answer to the question ‘how do we infer the motion of the Sun God
> Who circumambulates rightward the celestial axis (*Meru*) and the pole
> star (*Dhruva*), facing the motion of the Zodiac signs?’ — Just as the
> motion of the ants, etc., carried by a rotating potter’s wheel, and
> rotating along with it, is different indeed \[from that of the
> wheel\]; So too is the motion of the planets beginning with the Sun,
> rotating along with that (the celestial sphere) which circumambulates
> the celestial axis and the pole star, and carried by that \[celestial
> sphere\], different indeed \[from that of the celestial sphere\]; Due
> to \[their, viz., the Sun, etc.’s\] being observable in between the
> asterims marking their region and in between Zodiac signs. Therefore
> the word ‘**āditya**‘ (Sun) is for designating through implication the
> planets. And the mention of ‘**maṇḍala**‘ (orb) is with the intention
> of conveying the motion of the orb of the planets beginning with the
> Sun, because of it being commented upon by Vyāsa that ‘the motion is
> different indeed’.
>
> Even Sāyaṇamādhava in the *Kālanirṇaya*, explaining this *mantra* with
> the measurement of time, has rejected the primacy of the measurement
> of the orb — “If \[one thinks\] ‘aren’t the words \`**smṛti**\`, etc.,
> proposed as means of obtaining the measure of the orb and not time?’,
> then should it not be \[understood\] so. Due to the obtainableness of
> the \[size of the\] orb through empiricism known to all people, and
> the *smṛti*s, etc., not being employed there, they have been proposed
> with the desire of conveying time indeed, for \[obtaining\] the orb
> effected by time. So too is time measured with them through the orb.
>
> “Additionally, the desire of conveying time is exceedingly clear in
> the following *mantra*. There next *mantra* there is handed down thus
> —
>
> “**Sú̄ryo márīcim á̄datte. sarvasmā̎d bhuva̍nād a̱dhi.  
> tasyāḥ pāka-vi̍śeṣe̱ṇa. smṛtáṅ kāla-viśéśaṇam..**“
>
> –*[Taittirīyāraṇyaka 1.2.2](https://youtu.be/-zlkjezNKXo)*.
>
> its meaning is this — Having regarded the assemblage of all beings
> within the world, the Sun takes a ray capable of helping Him with
> maturing the sap and virility \[of the beings\], etc. And through the
> differences in maturity of beings caused by that \[ray\], the
> segmentation of time beginning with a *nimeṣa* (= 106.66 μs) all the
> way till a *parārdha* (=31,10,40,00,00,00,000 years) is understood by
> us.” Therefore, that even the first *mantra*, according to the maxim—
>
> “Whatever is heard as something is itself interpreted in that way”
>
> , is primarily about time indeed, is Mādhava’s opinion.
>
> \[Now,\] the distinction in motion of the planets is indeed the
> distinction in time. Therefore, the quartet beginning with ‘**Smṛti**‘
> is from the measure of time. Because of the statement ‘****sárvair
> eva****‘ (only with all \[these\]), it is the complete quartet that is
> the means of \[measuring\] time. Here, ****Smṛ́ti**** \[refers to\] the
> distinct treatises beginning with the *Sūryasiddhānta*.
> ****Pratyákṣa**** \[refers to\] the observation of eclipses,
> conjunctions of planets and stars, etc. ****Aitihyà**** \[refers to\]
> the understanding obtained from instruction about the eclipses,
> planetary conjunctions, etc., observed by a good teacher or such.
> Since accepting a *vyāpti* (universally pervasive claim of a
> syllogism, e.g., ‘where there is smoke there is fire’) requires
> repeated observation \[of the claim\], the motion of the planets is
> inferred having accepted the *vyāpti* like so \[as described above\].
> Subsequently, the specific ‘grasping’-rationales are understood with
> the *smṛti*s. Even among the *smṛti*s, there is pervasion by the
> instructions about the \[number of\] revolutions \[of the planets in a
> *Mahāyuga* (=43,20,000 years)\] and the circumferences \[of planetary
> orbits\].

###### \*Notes for the Pedant

1.  The translation attempts to be as close to literal as possible, even
    trying to maintain the formatting per Sarma’s edition, but takes
    some liberty when such a grammatical construction could obscure the
    meaning. Words in \[square brackets\] are implied in the text and
    can be supplied for grammatical completeness, while those in (curved
    brackets) are provided for additional context to the reader. 2.  The pitch accents on Vedic verses are shown with the conventional
    IAST style where it is possible to identify the *udātta* or
    *svarita* in each word; elsewhere, sentential pitch is indicated
    with *Taittirīya* style markings. 3.  The word ‘planet’ is used in the older sense of ‘that which wanders
    in the sky’, specifically including the Sun and Moon.

## Musings

Perhaps the most striking part of the above discussion is the exegesis of Nīlakaṇṭha giving a wholly unique perspective different from those of both BhaṭṭaBhāskara and Sāyaṇa on the same verse. Specifically, the former appears to lend itself most naturally to the *ādhibhautika*/physical meaning, the latter two perhaps being *ādhidaivika*/divine in approach. This is an illuminating example of how a scholar truly skilled in a *Vedāṅga* is able to arrive at an elegant exegesis that eludes most others — affirming the traditional notion of the *Vedāṅga*s as sciences that are primarily meant to help understand the meaning of the *Veda*.

It is known by now that the Vedic people were keen observers of the night sky and the lights that wander**ed** (the present tense is only appropriate in a few dark sky reserves in today’s enlightened age, but that is a rant for another time) in it from as early an era as when [Thuban sat at the north celestial pole as *Dhruva*](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/309710402_Ancient_Indian_Astronomy_in_Vedic_Texts) (incidentally, this is also recorded in the *Taittirīyāraṇyaka*). A particularly interesting hymn from the *Ṛgveda* even records an [eclipse observed by the seer Atri](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/372966609_AN_EXAMINATION_OF_'ATRI'S_ECLIPSE'_AS_DESCRIBED_IN_THE_RIG_VEDA) – interestingly, one of the types of observations listed by Nīlakaṇṭha (himself a staunch empiricist) as useful *pratyakṣa*. Thus, the Vedic people were no strangers to *pratyakṣa*, and by virtue of recording said *pratyakṣa* for posterity, neither were they unfamiliar with *aitihya*. Now all we need to locate are the *smṛti*s which would have been used for *anumāna* from this data. Unfortunately, the paper (rather, palm-leaf or birch-bark) trail for astronomical *smṛti*s runs very thin indeed prior to Āryabhaṭa (though it is generally agreed, as he himself states, that what preceded him was not vacuum). Nevertheless, this exegesis raises some interesting questions – were there already attempts at mathematically modelling the motion of the Sun in the Vedic age? If so, what did they look like, and what happened to them? Was this the elusive *VedāṅgaJyautiṣa* composed in *sūtra*s on which Lagadha’s extant prosodic one is supposedly based? Hopefully more research into pre *Siddhānta*ic astronomy, will shed more light on these topics (maybe Prof RN Iyengar has already answered some of these in his recent *[Mahāsalila](https://cahc.jainuniversity.ac.in/mythic/book-release/2024/12/28/mahasalilam-book.html)* – I do not know, for I have not read it yet).

At present, not much is certain, except that the empirical and inferential tradition of Indian astronomy has a clear root in the Veda itself (and if Nīlakaṇṭha was right, there were even treatises tracking the motion of the Solar Orb at this early period) thus predating contact with Greece or Babylonia – creating yet another hurdle to theories such as Pingree’s that used to be more popular in academia.

Another interesting aspect that warrants mention here is how it took a subject matter expert in an apparently orthogonal field to connect the dots and provide a useful exegesis that simultaneously improved our understanding of the Vedic hymn and established a basic yet crucial principle in Astronomical doctrine. This would not have been possible by someone who did not understand both to a sufficiently detailed level. Perhaps it was because of such considerations that the ideal astronomer was declared to be a polymath with expertise in at least thirteen subjects (I forgot the precise source of this verse by an early modern astronomer, though the verse itself has been etched into my memory):

``` wp-block-verse पाटी-कुट्टक-बीज-गोल-निपुणो वेदाङ्ग-पारङ्गमः काव्या-ऽलङ्कृति-शिल्पशास्त्र-कुशलो यो न्यायशास्त्रादिवित्। शास्त्रे ऽस्मिन्त्समस्त-वस्तु-सहिते तस्याधिकारो भवेच् चेदेवं न यथाकथञ्चिदभिधामात्रं प्रसिद्धिं नयेत्॥ ```

``` wp-block-verse He who is an expert in arithmetic, pulverization, algebra, spherics, and has surpassed the six limbs of the Veda, Skilled in poetry, decoration, and the doctrine of sculpture, and who is also educated in logic and such subjects, He alone shall have authority in this subject that is connected with all things, If he be like this; And not if he attain fame through mere namesake, by one way or another. ```

Finally, one can’t help but leave wondering — how many more such Vedic statements are there, whose understanding could be greatly improved by an interpreter skilled in the relevant *Vedāṅga*(s)?
