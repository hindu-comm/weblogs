The Molecules of Language: How Words are Like Chemical Compounds

``` wp-block-verse वागर्थाविव सम्पृक्तौ वागर्थप्रतिपत्तये। 
जगतः पितरौ वन्दे पार्वतीपरमेश्वरौ॥ ```

*For acquiring speech and meaning, I hail those who are inseparable like speech and meaning, the parents of the world, Pārvatī and Parameśvara*

``` wp-block-verse येन धौता गिरः पुंसां विमलैश्शब्दवारिभिः। तमश्चाज्ञानजं भिन्नं तस्मै पाणिनये नमः।। ```

*He by whom were cleansed the utterances of men with pure waters of words, and the darkness born of ignorance shattered, obeisance to* *him, Pāṇini*

Imagine you’re in the bronze age: a time when we had just wrought civilization out of chaos and earned the luxury of being able to pay attention to some of the finer things in life. The idea of *language* is well known to you – people make sounds that carry some meaning that is understood by other people. Such meaningful sounds you call *words*. Clearly, words have to be pronounced correctly in order to “work”. It is also evident that there are lots of words in use in the world, and one has to know a lot of words to express one’s thoughts correctly. Naturally, you begin to wonder – *“Can I learn all the words?”*. This is elegantly elucidated by the foundational myth of grammar in the Indian tradition.

## The Origin of Vyākaraṇa

Long ago, Indra, with the intention of learning all the words, approached the preceptor of the Gods, Br̥haspati. Br̥haspati started teaching him, listing one by one every single word in usage in the world. This continued for a thousand divine years, and this process was still nowhere near completion. This inspired the King of the Gods to devise a unique approach to learning the words – He broke them into Prakr̥ti-s (bases) and Pratyaya-s (affixes). All the innumerably many words were modelled as being the result of combining a Prakr̥ti with a Pratyaya – and these lists of Prakr̥ti-s and Pratyaya-s are smaller and easier to handle. This process of understanding words by breaking them into their constituent elements is called *Saṁskāra* *(≈refinement)* or *vyākaraṇa*. In this manner, language spoken in the world became *Saṁskr̥ta (one whose saṁskāra has been done)*, and Indra its first *Saṁskartṛ* *(He who performs Saṁskāra)*.

The genius of this process lies in explaining the infinite set of words as arising from these finite sets of inputs and rules. This story also illustrates the reason grammarians are dependant on the usage of words in the world *(prayogaśaraṇāḥ vaiyākaraṇāḥ*). “Usage in the world” here refers to the usage of words in the *Śiṣṭaloka (learned world)* – i.e, words used by the learned – and these are the “correct” words; not those blindly predicted by grammatical rules.

This method adopted by grammarians is remarkable: observe the zillions of words that exist in the world and invent sets of abstract “vocables” that combine with one another according to certain rules to create the familiar words. That is, a grammatical model can be understood as a machine that takes a few vocables as input, combines them according to certain rules, and produces a word spoken in the world as output. It is up to the grammarian’s imagination to invent these input vocables and rules in such a way that they are efficient and exhaustive. Grammarians throughout history have followed this general principle, and have kept coming up with more structured and efficient sets of base vocables, affix vocables, and rules by which they combine, to successfully “cover” as many words spoken in the world as possible.

Thus, there are many artificial “vocables” that combine with one another according to certain rules to form words. This process comprising of sequential steps to derive a word is termed prakriyā. One may find this analogous to organic compounds that react with one another according to certain mechanistic rules to form new compounds. In fact, this analogy can be observed quite clearly even as we delve deeper into the subject.

## The Aṣṭādhyāyī

With time, there came to be multiple grammatical traditions such as the Aindra, Cāndra, etc. Upon the exposition of the spectacular Māheśvara model by a grammarian named Pāṇini, all these older models became obsolete and have been lost to history. Such was the contribution of Pāṇini to vyākaraṇa that an alternate name of the discipline is pāṇinīya.

The Magnum Opus of Pāṇini, the Aṣṭādhyāyī, presents the most comprehensive method for vyākaraṇa, that covers (essentially) all the words, both of the loka and the Veda. It is a terse, concise, text with rules and definitions arranged in an intelligently thought-out sequence so that not a single syllable is superfluous. This particular machine consists of just over 4000 sūtra-s, and its inputs are mainly from 2 sets called the Dhātupāṭha and the Gaṇapāṭha.

The sūtra-s are aphorisms that convey some rules/metarules about the prakriyā. They are of 6 types. Let us look at each one in a little more detail:

### Sañjñā \| सञ्ज्ञा

It makes sense in grammar as in organic chemistry to classify these “reactants” based on the similarities/differences of their behaviour in “reactions”. Sañjñā-s are labels given to vocables based on this kind of classification. For example, vocables tagged with the sañjñā “ṭi” follow a different set of rules from those tagged “bha” during prakriyā – just like reactants tagged “bases” and “nucleophiles” follow different sets of rules in reaction mechanisms. The sūtra-s that define a particular sañjñā (ie, define which vocables this sañjñā applies to) are called the sañjñāsūtra-s

Thus, the sañjñā-s in grammar achieve a classifiction of vocables according to the rules they follow during prakriyā – analogous to the classification of reactants based on the rules they follow during the mechanistic steps of a reaction in organic chemistry.

### Paribhāṣā \| परिभाषा

A paribhāṣā is a general rule that should be followed when there is no other rule explicitly superceding it. For example, in organic chemistry, we have a ‘general rule’ such as “heating aldehydes in basic medium causes the aldol addition/condenstion reaction” – which is followed in most cases, but not when there is no alpha hydrogen: in this case, it is prescribed that the Cannizaro reaction will occur. But even this has an exception – if the aldehyde in question is 2-methylpropanal, which does have an alpha hydrogen, it undergoes the Cannizaro reaction. There a further exception to this as well: if the other reactant happens to be formaldehyde, it undergoes adol condensation nevertheless.

Let us look at a similar case from vyākaraṇa: Whenever instucted (by some later sūtra-s), the letters ‘i’, ‘u’, ‘r̥’ and ‘l̥’ are to be changed into their ‘guṇa’ and ‘vr̥ddhi’ counterparts. However, they shouldn’t be changed if a portion of a vocable named with the sañjñā ‘dhātu’ gets deleted because of an affix that has been given the sañjñā ‘ārdhadhātuka’. They also shouldn’t be changed if the ‘g’, ‘k’ or ‘ṅ’ present in the affix has been given the sañjñā ‘it’. They also shouldn’t be changed for the specific vocables “dīdhīṅ”, “vevīṅ”, and cases where a phenomenon called ‘iḍāgama’ happens. This is a rather simple illustrative example of rules and exceptions, and there are more complicated nested rules in the Aṣṭādhyāyī.

It doesn’t take long for any student of organic chemistry to figure out that these ‘general rules’ have embarassingly many exceptions. Perhaps, a more honest view would be to look at these as rules that are applicable when none of these specific ‘exceptional’ rules are given.

A paribhāṣā is explained as a principle that is applicable when there is no specific rule that explains the procedure for the case in question – analogous to the ‘general rules’ in organic chemistry that are applicable when the case in hand isn’t an ‘exception’ with other specific rules explicitly defined for it.

### Vidhi \| विधिः

Vidhi-s are the rules that explain what exactly happens when two vocables combine: which syllables are deleted, which syllables are modified and how, which syllables enter and where, etc. These are analogous to the individual mechanistic steps in a chemical reaction that explain which group leaves, which bonds are broken and formed, which atoms of the reactants combine to form addition products, etc.

### Niyama \| नियमः

These are the specific rules explained for certain cases that fall within a specified scope – corresponding with the ‘rules for exceptional cases’ in organic chemistry.

To illustrate this, let us again consider the aldol and Cannizaro reactions: the aldol reaction would comprise the paribhāṣā, and the niyamasūtra-s would detail the cases where alternate rules such as the Cannizaro are to be applied. Another illustration could be the elimination reaction. The paribhāṣā is “elimination happens such that the resultant product has the most number of hyperconjugations/resonances”, and a niyama would be “if the base has been declared ‘sterically hindered’, elimination happens at the least sterically hindered hydrogen”.

### Atideśa \| अतिदेशः

This is the application of principles expounded for one category in another place. A chemistry analogue is immediately obvious: a lot of the properties of amines are the same as those already expounded for alcohols.

### Adhikāra \| अधिकार:

One of the goals of Pāṇini in the Aṣṭādhyāyī is brevity, and one of the ways he achieves this is by drawing context for a particular sūtra from previously stated sūtra-s instead of repeating them – like in an elliptical sentence. A sūtra that provides context for multiple other sūtra- s is called an adhikāra.

## Summary

We have looked at the fundamental problem of vyākaraṇa, that is to create a way to ‘derive’ all the words spoken in the learned world by splitting them into prakr̥ti-s and pratyaya-s. We then explored the structure of the Pāṇinian model, examining the 6 types of sūtra-s conatined in the Aṣṭādhyāyī, and understanding them with familiar examples from chemistry. This rather close correspondence isn’t very surprising if we compare the objective and methods of the two sciences:

|  |  |
|----|----|
| **Languge** | **Chemistry** |
| There is an innumerable number of words spoken in the world that undergo sound changes when used in different meanings or come in close contact while speaking | There is an innumerable number of chemical compounds in the world, that undergo changes when they react with one another |
| The ground truth comprises of the words spoken in the learned world, and the sound changes that occur when they are utlized. | The ground truth comprises of the numerous chemical compounds that exist, and the products formed upon mixing them |
| It is impossible to understand how to use these words by learning them one by one *(pratipadapāṭha)* | It is impossible to undertand the nature of these compounds and how to use them by studying their properties one by one |
| Thus, it has been attempted to explain these as arising from the combination of vocables that combine according to certain more universal rules: this process being called prakriyā | Thus, it has been attempted to understand them by studying their constituent functional groups and the way they interact with other reactants, by framing mechanistic rules for reaction mechanisms |
| The invention and classification of vocables and prakriyā rules are purely abstract exercises, their only object being to explain the observed reality | The Lewis structures for compounds and rules for mechanisms are purely abstract exercises, their only object being to explain the observed reality (including the intermediates formed) |
