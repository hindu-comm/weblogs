# Operation Holi: The Fateh Shibji Paltan’s Revenge at Hussainiwala

### In March 1956, a little-known border clash flared along the banks of the Sutlej at Hussainiwala, where a battalion of the erstwhile Jammu and Kashmir State Forces wrought havoc on Pakistani ranks.

The Fateh Shibji Battalion or 4 JAKRIF was among the most distinguished units of the former Jammu & Kashmir State Forces, tracing its lineage back to General Zorawar Singh’s campaigns, where it earned glory by capturing the famed Mantalai flag. Even in the Second World War, the battalion saw service on the Burma Front (1944) earning high acclaim for its gallantry. Its Commanding Officer, Lt. Col. Narain Singh Sambyal, received the OBE, while the unit as a whole amassed an impressive array of honours — one MBE, five MCs, one OBI (1st Class), nine Military Medals, one IDSM, and twenty Mentions in Despatches.

However, during the Pakistani invasion of Kashmir (1947–48), the battalion suffered a severe setback when its Muslim companies overcome by religious fanaticism deserted to join the invaders and turned on their former comrades. As a result this betrayal the battalion was badly mauled and virtually disintegrated. It thus in a way had to be “re-raised” in 1949 at Pathankot by Col. Prakash Chand, with Lt. Labh Singh’s company forming its nucleus.

The memories of that betrayal left the men of the Fateh Shibji eager to settle old scores with the Pakistanis. Their long-awaited opportunity finally presented itself in March 1956, when, during collective training at Ferozepur, the battalion (then under Lt Col Vakil Singh) received orders to move to Hussainiwala Headworks and assume its protection from the 7 Border Scouts.

### **Background of the Dispute**

In early 1947 the Radcliffe Commission had been appointed to deal with the disputes arising out of the partition of Punjab. In the dispute on Hussainiwala Headwork on the Sutlej River, the Headwork and the area immediately around, including the Bund and the banks on the Pakistan side was allotted to India. This award was, however, not acceptable to Pakistan, who disputed the allotment of the Bund to India. Pending settlement of the dispute it had been agreed upon by the two Governments that while the responsibility for repair and maintenance of the Bund would continue to be with that of the Indian Union, no troops would be located.

However, during the unprecedented floods at the close of 1955 the right guide wall of the Headwork got damaged and the Indian Government was obliged to carry out the necessary repairs so that the water could be channelled into the Dipalpur canal - a feeder supplying water to the territory in Pakistan. Under this obligation Indian engineers started work on the Bund but when they started lifting earth for this purpose from the ‘Bella’ adjacent to the Bund, the Pakistanis objected and began to use military force to prevent Indian labour from undertaking this task.

The ‘Bella’ was a stretch of land about 350 metres in length and approximately 25 metres in width, extending laterally alongside the right guide bund of the Headwork, on the far side of the river. It has sandy soil and is covered with thick elephant grass. As per the Radcliffe award the ‘Bella’ belonged to India but it was argued by Pakistan that the dispute over the Bund automatically covered the adjacent ‘Bella’.

When on 23 January 1956, a group of six labourers was sent to the Bund for repairs the Pakistan Border Police refused to allow them to proceed, stating that only joint patrols were permitted. This was a departure from the usual norms since Pakistani authorities had never objected to canal authorities conducting inspections and repairs before.

Despite the matter being raised with Pakistani authorities at the highest level, in February they again hindered repair work with the Pakistani Border Police issuing threats when a small quantity of Sarkanda (Elephant Grass) was cut from the Bella on 27th February.

Therefore the following day, the GOC Eastern Punjab Area, Major General Gurbaksh Singh visited the site and was briefed on the situation. In a conference at Flag Staff House, Ferozepore, with Mr. Talwar, Superintending Engineer (Canals), and the Brigade Commander (167 Brigade Area) the GOC emphasized that

> *“the Bund and the Bella were Indian territory and it was within our
> rights to do any work we wanted on them. The question of referring
> this matter to higher authorities should not have arisen as it was
> within the jurisdiction of the local commander.”*

Orders were issued to the Brigade Commander, Brig MM Badshah to ensure protection for the labourers, enabling canal authorities to proceed with work on Bella the next day. The Superintending Engineer agreed, and detailed protection plans were made. Unfortunately the very next morning Mr. Talwar informed Brig Badshah that no labour could be sent to Bella as the matter was now with the higher authorities, who had decided not to proceed until a government decision was received.

Brig Badshah informed the GOC of the situation, and the matter was relayed to Headquarters Western Command seeking permission to reaffirm India’s claim on Bella and the Bund. On 3rd March 1956, the Brigade Major of 167 Brigade Area was dispatched to Army Headquarters with a detailed sketch of the Hussainiwala area and an update on recent developments for the DMO. Instructions to go ahead were subsequently issued via phone by the BGS Western Command on 6 March 1956 and confirmed in writing on 10 March 1956.

Meanwhile, increased activity and troop movements were observed in Pakistan’s forward positions, with reports emerging through civil traffic that a Brigade Group had concentrated in the Kasur-Gandasinghwala area. On the Indian side detailed reconnaissance and planning had already begun on 29th February itself when the matter was escalated to the Western Command.

Orders were issued to prepare for the operation by moving troops across the river to Bella under the cover of darkness. The objective was to secretly reinforce the garrison at Ghatti and fortify perimeter positions, as no reinforcements would be possible once the action started. Adequate mortar and artillery support was also arranged for localities across the river, and additional MMG posts were established at Ghatti to cover the dead ground between the Right Guide Bund and the Pakistani tower position.

To counter any nighttime enemy actions, the troops at both localities were issued a “liberal” supply of grenades. Strict fire discipline was enforced, given the challenges of ammunition resupply and to avoid revealing troop positions or strength. Additionally, provisions were made to stock localities for 15 days, allowing for sustained defense in case of a prolonged engagement.

The instructions also included orders for the bridgehead to be extended to the tower position, providing depth in case of enemy pressure, with orders for anti-tank cover to be provided for both the forward localities and the main bridge. The entire Operation was code named “Holi”.

### **Operation Holi**

Since repairs to the Headworks were in progress at the time, the gates had been lifted to allow the riverbed upstream to dry. Under these conditions, it was neither feasible to ferry the troops across by boats nor was the riverbed fordable owing to the mud, marsh, and the presence of intermittent channels.

Accordingly, on the night of 8/9th March 1956 the code word “Radha” was issued as the troops from ‘B’ and ‘C Companies of the Fateh Shibjis under Captains Ajit Singh VrC (a Veteran of the Siege of Skardu in 1947) and Hoshnak Singh began moving in to occupy the Bella. Advancing along the main bridge up to the Right Divide Wall, they proceeded over the Divide Wall itself with the three-foot parapets flanking the wall providing them cover as they crawled forward. The sounds of their movements were masked by **pumping sets that** had been run every night beforehand, their mechanical din blending seamlessly into the nocturnal routine.

To prevent the soldiers from being silhouetted against the night sky while crossing the bridge and the Divide Wall, the gates were raised to their maximum height, casting a deep shadow that concealed their advance. In addition, all lights on the Headworks had been extinguished at irregular intervals in the preceding days, a calculated measure to lull the Pakistani forces into a sense of complacency.

Beyond the Divide Wall, a sandbag ramp constructed by the Irrigation Department led down to the riverbed. From there, the troops traversed a dam and a temporary bridge, also erected by the department, spanning a narrow channel that separated the Bella from the mainland. The code word “Fateh” (Victory) was transmitted back to signal the completion of the first phase of the Operation.

### The Interlude

The following morning, an Indian officer was dispatched to the Joint Check Post (JCP) to meet the Pakistani detachment commander and inform him about India’s occupation of the Bella area. The officer explained that this step had been taken solely to ensure the safety of Indian labourers who had refused to work without adequate protection. It was reiterated that India had no intention of violating the 1950 Agreement.

`NOTE: Tactically it would have been advisable to occupy the Bund itself but the Battalion had strict instructions not to do so in view of the earlier agreement between the two Governments of keeping the Bund free of troops. For this same reason when ‘D’ and ‘A’ Companies under Major Mahel Singh and Major Rashpal Singh respectively were pushed out to the far bank, they also took up positions in the low-lying areas between the bank of the river and the perimeter Bund to the left of the Headwork. Both these localities of the Battalion could not support each other mutually due to the lie of the ground and the intervening Headwork.`

The Pakistani detachment commander assured the Indian officer that Pakistan had no objection to the removal of sand from Bella and further stated that they would not interfere with the labourers’ work. He therefore suggested that Indian troops could be withdrawn. The Indian officer, however, replied that such a withdrawal was not possible, as the labourers remained unwilling to continue their work without proper protection. To this, the Pakistani DSP helplessly responded, “What can I do?” and the matter was left at that.

From 9 March 1956 onward, work at Bella proceeded smoothly, followed by steady progress on the bund from 11 March 1956 till the 17th. However all was not normal. Reports by OPs from 10th March onwards continued to point towards considerable vehicular activity in the area of Gandasinghwala.

On 11th March, around 1230 hours, a Pakistani NCO was observed waving his hand, following which two Pakistani platoons about twenty men each advanced with one of them taking position behind a heap of stones along the right guide bund. This was immediately countered by an Indian platoon which occupied defensive positions opposite the heap. What followed was a three hour face off, before the Pakistani detachment finally withdrew. The manoeuvre was aimed at intimidating the civilian labourers, but the workers, showing commendable calm and discipline, continued their tasks undeterred.

Three days later on 14th March, a Pakistani Border Police Sub-Inspector approached an Indian JCO at Bella, questioning the Indian presence. The JCO firmly responded that Bella lay within Indian territory and its occupation was fully justified. Soon after, a Pakistani Border Rifles platoon advanced toward the bund. But on being challenged by the Indian sentry they withdrew to about 100 yards, remaining there until nightfall.

At 1430 hours, the Pakistani Border Police opened fire on Indian positions at Muthianwala Gajjal and Jugian Nur Mohammed. The Commandant PAP, sought assistance from GOC Eastern Punjab Area and Gen Gurbaksh Singh assured him that two companies of Border Scouts stood ready to repel any intrusion into Indian territory.

The very next day, a Pakistani Major General was spotted alongside a Brigadier, a Colonel and three officers observing Indian positions from the Pipal Post for about ten minutes at 1245 hours. The following night on the 16th of March, six-blade graders were deployed on the Pakistani side in an apparent attempt to mask the movement of their military transport. They began continuous work in the Dipalpur Canal bed non-stop until 0010 hours on 17 March 1956 (coinciding with the time indicated in intelligence messages for the completion of troop concentration on the Pakistani side that night).

Intercepted messages further confirmed that something was amiss, reporting the concentration of a brigade and a field regiment on the Pakistani side.

The intercept on 17 March 1956 at 2125 hrs had more detailed instructions

> *“One Coy BALUCH and same strength of 14 PUNJAB will concentrate in
> area ULLAKE (not confirmed) by 0010 hrs. All first line amn will be
> carried. Mor Pl will take position on the target mentioned in the
> conference with immediate effect. Disputed bund still occupied by
> Indian troops. All info regarding movements will be communicated on
> line being laid shortly. 10 Inf Div and 7 Inf Div ready for
> reinforcement. Arty Regt will be ready for operation at half an hour
> notice . GANDA SINGH WALA will be occupied by a Sqn If necessary,
> Listening watch to be kept on wireless.”*

Simultaneously, civilians from Kasur onwards up to the Joint Check Post were brought blindfolded in closed police vans, to conceal the extent of troop concentration in the area. Attempts at reconnaissance by four Pakistani officers were again reported on the 17th.

In light of these developments, Indian forces anticipated the intended Pakistani action and consequently heightened their vigilance.

### **The Battle at Hussainiwala**

On the night of 18 March at 2130 hours, a Pakistani force approximately a battalion strong (two companies formed up according to Gen Thorat) advanced from the direction of Pipal Post, moving parallel to and along the Bund towards the Nose. In a clear violation of the existing agreement between the two countries, the Pakistani troops attempted to gain a tactical advantage by occupying the Bund. Establishing a light machine gun post at the nose of the right guide bund which assured them of covering fire they began converging upon the Indian positions in the Bella.

The 4th Battalion of J&K Infantry, however, was under strict orders not to open fire unless fired upon, regardless of the provocation. In the past the Indian garrison resorted to deception by deliberately displaying only two sections near the Bund whenever a joint patrol visited the area restricting all troop movement to nights. This ruse led Pakistanis to believe that the Bella, a difficult and unapproachable feature, was lightly held by Indian troops just a platoon strong.

Deceived by this impression, the Pakistani troops advanced unhindered to a dangerously close distance, from where they began hurling taunts and abuse at the Indian defenders. Mistaking the defender’s silence and restraint for weakness, and grossly underestimating the strength of the garrison, the Pakistani soldiers went in for the assault.

The Fateh Shibs were on the other hand waiting for a fight to be able to wreak revenge for what they had suffered in 1947. A hail of machine gun fire and grenades greeted the Pakistani attackers followed by fifteen minutes of bitter hand to hand fighting, where the Pakistanis finding things too hot beat an unceremonious retreat, leaving behind eight of their dead.

### Combat at the Joint Check Post

Almost simultaneously, with the detonation of the first grenade at Bella (likely the prearranged signal for the attack) the Pakistani sentry at the Joint Check Post attempted to bayonet the Indian sentry. The latter however displaying remarkable presence of mind deftly parried the thrust and retaliated by swiftly pulling out a grenade from his pocket and hurling it into the Pakistani guard’s tent. Taking advantage of the confusion that followed, the Indian sentry successfully withdrew to his position at the bridge. It was later confirmed that his action resulted in the deaths of two Pakistani guards.

### Indian Assault on the Bund

The Pakistanis having fired first the Fateh Shibs were now free to go on to the offensive and as a first step they decided to improve their tactical position by evicting the Pakistanis from the Bund and occupying it themselves.

However, their advance was held up by the Light Machine Gun (LMG) nest established by Pakistani forces at the nose of the position. From its vantage point, the enemy swept the Bella, the right guide bund, and the boat point on the left guide bund across the river with continuous bursts. Under such intense fire, it became exceedingly difficult for Indian troops not only to launch an assault but even to operate boats essential for resupplying ammunition, evacuating the wounded, and bringing up reserves.

At this critical juncture, volunteers were called for to silence the Pakistani LMG nest. The name of L/NK Sunder Singh, who was stationed with the platoon holding the central sector of the bund and had a reputation for exceptional bravery during the 1947 operations, was proposed.

Sunder Singh readily volunteered without hesitation. Armed with six hand grenades, he crawled nearly one hundred yards across the fire-swept bund towards the enemy position, and then another fifty yards through Pakistani-held territory, scaling a pile of stones along the right bund. Closing in on the enemy position he hurled his grenades with precision, completely silencing the LMG nest and killing all three Pakistani soldiers manning it.

*President Rajendra Prasad decorating Hav Sunder Singh with the Ashok Chakra, Class I on 26 January 1957 \[Source: Kashmir (February 1957), Issue No 2, Vol 7\]*

Not content with merely destroying the post, Sunder Singh recovered the enemy’s light machine gun, two Bren magazine boxes, and fourteen Bren gun magazines which he brought back with him. Returning to company headquarters, he reported the fulfillment of his mission before going back to the enemy position three separate times and bringing back the corpses of the fallen Pakistanis.

This done, the ‘B’ and ‘C Companies charged the enemy positions on the Bund, and after dislodging him from there, occupied it themselves after inflicting heavy casualties on the Pakistani troops. The Indian medium machine gun section positioned at Ghatti was also ordered to open fire. Laying down a devastating barrage over the area behind the RT Guide Bund towards the Pakistani side, it managed to effectively disrupt the movement of Pakistani reserves attempting to reinforce the front.

The Pakistani side also resorted to MMG and mortar fire throughout the night. The bridge itself was under heavy fire and, as such, could not be used. Pakistani mortar fire was not returned by own troops on specific orders of the GOC that unless the Pakistani fire was effective, own mortars will not be brought into use.

### **Ceasefires and Betrayals**

At 2215 hours, the GSO I of Pakistan’s 10 Inf Division Lt Col Mahmud Jan contacted the Commander of India’s 167 Brigade, Brig MM Badshah requesting a ceasefire and a meeting at the Joint Check Post within half an hour. This was followed by a personal call from General Azam Khan, GOC 10 Infantry Division (Pakistan) to Maj Gen Gurbaksh Singh, GOC EP Area at 2230 hours, complaining that Indian troops had opened fire at Hussainiwala and demanding that they be ordered to cease fire immediately. Maj Gen Gurbaksh Singh responded that according to his information, it was the Pakistani troops who had initiated the engagement by attacking Indian positions at the Bella. He suggested that Gen Azam Khan first instruct his troops to halt firing. Both commanders then mutually agreed to an immediate ceasefire. Gen Gurbaksh Singh subsequently directed Brig M.M. Badshah to cease fire once the firing from the Pakistani side had stopped.

However at 2300 hours, while Indian troops had ceased firing, sporadic fire from the Pakistani side continued. Brigadier M.M. Badshah therefore suggested to General Gurbaksh Singh that both sides should agree on a clear ceasefire signal.

Accordingly at 2315 hours, Gen Gurbaksh Singh contacted Gen Azam Khan, informing him that India had stopped firing while Pakistan’s fire persisted. He proposed the following measures:  
(a) Both sides to fire three green Verey lights after warning their troops, as a signal for ceasefire.  
(b) No troop movement during the night.  
(c) Brigade Commanders from both sides to meet at 1200 hours on 19 March 1956 to settle the issue.

The proposal was accepted by both commanders. At 2330 hours, Gen Gurbaksh Singh ordered the 167 Brigade to implement these instructions. Fifteen minutes later, Indian troops fired the three green Verey lights, but Pakistan did not reciprocate and continued firing with MMGs and mortars.

Subsequently at 0015 hours, General Gurbaksh again telephoned the GOC 10 Infantry Division, Lahore, pointing out that India had fired the ceasefire signal while Pakistan had not, and firing from their side was still ongoing. The Pakistani commander made excuses claiming his telephone lines were cut and that the Border Police might lack Verey lights, which were being fetched from the rear. He also alleged that Indian troops were still firing a charge firmly denied by Gen Gurbaksh, who reiterated that India had ceased fire after firing the lights. Unsatisfied, Gen Azam Khan nevertheless requested that India issue ceasefire orders once again.

Far from refraining from hostilities, just 45 minutes after Gen Gurbaksh’s call the Pakistani troops went on to launch a counterattack. The Fateh Shibs met the assault with equal ferocity, inflicting heavy casualties and forcing the attackers to retreat in disarray. The relentless exchange of fire set aflame the elephant grass on the Pakistani side. Carried by a gentle breeze, the flames spread swiftly through the camp, engulfing large sections and claiming the lives of Pakistani soldiers, including those not directly engaged in combat. 15 charred bodies were subsequently recovered after the cessation of hostilities.

While the counter attack was being beaten back, Maj Gen **Gurbaksh Singh rang up** Maj Gen **Azam Khan** at 0105 hrs and threatened to withdraw ceasefire orders given that Pakistani troops had launched an attack and no *verey lights* had yet been fired by their side. Azam Khan denied any Pakistani attack, but promised to enforce the cease fire orders and signal compliance. He also alleged that it was the Indian side that continued firing and proposed a meeting at the JCP which was declined given the prior agreement had already decided on a meeting at 1200 hrs.

Finally at 0215 hours, Pakistan fired its ceasefire signal (three green Verey lights) despite this the Pakistani troops continued firing through the night with LMGs, MMGs, and 3-inch mortars maintaining heavy fire over the river bridge. At 0310 hours, reinforcements in the form of one company of 3 Assam were ferried across the river to Bella. Between 0345 and 0400 hours, a series of telephone exchanges took place between the two GOCs and Brig Badshah, marked by mutual claims and counterclaims but to no avail.

### The Final Gamble

Around 7 a.m., Pakistan intensified its bombardment with the first light aiding the accuracy of its mortars, which had until then been largely ineffective. General Gurbaksh Singh, who had personally flown in to Ferozepore, took stock of the situation and authorized Indian mortars to launch a counter-bombardment to silence the enemy. Besides a 4.2-inch mortar battery, all battalion 3-inch mortars had been placed under Captain Labh Singh of the Fateh Shibji. The officer had earlier distinguished himself in the 1947 war where he had helped evacuate thousands of refugees and fought a running battle with Pakistani forces.

At 7:30 a.m., General Azam Khan requested Brigadier M. M. Badshah to cease fire and proposed a meeting at the Joint Check Post at 9:00 a.m., which was declined as the Brigadier was due to confer with Gen Gurbaksh Singh.

Around 8:00 a.m., Pakistani troops were again seen concentrating for an attack. Therefore around 8:55, Indian 4.2-inch and 3-inch mortars unleashed a devastating barrage, striking the enemy’s forming-up area with such intensity that the assault was abandoned before it could even begin. Following this, Pakistani mortar fire ceased, and only sporadic firing continued. According to Pakistani claims the Indian bombardment killed ten and managed to wound 18 of their troops.

At 10 AM the ADC to Gen Azam sent the following message to Brig MM Badshah with firing from both sides dying down by 1030 hrs

> “Gen AZAM will be available at the JCP at any time that Gen GURBAKHSH
> SINGH is able to meet him. Please tell him as soon as he comes to
> FEROZEPORE. If you cannot get JCP, ring me at LAHORE Mil 79”

A meeting finally took place between the two sides at 1200 hrs and ended at 1405 hrs. (The discussions that took place are beyond the scope of this article)

### **The Cost of Victory**

The fighting at Hussainiwala had lasted nearly 14 hours with the Pakistanis suffering heavy casualties. Maj. Brahma Singh, in his *History of the Jammu and Kashmir Rifles*, estimates that the 4th Jammu & Kashmir Infantry inflicted nearly 500 Pakistani casualties, with over 90 bodies recovered near the forward positions and subsequently handed over to Pakistan following the ceasefire.

The Indian High Commission in Pakistan reported the following

> “Though Pakistan has admitted only 10 dead, our information, collected
> from various Military Hospital sources also, is that the casualties
> suffered by the Pak Border Police and the Military Forces that went to
> their support include only 23 dead, 18 seriously injured and 68
> injured.”

While another Intelligence report put the casualties at 55 killed and over 220 wounded. The most accurate testimony however is probably that of an Indian officer who visited the scene of casualties as per the ceasefire agreement and got the Pakistani bodies removed. The Battalion’s victory came at a heavy cost with four brave soldiers, Naik Baldev Singh and Sepoys Sudan Singh, Faqir Chand, and Dharam Singh, laying down their lives while twenty-four others were wounded.

[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!saDo!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1e952700-a740-4131-8769-ac9cffa59f88_777x584.jpeg)

![Image](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!saDo!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1e952700-a740-4131-8769-ac9cffa59f88_777x584.jpeg "Image")

Lance Naik Sunder Singh’s fearless leadership earned him the Ashok Chakra Class I while Naik Parshotam Singh and Sepoy Pritam Singh were awarded the Sena Medal for eliminating enemy sentries in a surprise attack. Naik Mukhtiar Singh though grievously wounded by an enemy grenade which he had attempted to throw back to protect his section, continued to inspire his men and was honoured with the Ashok Chakra Class II. While Sepoy Janak Singh, though severely wounded in the arm, manned the platoon’s only working light machine gun for twenty minutes and forced the enemy to retreat, refusing evacuation until ordered for which he was awarded the Ashok Chakra Class III. The Commanding officer Lieutenant Colonel Vakil Singh too received a Commendation Card from the Chief of the Army Staff for his exemplary leadership.

In addition the battalion captured one light machine gun, six rifles, one sten gun besides other war like stores.

The Bella incident of March 1956 thus stands as a shining yet overlooked chapter in India’s post-Independence military history. Against superior odds the Fateh Shibji Battalion (4 JAK Rifles) reaffirmed the honour of its lineage and defended the sovereignty of the nation.
