The *Manusmṛti* has become the most vilified of Hindu texts. In recent history it has been burned, ridiculed, and misrepresented. Its very name today conjures the specter of “oppression,” not through intrinsic fault, but through the violence of mistranslation and the hermeneutics of suspicion imposed upon it by colonial and liberal scholarship. From William Jones onwards, Western Indology reduced the Dharmaśāstras to juridical instruments, stripping them of their cosmological and transcendent meaning. In this mutilated form, Manu was presented not as the lawgiver of Dharma but as the alleged architect of casteist tyranny.

It is in this climate of distortion that Nithin Sridhar’s *Catuḥ Śloki Manusmṛti* is both timely and necessary. Sridhar approaches the text according to its own *telos*, resisting the revisionist tendencies seen even in much of the non-left when faced with seemingly unsavoury verses concerning women and śūdras. He restores to the text its rightful dignity as a *pramāṇa-śāstra*.

The book is divided into two Sections.

Section I

The **first chapter** situates the *Manusmṛti* within the Hindu textual universe: addressing questions of authorship, the epistemological grounding of Śāstra as *śabda-pramāṇa*, the proper approach towards Dharmaśāstras, and their subject matter. Sridhar makes the case for the Bhṛgu recension as the authentic transmission, challenging the atomistic theories of Bühler, Doniger, and other Indologists who treat the text as a patchwork. Against their conjectures and interpolations, Sridhar invokes the authority of tradition itself: the *Mahābhārata*, the Purāṇas, and other smṛtis, particularly the *Nāradasmṛti*. He demonstrates that Dharmaśāstra follows the same organic law of transmission as Vedānta or Kāmaśāstra—through abridgement, expansion, and condensation—without losing its essence.

The **second chapter** opens with the criticisms frequently leveled against Hindu textual traditions, particularly Dharmaśāstra. These include the claim that Hinduism is not a “book-based” religion like the Abrahamic faiths, that Hindus should not rely on texts to guide their lives, or that smṛtis are peripheral compared to the Vedas or the Gītā. A common apologia also maintains that problematic verses regarding women or śūdras are later interpolations by malicious brāhmaṇas, or that Manu was never highly esteemed and was imposed only by the British through codification of Hindu law. This constitutes the *pūrvapakṣa*.

To respond, Sridhar takes a long arc, beginning with the definition of Dharmaśāstra and its place among the eighteen *vidyās*. He then turns to what is arguably the most crucial section of the book, especially given the lack of foundational knowledge among Hindus today: Hindu epistemology. Sridhar explains how the *āstika darśanas* accept *śabda-pramāṇa* as a valid source of knowledge and why the *Manusmṛti* and other Dharmaśāstras qualify as such, drawing arguments from Nyāya and Mīmāṃsā. He also discusses the procedure to follow when conflicts arise between *śruti* and *smṛti*.

The function of the smṛtis as *pramāṇa-śāstras*, Sridhar notes, is to reveal knowledge of Dharma—particularly the connection between karma and karmaphala. Dharmaśāstras address three principal domains: *ācāra* (practice), *vyavahāra* (jurisprudence), and *prāyaścitta* (expiation). While they provided the ethical and theoretical foundations of law and bore relevance in legal matters, they were never “law books” in the biblical sense. Instead, they articulate the first principles of Dharma, which are eternal.

After establishing Dharmaśāstra as a valid source of knowledge, the **third chapter** turns to the definition of Dharma itself and the means by which one acquires authentic knowledge of it. Of the six *pramāṇas* recognized in Hindu epistemology—*pratyakṣa* (perception), *anumāna* (inference), *upamāna* (comparison), *arthāpatti* (postulation), *anupalabdhi* (non-perception), and *śabda* (word)—only *śabda-pramāṇa* yields valid knowledge of Dharma. This knowledge is gained through śāstra and the instruction of the guru. Sridhar supports this with a verse from the *Bhagavad Gītā*, where Bhagavān Kṛṣṇa declares that śāstra is the authority in deciding what should and should not be done. Citing Rāmānujācārya, Sridhar notes that “śāstra” here refers broadly to the Vedas, Dharmaśāstras, Itihāsas, and Purāṇas.

From this foundation, he establishes the authority of the smṛtis—including the *Manusmṛti*—as valid sources of Dharma. He then emphasizes why *śraddhā* (faith) is essential in approaching a text, rather than adopting the hermeneutics of suspicion. He explains the role of *śravaṇa-catuṣṭaya*: *śravaṇa* (listening to the enunciation of a text from a qualified teacher), *manana* (intellectual reflection), *nididhyāsana* (deep one-pointed contemplation of essential truths), and *sakshatkara*(realization of those truths). A practical example is provided in his treatment of two controversial verses on women (5.146 and 9.3), showing how they can be understood cosmologically, temporally, teleologically, functionally, and relationally.

Section II

The **second section** of the book presents Sridhar’s commentary on the first four verses of the *Manusmṛti*. At first sight, it may surprise readers that he chose to focus on just four verses out of over 2,600. Yet this choice is profoundly traditional. Just as the *catuḥsūtrī* of the Brahma Sūtras encapsulates the essence of Vedānta, or the *catuḥ-śloki Bhāgavata* condenses the theology of the *Bhāgavata Purāṇa*, so too do the opening verses of the *Manusmṛti* encapsulate the metaphysical and ethical framework of Dharmaśāstra.

These verses articulate the *anubandha-catuṣṭaya* (fourfold connections):

1.  *Adhikārī* – the qualified student eligible to study śāstra

2.  *Viṣaya* – the subject matter of the text

3.  *Prayojana* – the fruit or end of the undertaking

4.  *Sambandha* – the relationship among the three

Sridhar carefully unpacks these terms, citing verses from diverse texts and the commentaries of Ācāryas such as Śaṅkara. He also addresses contentious issues concerning women, śūdras, and individuals of mixed varṇa. He clarifies that the prohibition on studying śāstra applies narrowly to direct study of the Vedas and the *Manusmṛti*, not to learning about Dharma more generally or to acquiring knowledge of one’s responsibilities. The prohibition also does not extend to other disciplines, such as grammar. Sridhar demonstrates that the *Manusmṛti* lays down Dharma for all four varṇas as well as for the *antarprabhavas* (those born of mixed varṇa) making them adhikaris, vishaya being Dharma and the fruit being moksha & fulfillment of purusharthas.

Sridhar clarifies that the *Manusmṛti* does not propose a modern, egalitarian utopia, but rests on a realist view of human diversity of temperament, capacity, disposition, circumstance, and karmic inheritance. Rather than erasing difference, it provides frameworks for each individual to pursue svadharma and the four puruṣārthas, thus fostering both personal and social wellbeing. In this dhārmika vision, what modern discourse calls “inequity” is not a flaw to be corrected by enforced sameness, but a condition within which each person, guided by svabhāva, may progress toward liberation. The verse thereby becomes a springboard for reflection on dharma, karma, justice, and the enduring relevance of śāstrika knowledge, whose validity rests in intrinsic truth, not contingent acceptance.

Following the commentary, **Appendix I** undertakes a comprehensive study of varṇa, offering four interpretive perspectives:

1.  Varṇa as a classification of individual *svabhāva*

2.  Varṇa as a division of human activity

3.  Varṇa as divine emergence

4.  Varṇa as a ritual state

**Appendix II** addresses a revisionist interpretation of *antarprabhava* in *Manusmṛti* 1.2 by Dr. Surendra Kumar, a Sanskrit scholar affiliated with the Arya Samaj. Dr. Kumar interprets *antarprabhava* as referring to the āśramas of life, rather than—as traditionally understood—to those born of mixed varṇas. Sridhar refutes all six of Kumar’s arguments, citing *Manusmṛti* itself. He shows how such revisionist attempts, motivated by discomfort with caste or meat prescriptions, distort the text. This refutation is an important defense of Hindu epistemes against both prejudice and apologia.

**Appendix III** concludes with a Sanskrit *stuti* to Svāyambhuva Manu composed by Kuśāgra Aniket.

All in all, Catuḥ Śloki Manusmṛti stands as a valuable addition to the growing body of Indic scholarship that seeks to recover our civilizational inheritance on its own terms. Sridhar’s work reminds us that the Dharmaśāstras are not merely historical curiosities but living repositories of wisdom, sustaining principles that continue to speak to the deepest questions of human life. The study of Dharma, approached through the categories of śruti and smṛti, of pramāṇa and puruṣārtha, restores to us a vision of order where action, responsibility, and transcendence are inseparably bound together.

In our own time, when public discourse often drifts without anchor, the recovery of Dharmaśāstra provides a framework that is both enduring and dynamic. It shows how life may be oriented toward mokṣa while remaining grounded in the obligations of artha and kāma, and how svadharma, far from being a constraint, is the pathway through which individuality finds fulfilment in service of the cosmic whole. To return to these texts with reverence and philosophical seriousness is to reawaken the civilizational memory of India, and with it, the resources needed for cultural renewal.

Sridhar’s commentary on the opening verses of the Manusmṛti exemplifies this task. By elucidating the eternal principles contained in these ślokas, he opens a gateway into the wider world of Dharmaśāstra, encouraging us to see in it not a relic of the past but a perennial source of guidance. His work thus contributes to the broader effort of rekindling traditional learning, and of situating contemporary concerns—whether moral, social, or ecological—within the larger epistemological and theological vision of Hindu thought. For those committed to conserving and deepening our traditions, this book is both timely and necessary.

### About the author

Nithin Sridhar is Director of the INDICA Centre for Moksha Studies, Mysuru, where his work focuses on Hindu philosophy with an emphasis on Vedānta. He is the author of seven books and one book chapter, including *Menstruation Across Cultures: A Historical Perspective*, *Samanya Dharma*, and *Isopaniṣad: An English Commentary*. His most recent publication, *Catuḥ Śloki Manusmṛti*, offers a commentary on the opening verses of the *Manusmṛti* as an entry point into the Dharmaśāstra tradition. In addition to his writings, he contributes to public discourse on religion, culture, and philosophy, and teaches Indian Knowledge System subjects at INDICA Courses.
