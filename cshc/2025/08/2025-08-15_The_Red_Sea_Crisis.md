# The Red Sea Crisis

### Indian Tactical Response And The Way Forward

The Houthis have taught the world an important lesson; freedom of navigation is a crucial part of the global supply chain. Termed the 'Red Sea Crisis', Houthi militia were able to blockade the Bab-el-Mandeb strait, rendering the vital shipping lane unnavigable for ships wishing to transit through the Suez Canal to reach Europe and vice versa. Officially, the Houthis claimed to have targeted only American flagged vessels destined for Israel, aiming to prevent the flow of arms and ammunition. However, the Houthis don't posses any credible method of discrimination and positive identification. Hence many ships, some with no link to Israel whatsoever, have been targeted. As a result insurance premiums have skyrocketed, with many insurers outrightly refusing to insure ships transiting through the strait. The bulk majority of ships are taking the long route through the Cape of Good Hope, taking us back to 1868 the year before the Suez opened. In response the United States launched Operation Prosperity Guardian in a bid to restore freedom of navigation through the strait. The European Union launched its own operation independent from the American effort named Operation Aspides. The US operation saw the US Navy's Fifth and Third Fleets being deployed to the Middle East. Carrier Strike Groups 2, 3, 9 and 8 successively rotated to provide 24/7 maritime security to vessels in the region. Each CSG was centred around an aircraft carrier, with a cruiser and around four to six destroyers. However, even with such a massive naval presence, the success of Prosperity Guardian is debatable. The operation has failed to effectively protect merchant vessels, with dozens of ships getting targeted and hit, the last of which being claimed by the Houthis on 27 December, 2024 on the Danish flagged vessel Santa Ursula. India, to protect her interests, launched her own independent patrols in the region. Consequently, Indian efforts were named Operation Sankalp. The Indian naval presence in the region has been measured and contained in nature. Contrasting the Americans, the Indian Navy didn't aim for show of force, rather the response was focused on the core objective of providing security and responding to distress calls of stranded ships hit by Houthi missiles. Somalia has long history of been a nation known for piracy. In recent years, due to a joint international effort by conducting anti-piracy patrols along the Horn of Africa, piracy in the region almost grounded to a halt. However, with the international effort focused on countering the Houthi vector, Somali pirates sensed an opening and quickly sprung into action. The first Indian success came with the successful rescue of MV Ruen from Somali pirates. MV Ruen was close to the Somali coast when on 15th December 2023, it became the first vessel hijacked by Somali pirates since 2017. The Indian response was swift once distress calls were received. INS Kochi continuously shadowed the vessel in a 'wait and watch' stance with the Spanish and Japanese Navy taking temporary positions. However, after the ship owner company gave its accent to a ship boarding operation, Indian MARCOS commandos prepared an assault plan. Finally on 16th March 2024, nearly three months after being hijacked, INS Kolkata intercepted the Ruen and the assault commenced. IAF C-17s in coordination with the destroyer dropped inflatable boats and two MARCOS teams to rescue the sailors. The operation was an astounding success, with no loss of life and 35 pirates captured.

A similar incident occurred on 29th January 2024, when FV Al-Neemi, an Iranian fishing vessel with a Pakistani crew were hijacked. INS Sumitra, a Saryu-class patrol ship, deployed the Dhruv helicopter as a show of force to psychologically weaken the pirates' morale. Finally when INS Sumitra approached the fishing boat and fired warning shots, the pirates threw their weapons overboard. A short while after, a MARCOS boarding party sanitised the boat and took the pirates into custody. This operation highlighted the effectiveness of psychological operations in the maritime domain. A simple helicopter flyby, coupled with warning shots, compelled the pirates to surrender, and demonstrated that such novel tactics can be used in future anti-piracy operations. An even amusing incident had earlier occurred on the 4th of January, when pirates who had hijacked MV Lila Norfolk, abandoned the vessel at the mere sight of INS Chennai approaching in the distance. When the MARCOS team boarded the vessel and carried out a search, there was no sight of a single pirate. Apart from the above piracy related incidents, many ships were victims of drone and missile attacks, India has assisted multiple merchant ships, such as the MV True Confidence, MSC Sky II and MV Andromeda Star. Ships reported fires and temporary loss of engine power after getting hit by Houthi AShMs. The Navy's specialized firefighting and explosive ordnance disposal teams boarded damaged vessels, extinguished fires, conducted residual risk assessments, and evacuated crew members, many of whom were Indian merchant matiners, into life-rafts or safe lifeboats. All rescued personnel received critical medical aid and were transported to nearby ports including Djibouti. These efforts underscore India's non-coalition, independent maritime security role in protecting vital shipping lanes. By the end of 2023, a total of 41 warships have been deployed by the Indian Navy to provide escort services for 503 Indian flagged merchant vessels. However, the earliest of these incidents happened on 23rd December 2023, when MV Chem Pluto carrying crude oil en route from Jubail, Saudi Arabia to New Mangalore Port, India was struck by an anti-ship missile (AShM) or drone. Indian Coast Guard deployed its OPV, the ICGS Vikram, to render emergency assistance, later a destroyer and P-8 Poseidon were deployed to escort the vessel. The United States Department of Defence claimed that Iran was responsible for the attack, an allegation which Iran denies. In unofficial circles, there were rumours of the possibility of a Pakistani submarine responsible for the attack. A forensic analysis claimed to have found parts of Iranian Shaheed drones, however the attacker was never definitively identified. In the coming weeks Indian Navy deployed a range of vessels including INS Chennai, INS Kochi, INS Kolkata and the then newly commissioned INS Mormugao to patrol the waters around vital shipping lanes to prevent any such attacks in the future. PM Modi had a talk with Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman and Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu regarding security issues in the middle east.

The above incidents highlight the importance of anti-piracy operations as a crucial method of ensuring freedom of navigation and uninterrupted flow of commerce. Crucial lessons must be learned and operating procedures updated to keep up with the changing environment. The invaluable role of MARCOS has been demonstrated in Operation Sankalp, a testament to the rigorous training and operational capabilities of the Navy's elite unit. However, there are challenges ahead. Given that the US Navy couldn't stop the Houthi attacks in its entirety highlighted that using naval power alone to prevent a determined aggressor from dealing damage remains an elusive concept. An entire Carrier Strike Group, armed with a plethora of weapons at its disposal, failed to cripple Houthi shore batteries. More so, US Navy lost an F/A-18 in a friendly fire incident, reminding us of the complexities of conducting naval warfare. If this is the situation when the Americans are not involved in a direct and total war, possessing overwhelming air and naval-superiority, we can only imagine what the picture would look like when all bets are off. In my opinion, the losses would have been higher.

The United States struggled to counter increasingly frequent, low-cost Houthi strikes on Red Sea shipping due to both operational strain and technical shortfalls. Arleigh Burke-class destroyers like USS Gravely and USS Carney have expended hundreds of high-end Standard Missiles (SM-2, SM-6, and SM-3), each costing between \$2-30 million, to intercept inexpensive drones and anti-ship missiles an unsustainable cost-exchange ratio. Limited missile capacity and the inability to reload Vertical Launch System (VLS) cells at sea mean ships risk running dry amid high-tempo engagements. Environmental conditions such as narrow, shallow waters and atmospheric interference make radar performance unpredictable, forcing frequent Aegis Combat System updates and radar configuration tweaks tailored to Houthi capabilities. Intelligence gaps further hinder effectiveness: the Navy lacked comprehensive data on Houthi arsenals and infrastructure, complicating assessments of target degradation or ongoing threat levels. These issues together highlight a tactical and strategic challenge: defending vital maritime lanes at prohibitive cost while working to adapt doctrine, sensors, and munitions supply chains for sustainability.

From the Indian perspective, its absolutely crucial that we structure our Navy in such a manner that we can ensure the security of our merchant ships even during a war against our adversaries. Although we posses a decisive numerical advantage against the Pakistani Navy, the numbers when it comes to battle might not be the same, given that a number of ships would be assigned to merchant protection roles. Kolkata-class or Visakhapatnam-class destroyers equipped with Barak-8 SAMs, BrahMos missiles, and MF-STAR AESA radars provide all round coverage. However, these should be supported by Shivalik-class frigates, Kamorta-class ASW corvettes, and fleet tankers like INS Deepak for logistical endurance. Integration of recon drones and MARCOS teams embarked for VBSS operations, would enhance asymmetric response. This layered force ensures sustained presence and rapid reaction in contested maritime zones. To augment its capabilities against asymmetric maritime threats, the Indian Navy is inducting several advanced platforms which are under construction. Under Project 17A, stealth frigates such as INS Himgiri and INS Taragiri will enhance area surveillance, anti-submarine warfare (ASW), and multirole strike capabilities. The upcoming Next Generation Corvettes (NGC) will bolster littoral operations with advanced ASW sensors and weapons. These vessels, featuring modular design, superior radar cross-section reduction, and network-centric warfare integration, are central to India's strategy of deploying leaner yet more lethal task forces in high-threat zones like the Red Sea and Western Indian Ocean.
