In a [previous essay](https://open.substack.com/pub/cshc/p/navigating-the-nuances-of-hindutva?utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web) , I considered some of the practical divergences between the modern Hindutva movement and the traditionalist strand within Hindu society. That exploration highlighted tensions around caste, priesthood, dietary injunctions, and the interpretation of Shastras. Yet those disagreements are only the visible surface of a deeper philosophical divide.

Beneath the surface lies a more fundamental question: *what does it mean to revive a civilization?* Hindutva and Traditionalism offer different answers. Both claim to work for Hindu resurgence, yet their starting points, their languages of justification, and their horizons of meaning diverge in important ways.

This essay seeks to explore that divergence.

## **The Two Logics of Revival**

The Hindutva movement emerged in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, shaped by the turbulence of colonial rule and the global rise of nationalist ideologies. Its founders, most notably V.D. Savarkar, articulated a vision in which “Hindu” ceased to be merely a religious or sectarian designation and became a comprehensive cultural–civilizational identity. In this vision, the Hindu people constituted a nation with a shared history, a common sacred geography, and a collective destiny.

This is the **logic of political consolidation**. It emphasizes unity above division, identity above diversity, and accessibility above exclusivity. Ritual differences, doctrinal disputes, and caste barriers are viewed less as sacrosanct truths than as hindrances to collective strength. Hindutva, therefore, tends toward a flattening of distinctions, reinterpreting or minimizing them where necessary to preserve a coherent sense of national unity.

Traditionalism, by contrast, works according to the **logic of continuity**. For the traditionalist, the task of revival does not lie in constructing a new political identity but in maintaining fidelity to the dharmic order as revealed in the Shastras and transmitted through parampara. What is sanatana does not need to be reinvented, only safeguarded.

Thus, while Hindutva regards “Hindu” primarily as a mobilizing identity, Traditionalism regards it as an inheritance rooted in divine authority. Both claim to serve the civilization, but they do so from different vantage points: the one with an eye to political survival, the other with an ear to metaphysical fidelity.

### **The Question of Modernity**

The most striking difference between these two positions lies in their respective relationships to modernity.

Hindutva is unmistakably a modern ideology. Its categories of nation, majority, minority, equality, identity are drawn from the political vocabulary of the 19th and 20th centuries. Its preoccupation with unity and mass mobilization is indebted to the democratic ethos, even if the movement is not itself democratic in the liberal sense. Hindutva is thus a modern attempt to recast a pre-modern civilization in terms compatible with the modern nation-state.

Traditionalism resists this translation. It is not a project of modernization but of preservation. It does not seek to reframe dharma in the idioms of modern political theory, but to maintain it as the ordering principle of life, in continuity with past generations. Hierarchy, ritual distinctions, and adhikara are not perceived as social evils to be reformed but as features of a divinely ordained structure that confers meaning and stability.

The philosophical question that arises is therefore profound: *can a civilization whose self-understanding has historically been ordered by dharma be adequately preserved by a modern ideology such as nationalism?* Hindutva answers in the affirmative, holding that without political consolidation, survival itself is at risk. Traditionalism hesitates, warning that survival at the cost of distortion may not truly be survival at all.

### **Sources of Authority**

Every social order requires a principle of authority. In this respect, Hindutva and Traditionalism again diverge.

Hindutva tends to situate authority in the collective will of the Hindu people, mobilized as a nation. The legitimacy of change lies in its capacity to strengthen unity. If, for example, the acceptance of female priests or the dilution of caste boundaries serves the larger cause of solidarity, Hindutva is inclined to endorse it. Authority, here, derives from the perceived necessities of survival and the consent of the mobilized majority.

Traditionalism locates authority elsewhere: in Shastra and in the gurus, acharyas, and mathas who interpret it. Decisions about ritual, dietary practices, or the functioning of caste are not to be settled by political expediency but by fidelity to revealed order. Authority derives not from numerical strength but from continuity with sacred knowledge.

This contrast produces a philosophical question: *is Hindu society to be ordered by majority opinion and national need, or by scriptural authority and parampara?* The answer given by Hindutva emphasizes survival through adaptation; the answer given by Traditionalism emphasizes truth through fidelity.

#### **The Politics of Dharma and the Dharma of Politics**

Another way of framing the distinction is to ask whether politics should guide dharma, or dharma should guide politics.

For Hindutva, dharma often becomes a tool of politics. Its value lies in its capacity to unify and inspire. Certain restrictions may be relaxed, certain injunctions reinterpreted, if such steps serve the goal of consolidating Hindu society against external threats.

For Traditionalism, politics must itself be dharmic. The model is that of rajadharma, where political authority operates within the framework set by the sacred order. Politics is judged by dharma, not the other way around.

In practical terms, this means that Hindutva subordinates eternal truths to temporal needs, while Traditionalism sub-ordinates temporal strategies to eternal truths.

## **Points of Tension**

From these different logics, a number of tensions follow.

1.  **On Equality**

    - Hindutva embraces an egalitarian rhetoric, arguing for the
      dilution or abolition of caste distinctions in favor of a shared
      Hindu identity.

    - Traditionalism accepts hierarchy as natural and integral,
      expressed through varna, ashrama, and adhikara.

2.  **On Universality and Particularity**

    - Hindutva tends toward homogenization, constructing “Hindu” as a
      broad category that smooths over local variations.

    - Traditionalism cherishes particularity: the plurality of
      sampradayas, local rituals, and temple traditions.

3.  **On History and Time**

    - Hindutva frames Hinduism in historical terms, as a civilization
      that must defend itself and assert itself in time.

    - Traditionalism treats Hinduism as *sanatana*—timeless, cosmic,
      beyond the contingencies of history.

4.  **On the Sacred and the Cultural**

    - Hindutva often interprets Hinduism as culture, identity, and
      heritage.

    - Traditionalism insists that it is first and foremost sacred,
      ordered by divine revelation.

These are not minor differences of policy but deeper divergences in outlook, shaping how each side conceives of Hindu revival itself.

## **Possible Futures**

Where might these two paths lead? Several scenarios can be envisaged.

1.  **Subsumption**  
    Traditionalists may be politically sidelined, continuing their work
    within ashrams and mathas while Hindutva dominates public life. This
    scenario already reflects much of the present reality.

2.  **Parallel Existence**  
    A distinct traditionalist movement could persist alongside Hindutva,
    occasionally offering critique but largely avoiding direct
    competition. Historical precedents, such as the Ram Rajya Parishad
    of Karpatri Maharaj, illustrate this possibility.

3.  **Synthesis**  
    A middle way might be found, in which Hindutva grounds its reformist
    impulses within the language and authority of Shastra, developing a
    more conservative form of politics that unites modern need with
    traditional legitimacy.

4.  **Continued Divergence**  
    The most probable outcome may be an ongoing tension, with Hindutva
    pursuing unity through adaptation and Traditionalism preserving
    order through fidelity, each aware of the other but rarely
    converging.

The contrast between Hindutva and Traditionalism lies in their first principles. Hindutva mobilizes Hindu identity for political consolidation, often bending Dharma to the requirements of modern nationalism. Traditionalism preserves Dharma as a sacred inheritance, judging politics by its conformity to timeless principles. Philosophically, the two represent different logics of revival.

And yet, to dwell only on division would be misleading. In practice, Hindu society has shown again and again that these currents can coexist, overlap, and even strengthen one another. This cooperation arises from a shared recognition of common adversaries — Islamist radicalism, aggressive missionary networks, and the many shades of the political left that seek to delegitimize Hindu identity and practice. In the face of such inimical forces, both Traditionalists and Hindutvavadis must perceive that internal debates, however necessary, must not prevent the formation of a united front.

At the same time, one must acknowledge the subaltern Hindutva element that has, in recent years, grown stronger. This current born from castes and communities long alienated from the custodianship of tradition has added a sharper, more defiant register to Hindutva politics. It was always present in some form, but its assertiveness today raises new questions: will it push Hindutva away from conciliatory engagement with Traditionalists, or will it eventually find a modus vivendi within the broader civilizational project? The answer to this will shape the tone of future dialogue.

Thus, the relationship between the two should not be imagined as one of absolute rivalry, but rather as a continuous conversation — a conversation that allows for critique, for the reaffirmation of principle, and for the correction of excess, while still preserving the possibility of standing shoulder to shoulder in moments of collective trial. The online world may amplify their divergences into sharp polemics, but on the ground, Hindu society continues to demonstrate that the preservation of dharmic life requires both fidelity to tradition and the political pragmatism of organized mobilization.

Another way to frame this relationship is as one between **idealism and realism**. Traditionalism, with its insistence on Dharma as an eternal standard, embodies the idealism without which revival loses its soul. Hindutva, with its emphasis on mass mobilization, negotiation, and political strength, represents the realism without which revival lacks the means to survive. Alone, either risks distortion — idealism lapsing into irrelevance, realism into opportunism. Together, they offer the possibility of a synthesis: a Hindu revival at once principled and effective, anchored in the eternal yet alive to the urgencies of the present.

Perhaps the ultimate question is not whether Hindutva or Traditionalism will prevail, but whether they can learn to sustain this rhythm of philosophical divergence and political convergence. If so, then their union — however uneasy — might ensure that Hindu identity and Hindu life are defended, while also being deepened and enriched by the constant dialogue between modern mobilization and eternal Dharma.
