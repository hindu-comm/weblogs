Since last year especially after the takeover of the online platform “twitter” by Elon musk. There has been an exponential rise of hate speech, blatant racism against the Indians in almost every aspect of the internet. Once confined to the dungeons of 4chan and some other fringe online websites, racism against Indians has steadily become a norm even among the generic and so-called “normie” crowd of our society. Several slurs have become known and have spread across the entire arena of the internet. Some people have called it as a backlash against the supposed takeover of the internet by Indians who have become an influential part of it since the rise of Jio and subsequent reduction in the prices of the data packs. While others have described it as a well thought plan curated by enemies of the Indian state to undermine the safety, security and wellbeing of Indians whether they live abroad or in the subcontinent. One thing which is the most important aspect of all this discussion is the people's ability to voice such opinions on the internet.

The “Freedom of Speech” or the right which is conferred to the people to voice their beliefs without any sort of backlash or reprisal has been one of the most influential theories of modern polity. Keeping this in my mind, I will be writing a series of articles starting from an elaborate account of how this theory came into being, what were the thoughts & circumstances which worked behind the scenes for the thinkers to develop it and in the final article I will be discussing how this theory has evolved and moulded itself in the Indian context. This is the first article which mainly will talk about the historical development of the idea and the role of the enlightenment thinkers in shaping and creating the first structures of this theory.

# Idea of Freedom of Speech

Over a period of centuries the idea that people should have the ability to voice their opinions in public have been part of a heated contest among the philosophical, political discussions and discourse. We have heard the legend of Socrates drinking poison for his beliefs, inquisitors who were the official arm of the catholic church for rooting out the hearsay and many stories of people who were prosecuted and sometimes executed for their held beliefs. Therefore, it becomes imperative to do a brief survey of some of the important figures who in their ability and mind, within their limited historical & cultural reasons were able to put forward their thoughts and develop this philosophical concept.

# Traditions of Discussions

Here is a brief and short overview of the history of public discussions in Ancient India and Ancient Europe ( namely *Athens* and *Rome*). In my opinion, the idea that people should be free enough to voice their thoughts is rooted in these debates where intellectuals subconsciously realised their need and this was an unwarranted experiment, one one of its kind, in which scholars took part whole heartedly, and over a period of time a very rudimentary idea of “Freedom of Speech” originated.

## India

Looking through the ancient Hindu traditions, one notices a healthy, though limited culture of debate and discussions. Mentioned in puranas and some other hindu texts, an ancient tradition of intellectual debate and discussions “*shastrartha”* developed over a period of centuries, mainly in the before common era period, where intellectuals in different part of the land, with their own rules and regulations participated in these discussions, to propound their own understanding of the meanings of the scriptures or “shastras”. In doing so, they debated with other scholars about the meaning of life, creation of the universe, human’s existence, ultimate reality, salvation etc. as far as i know, there were three types of *shastrartha:-*

1.  *Vadakatha*

    Here, *rishis* debated on the meanings and interpretations of texts
    and tried to approach the truth

2.  *Jalpakatha*

    Here, the main aim was to defeat the opponent, rather than to
    discover the truth

3.  *Vitandakatha*

    Here, the person does not present their own beliefs but only tries
    to refute the opponent’s position.

Different schools of thoughts profoundly expressed their views through these tools, voiced their own understanding. This tradition was one of the major reasons for development of philosophy in ancient India which enriched and widened its large literature. In the non-vedic traditions, we hear of Buddha travelling through the ganga valley, discussing and winning over people, making them his disciples, of Mahavira propounding his philosophy openly and many other sects who even influenced Kings and the high class of Indian society. In the political sphere, however, it was very much limited to only a few areas and groups like Licchavis in the ganga valley who were able to develop their own tradition of Republicanism.

## Ancient Athens and Rome

In Europe, it was Athens and Rome which developed and built the foundations of modern democracy. Historians have argued that Athens was one of the first examples, though with its limitation, of democracy. For some, it is the birthplace of democracy. Rome, especially, Roman republic though more conservative, was able to develop its own understanding of limited democracy. Discussions and debates were part of their political life, their assemblies were an arena of fierce verbal battles and constant word exchange which scrutinised every political development, policies, treaties but even the lives of their leaders. We hear how Roman assembly transformed into fragmented groups on the question of Caesar's ambitions, over Pompei’s rule. In the sense, at least in the political sphere, the discourse of discussions and debate was far more developed in comparison to ancient India where it was largely confined to religious and philosophical life albeit some oligarchies like Licchavis came up in Ganga valley as well.

The Roman Republic’s concept of Libertas or Freedom, was an important part of its political culture. Though, it would be a stretch to define it under the concept of “Freedom of Speech”, it however was one of the first experiments which was able to develop the ideas of legal and political freedoms. Citizens were given rights to participate in courts and assemblies, speak on political matters, give their opinions and influence the working of their Republic. Political speech, for example, was central to the working of the Roman Senate.

While, the Roman Republic was very much restricted and allow only few areas of openness and discussions, Athenian democracy was one of the first examples of the direct democracy where male citizens had the right to participate in the discussions in the public assembly or *Ekklesia* where all the decisions were made in front of the citizens regarding wars, electing officials, taxes etc. It was known as *Isegoria* or the right to speak in public assembly. Historians have described it as one of the first examples of a fundamental right given by a state. So important was this right for the citizens of Athens, when it was abolished by the oligarchic coups of 411BCE and 404BCE, it became a rally idea for the Democratic exiles who fought back and restored it. It is here we are able to find perhaps one of the first examples of the importance of free speech in political life. Funeral Oration, a speech given by statesman Pericles, when Athens was grappling with Peloponnesian War to honor the first soldiers who died in the battle. Here, he highlighted the Athenian values of freedom, openness and tolerance.

Another concept which became prominent in Athens was *parrhesia- “*freedom to speak freely” which was heavily used and incorporated by philosophers, playwrights and public figures. One of the key aspects of this idea was the liberty it gave to the citizens. While, in the case of *Isegoria*, the debates were limited to the political sphere, it extended far beyond and delved into the topics of arts, philosophies and social aspects. At Least from the theoretical perspective, a citizen could speak openly about any topic he wanted to without fear or any reprisal. One of the most celebrated users of it was Socrates who challenged the ethics and norms of the Athenian society.

While, the above basic discussion might paint a rosy and somewhat translucent picture of their society, one has to understand the limitations and various restrictions which were present in the ancient period. In the case of Ancient India as I have noted before, the culture of debate and discussion was largely confined to the religious and philosophical spheres, it was not totally ignorant to conflicts and violence. Though, it is highly controversial to actually say accurately that there were instances of state oppression, on the sect level, we hear of many conflicts and violent skirmishes. Romila Thapar, in her “ Asoka and the Decline of Mauryas” mentions about the riot which broke out in one of the holy caves of Patna among the monks and it required Asoka intervention to cool it down. There are frequent mentions of such scenarios in all the religious texts of Indic religion.

In Rome, over a period of time, especially after the rise of the empire, freedom to speak got more restricted and people were tried and executed for insulting the emperor, blasphemy etc. In Athens, the idea of *Isegoria* and *parrhesia* were tools of the wealthy and elite males of the society. The idea of being a citizen was not extended to women, slaves. The poor generally were unheard and their concerns and problems rarely came up in the discussions of *Ekklesia.* Socrates was tried and poisoned for the crime of corrupting the youth. Therefore, the idea of Free Speech in its modern sense was far from its conception.

# Era of Enlightenment

After the early days of the limited democracy and ideas which defined the theories of philosophies in the ancient period, Europe fell to the religions of islam and Christianity. In some parts of Europe, Christianity prevailed since its introduction while in some areas there was intense and brutal battle between Islam and Christianity for supremacy over Europe. By the beginning of the middle ages, the Catholic Church became the dominant institutional power which governed almost all aspects of society. This system of governance stayed all powerful for almost a 1000 years, remarkably changing the cultural, political and societal portrait of Europe. For some it was an age of gradually positive changes which enriched the European heritage and for others it was an age which destroyed the very foundations which made Europe and gave its identity. In the 14th century, Europe encountered perhaps its biggest and the most brutal adversary, the black death, which ravaged through it for 4-5 years, demolished its society, reducing its population by some accounts, almost half of what it was in the beginning. This tragedy shook the very foundations of Catholicism, common people found their faith shattered and their belief in their religion & current status of society was severely weakened. Over a period of time, scientific advancement, declining control of catholic church, reformation movements, growth of urban centres, development of printing press, age of renaissance and all the new theories associated with cumulated into an intellectual movement which today’s historians have named *“An age of Enlightenment*”.

An impartial and ignorant outsider who may have never heard about the lives of Athens and Rome will find absolutely no whatsoever similarity between these two periods. Yet, the thinkers of enlightenment saw them as their own heritage and built their ideas on the principles which were first voiced in the walls of ancient assemblies.

Enlightenment philosophers emphasized on reason over blindly following tradition, celebrated and propagated the ideas which developed through empiricism and science and They took their inspirations on liberty, freedom etc. from the discussions and debates of statesmen, expanded them and provided the first seeds for the Individual rights and Liberty in modern sense.

In this next section, we are going to explore the first thinkers who inspired the people of their own time and which greatly influenced the ideas of free speech. Due to the sheer volume of thinkers and their ideas, it would be hard to condense them to a few authors due to the limits of my own time and to avoid any sort of injustice to their ideas.Therefore, I would like to leave out thinkers like Francis Bacon whose works on empiricism and scientific method influenced many upcoming generations. I would strictly stay in the lane of the philosophers who provided one of the first understanding on the nature of the relationship between a man and the state, how they affect each other and how they can shape the life of human beings. The philosophers I will like to restrict myself would be - an elaborated account on John Locke as his ideas were the one which pioneered in developing the idea of freedom of speech and of Thomas Hobbes, whose idea of Leviathan is totally contrast to that of Locke & then a very brief account of Jean-Jack Rousseau and Voltaire.

## Thomas Hobbes

Perhaps one of the first thinkers who accurately was able to understand the relationship between the state and humans. Hobbes wrote one of the most influential books, “Leviathan” in 1651 after closely observing the havoc of the English Civil War or Great rebellion as some have called it. Hobbes' core ideas rest on the fundamental nature of man which he describes as revolving around “principles of three quarrel”. According to him, “ competition, diffidence and glory” are the three characteristics which control a man's action. He propounds the idea that so long there is not a neutral third party who can act as an impartial adjudicator and an effective deterrence, which he called as the “*state of nature*”, human life would be “ *solitary, poor, nasty, Brutish and short*”. In that scenario, there would be no peace and security - only a perpetual state of fear which would automatically create a state of security dilemma, a scenario which is called a “*Hobbesian trap*” in the perspective of international relations.

In order to overcome this “state of nature”, Hobbes proposes a need for a social contract where individuals willingly surrender their natural rights to a sovereign authority. This sovereign authority, which Hobbes called *Leviathan*, namesake for a demonic sea monster mentioned in the biblical legends. According to Hobbes, this sovereign body must have the absolute power and ability to prevent any collapse of the society which can descend it back to the “*state of nature*”. In doing so, he emphasises that it is the stability of the Leviathan that should be of utmost importance in order to preserve these social contract & order. Therefore, Hobbes rejected any notion of dissent or criticism of the state and thoroughly rejected any idea of providing the state subjects any sort of freedom of speech in the public sphere. At the same time, Hobbes curiously tries to distinguish between the aspects of Freedom of thought & Freedom of Speech. For Hobbes, *internal liberty* - people can think freely was moral and he saw no harm in it but, for him, *external Liberty* - people's ability to voice their opinion in the public must be restricted to maintain the peace. Therefore, he endorses the views of the Catholic Church & the crown, criticises the reform movements and finds them a danger to the peace.

One can quite clearly see the circumstances and the background which Hobbes was trying to understand and make his way through. His thoughts mostly laid emphasis on avoiding any sort of repetition of the tragedies of the English Civil War. His way meant to tolerate the sovereign authority and in doing so, he somehow undercounts the generic limit of Human tolerance in enduring cruelty and injustice. Contrasting to this John Locke’s ideas were far more sympathetic to the concerns of the people and provided a more balanced theory.

## John Locke

Born in 1632, raised during the English civil war. John Locke saw the tumultuous period of the English polity which saw the execution of king Charles I and the glorious revolution. During his studies, he was inspired by the works of Descartes which drove him to experimental philosophy and eventually he became a member of the Royal Society. Though, initially not interested in any political arena, he eventually became involved in the political life of the English society and after an encouragement from his friend Lord Ashley(1st Earl of Shaftesbury), he composed his one of the most celebrated work “Two treatises of Government” which was published anonymously by him in 1689. In this book, Locke propounded his beliefs and gave one of the first descriptions of rights in the modern sense. The core philosophy of John Locke revolves around what came to be known as “‘the natural rights”. The rights which are so intrinsic to human existence that they are conferred by the gods themselves not by the government. They are essential for a healthy and whole development of a human being. The rights which cannot be taken away by the despotic powers of the king or the divine powers of the church. Locke exclaims in his book “*Every man has a property in his own person… The labour of his body, and the work of his hands, we may say, are properly his”.* This belief or idea that a man is owner of his own self and body highlights the thinking of John Locke where he emphasises a man’s individuality, his freedom of his thoughts and actions. In this work, Locke introduced the concept of freedom of conscience, a tolerance for religion and autonomy of a man. Perhaps, the greatest section of this work was his critique of despotism and the divine rights of kings. He argued against patriarchalism and implied that the government can only exist and work if there is popular support for it. There exists a social contract between the governed and government where the governed expects the government to ensure that their rights such as liberty and of property remain secured and protected. In violation of the contract by the government, Locke believes people are morally reasonable to resist and if possible overthrow a tyrannical government. This theory, one of the first of its kind, was extremely revolutionary for its time. Locke not only rejected the divine nature of the king, he questioned his authority, his right which for up until then was bestowed to him since the day he was born. Locke’s theory demanded to hold the rulers accountable but also underscore that the sovereignty rests on people not on rulers. The main motive of his theory was to prevent tyranny and associated misery which has the ability to hinder the growth of the society. In order to prevent such tyranny, Locke expounded the usage of law and highlighted the need for the rule of law. As the rule of the law, he explained, is one of the best defenses against the arbitrary decisions of the state and the best weapon to restrict its powers. This belief can be summarised in the phrase “*wherever law ends, tyranny begins” of his book.*

Perhaps the book which explicitly touches upon the concept of “free speech” is Locke’s *A letter concerning toleration* which was published in 1689 in which argued for the religious toleration and took a contrary stand to Hobbes who though, believed that individuals can maintain their own beliefs as long as they support those held by the state. In opposition to Hobbes' ideas, Locke strongly stated that the state has no right to interfere with religious beliefs. Phrases like “ *The care of the souls is not committed to the civil magistrate*” touches upon a very new development which wants a clear distinction between the state and the religion. He argues that one cannot develop a genuine belief by force by remarking “ No man can be forced to be saved” and the people should be allowed to follow their own thoughts in the religious arena. He underscored the need for toleration which was essential for peace and religious truth to flourish, he highlights ““*All the life and power of true religion consist in the inward and full persuasion of the mind; and faith is not faith without believing”* For locke, a person compelled to “believe” something through fear and force is not real faith and will only act in detrimental to the faith itself. For him, faith must come from within, from his heart, from his own conviction, from his own rational reason. It should be his conscience and his conscience who should be the most important reason for someone to believe in some faith.

Locke played a philosophical guide to almost all the enlightenment thinkers. His ideas and beliefs helped in creating the deep understanding of modern polity and the laws & their concepts revolving around the political discourse.

## Voltaire

A somewhat eccentric lad as he was seen by his contemporaries, Voltaire is perhaps the best known enlightenment philosopher in India. He is best known for his positive and extremely hearty remarks about India, the respect he showed for her civilization and the ancient tradition. Voltaire remarks “*I am convinced that everything has come to us from India: mathematics, astronomy, metempsychosis, etc... We owe everything to India”* in his “*Essai sur les mœurs et l’esprit des nations” (Essay on the Customs and Spirit of Nations)”* which was published in 1756. Born as Francois Arouet in 1694 to a relatively well off family, he had always been a person for whom following the orders of the authority was a rather obscure affair. While he was in Paris, he was imprisoned twice,in the Bastille nonetheless, which later on became the first victim of the revolution. It was during his stay in Bastille, when he adopted the name ‘Voltaire’. Later, he was exiled in 1726 after he started a foul rumor about the Duke of Orleans, it is during this exile Voltaire transformed his thinking and ideas especially after stay in England. He was greatly influenced by the works of Newton and Locke. He wrote “ *Letters on the English*” in 1734, where talks about his travels through England and was able to create a very decent detailed account of the English society. Voltaire carefully observes almost every arm of the English society, their laws, their institutions and then, develops a very sharp comparison of English society with French. He praises Englishmen for their progress in science, their constitutional monarchy and curiously also religious tolerance. he was greatly impressed by Issac Newton who he praises remarking “*Newton has demonstrated that the motion of the planets, the ebb and flow of the sea, the fall of heavy bodies, all depend on one universal principle*.” One of the key things which Voltaire finds so profoundly interesting is the contrast between Newton’s empiricism and Descartes’ Rationalism. He calls Newton the symbol of rational mind, introduces his ideas to the French people where he strongly presses on the apparent shortcomings of Descartes philosophy and the superiority of Newton’s Ideas. Later on with the help of his friend, Emilie du Chatelet, who translated Newton’s Principia into French, he published “Elements of Newton’s Philosophy” to create a wider reach for Newton's ideas.

Apart from his role in popularising rational thinking and empiricism, Voltaire is perhaps best known for writing in response to the execution of Jean Calas, a protestant who was accused of murdering his own son to debar him from converting to Catholicism. William Doyle, in his book, Oxford History of French Revolution, highlights that it was not just the bigotry of the judges which angered Voltaire but also the cruel and monstrous punishment which was awarded to him. He was broken on the wheel, a brutal torture in which the punished limbs are smashed by iron bars and later the body is raised for a public display on a cartwheel. Brutal punishment of Damiens, condemned and later executed for regicide was still fresh in mind. Voltaire in his work, “Treatise on Tolerance”, builds up his ideas on theories which John Locke propounded in his works. He remarks “*Tolerance has never provoked a civil war, intolerance has covered the earth with carnage”.* He emphasises that no religion has a monopoly on truth and that such prosecutions contradicts the very spirit of Christianity. He argues for religious freedom, a need for justice and legal reforms, a need for more humane and dignified treatment of the man. Later on, his work “ *Philosophical dictionary*” published in 1764, took a more harsh and satirical stance and increased his attacks on superstitions, despotism, and fanaticism. Through his writings, he was able to inspire a generation of polemicists in France, as noted by William Doyle, where started the tradition of critically analysing every aspect of the state and society.

## Jean Jack Rousseau

Born in 1712, Rosseau is our last scholar to be discussed in this essay. Rousseau has been the most celebrated Enlightenment thinker of our time. Among the marxist circles, he holds a special place for his critique of social inequality and private property. Some scholars have regarded him as the proto-socialist. The core of Rousseau's philosophy revolves around Human Nature and the State of Nature. In contrast to Hobbes, who regards Human nature as the primary reason for a need of a state, Rousseau argues that Humans are naturally good and compassionate but its existence within the society which corrupts him. In his work “*Discourse on the Origin and Basis of Inequality among Men*” he expands on the idea of “state of nature” in which he describes as a peaceful and solitary existence where man is guided by basic needs and compassion. He develops the concept of “noble savage” i.e humans living in primitive conditions, untouched by the civilisation are inherently good and noble. It is the civil society which has corrupted his noble soul and has bred inequality, conflicts. The most radical thought which was developed by Rousseau was published in his work “The Social Contract” in which remarks “ Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains”, he criticises the institutions of the society and the state by comparing them to chains which have confined people in obscure existence and have capsulated their freedom and corrupted their human goodness. These chains are social inequality, artificial hierarchy, institutions which have collectively contributed to the loss of autonomy and will. Rosseau implies that it is primitive life which is corrupt free and expands on his concept of “noble savage”. Rousseau admits the impossibility to go back to that primitive state, therefore, proposes a different solution in terms of a “Social Contract” which will transform the society to just and free again. The social contract which Rousseau proposes is that individuals should come together to form a collective body called “volonte generale” or “general will”. Each person will discard his own individual rights to the community as a whole and become part of it. he remarks “ “*Each of us puts his person and all his power in common under the supreme direction of the general will”* where he underscore that the individual interests in the collective won’t be any of any basis and it is the collective which will represent the truest interests of all. he contrasts the individual will which according to him is largely driven by selfish interests and therefore, must be seen inferior and unnecessary in comparison to the General will which represent the common good and what is best for the society as a whole. He highlights strongly that not only a person gains a moral freedom but instead of losing his liberty, he will ascend to a new higher collective form.

A defender of public discussions and expression of ideas he strongly emphasises their needs, they are part of the general will and its civic importance. He introduced the idea of civic religion in which he argues that a set of civic beliefs and sentiments are imperative in order to create a social cohesion and promote good citizenship. Hence, the need for a set of rules for general will’s protection.

Though ideas of Rousseau and Hobbes totally opposite of each other in its inception, surprisingly meet at a curious point for similar reasons. For Rousseau, the existence of general will is far more important than individual right to express his views and therefore, he remarks ““*No one shall be free to break the contract, to deny the sovereignty of the people, or to incite others to do so*” i.e any speech which undermines the general will not be allowed at all. This is similar to the idea of Hobbes, who remarks that there should be a total obedience to the laws of the state in order to preserve the order and stability of the state.

Ideas of Rousseau have found their places among almost all the modern thinkers and statesmen.He was an important piece of the era of Enlightenment. So influential he was that his writings were embraced by the revolutionaries of French Revolution and became the slogans for the overthrow of the French monarchy.

Above authors had varied views about the nature of man and its understanding of society. Hobbes was able to accurately understand the dark side of a man and how his self interest drives him to do the evil deed without any remorse therefore, it is imperative to have an all powerful authority to check him. Rousseau in contrast to Hobbes finds man as an innocent creature corrupted by the greed and ambitions which were forced upon him by the state. Therefore, asks for building a collective will to “free” him from this life of corruption. At the same time, like Hobbes, he is against the idea of man’s unchecked individual freedom. Both Hobbes and Rousseau stressed upon the imperativeness of existence of Leviathan and general will for the man’s well being.

Locke and Voltaire were more sympathetic to the idea of an evil sovereign all powerful authority who has the ability to harm humans if it remains unchecked. Therefore, they wanted a strong and vibrant culture of criticism of the governments and the officials albeit within their own limitations (For example, Locke was against atheism who he found unethical).

By the end of the 18th century, enough debates and discussions had been conducted, a lot of ideas had been circulated that the foundations of a modern sense of “ Freedom of Speech” had been built which reached its climax in the thesis of John Stuart Mill. Mill published his “ On Liberty” in 1859 which has become the most significant text and a keystone in this philosophy.
