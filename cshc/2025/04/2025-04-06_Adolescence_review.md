# Adolescence review

### Too Busy With Political signalling, Adolescence Misses the Plot on Incel Subculture

Netflix’s four-part series Adolescence has been praised for its ambitious single-take cinematography and strong performances, but its glowing reception among liberal circles obscures deeper flaws. The show attempts to tackle the toxic allure of online radicalization and Incel culture through the lens of a troubled teenage boy, yet it ultimately falls into the same trap as much of the mainstream discourse on masculinity: reducing a complex social crisis to a mere morality tale. Rather than offering a sincere exploration of what drives young men toward extremism, the series settles for reaffirming liberal talking points - blaming bad actors like Andrew Tate while ignoring the systemic alienation that fuels such movements.

1.  **Oversimplification of a Complex Problem**

Adolescence follows Jamie Miller, a 13-year-old accused of murdering his classmate Katie after she labels him an “Incel” for his romantic rejection. The series positions itself as a progressive indictment of online misogyny, a stance applauded by critics. However, this framing oversimplifies the issue, attributing Jamie’s actions solely to his exposure to harmful online content. It’s a tendency to reduce complex social pathologies to simple moral binaries. By focusing on individual resentment rather than structural alienation, Adolescence avoids confronting the uncomfortable truths about economic anxiety, the lack of clear societal roles and the erosion of religious structures which used to be a source of meaning and identity for young men.

The series depicts Incel subculture as a mere fringe online phenomenon, detached from the broader socio-economic realities that make such ideologies appealing. This mirrors a common narrative that attributes far-right radicalization to "bad actors" while ignoring the underlying conditions that create fertile ground for extremist ideas to take root.

2.  **Taxi Driver: A Portrait of Systemic Abandonment**

Martin Scorsese’s Taxi Driver (1976) offers a stark contrast, providing a far more nuanced exploration of male alienation. The film refuses to disentangle Travis Bickle’s descent into madness from the decay of 1970s New York.

*“All the animals come out at night: whores, skunk pussies, buggers, queens, fairies, dopers, junkies... sick, venal. Someday, a real rain will come and wash all this scum off the streets.”*

Travis’s isolation, disillusionment, and desperate search for purpose are not simply individual failings but reflections of a society that has failed its working class. His taxi becomes a symbol of his isolation, transporting him through a city teeming with societal ills.

“*Each night when I return the cab to the garage, I have to clean the cum off the back seat. Some nights, I clean off the blood.*”

His violent actions, while reprehensible, are presented as the culmination of systemic neglect and a deep-seated longing for meaning. In contrast, Adolescence locates Jamie’s radicalization solely in his online consumption, neglecting the material conditions that make such rhetoric so seductive. The series fails to highlight the role that economic anxieties and lack of opportunity play in driving men toward extremist ideologies.

3.  **The Vacuum of Meaning**

Adolescence puts the blame solely on figures like Andrew Tate as catalysts in the radicalization process.

However, it fails to fully examine the societal vacuum that allows such figures to thrive. Tate's rise to prominence is not merely a story of algorithmic manipulation; it's a reflection of a generation starved for clear guidance and positive role models. His brand of hypermasculinity and simplistic worldview offers a perverse sense of belonging, filling the void left by the decline of traditional institutions and the absence of clear societal expectations for young men.

Young men are often searching for someone to tell them how to be. If the progressive left offers only spiritual lesbianism to young men, while figures like Tate provide a clear, albeit toxic, roadmap, it's little wonder that many young men are drawn to the latter. The left's reluctance to engage with questions of masculinity, combined with its focus on individual expression over collective identity, has created a space for figures like Tate to flourish. They offer a sense of belonging, a clear set of rules, and a narrative of male empowerment that, however distorted, resonates with young men who feel lost and adrift.
