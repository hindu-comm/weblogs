# Socialism in the Indian Constitution

### The Constituent Assembly’s Perception of Socialism

The word ‘socialist’ was inserted into the preamble of the Indian Constitution by the 42nd amendment. Hence, people assumed that, since the word ‘socialist’ was not in the Constitution before the 42nd Amendment, the members of the Constituent Assembly were not socialist. However, the evidence suggests otherwise. This article aims to offer a critical examination of the influence of socialist ideals on the Indian Constitution’s founding principles and the implications for contemporary governance. The Indian Constitution is not the work of a single person. It was drafted in multiple stages. Let us briefly understand how the Constitution was formulated.\[1\]The Constituent Assembly was founded in December 1946. Its work formally commenced when Nehru moved the Objective resolution on 13 December 1946. It then proceeded to appoint several committees to deal with different spects of the Constitutional problem. Several of these had as their Chairman either Pandit Jawaharlal Nehru or Sardar Patel to whom thus goes the credit for the fundamentals of our Constitution. These committees produced reports which were considered by the Assembly and their recommendations were adopted as the basis on which the draft of the Constitution had to be prepared.

This was done by Mr. B. N. Rau\[2,3\] who brought to bear on his task a detailed knowledge of the Constitutions of other countries and an extensive knowledge of the conditions of this country as well as his own administrative experience. The Assembly then appointed the Drafting Committee which worked on the original draft prepared by Mr. B. N. Rau and produced the Draft Constitution which was considered by the Assembly at great length at the second reading stage.\[4\] In the third reading, members expressed their general comments on the Constitution. Finally, the Constitution came into effect on 26 January 1950.

Now, how do we define socialism? It is a political and economic doctrine that advocates for the public ownership of the means of production. Public ownership implies ownership by the state, which can be either democratic or authoritarian. The degree of public ownership may vary, allowing space for private ownership even within a socialist economy. However, this does not mean that the economic system is capitalistic.

What if the means of production are in private hands, but the state adopts socialist policies? In such a case, the state may opt for nationalisation, where it takes over the means of production from private entities. The state may or may not provide compensation for this takeover. In the end, the question is whether the members of the Constituent Assembly preferred public ownership of the means of production or in other words, whether they favoured nationalisation.

Jawahar Nehru introduced the Objectives Resolution on 13th December 1946. Nehru said that through the resolution not only political democracy, but also an economic democracy was being introduced. He gave a reason for not introducing the word ‘socialist’ in the resolution. The word was not introduced to avoid controversy. Hence, he laid down, not theoretical words and formulae, but rather the content of the thing desired. He also stated that he was socialist and hoped that India would stand for socialism and further also made a prediction that the whole world would follow socialism\![5\] Prophetic words indeed!

The objective resolution was debated. During debates, some interesting points were made. On the 17th December 1946, M R Masani spoke on the objective resolution. He said that it would be wrong to provide socialism in the constitution since the constituent assembly had no mandate to go in for far-reaching economic changes in the country. He also said that these changes could be brought about by a properly constituted parliament. He also rejected the totalitarian policies in the USSR by quoting Gandhi.\[6\]

On the same day, Dr Ambedkar spoke. He advocated the nationalisation of industry and land. According to him, the ideals of social, economic and political justice were achievable only in a socialistic economy\![7\]

Seth Govind Das had no doubt that the republic would be democratic and socialist.\[8\] On 18th December 1946, R K Sidhwa enlightened the assembly about the importance of socialist principles in eradicating poverty.\[9\]

Socialism was not only supported by men. Dakshayani Velayudhan advocated an independent socialist republic.\[10\] S Radhakrishnan, the celebrated philosopher, accepted that the state regulation was necessary to improve the economic conditions, but cautioned that it should not be done at the expense of the human spirit.\[11\]N V Gadgil advocated the social ownership of the means of production.\[12\] He also used the expression 'race of workers'!'\[13\]

Algurai Shastri quoted a hymn from Rig Veda. He stated that the higher ideals as stated in Rig Veda and have been taught for years were enunciated in the resolution. He further goes on to say that the resolution embodied the scientific socialist principles. Just after that, he said that all the ideals of the state conceived in the Bhagvat were embodied in the resolution.\[14\] From Rig Veda to socialism and from socialism to Bhagat, the journey of Algurai Shastri is truly inspirational! Vishwambhar Dayal Tripathi supported socialism and the nationalisation of key industries.\[15\]

With this, an exhaustive yet brief survey of the opinions of the members of the Constituent Assembly regarding socialism in the early stages of the Constitution's making is completed. It affirms that since the early stages of the Constitution's making, the members of the Constituent Assembly were clearly favouring socialism. Now, let us briefly consider the opinions of the members of the constituent assembly during the discussions on the reports of various committees. On 29th April 1947, Mr Promatha Ranjan Thakur advocated the nationalisation of the key industries.\[16\] On 2nd May 1947, S Nagappa advocated nationalisation. However, he did not reject the role of private enterprises and also advocated compensation for takeover.\[17\] Dr Suresh Chandra Banerjee viewed socialism as a hope for peasants.\[18\]

Ajit Prasad Jain advocated the confiscation of property without compensation.\[19\] Maulana Mohanai thought that the world would become socialist! He also conjured the idea of provincial republics.\[20\]

HV Kamath mentioned the socialist tendencies of Netaji Bose. He also said, “We who have been guided by leaders like Mahatma Gandhi, Pandit Nehru, Sardar Patel and Netaji Subhas Chandra Bose, do not stand in need of instruction about socialism”.\[21\] On 20th August 1947, Maulana Hasrat Mohani reiterated his demand for the union of socialist republics.\[22\] When the union powers committee submitted its report to the president of the constituent assembly, the economic and social planning, a hallmark of the socialist economy was included.\[23\] On 21st August 1947, G L Mehta envisaged the social service state.\[24\] Mahavir Tyagi wanted the socialisation of property and politics.\[25\] While speaking on the issue of minorities, Pandit Govind Ballabh Pant roared that all loyalties must exclusively be centred around the state.\[26\] On 30th August 1947, R K Sidhwa endorsed the nationalisation of the big industries.\[27\] Vishwambhar Dayal Tripathi endorsed the prediction of Nehru about the world becoming socialist.\[28\]

Now, it is clear that, right from the beginning of the making of the Indian constitution, the members of the constituent assembly favoured socialism. When Ambedkar introduced the draft constitution in the assembly, there was a debate on socialism. Articles favouring socialism were placed in the directive principles of state policy. So, there are two important points. S. V. Krishnamurthy Rao said that DPSPs contained the germs of the socialist government.\[29\] One is the actual discussion on socialism and the view of the constituent assembly members on the directive principles of state policy (DPSP). First, consider the question of DPSP. Dr Ambedkar categorically states that DPSPs were binding principles.\[30\] On 15th November 1948, K T Shah introduced an amendment to include the words secular, federal and socialist in clause 1 of article 1.\[31\]

Dr Ambedkar rejected the amendment. He gave excellent arguments to support his rejection. According to Ambedkar, “The constitution is merely a mechanism for the purpose of regulating the work of the various organs of the state. It is not a mechanism whereby particular members or particular parties are installed in the office. What should be the policy of the State, and how the Society should be organised in its social and economic sides are matters which must be decided by the people themselves according to time and circumstances. It cannot be laid down in the Constitution itself, because that is destroying democracy altogether.” Golden words indeed! (I hope the contemporary constitution worshippers cabal will read the views of Ambedkar!) Further, he stated that socialist principles were already embodied in the constitution, hence it was unnecessary to accept the amendment.\[32\]

On 19th November 1948, an amendment was introduced to delete the word ‘Directive’.

M. Ananthasayanam Ayyangar said that DPSPs were important and it was the duty of the elected government to enforce them.\[33\] While speaking on the amendment, Dr Ambedkar said: It is the intention of this Assembly that In future both the legislature and the executive should not merely pay lip service to these principles enacted in this part, but that they should be made the basis of all executive and legislative action that may be taken hereafter in the matter of the governance of the country.\[34\] Again, Dr Ambedkar said: our intention is that even when there are circumstances which prevent the Government, or which stand in the way of the Government giving effect to these Directive Principles, they shall, even under hard and unpropitious circumstances, always strive in the fulfilment of these Directives.\[35\]

These views were repeated in the third reading. Nandkishore Das said that Directive Principles of State Policy represent the quintessence of all that is the best and the noblest in any code of social, political, cultural or economic ethics that prevails in any part of the world.\[36\]

Purnima Banerji also said that in the Directive Principles of State Policy we have said that although they may not be enforceable in a court of law, they are nevertheless fundamental for the governance of this country.\[37\] Hence, two things are clear. DPSPs are socialist and they are the fundamental basis for governance.

Now, I will quickly cite instances in the constituent assembly, where socialism was favoured, during the first, second and third reading of the Constitution. Alladi Ayyar said that the Constitution, while it does not commit the country to any particular form of economic structure or social adjustment, gives ample scope for future legislatures and the future Parliament to involve any economic order and to undertake any legislation they choose in public interests. Having said that, it is important to remember that Alladi was refuting the allegation that socialist principles were ignored in the constitution.\[38\] M. Ananthasayanam Ayyangar advocated the nationalisation of the means of production.\[39\] Mahavir Tyagi advocated Gandhian socialism.\[40\] Brajeshwar Prasad roared that the perfect democracy was possible only in a classless society\![41\]

H V Kamath was of the opinion that the words ‘secular’ and ‘socialist’ should find a place in the preamble.\[42\] K. Hanumanthaiya said the days of capitalism were over\![43\] Dr Ambedkar favoured the multiplicity of economic doctrines to bring an economic democracy.\[44\] It is important to remember that economic democracy is another fancy name for socialism! K T Shah pushed for collectivisation.\[45\] Shibban Lal Saksena batted for the nationalisation of industries.\[46\] According to Chaudhari Ranbir Singh, The duty of the state today is not merely to maintain law and order but also to resolve the economic complexities.\[47\] Algu Rai Shastri advocated the transformation of individualistic capitalism to state capitalism\![48\] K. Santhanam advocated the socialisation of industries.\[49\]

Damodar Swarup Seth advocated a classless society.\[50\] Shibban Lal Saksena was in favour of nationalisation.\[51\] Brajeshwar Prasad accepted that there was much truth in the Marxist theory.\[52\] Jadubans Sahay was not happy with the compensation mechanism. He thought that it would be a hindrance in the path of nationalisation.\[53\]

S Nagappa thundered that all property should be at the disposal of the country as a whole. He vehemently supported the nationalisation of land.\[54\] Dr P S Deshmukh predicted that sooner or later, there would be no private property in India.\[55\] Guptanath Singh predicted the elimination of capitalism in Bharat and the world.\[56\]

Another speaker, another (failed) prediction! Alladi Ayyar said that capitalism came in the wake of the Industrial Revolution and is alien to the root of our civilization. He also said that property exists for dharma and the sole end of property is *yagna*.\[57\] There is no doubt that Western capitalism is alien to our civilization. But it does not imply that socialism and secularism are our constructs! Socialism and secularism are alien and hostile to the root and whole of our civilization. The representation of *yagna* by Alladi is absolutely erroneous.

In the third reading of the constitution, M Ananthasayanam Ayyangar reiterated the importance of socialist democracy and socialist democratic republic.\[58\] Seth Damodar Swarup complained that the constitution makes nationalisation hard\![59\] Ajit Prasad Jain continued his assertion of the nationalisation of industries to solve economic problems.\[60\] B M Gupte admitted that the constitution is not certainly socialist but there are unmistakable leanings towards socialism.\[61\] G Durgabai admitted that people have all the freedom to make experiments with socialism or any ism in which they believe.\[62\] Jagat Narain Lal accepted that DPSPs are Gandhian and socialist.\[63\] In the concluding speech Dr Ambedkar said that the condemnation of the Constitution largely comes from two quarters, the Communist Party and the Socialist Party. The Communist Party wanted dictatorship and the Socialist Party wanted the absolute socialisation of private property and the absolute fundamental rights so that even if their party failed to come into power, they would have the unfettered freedom not merely to criticise, but also to overthrow the state.\[64\] The irony is that contemporary party members of the communist and socialist parties are striving to save the Constitution!

The exhaustive account of the Constituent Assembly Debates clearly demonstrates that most members of the Constituent Assembly were influenced by socialism. A cacophony of nationalisation and collectivization prevailed. Members were brainwashed by socialist ideals. There is only one instance where it was emphasised that the nationalisation of industries might not be in the state's best interest.\[65\] The policies formulated by Nehru and Indira Gandhi were not aberrations but were aligned with the vision of the founding fathers of the Indian state. In fact, the 1991. liberalisation was a deviation from the vision of the architects of the Constitution.

Today, we find ourselves in a paradoxical position. The post-1991 reforms cannot be reversed, yet we cannot afford to revert to the incompetent, parochial, and outdated pre- 1991 vision. However, our DPSPs, which form the foundation of legislative and executive actions, remain rooted in socialism. Therefore, it is crucial to abandon them and establish new principles suited to the current era. A serious review of the DPSPs offers an opportunity to correct the mistakes of the founding fathers of the Indian state.

Those who want to wash away the sins of ancestors should start from the correction of DPSPs and pave the way towards the dharmik (civilisational) state!
