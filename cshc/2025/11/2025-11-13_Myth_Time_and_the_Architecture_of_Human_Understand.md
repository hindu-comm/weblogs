Abstract: *This essay constitutes the first instalment in a sustained inquiry into the ontological foundations and cultural formations of myth. By situating myth within frameworks of temporal ontology and colonial epistemology. This opening study provides the conceptual vocabulary for analysing how different cultures instantiate archetypal patterns through divergent metaphysical commitments. The series as a whole aims to develop a systematic account of myth for which I neither reduce it to psychology nor romanticize it as primitive wisdom, but recognize it as a distinct mode of reality with its own logic, demands, and transformative possibilities.*

# **Myth Beyond History**

I have been trying to understand how myths function. What do they consist of? How are they presented in different cultures? My intuition of this grew when I encountered how people think of the Ramayan or the Mahabharat. Do these accounts act as historical texts or as a new Indic wing has started to claim “Ithihasa” that is “It did happen.” I wanted to bring a notice to how mythology and the myths it contains function within some cultural approximations. Myths and legends which many of the mythologies contain are not bound by the spatio-temporal coordinates of human experience. They are not “of” or “for” specific historical periods. They exist beside the parameters of the lived present as idealized forms of unmanifested experience within humans.

For Hindus, Myths sit above ordinary reality, not below it. They are more real, not less. When we say or quote some instance, suppose that of Ramayan or others, did not happen on the earthly plane at an exact datable moment, we recognize that mythic events don’t occupy the same ontological plane as history, which, as a profession, became more sharpened with the protestant reformation(will explain this further). When my mother sits to do the pooja of Chauth Mata, she recites other women a story of Chauth Mata, where they listen to a woman who was having problems and just by doing this pooja at some time and in some place, she was relieved just by doing this fast and following this tradition. Do these stories have any historicity? I guess not, but do they require one? These questions have been non-existent for women, but they form an essential condition if an Indologist or historian reads them, having an itch to find their origin years and territorialize this story. The modern social scientist and their kind make these mistakes by treating myths as either primitive science that got the facts wrong or nice moral stories for children, which is where they ruin things. Both readings place myth below everyday reality, as if myths are confused or simplified versions of ordinary life. This is exactly backward. But these myths reveal structures of reality that ordinary perception cannot reach. They operate at a different level of existence entirely, one that intersects with our lives constantly but sideways rather than forward through time. Puranic stories are timeless, unfolding eternally like existence itself. Though the limitations of our finite minds force us to access them in the past tense, they remain living metaphors perpetually enacted in our lives if we cultivate the recognition to perceive them.

But we must investigate why such notions are prevalent and where they originate from. The Western mind (which today has taken the normative structure of understanding of the world) struggles with this because it inherited from Christianity a fundamentally historical metaphysics. God created the world at a point. Christ was born at a point. Redemption happens in time. Everything is linear, progressive, and time-bound. Even secularized Western thought keeps this structure. This historical fixation sets the stage for modernity’s linear obsession, discussed next. History is going somewhere. We’re making progress or regressing. Events happen once and are done. Let me explain this further and as to why this has happened.

**The Historical Metaphysics of Christianity and Its Global Consequences**

The Western mind struggles to comprehend these non-historical myths because Christianity embeds historical occurrence as the foundation of religious truth itself. This isn’t accidental. A radical metaphysical claim that distinguishes it from nearly all preceding religious traditions and shaped everything that followed including secular modernity.

Christianity has been audacious enough to stake its ultimate truth on historical events. For them, God didn’t just reveal timeless principles. God entered history at a specific time and place. Christ was born under Caesar Augustus, crucified under Pontius Pilate, and rose on the third day. These became spiritual truths rather than symbolic representations; they were claimed as actual historical occurrences, which they claimed pagan traditions didn’t have. Greek and Roman religions didn’t depend on whether Zeus actually existed on Mount Olympus or whether Persephone literally descended to Hades. These stories functioned mythically articulating patterns and principles without requiring historical verification. The rituals worked, the meanings resonated, and the social functions operated regardless of factual historicity. The question simply didn’t arise in the same way for them; these were very alien to their understanding of the world.

Christianity changed this fundamentally. By making incarnation central, by claiming that God became flesh at a datable moment, by organising doctrine around the resurrection as a historical event that Christianity fused with a historical occurrence, or I say, the historicity of Christ became the basis for Christianity’s existence. Time’s arrow points toward eschatological fulfilment when history itself will end. Which is why you also see today’s biblical scholars attack this very premise willfully.

**The Secularization That Preserved Structure**

But what actually changed was the Enlightenment, and what it meant for our world needs careful attention. While many scholars have written on this, especially decolonial scholarship but my exposure to SN Balgangadhara’s ideas has been the most profound. So, when European thought secularised itself during the Enlightenment, it retained the superstructure of Christianity as completely intact. God might be removed, but linear time remained. Historical progress replaced divine providence. Scientific advancement replaced salvation history. Human reason replaced revelation. But the underlying temporal metaphysics persisted unchanged. Secular modernity still assumes time flows linearly from the past through the present into the future. Events still happen once and are done. Historical occurrence still determines truth claims. We still ask “did it really happen?” as if this question exhausts what we need to know about any narrative. The Christian fusion of truth with historical factuality became so naturalised that even atheists assume it without question. This explains why modern Western minds, whether religious or secular, cannot comprehend non-historical myths except as failed history or symbolic fiction. The categories available for thinking about narrative are either “factual account of what happened” or “made-up story with possible moral lessons.” There’s no conceptual space for myths that are more real than history precisely because they don’t occupy historical time.

Christianity’s historical metaphysics also generated the very concept of “religion” that modernity universalised. Religion became understood as a system of beliefs about supernatural entities and historical events, typically involving doctrines, sacred texts, and institutional structures. This model was derived directly from Christianity’s own self-understanding. When this concept was enforced globally through colonialism, it forced radically different traditions into Christian-shaped categories. Hinduism(assuming it was a religion) became “a religion” with “beliefs” about “gods” who either did or didn’t “exist” historically. Buddhism became “a religion” with doctrines about supernatural realities. Indigenous traditions became “animistic religions” with “beliefs” in spirits. This illustrates how categories were imposed without regard for the categories themselves, lacking a basis for such universalisation. West would rather prefer to call a religion a “False Religion” than re-examine its categorical mistake.

Many of these traditions were practical engagements with reality’s multiple dimensions, ritual participations in archetypal patterns, or I say experiential disciplines for navigating one’s existence. The very question *“do you believe the gods exist?”* which modern men ask so audaciously often makes no sense because these questions presuppose the Christian subject-object structure, where belief is an attitude taken toward potential facts. Traditional Hindu frameworks didn’t organise truth around historical factuality because they operated with different temporal ontologies where myths dwell in dimensions for which history is the wrong category. The tragedy is that colonised intellectuals often internalised the coloniser’s epistemology so completely that they forgot their own traditions operated differently. They began defending myths as “actually historical” or apologising for them as “merely symbolic,” both responses already conceding that historical factuality is what matters.

**Can Myths Give Knowledge?**

We encounter an epistemological problem here as to how we know anything through myth if myths don’t give us facts about the world? Normal epistemology says we need things through perception, reason, or testimony from reliable sources. But myths work through recognition, not discovery. The reason for this is that Indian philosophy believes that we have all the knowledge in ourselves, and it needs to be recognized rather than discovered outside. The knowledge was already inside you. To know, therefore, is not to uncover the unknown, but to recognize what was always present in the depths of one’s own being. The myth just gives it form in simple terms, a structure is being presented to something which has not been experienced or remains ingrained within the core of experiential. One might say it might reveal inherited memory, which Japanese and Indian traditions claim, but I wouldn’t go that far to conclude the approximation of it. This is why we can call myths hyper-real. Normal epistemology treats the material world as primary and ideas as secondary. You see the tree, then you form the concept “tree.” While in the same Indian thought, we form the idea or archetype of “Tree-ness” as *primary*. The physical tree you see outside your window is just a *manifestation* or *instance* of that eternal pattern. Mythic epistemology cognition inverts this completely. The archetypal pattern is primary, and material instances are secondary. Knowing doesn’t mean discovering something new, but recognizing a deeper pattern that your consciousness already carries.

**The Myth-aphysics Beneath**

If myths operate this way epistemologically, what does that imply about reality itself? Reality has layers, and the deepest layer isn’t material. It’s formal or archetypal. The gods and demons aren’t beings that existed “back then.” They are permanent features of reality’s architecture, like mathematical principles, but applied to existence itself. Just as gravity doesn’t have a birthday, these principles don’t “begin” or “end.” They are **formal realities**, as timeless as mathematical relations. Triangles don’t exist “in time”, and you can’t ask when the triangle started existing; these archetypal patterns don’t exist in time. Every triangle you draw participates in triangularity.

This is why myths are eternally unfolding. They aren’t events in the past that echo forward. They are eternal presents that manifest differently depending on the level you’re looking at. The myths play out vertically across all levels of existence simultaneously, not horizontally through historical sequence. I would like people to read SN Balgangadhara’s article *“Historicity of Rama, Krishna, Anjaneya”* and *“Ontology of Jain authors: Ramayana”;* he illuminates our understanding by critically recognizing that the absence of “belief” in Sanskrit isn’t a linguistic accident. Let me summarize this argument for you.

When Westerners ask “did Rama exist?” they’re already operating within a framework where belief necessarily entails existence claims. This framework assumes human beings are intentional creatures who form beliefs about objects in the world, and that believing in something logically requires believing it exists. Under this picture, “believing in Rama” has the same logical structure as “believing in Christ” and therefore commits you to claims about historical existence. It’s evidence that Indian traditions developed a richer theory of human beings than Western common sense ever produced. Western epistemology, for all its sophistication, remains an elaboration of common-sense perception parading as theory. It takes as its premise “we perceive that humans have intentionality” and moves from there, never calling into question whether that perception itself might be as illusory as perceiving the sun moving around the earth. This radical claim cuts right to the root of cross-cultural misunderstanding. He argues that Indian traditions don’t simply have different beliefs from Western traditions; they operate with a fundamentally different understanding of what belief itself is and how consciousness relates to reality. The Western framework assumes that human beings are intentional creatures that form beliefs about objects in the world. This means: I am a subject, there are objects out there, and I have mental states-beliefs-that are “about” those objects. When I believe in God or Rama, I’m forming a mental attitude toward an entity which either exists or doesn’t exist independently of my belief. This subject-object structure feels so natural to Western minds that it seems like a description of consciousness itself rather than one interpretation among others. From this framework, certain things follow automatically. If you take the proposition that Rama exists and you believe in Rama, then you are committed to the truth of the proposition that there was a person by the name of Rama who existed at some time and place. You secularise yourself of time, stepping out of your immediate moment to occupy a pattern-space that transcends historical contingency. This is not an escape into fantasy; it is access to archetypal dimensions which ordinary perception obscures.

Belief logically entails existence claims. There’s no way around this if you accept the basic picture of intentional consciousness forming beliefs about potential objects.

The assumption that human beings are intentional creatures forming beliefs about the world is itself considered a pernicious illusion. Sanskrit apparently lacks a word for “belief”, not because Indians were primitive, but because the concept itself doesn’t map onto how Indian traditions understand human consciousness and its relationship to reality. You can be a bhakta of Rama without believing Rama existed. This sounds paradoxical only within Western frameworks where devotion presupposes belief in existence. Within Indian frameworks, bhakti is participation in an archetypal pattern, not a belief-relation to a historical particular. Let me explain this more deeply. I will give credit to a bhajan of Kabir “**Sakal Hans Me Ram Viraje**” where Kabir describes Ram is described in 4 forms - Son of Dashrath, One who sits on every Gatha (residing in every human being), A source of energy, and the fourth is an immaterial one which can’t be even taken from the mouth.

Rama is a pattern, a form, an archetypal structure that manifests at different levels. At the mythic level (Zamani, the eternal dimension), Rama exists as an archetypal pattern of ideal king, dharma and at the devotional level, Rama exists as the form through which the devotee relates to the divine. At various historical levels, some persons may have instantiated the Rama pattern to varying degrees. The devotee isn’t forming beliefs about whether an object existed. The devotee is devoting itself to a pattern that exists more fundamentally than any historical particular, as Historical questions about when and where are category mistakes. To put it in from a crude understanding of a Bhakth — pattern is what’s real. Myths aren’t just primary over material instances epistemologically. The entire epistemological framework that generates questions about belief and existence is itself illusory from the perspective of traditions that developed richer theories of consciousness. We don’t just need to recognize that myths are more real than history. We need to recognize that the framework generating the myth-versus-history question is itself operating at a lower level of reality than the mythic patterns it attempts to judge.

**Secularization of Reality**

Now we must understand how Myths function in more practical forms of our lives. We have already established that they provide archetypal topoi, foundational patterns or “places” of meaning that we transplant from the mythic realm into ordinary experience to make sense of our lives. This process might be called secularization, but we must be precise about what this means. We must understand why we are using Topoi here and what it means for our argument. The use of such words and their processes was very aptly elaborated by Jacob De Roover, Sarah Clearhout and Balu in his article ***“Liberal Political Theory and the Cultural Migration of Ideas: The Case of Secularism in India”***

*“All cultures and societies have their own commonplaces. We would like to introduce the term “topos” to refer to a particular kind of commonplace idea, which plays an important role in the conceptual world of a society.33 Not all commonplace ideas are topoi, even though each of them has the potential to become one. Whether or not a commonplace becomes a topos depends upon its ability to be elaborated in the form of a theory or hypothesis. Hence, we can identify a commonplace as a topos only after some thinker develops it into a theory. Alternatively, a commonplace idea can become a topos in a different way: by playing the functional role of a heuristic that allows one to develop new theories or hypotheses. That is, topoi can give direction to the process of transforming other ideas into theories. An important characteristic of topoi is that they exhibit some sort of structure or coherence. They are not isolated commonplace ideas but consist of clusters of related ideas. This is not to say that, taken together, all topoi form a coherent whole. The relationship between clusters of topoi could be one of relevance or irrelevance, compatibility or incompatibility. We can now state our hypothesis about the secularization of religious ideas succinctly: some ideas developed in the theology of a religion are “secularized,” that is, spread in an apparently secular or non-religious guise in society, in the form of topoi. Secularization in the realm of ideas, then, is the process of transforming religious ideas into the topoi of a culture”*

To put it simply, topos isn’t just any commonplace idea. It becomes a topos when it can be elaborated into a theory or when it functions heuristically to generate new theories. Topoi exhibit structure and coherence. They aren’t isolated concepts but clusters of related ideas that constrain how problems can be framed and solutions imagined. Similarly, Myths provide primordial templates, which classical rhetoric termed topoi or commonplaces. Human consciousness then appropriates these as cognitive and existential frameworks. This appropriation mirrors what happens in literary engagement.

When we invoke or recite some puranic story or a mythological story in our conversation we organize perception; we are describing a structured cluster that generates interpretive possibilities while constraining them within certain limits. The myth provides the original cluster. We secularize it not by extracting a single idea but by appropriating an entire constellation of related patterns that then organize how situations disclose themselves.

When a reader inhabits a fictional character, they enact a temporary suspension of their own temporal situation to access alternative configurations of experience. In this self-displacement, consciousness secularizes itself of time, suspending its embeddedness in chronological particularity to occupy a pattern-space that transcends historical contingency. These transplanted topoi don’t function as analogies or comparisons. They aren’t supplements to experience. They are constitutive structures that pre-organize perception itself. It activates as an immediate orientation, a ready-to-hand schema through which the situation discloses itself. These transplanted topoi function not as analogical supplements to experience but as constitutive structures that pre-organize perception itself. The myth furnished the archetypal form. That is, mythological truths/stories give us original form. We secularized it into practical equipment for navigating existence.

Secularization has transmuted it into practical equipment for navigating existence. These patterns retain their effectiveness independent of confessional belief. They map genuine structures of human possibility rather than merely projecting cultural artefacts onto neutral experience. This is why myths remain functional even for non-believers. The topoi work regardless of faith because they correspond to something real about how human existence is structured.

This explains why we can use mythic topoi without religious belief. When one says he is fighting a Kuruksheera war or Krishnas divinity, these topos, these things don’t require believing in Krishna’s. It has been secularized into a pattern for navigating moral paralysis. But the pattern still carries the structure of the original myth. You can reinterpret it creatively, but you can’t make it mean just anything. The archetypal form constrains interpretation. When we say “you can reinterpret it creatively, but you can’t make it mean just anything,” the reason for me stating this is that myths and their archetypal forms have an internal structure containing a grammar of meaning that sets limits on interpretation. You can renew or restage a myth in different historical or psychological contexts, but your reinterpretation must still resonate with the underlying pattern that gives the myth coherence. The archetypal form acts like a deep syntax that is of variation, not randomness. Myths endure precisely because their structures are elastic enough to allow reinterpretation, yet their axis is stable enough to prevent dissolution into arbitrary meaning.

We now move onto another concept where we see the condition of necessity that we require for these Topoi requires us to call this essential component “conditions of intelligibility.” A topos makes sense only within a constellation of surrounding ideas. When topoi migrate across cultures, their conditions of intelligibility don’t travel with them. This causes the fundamental problem. One can understand that Liberal secularism emerges from Christian theological topoi: the two kingdoms, Christian liberty, the fallen flesh requiring political restraint, and the soul’s relationship to God are very essential parts of this. These clusters made sense in European contexts because surrounding cultural concepts sustained their interpretation. When these topoi arrived in India, the surrounding conceptual infrastructure wasn’t there. Indians tried to interpret “separation of politics and religion” using Indian topoi instead. But Indian topoi reflect a different ontology. Transplanting here would either create a problem or act as **hermeneutic displacement**, where the topos is re-inscribed within an alien semiotic field, generating surface familiarity but deep semantic incongruence. In simple terms, it is a **conceptual distortion** which produces a hybrid formation that appears coherent, but only by masking the loss of its original conditions of intelligibility, functioning only performatively.

I think one in very simply terms can understand this problem by thinking of the whole “Girlfriend and Boyfriend” concept when they began to popularise in India since the 2000s. We can see that there exist no exact words for such things in Hindi, and it has always been a problem of translation, but also, we notice that since these concepts came in, not only do we acquire new forms of concepts, such as Valentine’s Day, Chocolate Day, Situationship, etc or other similar things that validate of this concept, I hope you get the point, not only did they create new capitalist products to make these things intelligible for us to have a Girlfriend/ Boyfriend experience, but also a whole new grammar or west started to creep in without any forms existing in India. It is not that love didn’t exist, but its cultural form changed, and a new language was needed to make it intelligible. The failure of the West lies in its assumption of having universal topoi when they were actually secularized Christian theology. In cultures with different metaphysical frameworks, these distinctions don’t map cleanly onto experience. We can clearly conclude here that mythological secularization as a process depends on surrounding constellations of ideas that serve as conditions of intelligibility. When mythic topoi migrate across cultures or when cultures encounter each other, intelligibility breaks down unless the receiving culture has conceptual resources that can sustain interpretation.

**Emergence of Culture**

Now that we have seen the emergence of myths and their transplantation and, in some sense, understood their limits, what remains of them when they are planted in a different culture? What I see is that they have tried creating truth. Myths, strictly speaking, refer to unchanging principles of existence. Cultures emerge from myths, not the other way around. Cultures evolve, myths are perennial. You cannot discard or change myths without entirely abandoning the culture. The myth of the Purusha Sukta gives us the Hindu social order. You can annihilate caste only if you discard the Rigveda. Myths are maps that help us navigate the broad spectrum of consciousness that constitutes selfhood. Without myths, we stand the risk of losing touch with reality. Therefore, myths are like the air we breathe - we cannot survive without them. The traditional myths that we get as part of cultural inheritance are about clean air. If we are not lucky enough to inherit such myths, we make our own myths from the raw material of our individual experience and inevitably fall sick from breathing heavily polluted air. A symptom of that sickness is that our consciousness gets stuck within a very narrow band that is our social persona.

**Conclusion**

I by means for understanding and explaining these methods to explain why Indians fail to grasp Western ideas. It’s a revelation that Western ideas presuppose metaphysical frameworks that aren’t universal. What seemed like universal political truths turn out to be topoi derived from secularised Christian theology, requiring specific temporal ontologies and conceptual infrastructures to remain intelligible. This doesn’t mean all frameworks are equally valid or that truth is relative, as many postmodern theories have claimed, and I do not wish to take that sort of stand. Different temporal ontologies aren’t competing beliefs about the same thing. There are alternative ways reality can be structured or seen, totally depending on which level you’re examining, nor do I hold a presumptive bad taste for Linear time. My article is focused on showing a highlight of presumptive essences, which then takes the form of a problem, but linear time as a concept accurately describes certain aspects of material causation. Events do seem to flow from the past through the present into the future when you’re tracking mechanical processes or planning practical projects. For building bridges and scheduling meetings, linear time works perfectly well.

But linear time fails to capture how meaning operates, how patterns persist, how archetypal structures organize experience. For these dimensions, frameworks like Sasa-Zamani, which the African philosopher John Mbiti, in his work “African religions & philosophy”, has presented, seem to provide superior access. They recognize that experiential time has a different structure from clock time, and mythic patterns dwell in dimensions that temporal sequence cannot reach. I am still looking for what Hindu equivalents to such concepts but the question isn’t which framework is true. The question is which framework illuminates which aspects of reality. Western obsession with linear time and historical factuality has generated enormous technological progress and scientific understanding. Simultaneously, blinding West culture to the dimensions of reality that African and Hindu traditions maintain a clear perception of.

The mythic dimension isn’t primitive superstition, which social scientists claim to be and on which non-Abrahamic religions have to prove themselves in order to make their heroes exist or truth whether for practical proposition or for legalities to treat them on par with others for the purpose of governmantality if these essential condition seems to be missing then they must be reformed, reorganized or and essentiality of them must be extracted to t to bring them on the same level as other religions. Cultures maintaining a connection to this dimension aren’t backward. They’re accessing something Western modernity has largely lost. But this explains why Hinduism’s survival matters so profoundly. It represents an intact alternative system where different mythic topoi emerged from different metaphysical assumptions. Hindu myths don’t presuppose the subject-object structure, the belief-existence relation, or the linear historical metaphysics that Christian theology does. They operate from frameworks where archetypal patterns are primary and material instances secondary, where time is multi-layered rather than linear, and where devotion doesn’t require existence claims. Myths are beyond the parameters of human experience. They are the grammar of the timeline, the rules by which events can have meaning at all.
