# Towards a Stronger Hindutva: Reflections on Unfinished Tasks

### An introspective look at the challenges that remain and the work ahead

The Hindutva movement has had major successes across domains and sections of society in recent decades as a socio-political ideology. From the mainstreaming of Hindutva to repeated electoral victories, we have witnessed multiple historic moments like the reconstruction of the Ayodhya Ram Temple and the abrogation of Article 370. The list of it’s successes is decently long. However it has also suffered many setbacks and failures. It is now important to look at them and understand why they happened and reprimand them if possible. Here the word Hindutva has not been used in the sense of just the Ideology propounded by Veer Savarkar in the 20th century, but the collection of various modern Hindu movements of various hues and shades towards temple protection and reclamation, cow protection, cultural conservation etc. which were or were not directly or indirectly influenced by Savarkar’s views. This article does not seek to disparage or belittle Hindutva or it’s supporters for these failures but to humbly present a set of problems that the author sees and believes are pertinent to the well-being of Hindus and Hindutva, a reminder that much work remains to be done.

1.  Failed to protect the cow

The single-biggest and gravest failure of Hindutva is the failure to protect the mother cow from slaughter and ill-treatment. Cow protection is of central importance to Hindutva. It is one issue which gets near complete support from Hindu society cutting across castes, sects and ages. But ever since the advent of Muslim rule, cow slaughter has become the norm. Hindus, driven out of political power, could not stop the killing of cows. Even then, many Muslim rulers were successfully convinced by Hindus to ban cow slaughter, including Akbar. Nevertheless cow slaughter continued to be practiced openly across India wherever Muslims were in a dominant position. This continued under European rule, despite requests by Hindu rulers to protect cows. In fact, it was declared a Muslim tradition to use cow flesh for their own consumption and use and any request for cow protection was denied in the name of maintaining neutrality. Another noteworthy case is of Derozio and Young Bengal. His supporters openly engaged in the consumption of beef under the pretext of breaking taboos and superstitions. By the late nineteenth century, cow protection movement was already gaining ground because of Hindus mobilizing against cow killing. In the 1880’s, cow protection sabhas were formed across North and Central India and Punjab. In the beginning, these were often supported by English-educated urban

Hindu middle class and elites, and then later on by lower level elites like less-educated elites. By the end of that decade, the movement had consolidated considerable support even in western and eastern India and among the masses who saw the slaughterhouses operated by the British as a threat to their cattle which were their assets. Petitions to the colonial govt. were made by the community, pamphlets and cow processions used to be taken out. In 1893, massive riots broke out between Hindus and Muslims over cow slaughter across provinces. The colonial govt. used this as an opportunity to crackdown on cow protection Sabhas. The cow protection movement remained largely dead for the next few decades till the Independence when demands for cow protection were once again made. This time the Govt. of Independent India itself advised the states to not close down slaughterhouses. In more recent history, this zeal for cow protection was exemplified best by the cow protection agitation of 1966 when thousands of Gausevaks, men and women, young and old, gathered in the National capital to demand a bill prohibiting the slaughter of cow. This attempt failed and the crowds dispersed after they were showered with gun fire by the regime. However, the desire for cow protection has not died down and youth across different states have taken it upon themselves to catch cattle smugglers and slaughterers. In spite of all these efforts of almost 150 years, cow slaughter continues to remain unprohibited in India. The question of cow protection has been left to the states which are usually less efficient and less functional than the Centre and due to this, cow smuggling and slaughter continues even in states where it is officially prohibited. In some states, it is partially or even completely allowed. This is Hindutva’s biggest shame.

2.  Failure to raise a Hindutva-oriented intellectual and artist class

The Hindutva movement has fell flat on it’s face when it comes to cultivating a loyal intellectual elite. Ideally, it should have raised a class of friendly intellectuals, artists and legal, journalist and academic professionals influenced by Hindutva. These are the sections of the society that mould and shape the way a society thinks. It has not only failed to do that but has almost been completely successful in attracting and drawing this cohort from the opposition’s camp. The lack of an intellectual class keeps its growth paralysed among the elites and the middle class when the markets should have been flooded by the flourishing of hundreds of pro-Hindutva authors dealing with topics and subjects across disciplines. It’s summits and gatherings, of whatever thinkers it has, feel repetitive and pedantic. Instead of making dent on the outside world and other ideologies and groups, it gets influenced by them. It also suffers artistically, lacking a unique artistic style which can be confidently attributed to it. Any work of art or media associated with Hindutva is either mediocre, ugly or unsubtle, and sometimes all of them. Nowhere is this more pronounced than in cinema and literature, where films supposedly made to cater to Hindutva audiences are loud and overt displays of nationalism and religion. The movement seems to never really have picked up on literature. Hindi literature, where optimistic Hindutva literature should have been pre-eminent, is dominated by Left oriented categories like social justice literature and women’s literature(pre-dominantly feminist in nature). Apart from a few exceptions, the reading list of a typical Hindutva-influenced reader is often made up of just what should have been a part of school history curriculum and some military history and biographies. In contrast, Japan when it had not even started being ascendant, while it was supposedly closed, was already taking note of the developments abroad. It had prepared for the Meiji restoration and laid it’s roots in the Tokugawa and Shinto periods itself. We see that it is because we start off so poorly that we don’t go really far. Similarly, a crop of ideologically motivated legal professionals should have been prepared to take up issues on the behalf of Hindu society. A similar contingent of ideologically charged journalists would have been helpful in bringing issues important for Hindus to the limelight. There is no dearth of cases where Hindus, Hindu society and it’s beliefs have been questioned and misrepresented by these groups of professionals. It’s a huge disservice to an ideology that claims to be the heir of people like Kautilya, Kalidasa and Bhasa and to a nation that prides itself as the heir to the greatest knowledge tradition in human history, a nation that prides itself for it’s magnificent and evocative and yet graceful and ethereal art.

There is no school or academy of art that has sprung up to bear responsibility for the preservation and propagation of Indian fine arts.

This is sometimes owed to the fact that Hindutva has grown up on the back of generations of selfless public service and ground work by it’s supporters and not sophisticated closed-room debating, argumentation and discussion. The quest for ideological grounding that started with Savarkar has also not gone very far after him.

Organisations like Bharat Vichar Manch, and Prajna Pravah have not bore the desired fruits either. The cultural and intellectual hegemony of thought and intellectualism in this country has remained in the favour of the Left. Hindutva has failed to eradicate this paradigm of thought or produce something of it’s own to counteract it with the exception of Integral Humanism which again failed to draw followers. What this means is that it is losing grip on the traditional, cultural and social consciousness of the society. People do not think of their actions in terms of Indian thought systems and beliefs but instead understand them in terms of western schema. Youngsters are easily manipulated against it and the aforementioned misrepresentations of it’s plans.

Dharampal suggested that Indians should be made ‘consciously aware of the fundamental contradictions between alien thought categories with which she is burdened today and those which have been India’s own since early times’. This can be a good starting point for Hindutva’s future intellectual outlook.

3.  Failure to give a vision and a goal for the future

The Hindutva supporter today is obsessed with history and even more with the correction of history. A vision for the future is completely missing. What does the future in a world where Hindutva is dominant look like? What things would change? What would remain the same? This problem seems to be rooted in the struggle Hindutva has waged against it’s place in history. It views itself as the heir to a civilization that has suffered unjust and barbaric persecution for centuries. It sees it’s place in history to be that of a harbinger of an Indic Renaissance where Indian values and traditions are honoured and preserved. It realises it’s in a unique position in history, and plans to attain a great goal. The conceptualisation stops here. What happens after this? What steps does it take towards the Renaissance? What exactly does the Renaissance result in? These are questions no one seems to have an answer to. Over the decades, it has wrestled with falsified or misrepresented history and narratives. There’s a constant tussle against it but Hindutva needs to grow beyond this. It always seems to be a subordinate contender in this tussle stuck scratching the surface. It is for this reason that we see it’s enemies and opponents so easily project their misrepresentations on it.

4.  Failure to harmonise religious and politically-oriented strands

It is common to see Hindu religious leaders, politicians and activists speak against each other. It is sad to see that they failed to come on the same page even on important issues. The religious bloc has rightly and understandably emphasised on upholding traditional practices but the political bloc has failed to even show eagerness for cow slaughter prohibition and temple protection.

5.  Failure to give a popularly accepted definition and
    conceptualisation of a Hindu Rashtra

It is another example of the previous proposition. We have many groups from politicians to Sanyasis demanding a Hindu Rashtra. The desire for it can be seen even among the laity, but despite this, even today, there is no definition or shared framework of what a Hindu Rashtra would look like. What would it’s constitution look like? What would it’s administrative structure look like? What would be it’s policies? The more orthodox sections would want regard and respect for the traditional norms, values and rules while the most liberal sections would want nothing that seemingly obstructs their freedoms even a little. Some others might say that we are already a Hindu Rashtra. A middle ground has to be reached here through mutual dialogue.

6.  Failure to consolidate various castes and groups

  
It is very common to see people of various cohorts, castes and sub-castes declare themselves to be non-Hindu because of supposed or actual cultural differences. Whether or not they are Hindus and what are the reason for such calls is not the concern of this article, but it is the failure of the Hindutva movement that it has not been able to convince these various castes and it’s people of the oneness of the Hindu people.

7.  Electoral victories have not given sufficient results

  
These repeated electoral victories have given little visible results when it comes to aiding other causes of Hindutva. Why could we not stop cow slaughter even after all these years? One can say that it has managed to keep out worse people out of power but that is not enough. In fact, this obsession with winning the next elections led to absorption of the very same problematic, and often anti-Hindu, elements of the other side.

8.  Reliance on one party and organisation  

Modern mainstream Hindutva suffers from only one organisational challenge, it is almost completely reliant on just one party and one organisation, or its allied groups. What should ideally have been the case is that the opposite camp should also have had elements tolerant or even friendly to Hindutva by now.

There is also the question of non-subcontinental of Indian descent and non-ethnically subcontinental Hindus. What is their place to be in the Hindu world? What should be done to get more people to practice Hinduism? What would be the future of the countries outside the subcontinent? What would be their relations with a truly Hindu India?

# Conclusion

These problems are what drag Hindutva back today. It is the author’s humble attempt at bringing forth these complex issues to attention so as to tackle them better as a collective. The author is of the belief that these problems are certainly solvable with guidance and blessings from the religious Gurus and help of the mass appeal of the political and activist sides. No Indic Renaissance is possible without coming on common grounds on these questions. The author wishes to see only the betterment of Hindutva.
