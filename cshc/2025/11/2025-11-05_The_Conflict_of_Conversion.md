### **Introduction**:

In my previous two articles, I sought to understand the idea of secularism, and its relevance (or rather the struggle) with the society, politics and culture of India. The question of religious conversion, as we will see, is deeply intertwined with the same. It can be understood as complementing our study, and thus not just carries forward, but also greatly adds to the quest of answering the questions posed by our previous discussions on secularism.

In the course of writing this article itself, a law recently passed by the legislative assembly of Rajasthan caught my attention. The salient provisions of the *The Rajasthan Prohibition of Unlawful Conversion of Religion Bill, 2025* include prohibition of “religious conversions by force, coercion, misrepresentation, undue influence, allurement, marriage, or any fraudulent means”. The relatives of the converted are empowered to lodge an FIR against it. Mandatory declarations have to be made in front of the DM, who has been empowered to conduct an inquiry, and even invite objections. \[1\]

Such stringent provisions reflect a deep discomfort with respect to the phenomenon of religious conversion, which in India mostly includes Hindus converting to Christianity or Islam. Thus, it is often argued that anti-conversion discourse is fueled by regressive, Hindu supremacist-nationalist elements, who seek to curb the religious rights of ‘minorities’.

# **Propagation and Propaganda**

The legal intricacies of the debate are beyond scope of this article. However, let us have a brief look at the major regulations and judgements on the same, to understand the controversy around conversion in independent India.

The constitution of India guarantees to all citizens the “freedom of **conscience**,\[…\] **practice** and ***propagation*** of religion” (Article 25). Critics of anti-conversion laws argue that these violate the spirit of secularism, and the freedom granted to minorities to “propagate” their faiths. The supporters, on the other hand, describe attempts by missionaries to convert Hindus as attacks on the freedom of conscience (as they feel it is an intrusion) and therefore advocate strict anti-conversion laws to be enacted by the legislature.

The words ‘conscience’ and ‘practice’ in the constitution are on expected lines, given India is a liberal secular democratic state. However, the word *‘propagation’* in the above article, presents great contention in its interpretation.

## States and Courts

Like Rajasthan, numerous other states have had anti conversion provisions throughout decades. Recent ones include The Uttarakhand Freedom of Religion Act, 2018; The Uttar Pradesh Prohibition of Unlawful Conversion of Religion Ordinance, 2020 (which later became The Uttar Pradesh Prohibition of Unlawful Conversion of Religion Act, 2021); The Himachal Pradesh Freedom of Religion Act, 2019; and the Madhya Pradesh Freedom of Religion Act, 2021.

In the aftermath of a mass conversion in 1981, which saw hundreds of Scheduled Caste members in the village of Meenakshipuram (in Tamil Nadu) convert to Islam,

> “the Ministry of Home Affairs, Government of India, is reported to
> have advised the State Governments and Union Territory administrations
> to enact laws to regulate change of religion on the lines of the
> existing Acts in Madhya Pradesh, Orissa and Arunachal Pradesh.”\[2\]

The Supreme Court has ruled:

> “\[…\]for what the Article grants is not the right to convert another
> person to one’s own religion, but to transmit or spread one’s religion
> by an exposition of its tenets. It has to be remembered that Article
> 25(1) guarantees *‘freedom of conscience’ to every citizen, and not
> merely to the followers of one particular religion, and that, in turn,
> postulates that there is no fundamental right to convert another
> person* to one’s own religion because if a person purposely undertakes
> the conversion of another person to his religion, as distinguished
> from his effort to transmit or spread the tenets of his religion, that
> would impinge on the ‘freedom of conscience’ guaranteed to all the
> citizens of the country alike.” \[3\]

Similar conclusions were drawn by the Law commission report in 2010.

Kerala HC in 2009 observed:

> “Religious conversions may appear to many in the Indian mindset to be
> unnecessary, puerile and negation of the very concept of respect for
> both religions as also the followers of such religion.” \[4\]

## The Constituent Assembly

The initial draft articles interestingly contained conditions on freedom of religion that put restrictions on conversion. K.M. Munshi’s draft prohibited conversion for minors and by inducement, coercion etc for all. \[5\]

The word ‘propagate’, earlier absent, made its first appearance in the examination of the fundamental rights by the Sub-committee in Minorities. A member suggested that “Christianity and Islam, were essentially proselytising religions” \[6\] and thus it was imperative to let them convert people as per the tenets of their faiths.

Chairman of the advisory committee, Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel expressed the concern to fully satisfy the minorities (and hence adjust them into the project of building independent India, of which the constitution an instrument). His concern was understandable, as Clarehout and Roover note-

> “\[…\]a discussion in the British Parliament claimed that the British
> Government had a special responsibility and obligation for the
> protection of minorities’ interests, asserting they had a more special
> interest than others. Several debates in the House of Lords and House
> of Commons dismissed the Constituent Assembly as a “Caste Hindu”
> institution and a “body of Hindus” that would create a “Hindu Raj”
> oppressing minorities. Responding to these statements, Vallabhbhai
> Patel noted that it was for India to prove this claim false and that
> *no one could be more interested than India in protecting its
> minorities. He concluded that their mission was to satisfy every
> minority and safeguard their interests to their satisfaction*.” \[7\]

Later on, in the Constituent Assembly Debates, we see K.M.Munshi talk about how in his region the days of coercive conversion are gone, and the only conversions that take place are ‘result of persuasion and not material advantages’. In his view the secular state can not grant advantages to one community or the other, hence “the word ‘propagate’ can not possibly have dangerous implications”. He further states:

> “Moreover, **I was a party from the very beginning to the**
> ***compromise with the minorities, which ultimately led to many of
> these clauses being inserted in the Constitution*** **and I know it
> was on this word that the Indian Christian community laid the greatest
> emphasis, not because they wanted to convert people aggressively, but
> because the word “propagate” was a fundamental part of their tenet.**
> Even If the word were not there, I am sure, under the freedom of
> speech which the Constitution guarantees it will be open to any
> religious community to persuade other people to join their faith. ”
> \[8\]

Munshi’s statements clearly demonstrate that the insertion of the word ‘propagate’ was a compromise with the (Christian) minority. But if it would be covered under free speech anyways, why were Christians so adamant on inserting this one word, calling it a fundamental tenet?

The exchange later ended with Govind Ballabh Pant calling it “at worst redundant”. There was a simple voting by raising hands and the amendment was accepted. In the words of Roover:

> “no clarity was created about the points of disagreement to be
> discussed in the plenary Assembly. It was simply set aside by reducing
> the question to one of voting for or against retaining a specific word
> in the Constitution, without knowing what this word ‘propagate’ meant
> and what the right would imply.” \[9\]

But what did the right to propagate imply exactly? And what exactly did the Christians find inadequate in the rights of free expression that would be completed by including ‘propagation’ as a fundamental right?

On barring minors from conversion, member Reverend J.J.M Nichols Roy protested

> “\[…\]there is the spiritual aspect of conversion, that is, the
> connection of the soul of man with God, which must not be overlooked
> by this House. \[…\]When a boy feels that he is called by God to adopt
> a different faith, no law should prevent him from doing that.” \[10\]

Purushottam Das Tandon expressed surprise on the responses by Christian members, and described how congressmen were against conversion in principle, and the induction of the right was a mere measure to keep the Christians on board:

> “**We** ***Congressmen deem it very improper to convert from one to
> another religion*** **or to take part in such activities and we are
> not in favour of this**. In our opinion it is absolutely futile to be
> keen on converting others to one’s faith. But *it is only at the
> request of some persons, whom we want to keep with us in our national
> endeavour that we accept this*. Now it is said that they have a right
> to convert young children to their faith. What is this? Really this
> surprises me very much.” \[11\]

A Muslim representative Tajamul Hussain expressed incomprehension of the need to convert others:

> “\[…\]why should I ask you to attain salvation according to my way, or
> why should you ask me to attain salvation according to your way?
>
> \[…\]If you start propagating religion in this country, you will
> become a nuisance to others. So far it has become a nuisance.” \[12\]

Loknath Mishra called the religious freedom clause a “charter for Hindu enslavement” because while it proclaimed the state to be secular, it granted ‘unjust’ rights of converting others. According to him, this could only mean the complete annihilation of Hindu religion. \[13\] Most importantly, Mishra concluded with a plea to drop the word ‘propagate’. \[14\]

When the debate was concluded, the vice-president called upon Dr. Ambedkar to reply on the amendment as the chairman of the drafting committee. Dr. Ambedkar stated that he had nothing to add on the matter and accepted only one amendment. When a member objected, and demanded a rather detailed explanation be given, the vice president expressed his inability in compelling Ambedkar to say anything more. The debate was then concluded and the right to propagate religion was included in Article 25 of the Constitution.

What is striking here is the two sides’ conception of conversion. One side believed that conversion was a matter of thoughtful deliberation, persuasion and maturity (hence minors couldn’t be allowed). Based on their leanings, some were opposed to it as an interference in tradition, while some supported it as a matter of individual liberty. The Christian side was convinced of it being a fundamental tenet of their faith and a matter of innate human conscience, that guides all humans towards the ‘true’ path of salvation, and affirmation of the one ‘true’ God.

Thus, we see-

1.  Incomprehension by the Indian side on the whole premise of
    conversion itself. In their view it is futile-redundant at best and
    coercive-intruding at worst. Propagation was taken to imply
    dissemination of (religious) ideas and traditions, and thus
    coverable under freedom of expression etc. Disrupting others’
    traditions was a violation of their conscience.

2.  Conviction of the Christian side on converting being a fundamental
    tenet of their faith. Frustration at the fact that the other side is
    not able to realize the implications of their conceptualisation of
    religious conversion where propagation is a core duty, and the word
    of God to be propagated is above the realm of human expressed ideas.
    Freedom to convert was essential to freedom of religion itself.

As we will see in the further parts, this theme has been recurring since the earliest encounters with missionaries, and continues even today.

# **The Problem With Conversion**

In this section we seek to identify the causes behind the deep seated hostility towards conversion. For two centuries, the themes of distrust and ignorance of the other side’s postulated has pervaded the cultural encounters between Christian evangelical missionaries and Hindus. We will attempt to uncover what is at stake in this conflict.

As stated earlier, to narrow this down to a dispute between progressive secular liberals and regressive revivalist Hindu nationalists would be injustice.

## Perspectives

Speaking in response to an anti-conversion law in Himachal Pradesh, Archbishop Felix Machado of Vasai Diocese explained that for Christians it goes against religious freedom:

> “All human beings, he said, are born with the right to choose in
> conscience the religion of their choice. But *the understanding in
> India is that all religions are good, and everyone born in a religion
> should not change it. Any conversion is coercion, they say*.” \[15\]

Responding to claims from the Niyogi Committee Report in the early 1950s for enquiring into Christian missionary activities, preacher A. Soares writes:

> “It is not something imposed from outside. It is something which moves
> from inside. It is the response of the human heart to the attraction
> of Christ, his life, his works, his teachings, and his death. The
> missionary does not claim to convert a man. That is beyond his power.
> It is the grace of God acting on a humble and generous heart that
> affects the conversion…*A forced conversion is, therefore, a
> contradiction in terms*.” \[16\]

Compare this to this statement by a Hindu journal editor from 1843:

> “\[…\]while the missionaries were at liberty to teach their doctrines
> in any and every way they liked to the Europeans, “whose national
> religion it was”, they had no right to revile and interfere with the
> religion of the people of India.”

Meanwhile in his open letter to the Pope during the papal visit, Swami Dayananda Saraswati wrote:

> “Religions that are committed by their theologies to convert, on the
> other hand, are necessarily aggressive, since *conversion implies a
> conscious intrusion into the religious life of a person, in fact, into
> the religious person.* …Any protest against religious conversion is
> always branded as persecution, because it is maintained that people
> are not allowed to practise their religion, that their religious
> freedom is curbed. The truth is entirely different. The other person
> also has the freedom to practise his or her religion without
> interference. That is his/her birthright. *Religious freedom does not
> extend to having a planned programme of conversion*. Such a programme
> is to be construed as aggression against the religious freedom of
> others.” \[17\]

These statements represent that Indian society contains “two conflicting but related sentiments: the first is that *conversion inflicts violence*; the second is that *restrictions on conversion inflict violence*.” \[18\]

All the legislations and statements given above share a common disregard for coercive and deceptive conversion. But here we encounter an even bigger problem. What even is the meaning of genuine and fraudulent conversion?

> “Discussing the Himachal Pradesh Freedom of Religion Act (2007), the
> National Commission for Minorities expressed concerns about its
> terminology, noting that the definition of “force” includes “threat of
> divine displeasure” and “social excommunication,” neither of which is
> considered an offense in the Indian legal system. Other
> anti-conversion laws have drawn similar criticism, with local pastors
> suggesting “inducement” could encompass anything, including giving
> alms to the poor. Critics also highlight that the language used is
> often extraordinarily broad and vague, making the imprecision of
> definitions like “fraud” apparent and the potentially broad scope of
> “allurement” troubling. Each of these definitions, such as those in
> the Orissa Act, is amenable to varied interpretations, and the scope
> for abuse is inherent. As one scholar questions, how would one prove
> “misrepresentation” of metaphysical beliefs, or could a judge assess a
> “threat” involving divine displeasure, or determine whether converts
> have been forced, lured, or tricked?
>
> The difficulty in understanding Indian disputes about conversion is
> not merely one of obscurity in meaning that could be resolved by
> clearer definitions or by identifying how different parties use terms
> like “conversion,” “religion,” and “force” differently. Instead, a
> more general confusion is at play.”

## The Hindu Meets the Missionaries

Roover mentions an interesting incident with the French 17th-century traveller François Bernier

> “\[…\]when he told some Brahmins that their laws clearly were a mere
> human invention since those laws could not possibly be followed in
> cold countries during winter: they said that God had made their law
> only for them, rather than for all of humanity, and refused to hear
> that Christianity was ‘the general religion for the whole earth and
> that theirs could only be a fable and pure fabrication’” \[19\]

Similarly, when the Tamils were informed that they were heathens worshipping false gods, they responded by emphasising how in every faith there are good people, and that God wished to be worshipped in a variety of ways (hence the diverse religions in the world). \[20\]

One and half century later, the Europeans were met with a similar attitude regarding the matter of adopting another religion. This time, in Karnataka:

> “After a few visits \[by the Missionary and his assistant\], when
> information had increased a little, there was still a manifest
> disinclination to accept the truth. Because, for a Hindu to *be told
> that in order to salvation he must forsake the idols which his
> forefathers have worshipped for hundreds of years, and adopt the creed
> laid down in the Shastras of another nation, is to him the height of
> absurdity.* And it very frequently happened that at the conclusion of
> a sermon the *Missionary would hear someone say, ‘Very good, all very
> true; your religion is good for you, and ours is good for us’.*”
> \[21\]

Later on in 1860, an orientalist was informed by Pandits in Kashi that their refusal to engage was not an admission of defeat, but rather the expression of the unreasonability of Christian religion in their view. They highlighted the core of the problem, that was effectively short-circuiting the debate:

> “We (Pandits) admit that there is not an impossibility in conceiving
> wrongness in the Vedanta, and so do not refuse to listen to the
> arguments intended to show its wrongness. But *if our opponent will
> not accept the terms which occur in the Vedanta books, in the same
> sense in which we use them when explaining the theory, he is not an
> opponent to us but to a man of straw framed by himself. Hence, an
> imaginary refutation of the theory, in the shape in which we accept
> it, or his unfitness to refute it, makes us indifferent about what he
> says of Vedanta, and does not raise a desire in us to hear what theory
> of religion he himself holds*.” \[22\]

Another missionary, William Smith, in his *‘Dwij: The Conversion of a Brahman to the Faith of Christ*’ (1850) noted this widespread Hindu conception that Christianity was absolutely fine for Christians, just like Islam was fine for Muslims (in pleasing God). It was supposed to be one of the infinite manifestations of the same divine. “No Hindu will quarrel with you on this point”, he noted, and those who did were termed bigots! \[23\]

In another debate with a Christian scholar, a Hindu scholar noted how any faith was not true/false in the empirical sense and that:

> “According to the nature of these several doctrines, do they severally
> obtain rewards from God. As long, therefore, *as the inhabitants of
> India observe the ordinances prescribed to them in the sacred books
> prevalent here, they obtain the favour of God; and, on the other hand,
> the people here who embrace the religion of foreigners only depart
> farther from God.* Of this we have experimental proof.” \[24\]

God was compared to a father who gave different sets of rules to his sons based on their capabilities, but with a single goal of their welfare in mind. For the Christians however, it was absurd to argue that God would give *contradictory* instructions to different nations. God’s word was free of all contradictions and human falsity, which if present were proof of the text’s inferior impure nature.

The missionaries took upon themselves the ‘noble and pious’ task of converting Indians with great zeal. Roover, for example, quotes a missionary from Kashi, William Buyers, who says that the fact that idol worship and ‘superstition’ was thriving in India “involves a responsibility the most tremendous that can possibly be conceived.” This responsibility involved uplifting the ‘fallen’ souls of these ‘heathen’ nations and having them practice a life of Christian morals “from cradle to grave.”\[25\]

Similar convictions were shared by George Gogerly who wondered about the magnitude of the work that was to be carried out to demolish the mighty system of ‘superstition’, ‘cruelty’ and hateful rites. The “dreadful power” that was holding ‘millions enslaved”. \[26\]

Obviously, the notorious effort met with resentment, as noted by a newspaper:

> “Moreover the Hindoos never attack or find fault with the religion of
> others; but these white-faced, crafty missionaries, whose sole aim it
> is to destroy the religion of other people, have erected in different
> places tiled or thatched houses (bungalow native chapels), where they
> stand with fear-inspiring looks, and, agreeably to the doctrines of
> their own foreign shaster, called the Bible, proclaim the acts and
> praises of the Son of Lady Mary, lifting up both hands, and moving
> backwards and forwards, as if they were dancing; and by every kind of
> wily contrivance, are destroying the religion and caste of the
> Hindoos.”

Another instance of attempts to convert children led to them being called “the most cruel people in the world” \[27\]

Hindus of Madras found the conversion enabling *Lex Loci Act 1850* “a palpable invasion of their ancient rights, *a direct attack upon their religion,* and a peremptory subversion of their ancestral and inalienable law.” \[28\]

These debates had one common feature: both sides spoke in distinct worldviews and metaphysical categories; and thus never really reached any meaningful ground. This would later prove problematic for the Hindu side. As seen above, first there was no attempt to bridge the conversation from either side. Later, when the British government seemed to be enabling the missionaries, the Hindus decided to voice concern in western terms. They appealed for the restoration of the values of tolerance and non-interference, which were supposed to be upheld by the British state. However, the state found it perplexing because this is exactly what it was doing. For the state, non-interference also meant no restrictions on missionaries, but Hindus perceived the same as being violated by the conversion efforts. Later when the western educated Indians were charged with framing a constitution for *independent* India, they encountered the same fundamental dissonance, and as seen in the previous section, chose to brush it aside. This brings us to our next subtopic of discussion: western education in colonial India.

### Education as a Tool for Conversion

As seen above, missionaries were frustrated with the ease with which Hindus accepted the contradictions in their tradition and texts, and how comfortable Hindus were in accepting Christianity as a valid path to the divine, suited for Europeans.

In ‘*Matapariksha’* a text critical of Hinduism, which sought to establish Christian supremacy, John Muir attempted a solution to this problem. He opined that Indian ignorance to European terms and ideals was the central hindrance and by clearly expounding the terms-implications, the gap could be bridged. Education and reform thus came to be perceived as the new viable avenue.

Ballantyne, the principal of Benares Govt. College and a noted orientalist, expressed his distate for proselytising attitudes of evangelists and expressed another way:

> “My proposed end is the *making of each educated Hindu a Christian*, –
> on principle and conviction. This end, as I propose here to indicate,
> implies every thing that the amplest course of education can comprise.
> Let us trace the assertion backwards, – as thus. *That a Hindu should
> on principle and conviction, embrace a religion which, like
> Christianity, bases its claims on historical evidence, presupposes not
> merely an acquaintance with historical assertions, but a cultivation
> of the critical faculty, so as that the force of the historical
> evidence may be intelligently felt*.” \[29\]

Missionary William Smith struck gold when he noticed that Hindu pupils were rather easily swayed by assertions of ‘facts’. Clarehout and Roover describe how when the structure of the universe was being explained in the class, the pupils first repeated the Hindu mythological explanations. When it was proved that those were in fact not ‘true’, and that ‘scientific’ explanations were correct, it, in the words of Smith, caused the student’s confidence to be “misplaced in one part of Hinduism, he naturally and of necessity began to feel it might be so in other parts too.” This solves the ignorance problem Muir described earlier:

> “In other words, the frustration was that Indians did not look at the
> Puranas and other texts in the right way. The aim of the missionary,
> then, was for the Indians to begin to view their texts and stories in
> a different way than before: namely, as collections of factual claims,
> which were incompatible with the facts discovered by the sciences.
> Once they learned about scientific theories and results, they would
> then see how ridiculous their traditional texts and stories really
> were and reject them as falsity and superstition.” \[30\]

Scottish missionary Alexander Duff perceived this discovery as an engine to demolish Indian cultural systems and compared it to Newton discovering gravitation after the trivial incident of the falling apple. The seeds of disbelief and suspicion being sown were of huge importance, as Duff says:

> “\[…\]there was now the sudden injection of a doubt, where all doubt
> was believed to be impossible; there was the sudden starting of
> suspicion, where suspicion was believed to be an insult to the memory
> of an immortal ancestry – an impious contempt of the authority of the
> gods.”\[31\]

“Scientific temper” and “rational thinking” were presented as **strict anti-thesis of tradition** and ‘superstition’. The purpose of the education in colonial institutions was thus the purification of the native religion through reform, which would eventually lead the natives to perceive the inherent truth value of Christianity. The same talking points are alive even today in the Constitution’s article 51A which proclaims it a fundamental duty of all Indians to “*develop the scientific temper, humanism and the spirit of inquiry and reform*.”

Thus, we observe the following:

1.  One side says that Indian traditions are false, corrupt and
    superstitious and hence Indians would be better off converting to
    the *true* religion of Christianity. They present arguments rooted
    in Christian theology for the same.

2.  The other side believes that all traditions are different ways of
    reaching the divine, suited for their respective nations for
    historical and cultural reasons. Thus conversion is uncalled for,
    and attempts to convert are a seriously violent intrusion into their
    life.

3.  Both sides, while arguing based on completely different systems,
    however use the same set of terminology from the English language,
    rooted in Judeo-Christian traditions (e.g. *religion, truth,
    falsity, divine origin, God, conversion* etc.). Not only does this
    make the platform skewed, it also greatly contributes to further
    entangling the already serious dispute, as

    1.  Neither side is able to make sense of the other.

    2.  The medium belonging to one side grants an obvious unfair
        advantage, esp. when the institutions being appealed to are
        ‘liberal-secular’\* in nature.

In the second and final part, we will analyse precisely this- the Christian conception of Conversion, Gandhi’s writings on the matter being representative of Hindu points of view in the twentieth century, and at last, the question of state neutrality in a broader sense.
