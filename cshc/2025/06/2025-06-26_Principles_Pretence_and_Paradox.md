In the previous part ‘A Promise Of Paradox’, we discussed the structural and conceptual issues with the liberal-secular model of secularism. In this one, we will discuss its trajectory in the Indian landscape while trying to point out the specific undercurrents. We will also have a brief look at the harsh political realities surrounding the subjects of secularism and its meaning in India’s political, cultural and social parlance.

# Different Contexts

## Western

As discussed in the previous part of this article, secularism as a concept acquires significant difference in meanings depending on the relevant cultural context. In this portion, we shall have a brief look at its evolution and relevance in western and non-western contexts. According to Toynbee,

> the distinctive Medieval Western Christian way of life began to break
> down under the pontificate of Pope Innocent III (fungebatur A.D. 1198-
> 1216), after a period of growth which had gone forward for about a
> century and a half.

In this background, three factors are of note:

1.  Breakdown of Christian morals

2.  Theological controversies

3.  Religious-sectarian conflict/intolerance

In the same period, as curiosities grew and discoveries made, science, and the truths established through it became a potential answer; a way to address these deep seated divides and bridge the gaps in the society.

Sprat, in The History of the Royal Society, says:

> "Whatever other hurt or good comes by such holy speculative wars (of
> which whether the benefit or mischief overweighs, I will not now
> examine), yet certainly by this means the knowledge of Nature has been
> very much retarded. . . . The wit of men has been profusely poured out
> on Religion, which needed not its help, and which was only thereby
> made more tempestuous, while it might have been more fruitfully spent
> on some parts of Philosophy which have been hitherto barren and might
> soon have been made fertile.
>
> Experimental Philosophy will prevent men's spending the strength of
> their thoughts about disputes, by turning them to work.

And Hobbes writes:

> The doctrine of right and wrong is perpetually disputed, both by the
> pen and the sword, whereas the doctrine of lines and figures is not
> so, because men care not, in that subject, what be truth, as a thing
> that crosses no man's ambition, profit, or lust

This clearly demonstrates how the need for an alternative was felt, in wake of religious fanaticism, persecution and wars. As clear in the attitude of writers quoted above, the outlook towards science was that of autonomy and independence from religious ways of ascertaining truth. That is, science would be pursued without interference in a pragmatically oriented way. As we shall see, this ‘secularised’ form would later pose a rival to religion itself. The below stated three factors encapsulate much of the grievances against the existing religious parlance. For one, as AK Saran writes:

> “it is implied that fanaticism is not something wholly extraneous to
> religion but unavoidably incidental to it because religious thinking
> and beliefs lack any method for coming to an agreement. Let us not
> miss the profound significance of this argument. Not only is it the
> beginning of all positivism and the modern temper; it is, in fact, not
> an argument, but an accusation.”

It is to be noted here that secularism didn’t make science replace religion, but it infact was a step in the direction of resolving the conflict between the two (just as it represented the resolution of the one between the Church and nation-states). Saran chooses to call this principle, **“Tangential Co-Existence”**, i.e. the arrangement where the interactions between coexisting science/state and religion are ideally minimal. Thus it will be sound reasoning to state that in western societies, secularism has played the role of a ‘mediator’ in the relations between religion and politics/science.

## Indian

Religious pluralism was not exactly an alien idea to India before our encounter with the west. Similarly persecution was also more or less present in the form of Islamic invaders waging holy wars etc. However unlike the West, Indians always sought the solution of these conflicts within the religious frameworks. Saran goes on to study the possible causes for this:

> “This was partly due to the fact that "scientific" investigation of
> nature was not thrown up from within the Indian tradition, Hindu or
> Muslim; partly also because the nature of religious pluralism, heresy
> and persecution was importantly different from what it was in the
> Western context.”

Let us have a look at Ram Swarup describing how the reason-religion dynamic plays out in the Indian tradition:

> In mystic tradition, faith and reason are united in a happy marriage.
> But when they stand apart, they fall both in worth and status. Faith
> lacking in philosophy becomes narrow, ignorant and intolerant;
> philosophy without faith ends in mere doubts and denials and makes a
> man footloose, spiritually speaking. Such philosophy is not wisdom or
> prajna of Yoga, but merely prajnavada, pretence to wisdom. This kind
> of philosophy lacks discrimination, will, strength, purpose. It
> becomes blind. It entertains every idea as equally valid without
> looking into its heart. It admits every claim or pretence backed by
> sufficient force. It cowers before any fanatic creed. It happened with
> the pagan religions of Greece and Rome; it happened and is still
> happening with Hinduism; on another level, it is happening with the
> democratic world. Liberal forces are in retreat before the onslaught
> of fanatic ideologies.

Saran further draws from van Leeuwen, and points out three things of relevance that better explain the ramifications of the history-centrist attitude.

- The Judaeo-Christian tradition rejects the mythical world-view in
  which man, the world and the gods constitute a total reality,
  self-existent and self-sufficient, without Creator, God or history.
  Rejection of this gives to man's life and history in this world, a new
  and, in some sense, absolute reality.

- In the Old Testament, man is given a superior role in relation to the
  rest of the creation. This opens the way for the conquest of nature.

- According to van Leeuwen, this tradition emphasises the Kingship of
  God and thus, in effect, makes human kingship and all other
  socio-political institution non-divine or secular: "The relation
  between the possible within a society wherein religion is a living
  Hebrew monarch and his people was as nearly secular as is force.”

Now it becomes clear that in this view, man and nature were both God’s creations. Thus when the Christian god was deposed, nature was now no threat to man in claiming the divine vacancy. This **worldview of absolute creation and monotheism, is exactly opposite to the sacred and cosmic-order worldview** of Hinduism etc.

Ram Swarup accurately pens down the stark contrast:

> The Hindu approach is speculative, pluralistic, introvert; the
> Christian and the Islamic approach is extrovert, monolithic, dramatic
> and spectacular. There is Adam and Eve, the forbidden fruit, the Devil
> and the Original Sin. Then the only God sends his only Son and he pays
> the ransom for the original transgression with his blood. Then there a
> final Day of Judgment and a throne surrounded by angels. On this day,
> some are saved but many more are damned. These differences are not
> merely differences of idioms and expressions. They are of a more
> fundamental nature. They arise because the two scriptures deal with
> two different though not unconnected subjects.

While one represents man’s power over nature, the other represents man’s bondage to the powers of the cosmos, and navigating the way around it. With its Christian roots, Saran says, “secularity today consists in the realisation of man's autonomous responsibility.”

Wilfred Smith presents another interesting perspective to analyse the genesis and growth of secularist ideas in European society.

> modern Western civilisation (alone among the great cultures of man) is
> dual. It is explicitly a civilisation composed of two traditions,
> which it has never integrated: one from Greece and Rome, one from
> Palestine. Throughout, these two have existed and developed side by
> side: sometimes in conflict, sometimes in uneasy tension, sometimes in
> harmony, but never fused. Though they have influenced each other
> profoundly, they have remained distinct. For the West it is axiomatic
> that such things as grammar, and hygiene, and politics (which it
> derives from its Greco-Roman background) are to be distinguished from
> matters of the spiritual life (from the Palestinian, Biblical
> heritage). And in both its traditions it is deeply persuaded that the
> distinction is important and ought to be retained-the discrimination
> between the secular (the word originally means, in effect, the
> 'historical') and the religious.

This line of thought strongly suggests that:

1.  Secularization is not an inherently Christian idea but mayhaps the
    result of historical experience of the dual heritage.

2.  This division did not take place in Islamic theology because of the
    absence of similar circumstances.

3.  Secularism having non-Christian sources implies that there is a
    possible common context for growth of it in India too.

To summarise, the step from sectarianism and persecution to science and technology taken by the West was unique in the sense of attempting solutions outside the religious paradigm. Saran holds that in India, this “was a step not taken in India independently of Western encounter and conquest.”

# Secularism In Independent India

The word ‘secular’ is derived from Latin ‘saeculum’ which means ‘present age’. Being secular thus implies being concerned with the present age and being involved in the affairs of this world. Hence secular becomes opposed to the sacred, in being dissociated to other-worldly, eternal, non-temporal affairs. Secularization therefore means the demarcation of lines between the spheres of the sacred and the political. Each is supposed to function independently of each other. This is basically “a political use of the concept of secularism, since it is based on the separation and balance of powers.” \[Saran\]

The theories of secularism can be analyzed in three concepts:

1.  Contemporaneity: corresponding to theological facets

2.  Co-Existence: corresponding to political facets

3.  Autonomy: corresponding to economic-philosophical facets.

It must be noted that these three are mere tools to conveniently understand secularism, and not mutually exclusive categories/types.

> If the religious (sacred)-secular dichotomy is maintained, the third
> is the fundamental meaning of secularism: all the other meanings that
> have been mentioned here either presuppose it, imply it, or logically
> lead to it. More specifically, the second interpretation
> (co-existence) cannot be maintained without an eventual acceptance of
> the third (autonomy) interpretation.

### Roots In The Freedom Movement

Now, we shall take a look at the Indian freedom movement. Among others, nationalism, reform etc were the main ideas that shaped it. Saran explains it better:

> Nationalism had at once a secular and a Hindu face, the latter
> obviously involving a kind of religious revivalism. Again, what is
> generally called the Renaissance in India was a rather curious
> phenomenon. It represented strong tendencies towards Anglicization and
> Westernization; at the same time, it zealously promoted patriotism and
> Indianness.

The nationalists movement is, by nature, a secular concept which doesn’t find much meaning outside secular framework. **The churning of various political and ideological forces in India were giving rise to Hindu Nationalism, which is, in a way, a contradictory term**. Post-Independence, the Indian state formally adopted secularism but Hindu Nationalism still remains a potent social force. It is in fact the main decisive force against the secularist camp, while being a combination of “two heterogenous ideas, Hinduism and Nationalism.” Amid the pre-existing conflict between Hindus and Muslims, the Hindu/Muslim nationalist here would be someone who subordinated their concerns of Muslim/Hindu dominance to fight against British rule. The communalist Hindu/Muslim would be one whon subordinated their concerns regarding British paramountcy and hold Muslim/Hindu dominance to be a bigger threat to deal with.

### Role Of Gandhi

This interplay of secular and counter-secular forces being carried by the national movement can be very well understood by studying Gandhi’s role on the scene and the consequences of his choices. Under Gandhi’s leadership the INC, earlier confined to elite-educated sections of society, now became a party with wide appeal to even common-folk. The struggle was now transformed

> from an elite political process for liberalising the British Rule into
> a mass movement for Swadeshi and Swaraj and against the materialistic
> values of the modern Western Civilisation; India's opposition to
> British Imperialism was seen as a vanguard action in a global war
> against all forms of exploitation; and thirdly, the very nature of
> politics was changed: it was no longer to be a power game but a
> patient collective effort for the advent of a just society and the
> (moral and spiritual) upliftment of man. All these were but aspects of
> a total vision whose essence was radical sincerity and which was
> focused on redeeming a tragic falsification of the Indian
> consciousness that was the fruit of our deep-seated ambivalence
> towards British Rule.

His ways of life and ideas defined the movement as an embodiment of the struggle of the oppressed of the world against colonial exploitation. Thus, making the fight for a better world order in his conception and not just a free India. This expanded the scope of the freedom movement from a national campaign to a universal, and human mission. Saran compares this to the global scope of the Marxist movement.

Gandhi adopted and advocated a socio-political order based on intrinsically Hindu ideas of ahimsa (non-violence), aparigraha (non possession), satya(truth) etc. He modified the concepts of both nationalism and Hindu revivalism by seeking to circumvent the problems of unity and identity, which according to him lay at the root of the controversy.

We now reach a curious situation. Gandhi’s stance can be described as:

- secular; if the meaning of secularism is taken as religious tolerance
  and concern for practical needs of the people

- counter-secular, if we perceive his vision as being rooted in
  spiritual values and the suggestion of a socio-political order that
  was built to solve India’s problems through a spiritual movement.

### ‘Modal Ontology and Negative Metaphysics’

India’s medieval history is filled with violent clashes between the zealous Islamic invaders and Hindus. The animosity never really went away, and continued (and still continues) manifesting in the form of different social and political complications. The communal question thus becomes one of the most important issues that have to be addressed in the study of the interplay of secular and non secular forces. This is crucial to even the fundamental idea of India’s unity. Going the Gandhian, ‘spiritual solutions’ way, the solution to the Hindu-Muslim problem is supposed to be found in the spiritualization of religion and re-spiritualizing of the socio-political order. This is exactly what Gandhi tried to do. However, as Saran makes clear, he

> “(…)accomplished it only for the Hindus: there occurred no parallel
> movement among the Muslims for the moral-spiritual unity with the
> Hindus. The line of medieval Sufis was not continued. While Gandhi
> could not speak to the Muslims as their religious leader, it is
> doubtful if he was successful in spiritualising even Hinduism. The
> difficulty here seems to be a fundamental one. The traditional Hindu
> idea of toleration and unity is conceived at the transcendent-mystic
> level: the idea is that ultimate Truth and Reality is beyond thought,
> word and manifestation; hence all formulated religious truths are true
> and false at the same time. They can be but modes of the Absolute.
> Hence from this point of view a Hindu can accept the validity of other
> religions and their revelations and prophets without in any way
> compromising the absoluteness of his own religion, its doctrine or
> faith. This provides the basis for the Hindus' acceptance of the truth
> of the other religions. Let us call this basis **modal ontology and
> negative metaphysics**.”

However, since in the other tradition, the acceptance of other religions as full/partial truths itself is classified as heresy or even blasphemy, there was no reciprocation of this initiative from their side. This reflects how Gandhi (and historically, Hindus) have been largely ignorant of the sheer absence of the grounds of mutual understanding with Islamic tradition.

This lack of depth made the idea dysfunctional. Instead of bringing about the common grounds for Hindu-Muslim coexistence religiously, it backfired, and “appeared to others to be only a subtle and disguised form of Hinduization.” In simple words, Gandhi’s policy wrt tolerant religious engagement found no reception on the Muslim side and ended up being counter-productive by suggesting an underlying aim of imposition of adverse values to Islamists.

In this case, while the shortcoming of Gandhian ideas proved to be negatively consequential, one could seek an alternate basis- not spiritual but secular. A number of leaders had such visions, of industrialization, urbanization, technological society etc for their country. These however, were underplayed and kept in the back of the national scene. The reason for this was obvious:

> “Both visions could, however, be realised only in a free India, and it
> was clear by now that mass support was essential for success in the
> freedom struggle. Such support was commanded by Gandhi alone.”

This lack of adequate critique and questioning prevented the reframing of the model that could make it jointly acceptable for Hindus and Muslims. But apparently, it was deliberately kept on the backseat, thus protecting the congress organisation at the cost of the development of broader Indian unity.

When INC accepted the partition of India, it was a betrayal of the nationalist cause. To quote Saran at length:

> Though the unity of India had been a serious problem for long, the
> acceptance of Pakistan as a solution was indeed a tragic paradox. I do
> not pretend to understand it, but, in addition to vested economic and
> political interest among the supporters of the Congress and the Muslim
> League to which Dr. Abid Husain refers, another important thing was
> the Hindu betrayal of the nationalist Muslim cause. The Hindus once
> again failed to rise to that level of integrity which their religion
> required of them and once again allowed hypocrisy and unrealism to
> dominate their feelings and actions. Only by betraying their
> respective political ideologies and taking refuge in political
> unrealism (hoping that a free India would be able to reunite
> Pakistan), could both Patel and Nehru agree on Partition in spite of
> their very different political philosophies. Patel and Nehru were not
> prepared to follow Gandhi and let go this opportunity, wait for
> freedom and engage in another struggle. Gandhi's attitude was
> courageous and revolutionary; the course that the Congress actually
> followed was neither. In our opinion, the roots of the religious,
> spiritual and political weakness the Hindus and the Congress leaders
> showed in this great crisis have to be traced back to the religious
> and spiritual pseudo-withdrawal with which theHindus had, in the
> Middle Ages, responded to the Islamic challenge. At the same time it
> is also perhaps a fact that Muslim theologians and religious leaders
> did not succeed in bringing about any real meeting of minds between
> the two religions on the spiritual plane.

> Hindus are a terrorised society which has internalized and made a

### The Solution In Question

We must question whether partition provided a lasting solution for the above described problems or not. The creation of the Islamic state of Pakistan, and the adoption of secular outlook by the independent Indian state still having a sizable Muslim population, pose a variety of curiosities.

1.  Does this arrangement really take care of the communal conflict?

2.  Why is it required for the Hindu majority of independent India to
    have a different attitude for Muslims who are Pakistani nationals?

3.  How does the acceptance of partition not admit the failure of the
    nationalist idea?

There are many more such questions, but neither is it possible to list them all here, nor to answer each. Therefore I present a short analysis in attempt to understand the situation.

This arrangement is clearly another variation of the “compensatory/redemptive reaffirmation” of Indian unity. The implication of a secular India would be that eventually, at some point in time, India’s secularism could work not only for friendly ties but some form of ‘unity’ between India and Pakistan. We know however that the governments and states of both are strictly opposed to this. Further, Saran points out the two main reasons for the avoidance of unity:

1.  It annoys Pakistani Muslims because it threatens the very reason of
    their existence.

2.  It annoys Indian Muslims because Pak satisfies their desire for a
    non-secular Islamic state for Indian Muslims.

Political secularism in India can therefore not be a a real thing, because it lacks the very socio-political foundations required. We are short on space here, so we will only briefly mention the relevant conclusions. (Detailed exposition can be found in books mentioned at the end of this article.)

1.  This arrangement presents a double and divided loyalty to one
    Islamic and one secular state. Thus, from a Muslim pov, the secular
    one shall always be inferior, a second best option.

2.  If Hindus deny the existence of Indo-Pak unity, there can be no real
    acceptance of Muslim in current India, and the secular state is
    founded merely on toleration of each other.

3.  As long as Pakistan exists, it will always be a potential cause of
    distrust between Hindus and Muslims of India.

4.  Point 3 directly implies that Pakistan can never be completely
    disinvested in the relations of Indian Hindus and Muslims. Pakistan
    will always seek to make Indian Muslims potentially reject the
    secular state, thus undermining the sovereignty of the secular
    Indian state.

5.  Indo-Pak relations are unique in the sense that the wars, past and
    future between India and Pakistan acquire a religious character. If
    Hindus and Muslims in India unite under secularism, it may even be
    called a kind of civil war.

## The Plan

Just like nationalism in colonial India, modernisation can be called a characteristic feature of secularisation in independent India. It is notable that generally, while explaining the scope of modernisation of India, it is stressed that this specific model does not completely reject traditional ways, instead it seeks to modify them as per needs of the current era- to reform them. This endeavour is not supposed to be anti-religious, or even non-religious.

Thus, ‘reform’ comes out as the primary initiative the modernisation campaign seeks to pursue. The genesis of the concept of ‘modern India’ took place with the Hindu reform movements . These were, Saran believes,

> one of the major pre-independence expressions of secularism as against
> revivalism. Social reform, thus, became not an obligation (unlike the
> imperial state) but an obligatory task, a duty for the secular Indian
> state; which was pursuing central planning under Nehru, the chief
> agent of change in which, is the state itself.

The independent state ‘reformed’ Hindu personal law, while not touching Muslim personal law. This is secular because it guarantees religious freedom to minorities, and non-secular because it is discriminatory on lines of religion. Modernising development here is in apparent conflict with religious neutrality. The principle of separation of spheres (discussed in previous part) is of no assistance, because an unorganised and expansive religion like Hinduism is practically impossible to clearly demarcate. Supposing somehow we succeed in even that, the separation of the spheres sacred and profane, religious and secular will be on a **prior commitment to modernity** (and not Hindu/Muslim traditions). The idea of utilising religious ideas to further the modernisation is problematic. Apart from the obvious violation of neutrality, this also digresses from the enthusiastic claim of ‘religious’ reform- as exploiting religious beliefs for non-religious motives is irreligious (even disrespectful in certain contexts).

Saran writes:

> (…)the idea of synthesis with and preservation of Indian values is
> always maintained. In doing this we are making a grave error. For,
> this **synthesis syndrome** induces in us a wholly false sense of
> immunity to the serious consequences of a modern, technologically
> oriented society and an absolutelY secular world-view, and prevents us
> from realising that, instead of advancing to a definite goal, we may
> be just drifting; instead of planning our way, we are only trying to
> muddle through.

When the official and non-official claims of a modernised India ‘retaining’ the best of its traditional characteristics are made, we are not informed which values will be preserved as heritage, which ones given up as dogma from the middle ages. Even if it’s one or the other, would that not interfere with the secular character of the state?

Coming back to planning, Saran posits that the planning is, in its basic form, no other than the problem of social order. It is essentially the problem of human freedom.

> (…) human freedom creates a gap between the part and the whole,
> between the past and the present on the one hand and the future on the
> other. It is the task of the social system to bridge this gap, this
> discontinuity. How can the social system do it? The Marxist answer,
> which is increasingly also that of the (non-Marxian) modern thinker,
> is that the gap is bridged by human planning.

The main implication here is the establishment of the human nature of the problem, thus suggesting a certain solution of it within human capabilities. Human freedom becomes absolute and therefore the basis of morality must be sought within it. In this form, where all plans in principle can be completely guaranteed, we face an obvious unavoidable paradox: greater certainty and detail in planning increases inflexibility, ultimately reducing real control over complex, changing systems. In simple words, the more one can predict, the less one can control. This essentially means that:

1.  If planning refers to enhancing prediction capability, then it is
    useless.

2.  If it refers to increasing the power to control, then it will always
    be incomplete.

3.  Long range of planning means less power to undo.

At another level, Saran says,

> it means that the problem of undesirable and unanticipated and
> unintended effects of technology can be solved by further technology.
> This again is self-inconsistent, for the remedial technology is bound
> to have its own unintended effects, and hence technology can only
> lengthen and strengthen the chain.

Planning is pursued keeping in mind the notion of ‘felt’ needs of people, yet it ends necessarily with the ‘*imputed*’ needs because of the scale and the futurity of the plan. Saran describes these imputed needs in a planned system as being analogous to induced needs in a free economy.

The induction of these imputed needs requires a coherent philosophy, which in India’s case is general welfare and socialism. Here we encounter another contradiction: these two ideas are irreconcilable. Welfare state is fundamentally a capitalist idea and will get eliminated as unnecessary and incompatible under a socialist state. Attempts to merge these retain capitalist dynamics, conflicting with the core socialist goal of abolishing private capital. Further, Saran writes:

> Our main point is that a socialist society, in terms of which welfare
> goals and, ultimately, the overall direction of social and economic
> planning are set, implies a positive secular ideology in the sense of
> the autonomy of man and an immanentist philosophy of history,
> rejecting the traditional mythical world-view. This is not true to the
> Indian tradition, and thus inconsistent with the idea of a spiritual
> and largely instrumental, negative secularism which India officially
> cherishes.

# Secularisation and Indian Tradition(s)

In this part, we will take a look at the historical trends and try to draw our conclusions regarding the present state of secularism and tradition. Hindu society through the ages has had considerable interactions with alien people. The Greeks, Scythians, Parthians, Huns etc ruled large parts of India for a significant time. They came as foreigners but later assimilated into the Hindu(or Buddhist) fold. The rise of Buddhism, but despite all the differences in philosophy, its metaphysical structure remained more or less the same, making it an alternative form and not a proper adversary of Hinduism.

The dynamics changed with the advent of Islam. Muslims had no interest in adhering to ‘*sanatana dharma*’; instead, invasion campaigns had special interest in destroying Hindu-Buddhist sacred spaces, killing priests and mass conversions by forcefully feeding beef etc ways of persecution. This added, as Saran says, a violent and foreign dimension to the landscape of religion in the subcontinent. For the first time, Hinduism witnessed the compulsion of living in a non-Hindu state. Under the circumstances where one culture was being subjugated by the other, the distinction between the religious and non-religious spheres became necessary for survival itself.

Hindu society responded in a manner that was mixed, and primarily took two directions which Saran concisely defines as:

1.  **Formalism and Ritualism**: the aspects which were previously
    integrated, now became formalised, end-in-themselves or means to
    essentially non-religious ends. E.g. dining and marriage
    restrictions.

2.  **Emotionalism and Interiorization**: interiorization can be looked
    at here as balancing the above hippocratic formalism. While the
    physical realm was overrun by the invader, religion had to take
    refuge in feelings and emotions, experience and devotion.

Centuries passed in resilient adherence to *dharma*, while physical and metaphysical territories were recaptured and lost again. Soon enough, the Europeans arrived, and with them, Christianity (formally).

This encounter was very different from all previous ones. The reasons can be understood as-

1.  Greeks et al were invaders **open to assimilation**, their faiths
    unopposed to synthesis with Indian ones.

2.  Islamic invaders were **resistant to assimilation**. Their
    religion’s expansion was **violently aided by their state**
    machinery.

3.  Christian powers were **resistant to assimilation**. Their
    differently mannered missionary activity was however, self-propelled
    i.e. their **accompanying imperial power didn’t identify with them
    the same way as (b.)**.

4.  Meanwhile the Hindu side was seeing a resurgence through uprooting
    the Mughal power, which indicates they were **disillusioned with
    assimilation and acceptance.**

These unique characteristics of the Christian encounter, according to Saran,

> gradually developed an asymmetrical relationship between Christianity
> and Westernise: **one could be Westernised without being a Christian
> but one could not be a Christian without being Westernised.**

The joint arrival of modernity and Christianity resulted in reform on the Hindu side. Christianity alone was not the adversary now, but the west as a civilization too. Here took root the idea of nation, the internal tension of which was between its conceptions secular and religious.

> But in the reform and renaissance movements of Hinduism that the
> encounter with the West produced, Hinduism and nationalism were
> almost, though asymmetrically, identified: that is to say, while all
> nationalists were not Hindus, all Hindu revivalists were nationalists;
> and the orthodox Hindus never attacked the idea o f Hindu nationalism,
> though nationalists have sometimes attacked Hinduism. From Tilak,
> Ranade, Dayananda, Annie Besant, Vivekänanda through Aurobindo, Tagore
> to Gandhi, Vinoba and Rädhäkrishnan, it is an unbroken line of those
> who have glorified religion and Hinduism, however radically they may
> have sought to reinterpret or reshape it.

The underlying idea here, as described earlier, was seeking solutions to modern problems in the religious sphere. A compromise or accomodation, whichever possible to be found. Saran calls this image akin to Shiva consuming poison and holding it in his throat (*neelkantha*: the one with the blue throat).

As we conclude, the questions that must have our attention are:

1.  Is the *neelkantha* approach still tenable?

2.  If not, then how should we begin the search for another?

Scholars like Sitaram Goel and Saran himself have written, how the reluctance to see the uniqueness of first Muslim and then Christian incursions proved to be fatal for Hindu society. I hope we learn from the past, to navigate the ages to come.

# Conclusion

The preceding analysis has sought to illuminate the complex and contradictory trajectory of India’s engagement with the liberal-secular model. We've delved into the profound structural and conceptual challenges inherent in its application, tracing its evolution against the unique backdrop of the Indian subcontinent and comparing it to the west. This calls for an intellectual and civic courage to dismantle entrenched pretences, paving the way for a redefined attitude to society, culture and politics. A question of principles, a call to shed pretences and a paradox of goals and outcomes.
