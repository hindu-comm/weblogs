# **1. Kala Amb**

*24th January 1761*

Peshwa Nanasaheb encamped at Bhilsa. Suddenly the guards apprehended of a moneylender carrying a cryptic note. The note, intended for the Peshwa, read: “Two Pearls have been dissolved, twenty-seven Gold Mohurs have been lost and of Silver and Copper, the total cannot be cast up...” \[1\] This message was supposed to deliver the unfortunate news of loss at Panipat. The two pearls—Sadashivrao Bhau, cousin of the Peshwa and Vishwasro Ballal, his heir apparent both martyred while leading thw Swarajya’s forces against Ahmed Shah Abdali. Gold Mohurs—eminent sardars like Janoji Shinde, Antaji Mankeshwar, Yashwantrao Pawar, etc attained Veergati on the battlefield. Countless officers and soldiers laid their lives in service of Swarajya. The loss struck Peshwa Nanasaheb with profound grief, especially due to the uncertainty surrounding Bhau's fate—his body had not been recovered, and some believed he might have survived. Clinging to hope, the Peshwa dispatched search parties to the north in an effort to locate him. Nana Fadnavis and Nana Purandare later met the Peshwa and gave him the first eye witness account of the battle. He still refused to accept Bhau’s death. But on 24th February 1761, Kasiraj Pandit, the diwan of Shuja ud Dawlah confirmed Sadashivrao Bhau’s demise and arranged for the cremation of both members of the Bhat family.\[2\] This was later corroborated by Anupgir Gosain.\[3\] According to the Bhausahebanchi Bakhar, overwhelmed by grief and a desire for retribution, Nanasaheb initially intended to march north with a force of nearly 40,000 troops. But Raghunathrao, Peshwa’s younger brother, strongly advised not to.\[4\] By this time, Nanasaheb was already in Jhansi and decided to proceed south as Abdali sent his vakils for an amicable peace treaty.

Even prior to the battle, Nanasaheb’s health was worsening by the day. His weight had drastically reduced from 83 kg to 53 kg.\[5\]The calm and calculated Peshwa became very rash while speaking to his chiefs.\[6\] By 2nd April, Nanasaheb entrusted the responsibility of north to Malharrao Holkar and began his journey south.\[7\] By 3rd June, the ailing Peshwa returned to Pune and isolates himself to find solace in building a bridge over the Mutha River. Despite his deteriorating condition, he remained unchallenged—no one dared to act against him. His stature continued to command respect across the subcontinent. Many kings and sovereigns looked up to him for alliance and safety. Letters from Madho Singh of Jaipur and Bijay Singh of Jodhpur requested for a united hindu front against Abdali.\[8\] Even Nizam Ali also disbanded the troops in Bidar.\[9\]

On 18th June, Nanasaheb went up to Parvati where he spent the last five days of his life. According to Panipatchi Bakhar by Raghunath Yadav, the Peshwa advised Raghunathrao from his deathbed,

> “Now, the time for me to govern the kingdom is over. My life is near
> its end. Take care of these two and the kingdom. Do not ever distance
> them from yourself…. Do not abandon the righteous path or ethical
> conduct. The cow, the Brahmin and the twice born should be protected
> in accordance with precepts of justice…. My incarnation on earth is
> near its end. Now we hand over this kingdom and the welfare of its
> subjects into your hands. With the blessings of Bhonsle, if you look
> after your subjects, you will be victorious. Those who have strived
> with us with all their family and community, you should take care of
> their families and successors. Make sure our revered father's name is
> not tarnished after me. I will not live long now.”

On 23rd June, Nanasaheb breathed his last on Parvati Hill. The following morning, he was cremated beside the wooden bridge he had constructed over the Mutha River. The next proceedings began. Raghunathrao was the seniormost member of the Bhat family, the position of heir apparent belonged to Madhavrao, Nanasaheb’s son, who was just sixteen years old and had not yet reached the age of majority. Thus, the immediate responsibility of administration fell upon Raghunathrao.

A muhrat was chosen for conferring the robes of office. Madhavrao accompanied by his uncle travelled to Satara. Maharani Tarabai bestowed the robes of office to Madhavrao and said,

> “The burden of the entire kingdom rests on you. You should serve us as
> loyally as your father served the late maharaja. Similarly, you should
> stay. There is no reason to worry about anybody. The duty that you
> will do in future should be done as per orders.”

A regency was established to assist the young Peshwa in the affairs of state. Raghunathrao, alongside Sakharam Bapu Bokil, assumed the reins of administration. Aba Purandare become Peshwa’s Mutalik and Sakharam Bapu as diwan. Nana Fadnavis was appointed as the Phad (Chief Finance Officer) by Madhavrao.

However, peace was short-lived. Alarming intelligence soon arrived—Nizam Ali, the brother of Nawab Salabat Jung of Hyderabad, had advanced into Swarajya territory and was encamped at Dharur with a sizeable force. The news triggered concern across Swarajya, and both sides began preparations for war. Armies started mustering, and the political landscape shifted once more toward confrontation.

## **2. Nizam Ali at the gates**

One might rightly ask: why was Nizam Ali at Dharur—so deep within Swarajya territory? After all, had not the Peshwa and the Nizam of Hyderabad signed a treaty following the latter’s defeat at the Battle of Udgir just a year earlier? Nizam was preparing a plan to directly strike Pune and restore his glory. To understand the casus belli, we need to go back to the day when the treaty at Udgir was signed.

The State of Hyderabad was ruled three brothers—Nawab Salabat Jung from Hyderabad, Nizam Ali from Bidar and Balasat Jung. They had another brother, Mir Mughal Nasir Jung. Though the three brothers ruled in concert, Salabat Jung was acknowledged as the Nawab due to primogeniture.

On 3rd February 1760, Swarajya forces led by Sadashivrao Bhau decisively defeated the Nawab at Dharur. The resulting treaty compelled Salabat Jung to cede territory worth sixty lakhs, including the important cities and forts of Burhanpur, Asirgarh, Bijapur, and Daulatabad. Ahmednagar was acquired through separate arrangement.\[10\] But suddenly disastrous news struck. News arrived from North that Dattaji Shinde lost and died at the Battle at Burari Ghat against Ahmed Shah Abdali. This tragedy overshadowed the triumph at Udgir—much like the British naval victory at Trafalgar later eclipsed Napoleon’s strategic brilliance at Austerlitz. A conference was conveyed at Patdur. After a deliberation of 7 days, decision was made that Bhau was to lead 50,000 troops north with Vishwasrao as the nominal head. Given the urgency, Bhau departed Desh with 25,000 troops without enforcing the treaty signed by Nawab.

Consequently, the forts and territories ceded by treaty did not surrender amicably. In fact, after the war, they had to be subdued militarily. On 19th March 1760, Bijapur was taken while Bhau captured Burhanpur en route to the north. Daulatabad was put under siege by Gopal rao Patwardhan as the killedar refused the transfer. Asirgarh was also taken by force.\[11\]

While on the other hand, this defeat brought problems in Nawab Salabat Jung’s camp. On 10th March 1760, troops started clamouring for pay. Twenty Lakh Rupees were to be paid for four months of service. The Nawab attempted to delay payments, but resentment escalated rapidly. Matters spiralled out of hand when some of the gardis (European trained Indian soldiers) stormed the tent of Nawab’s Diwan, Rajaji Vitthal Sunder, and roughed him for payment. Troops from The Royal Guard had to be deployed to rescue the Diwan and restore order within the encampment.

Sensing opportunity, Nizam Ali stepped forward and took upon himself the responsibility to settle the arrears. This resulted in mass desertion of troops from Salabat Jung’s ranks to Nizam Ali’s camp, significantly shifting the balance of power.\[12\] However, Nizam Ali’s situation was no different—he had no means nor his jagir could afford the promised payments. In a desperate move, he began forcibly collecting revenue from territories that had been ceded to the Peshwa under the Treaty of Udgir.

One of the major reasons for the delay in transfer of cities and forts was in the prospect that the Peshwa, being preoccupied with northern deployments under Bhau and with Nanasaheb himself leading reinforcements out of the Deccan, would not be able to enforce the treaty effectively.

On 7th April 1760, while encamped at Nilanga, Nizam Ali dispatched his Bakshi, Brijlal, with a contingent of 3,000 troops—including 500 gardis—to extract revenue from the Akkalkot State. Ghasdana (field levy for sustaining a marching army) was forcibly collected from Aland and Gunjoti. In response, a Maratha force of 5,000 under Ramchandra Ganesh was mobilized to intercept Brijlal, but this force was recalled by the Peshwa on 9th July to join the departing northern expedition.\[13\]

Situation escalates to the point where a letter dated 1st December 1760 reported that a combined force of 7,000–8,000 men along with 10,000–12,000 gardis and 40 artillery pieces had entered the Burhanpur region. The Peshwa was compelled to redeploy his reinforcement columns, combining them with a strong detachment from Nagpur Bhonsles almost totalling 20,000 forces under Mahipatrao Kavade to contain the advancing and raiding force. The enemy’s campaign relegated into open plunder; the civilian toll was horrific, with numerous women reportedly widowed or violated in the wake of the raids.

Upon the Swarajya army’s arrival, artillery batteries were promptly deployed and bombarded Nizam Ali’s positions. By this point, Nawab Salabat Jung had lost all control over the army, now acting independently under Nizam Ali’s command. In a final attempt to de-escalate, Salabat Jung personally travelled from Hyderabad to Burhanpur to mediate the conflict. However, the troops—emboldened and undisciplined—now demanded an additional ₹25 lakh in unpaid dues. Fortune, however, did not favour the invading army. With supplies running low, the force began to disperse—bringing a temporary end to Nizam Ali’s aggressive incursion into Maratha-held territory.\[14\]

### 3. Situation escalates

The strategic landscape of the Deccan altered significantly on 20th December 1760 with the demise of Fateh Singh Bhonsle Akkalkotkar, the adopted son of Chhatrapati Shahu Maharaj and ruler of the Akkalkot State. His successor, Shahaji Raje, ascended the gadi. Being young and politically inexperienced, Shahaji Raje had to rely heavily on his Diwan, Babuji Naik, for administration.

Concurrently, Hyderabad’s central authority had weakened considerably due to Nawab Salabat Jung’s severe financial distress. Powerful local chiefs began to assert autonomy and engage in unilateral actions. One such figure, Mir Mughal, exploited the vacuum and launched a series of raids into Akkalkot State between December 1760 and January 1761.\[15\]

To counter this aggression, Nilkanthrao Patwardhan was dispatched from Pune. Upon arrival, he fortified Panchmahal. These developments coincided with a crucial diplomatic maneuver: Around 8th January 1761, Raghunathrao, passing through Naldurg, was en route to meet Nizam Ali. This meeting was part of a broader strategy by Peshwa Nanasaheb, whose northern expedition for Panipat had already been delayed multiple times due to rising instability in the Deccan. Nanasaheb sought to stabilize the home front and attempted to persuade Nizam Ali to join him in the Panipat campaign— possibly as a gesture of reciprocation, since the Peshwa had earlier supported Nizam Ali during his internal struggle for power in Hyderabad.

To that end, a 10,000-strong Maratha detachment, including Raghunathrao, Sakharam Bapu, and the Raste brothers, was separated from the main column at Daulatabad and marched southeast towards Hyderabad. As the force approached the borders of Akkalkot, Raghunathrao called upon Nilkanthrao Patwardhan to join and serve as the forward party. By this time, Mir Mughal had withdrawn from the region, possibly due to the approach of Dadasaheb’s army, though specific details remain uncertain.\[16\]

The proposed rendezvous with Nizam Ali was scheduled at Malkhed. However, the outcome of this meeting is not available in the sources. It is clear, though, that the talks failed to yield the desired alliance. Nizam Ali declined to accompany the Marathas to the north. Following this breakdown, the Rastes detached from Rao’s contingent and proceeded to the Mysore front to reinforce the southern Swarajya campaign. \[17\] Rao and Sakharam Bapu returned to the Desh via Bijapur. Nilkanthrao as well returned back to Akkalkot.

Two months after, around 13th March 1761, Lala Brijnath, Nizam Ali’s Bakshi once again led an incursion into the Akkalkot region with a combined force of 2000 men and 2000 gardis. He started collecting the revenue in the area of Aland, Gunjoti, Akkalkot and Naldurg—territories which Nizam Ali claimed as his rightful domain. He started creating chaos to the point where the sardeshmukh was killed.

A Swarajya sardar with 3000 men and 1200 gardis along with 2 cannons marched from Bijapur and swooped into Dharur to check Lala’s advance. Lala on the other hand was chased by Abaji Naik with 5000 army sent by Nanasaheb. The first engagement occurred at Tuljapur, where Lala’s force was routed by a 2,000-strong detachment and compelled to retreat into the fort at Ausa. The combined forces of Abaji Naik and Nilkanthrao Patwardhan laid siege to Ausa, successfully flushing Lala out. He regrouped at Dharashiv, where he was reinforced by Krishnarao Nivalkar and Makrandasing Naik Nimbalkar, increasing his force strength to approximately 2,000 2,500 regular troops and 3,000–4,000 gardis.

The Sardar pursued and skirmished them till reinforcements from Pune arrived in the form of 5,000 troops under Ramchandra Ganesh, who took operational command. Lala was chased up to a village near Bunge. Setting up the artillery, his position was bombarded and captured Lala. As a result, his army surrendered. Taking ample loot from the enemy, Ramchandra Ganesh disbanded his forces but regional security remained fragile. Nizam Ali had by then advanced his main camp to Udgir, dangerously close to the Swarajya frontier. His force numbered now between 10,000 and 15,000 troops, supported by 5,000 gardis and a modest artillery train.\[18\]

Meanwhile, Nawab Salabat Jung, following a significant fallout with his brother Balasat Jung, traveled to Kalburga for talks with Nizam Ali. Raghunathrao even dispatched vakils to mediate between the brothers, attempting to contain the internal instability at Hyderabad that may spill into Swarajya territory.

The constant raiding and destruction had taken a serious toll on the stability of the Akkalkot region. In a decisive move, Peshwa Nanasaheb appointed Nilkanthrao Patwardhan as sabhasad and caretaker of the Akkalkot territories, tasking him with administration and defence. However, this decision provoked the local Deshmukh, who saw the appointment as a threat. The Deshmukh raised a force of 4,000 levies and revolted against Patwardhan's authority. In response, Nilkanthrao assembled 1,000 cavalry, 400 gardis, and 5,000-foot soldiers. He was further reinforced by Konhererao Patwardhan with an additional 400 troops.\[19\]

By late June 1761, the Deshmukh's forces were defeated, suffering 500–600 casualties. A treaty was subsequently concluded, wherein the Deshmukh acknowledged Nilkanthrao’s authority as superior and accepted his authority.\[20\]

Despite this success, the widespread use of scorched-earth tactics and repeated raiding had left the region militarily strained. Significant manpower had to be diverted to curbing rebellions and revolts from garrison duties, weakening the Swarajya’s frontier defences.

#### 4. The Game Begins

On 23rd June 1761, Nanasaheb Peshwa passes away at Parvati hill. His death came barely six months after the losses at Panipat, following the martyrdoms of Sadashivrao Bhau and Vishwasrao. The cumulative effect of these three successive deaths left a leadership vacuum at the highest levels of the Maratha state. Fearing riots in the city, gardi chowkis were stationed across Amid this uncertainty, succession proceedings began. On 20th July 1761, Madhavrao, son of Nanasaheb, formally received the robes of office and assumed the role of Pandit Pradhan (Peshwa), becoming the new head of government. But the internal transition coincided with fresh threats on the frontier.

While Nilkanthrao Patwardhan was still engaged in suppressing the rebellion of the Deshmukh in the Akkalkot region, Nizam Ali began mobilizing troops at Bidar. By the time Maratha intelligence responded, Nizam’s forces had already shifted camp, catching Swarajya off guard.\[21\]

Taking advantage of the instability following Nanasaheb’s death, various frontier commanders and opportunistic nobles began aligning with enemies of Swarajya. One of them was Ramchandra Jadhavrao, grandson of Dhanji Jadhavrao. Ramchandra had been involved in all major conflicts of Deccan since 1750s be it the battle of Bhalki, Sindkhed or Udgir—having a reputation for playing both sides, but this time declared for Nizam Ali in his endeavours.

His alliance was not without internal controversy. Vithal Sundar, the Nawab’s Diwan, distrusted Jadhavrao due to his history of duplicity and even ordered the confiscation of his jagirs. However, this order was reversed by Nizam Ali as he could lose a big ally from a prestigious house.\[22\] Nizam Ali even went beyond and awarded Jadhavrao with a jagir worth Rs. 25 lakhs.\[23\]

On receiving news of Jadhavrao’s switch, Raghunathrao acted decisively. He ordered the confiscation of all jagirs belonging to Jadhavrao within Swarajya-controlled territory—specifically in the region between Akkalkot and Ferozabad. Enraged by the move, Jadhavrao initiated a counter-offensive to reclaim those holdings. From Bhalki, he dispatched his two sons, Shripatrao and Shrinivasrao, with 1000 cavalry, 1000 gardis and 3 cannons and they sieged the fort of Naldurg. Though the initial siege failed, one of Jadhavrao’s officers, Lachiram Karol, successfully incited defections among the defenders. With morale broken, the fort capitulated without serious resistance. Abzalpur was also overrun. These developments placed the Akkalkot region in grave danger. Its garrison consisted of merely 400 cavalry and 800 infantry—insufficient to repel a determined assault. Around 14th July, Jadhavrao proceeded to Dharur to join Nizam Ali.

Meanwhile, Pune’s succession crisis concluded by 20th July. With Madhavrao formally installed as Peshwa, Raghunathrao began mobilizing for a military campaign. Reinforcements from the Mysore front under Visaji Krishna also arrived, adding 10,000 troops to the muster.\[24\] On 16th August 1761, on the auspicious day of Dasara, Shrimant entered tent to lead the campaign.\[25\] Military preparations escalated rapidly. By October 1761, it was expected that an army of 70,000 would be mobilized. Further reinforcements were expected from the Nagpurkar Bhonsles and Malharrao Holkar, with their joint contribution estimated to raise the total strength to approximately 85,000 troops.\[26\]

Nizam Ali remained stationed at Bidar until 4th July 1761, commanding an initial force of 10,000 troops supported by approximately 5,000–7,000 gardis. On 14th July, he advanced his camp to Dharur—a strategically chosen location owing to its abundance of firewood and fodder, and its defensibility amidst surrounding hills. At Dharur, Nizam was formally welcomed by Ramchandra Jadhavrao and was granted an additional three mahals.\[27\]

Anticipating a broader conflict with the Marathas, Nizam Ali began searching for external allies as early as June 1761. Letters were dispatched to various regional powers, including the East India Company, Murarrao Ghorpade of Gooty, and Hanmantrao Nimbalkar of Kharda. Murarrao expressed conditional interest, requesting jagir grants as compensation. The EICo., too, accepted a proposal—2,000 European soldiers and 10,000 sepoys in exchange for a jagir valued at 52,000 hons (approximately ₹2,00,000). While this arrangement made the Bengal Presidency excited, but it was flatly rejected by the Madras Presidency.\[28\]

This breakdown in Anglo-Maratha-Hyderabad relations had its roots in the shifting dynamics post-Plassey (1757). Although previously cordial, Maratha perceptions of the EIC shifted following the Company’s increased aggression and interference. Peshwa Nanasaheb had even attempted to ally the French against English during the Seven Years' War, but the debacle at Panipat effectively ended all initiatives. (proposal in appendix) In 1761, the Bombay EIC reportedly was planning to launch an attack on Vasai and had earlier interfered in the Maratha siege of Janjira (1760–61).\[29\]

In response to Nizam Ali’s outreach, Raghunathrao offered a counter-proposal to the Bombay Presidency, proposing an alliance with Swarajya on superior terms. He sought 2,000 trained artillerymen from the Company.\[30\] However, the EIC ultimately chose to remain neutral and refrained from further entanglement.

On 25th October, Nizam Ali moved his base to Shahgad. Reacting to this development, Raghunathrao stationed a detachment at Bhinger—a key position controlling the axis between Shahgad and Ahmednagar. Following several minor skirmishes, Nizam deviated from his intended path and sought refuge in Aurangabad. Although the city of Aurangabad remained under Nizam Ali’s control, the surrounding subah had been absorbed into Swarajya’s domain. Maratha kamavisdars had already completed the annual revenue assessment and collection, leaving the region financially drained for the Nizam’s purposes. With no state revenue to draw from, Nizam was forced to rely on traders and local savkars to raise funds.\[31\]

During this period, Nizam Ali’s army expanded significantly. Reinforcements arrived from various Swarajya dissidents and Nizam’s sardars, raising his effective strength to nearly 30,000 men—comprising 15,000 from disaffected Marathi nobles, 10,000 of his own regulars, and around 5,000 gardis. On the other side, Swarajya forces numbered between 30,000 and 40,000 troops. Shrimant Madhavrao’s main force had not yet joined the Aurangabad front. Though small detachments sent to reinforce were intercepted and routed, they later regrouped after linking up with Murad Khan, forming a consolidated force of nearly 50,000–60,000. On 30th October 1761, he vacated Aurangabad.

Around the same time, Raghunathrao joined the Swarajya field camp. However, his arrogance and pompous attitude didn’t allow him to lead in joint effort. The rift in the camp rose to the point where a 16 year old Peshwa Madhavrao was running the show.32

### 5. Nizam crosses the Rubicon

With his financial reserves exhausted, Nizam Ali took a drastic—and ultimately disastrous—decision that would permanently damage his prospects in the ongoing conflict. In a bid to raise funds, he plundered the revered Hindu temples at Toke, Paithan, Pravara Sangam, and even Shri Siddheshwar. These temples, centuries old and deeply venerated in the Deccan, were sacrosanct to many of the Marathi sardars serving under his banner.\[33\]

The fallout was immediate and severe. Widespread outrage gripped the Hindu commanders in Nizam’s camp, sparking defections to the Maratha side. Among the most prominent was Ramchandra Jadhavrao, alongside Mir Mughal, Nizam Ali’s own younger brother. Unable to tolerate the sacrilege, they opened a channel of communication with Peshwa Madhavrao through Babuji Naik, and withdrew under the pretext of launching a sortie against Maratha troops. Instead, they turned against the Nizam’s camp, retreating with nearly 5,000 troops and several field guns.\[34\]

Veteran commanders such as Murad Khan and Hanmatrao Nimbalkar urged Nizam to seek terms and sue for peace. Their counsel, however, fell on deaf ears. In an attempt to hold his crumbling coalition together, Nizam compelled the remaining Hindu commanders to swear oaths of allegiance on the bel bandar. Simultaneously, the party that supported to continue the conflict suggested contacting Hyder Ali.\[35\]

These developments sent shockwaves through the Maratha camp as well. Raghunathrao, already under stress and vacillating in leadership, was visibly shaken. Many around him began to question his stability of mind. He leaned heavily on Gopikabai, mother of the Peshwa, who had been his staunch opposer. His correspondence with her during this time turned increasingly religious in tone. Madhavrao began stepping into a more decisive leadership role, taking partial charge of decisions.\[36\]

Nizam departed from Aurangabad to proceed towards Pune in order to force a defeat on Swarajya. His army crossed the Nimbedehar Ghat and descended onto the Desh region, trailed closely by the Swarajya field army which maintained a series of running engagements and skirmishes to delay and harass the invader’s progress.\[37\] Meanwhile, on 29th September, Madhavrao sent Ganesh Vitthal Waghmare with 1200 gardis to recover Toke, Paithan and other religious sites along with Ellora.\[38\]

As the Nizam’s forces advanced, they moved towards Loni, near Kharda, the jagir of Nimbalkars. His next intended destination was believed to be Shrigondha or Chambargondha. Nizam sent an advance guard to secure the area and begin plundering the city. Even the ancestral wada of the Shindes dug up in a futile search for hidden treasure.\[39\]

### 6. The High Noon

As soon as Nizam descended the ghats, the intensity of skirmishing increased substantially. Maratha troops continuously harassed Nizam’s column with artillery and rocket fire. Then came a significant confrontation near Chambargondha, approximately 4.5–6 km away from the city.

On 12th December 1761, at approximately 9:00 AM, Gopalrao Patwardhan intercepted and engaged Nizam Ali’s forces just outside Chambargondha. Patwardhan seized a rocky high ground on the Nizam's left flank and immediately opened artillery fire. Rather than retreating as in previous engagements, Nizam chose to stand and reorganised his column into battle lines, aiming to annihilate Patwardhan’s 10,000 strong force before the rest of the Maratha army could arrive. But it was too late—the main Maratha contingents as well as Raghunathrao and the Huzurat had already begun closing in.

The manoeuvre the left flank made allowed them to fire on Nizam’s rear. As the Maratha line opened with intense artillery and rocket salvos, the Mughal forces under Nizam were thrown into disarray. Attempting to stabilise the situation, Nizam pushed his vanguard forward to engage, but their morale broke under the relentless projectile fire.\[40\] As casualty number started to escalate, Nizam withdrew his advance before they could come within musket range. Meanwhile, Patwardhan’s forces maintained pressure on Nizam’s left. Despite stiff resistance, they held their ground.

The artillery from Swarjya’s left relentlessly fired on Mughal rear. (Nizam is also referred as Muhal in many letters) Nizam then attempted a countermeasure—he sent a strong detachment to seize the guns. They temporarily overran the battery and pushed back the gardis and Shinde’s troops. At this critical moment, Janoji Bhonsle launched a countercharge from the right. The Mughal detachment was routed, and the guns were recovered. By 7:00 PM, the entire Mughal line had broken and was in retreat.\[41\]

The Mughal camp was now short of food and water, and the morale of his troops at all time low. Although the Maratha army did not immediately pursue—owing to the disciplined retreat of Nizam’s army and relative inferiority in numbers—Raghunathrao ordered the Patwardhans and Rastes to continue the pursuit.\[43\]

Nizam Ali retreated to Shrigondha, staying only one night before plundering and burning the town. He took a few field guns and climbed back up the ghats, marching through Pimpri and moving towards Nimbgaon. Then, taking a sharp northwest turn, he advanced toward Ahmednagar, hugging the outskirts of the city. Keeping the city right, he was caught again at Chase.\[44\]

At Chase, the Maratha vanguard under Raghunathrao made contact with Nizam’s rearguard. Patwardhan's troops quickly reinforced. Maratha artillery opened fire, forcing the Mughals to raise their standards and form lines behind a small river. Nizam’s artillery concentrated fire on Patwardhan’s pathaks, causing 50–100 casualties. The Huzurat, however, successfully charged, capturing several enemy standards and compelling another retreat.\[45\]

For Nizam, the success of winning depended on reaching Pune. Panic gripped the city. Pune as a city never had walls. It was protected by 3 big forts- Chakan, Sinhagad and Purander with other numerous small ones. People started taking refuge in these forts. Even Government’s treasury was shifted to Purandar.\[46\]

At Kamargaon, Nizam’s left flank encountered a combined Maratha force comprising Janoji Bhonsle, Babuji Naik, and Huzurat. Fighting erupted fiercely. Both sides incurred casualties between 100–150. In a bid to hold ground, Nizam deployed a large portion of his field army, including karols and gardis, forming lines behind a river. The Rastes and Patwardhans responded with coordinated musket fire and a charge. The Mughals lost 150–200 men just to musket fire before Nizam ordered yet another retreat.\[47\]

## 7. Marching on an empty stomach

While the main body of Swarajya forces pursued Nizam Ali’s retreat, Raghunathrao sent troops to cut off the Nizam’s communication lines up to Bidar. He dispatched Jivanrao Keshav to recover all outposts and vital road networks between Ahmednagar and Dharur, effectively severing western access.\[48\] Simultaneously, Mahipatrao Kavade was sent towards Jalna, Bidar, and the fort of Naldurg, which was successfully recaptured.\[49\] These moves were intended to isolate Nizam Ali, denying him any access to reinforcement, supply, or safe retreat.

The situation inside the Mughal camp became increasingly desperate. His troops began to suffer starvation. The price of foodgrain in his camp soared to 1 seer per rupee (~0.93 kg/Rs),\[50\] a stark contrast to the prevailing rate of 26 kg of jowar per rupee in Pune at the time.\[51\] Fodder and firewood were completely exhausted by 29th December,\[52\] and Maratha patrols were actively capturing Nizam’s cattle and pack animals.\[53\]

At Ranjangaon, the Mughal force was again intercepted—this time by three pathaks under the Patwardhans. An unimpressive artillery bombardment was initiated by Raghunathrao from a long range, but due to confused and contradictory orders issued by him—initially recalling and then reissuing the same command—the opportunity to exploit Nizam’s vulnerable position was lost.\[54\] The next engagement, however, proved devastating. At Bhuleshwar, a critical skirmish led to heavy losses for Nizam Ali. The casualty count approached 1,000, this was the straw that broke camel’s back.\[55\]

Nizam Ali’s momentum broke—halted by hunger, resistance, and miscalculation. On 30th December, his phalanx came to a halt at Uruli—30km from Pune.\[56\]

Recognising the strategic opportunity, Raghunathrao began assembling all available forces for a decisive confrontation. The army consolidated at Pedgaon, swelling to nearly 90,000 troops, including the elite Sarkari Huzurat, 10,000 gardis, and the Topkhana (artillery). The situation bore a striking resemblance to the eve of the Third Battle of Panipat, except now, Raghunathrao held the numerical and strategic superiority that had once belonged to Abdali.\[57\]

Now completely encircled and unable to advance or withdraw, Nizam Ali lost all hope of salvaging his campaign. On the eve of annihilation, he dispatched his senior commanders—Anandrao Nimbalkar, Hanmatrao Nimbalkar, and Murad Khan—to negotiate peace with the Peshwa. While many leading Sardars, including the Patwardhans and Bhonsles, wanted to force an outcome on the bettlefield, Raghunathrao’s close confidant and the Peshwa’s Diwan, Sakharam Bapu Bokil, strongly urged acceptance of the peace overture.\[58\] This wasn’t taken well in the Swarajya camp.

### 8. The Negotiations

Formal negotiations commenced:

• First: Both parties agreed to cessation of hostilities.

• Second: Nizam Ali demanded Jadhavrao’s and Mir Mughal’s return. The demand

was rejected. Jadhavrao remained a vassal under the protection of the Peshwa

• In lieu of Jadhavrao’s return, Nizam Ali requested the return of territories that he

had previously conferred on Jadhavrao as jagirs. The Peshwa, wishing to preserve

his holdings, negotiated an alternative arrangement: territories worth ₹17 lakhs,

previously captured by Mahipatrao Kavade in Bidar Subah, were released to Nizam.

• A third provision addressed the Fort of Kalyan, which was now part of Jadhavrao’s

lands but previously under Nizam’s control. As a balancing measure, the fort of

Naldurg—captured earlier by Swarajya—was exchanged with Kalyan.

• The final point of the settlement involved Mir Mughal, who had explicitly expressed

his desire to join Swarajya. The Peshwa mediated an arrangement whereby Nizam

Ali granted Mir Mughal a jagir worth ₹20 lakhs.\[59\]

The war ended with Swarajya emerging victorious, gaining territory valued at approximately ₹60 lakhs, though not all of it came under direct control. As per the Treaty at Uruli, Nizam Ali retreated to Hyderabad. However, during his withdrawal, he violated the spirit of the agreement by raiding Ramchandra Jadhavrao’s jagir, despite Jadhavrao being formally recognised as a Swarajya vassal.

In the next article, I will examine the shortcomings of the Treaty of Uruli and assess the broader significance of this campaign. Alongside a brief contextual discussion, I will delve into the emerging schism between Peshwa Madhavrao and Raghunathrao—a rift that ultimately culminated in the Battle of Rakshasbhuvan, a confrontation bearing striking resemblance to the famed Battle of Palkhed.

The letter was written by Visaji Krishna to Nanasaheb outlining the proposal:

Shri

17th September 1760

To the Honorable Rajshri Pantpradhan Saheb,

The intended terms from Fulcharikar (French) as per the list:

In the year Hijri 1175 and Vikram Samvat \[unspecified year\], on the 7th of Safar in the

month of Bhadrapada:

1\. The Chandi fort, which is presently in your possession, should also be handed

over—half of the revenue and military equipment thereof—to the government.

2\. The government forces that arrive for reinforcement shall be given ₹1 per day

per soldier, and the commanders and officials shall be paid according to their

respective expenses, starting from the date they descend the ghats until they

return to the same.

3\. For government work in Karnataka, Hindustan, and our territory, wherever

summoned, two thousand Europeans along with artillery, ammunition, and all

necessary equipment and provisions shall attend and perform the tasks. They

shall be kept content. No expenses shall be demanded for assistance in Karnataka

and Konkan. For deployments in our territory and in Hindustan, the government

shall pay part of the expenses.

4\. The government’s commanders, officials, and envoys shall be honoured in an

excellent manner.

5\. Since the Emperor is against us, you shall not support him but instead join the

government’s side, and whatever equipment is required shall be brought and

Janjira fort shall be captured and handed over.

6\. After this agreement is concluded as per the main list, once the government

forces reach the top of the ghats, the English siege will lift out of fear. If your

ships arrive from England before that, the siege will be lifted nonetheless. In both

cases, you must fulfill your obligations according to the agreement.

7\. Any enemies or opponents of the government must not be given asylum by you.

8\. Upon reaching the top of the ghats, this entire agreement shall be authenticated

with the seal of Musa Lali and the signature of Musa Lawali and submitted. After

descending the ghats, a government garrison shall be stationed in Chandi fort.

Initially, a cash deposit of ₹5 lakh shall be made and a note executed. One

respectable individual from among the seven commissioners shall come to escort

the sum. Musa Lali himself and Dilawar Jung shall arrive at Chandi to discuss

future plans.

9\. A total of ₹12 lakh shall be paid—out of which ₹5 lakh shall be paid immediately,

and a promissory note executed. The remaining ₹7 lakh shall be paid in two

installments of four months each.

10\. The district (taluka) under the French shall now be divided. In the future, any

forts or territory recovered from the English shall be divided equally—half to the

government, including garrisons and forts. Among these, Chandi, Tripati, and

Chandragiri temples shall remain under government control.

11\. A loan of ₹1 lakh shall be immediately given to Rajshri Visaji Krishna and Hari

Gopal with a proper promissory note.

12\. The cannons and war materials from the port that are deemed suitable by the

government, and ten to twelve cannons of good quality, five elephants, and five

horses that are preferred by the Shrimant, shall be handed over.

13\. When you allied with us, the English were displeased. If in the future, the English

cause harm to any fort or territory of the government, you shall assist with all

materials and support, without demanding any expenses, and suppress them.

Furthermore, you shall continue to act with honesty and loyalty. In that case, the

government shall reward you accordingly.

14\. Any horses lost during battle or disturbances shall be compensated.

15\. If we jointly defeat the enemy and loot is obtained, it shall be equally divided

between you and us. If we go elsewhere to confront another enemy and during

that time you obtain loot from the opposing side, then half of it shall be given to

us and half shall be kept by you.

In total, fifteen clauses have been written. 7th Safar (seal).
