A lot of chatter took place about flooding in Gurugram, a city that simultaneously suffers from an acute groundwater depletion problem. At the foothills of the Aravalis, it claims to be the city of aspiration, vying with Mumbai for that spot. A peek into its mess of urban infrastructure though reveals much more to it. This neo wave of chutzpah has also masked the historicity of Gurugram as a small outpost next to Delhi.

As an author, I find Gurugram fascinating for a mix of good and bad reasons. There is a vibrant, rapidly changing urban landscape that gives it an aesthetic unlike any city in India; however, the chaos and the rural pockets that stand out as its warts constantly reminds me of the sheer contrasts within the city. These thoughts about Gurugram made me realize one day that there is much in common with what I have read and seen about Iran’s capital Tehran and its urban landscape. There are several parallels between their stories of transformation, the collision of old and new, and the ways both cities grapple with the challenges of growth. Gurugram’s blend of aspiration and complexity mirrors the layered evolution seen in Tehran, making the comparison both intriguing and deeply resonant.

Tehran, the capital of Iran, and Gurugram, a rapidly expanding corporate hub on the outskirts of New Delhi, India, present a fascinating study in contrasts and similarities regarding urban layout, development trajectories, and the challenges posed by rapid urbanization.

## **Urban Layout: Historical Roots Versus Rapid Modernization of Tehran**

Tehran’s urban form is deeply layered with centuries of Persian architectural and planning heritage. Its cityscape is defined by a historic core characterized by winding lanes, traditional markets (bazaars), and courtyard homes, gradually enveloped by layers of suburban and peripheral expansions. Its street network tends to follow axial patterns centered on north-south and east-west boulevards, forming a hierarchical spatial organization that integrates governmental, commercial, and residential zones.

The current urban structure and expansion of the city, excluding its historical core, largely originate from the Pahlavi era (1925–1979). During this period, the Pahlavi dynasty made deliberate efforts to develop a national public sphere intended for a global audience. As a result, Tehran was transformed into a showcase for modernisation initiatives, including the establishment of an extensive infrastructure network that contributed to Iran’s broader urbanisation. The 1930s marked a particularly transformative phase with the introduction of the ‘Street-Widening Act of 1933’, aimed at enhancing the city’s built environment. Officially implemented in 1937, this plan involved constructing new boulevards and expanding existing streets. New public buildings and educational and commercial facilities were strategically located along these avenues, primarily extending to the west and north. Notably, north-south thoroughfares and the new east-west Shah Reza Avenue in the northern district were equipped with superior infrastructure and services. Concurrently, a new administrative area was developed on two sites: the former walled Qajar Royal District and the previous military parade ground. Consequently, the city’s northern regions became centres of wealth and influence, whereas the southern districts evolved into industrial zones, predominantly aligned with routes connecting Tehran to other cities and provinces.

Following a halt due to turbulence and the Second World War, modernization of Tehran picked up steam in a big way, as five year plans paid attention to urban development alongside other key issues. The Karaj Dam Project took birth in the 1960s to cater to booming population and its electricity requirements – by then, 30% of Iran’s population was a resident of Tehran. Planners by then had decided to create a multi node growth model for Tehran - The Tehran Comprehensive Plan (TCP) that was approved in 1968 proposed decentralisation, relocation and displacement of the poor and had the vision that Tehran as a metropolis in 1991 would consist of ten urban centres, each with half a million inhabitants, separated by large green areas and linked with a network of highways. A total length of 150 km of highways was planned for the city, and a transportation network design in the east-west direction to support the linear growth of the city towards the west (Emami 2011). This plan intended to accommodate commercial markets and facilitate the movement of automobiles. Tehran was to become ‘not a diagram of power, but a machine for profit’; however, growth was unequal in distribution, as the elite and the upper middle class of Tehran saw a betterment of their living standards unlike the poorer sections.

Post revolution Iran adopted a development strategy centering around the three main tactics of controlling the expansion of Tehran, redistributing various functions to major regional cities, and promoting the growth of smaller cities in rural areas. This was particularly adopted to control depopulation of the other urban centres of Iran, a phenomenon that as particularly seen in the pre-revolution era[\[1\]](#_ftn1). However, several key projects that could have helped the city, such as the Tehran Metro, stood abandoned, only to be picked up again in the 1990s by a city struggling to manage the flow of people. Mayor Karbaschi’s approach of ‘selling extra density’ to people created a kind of chaos in this post Khomenei Iran, as it sought to decentralize governance of the city and ended up creating significant population on the outskirts, and “luxury” projects in his tenure allegedly led to marginalization of the poor and those seeking affordable housing.[\[2\]](#_ftn2)

### **Gurugram – A Story of Some Parallels**

In contrast, Gurugram is a product of late 20th and early 21st-century urban development driven by economic liberalization and corporate expansion. That however discounts the history of the region which goes back to the Mahabharata era, while I will delve into only for the last two hundred years. With the decline in Mughal power, Gurugram was torn between contending powers. By 1803 most of it came under the British rule through the treaty of Surji Arjungaon with Daulat Rao Scindia. The town was first occupied by the cavalry unit posted to watch the army of Begum Samru of Sirdhana[\[3\]](#_ftn3). It become a Part of the district, which was divided into units called parganas. These units were given to petty chiefs for the military service rendered by them. Eventually these units came under direct control of the British, with the last major administrative change in 1836. After the revolt of 1857, it was transferred from the North-Western provinces to Punjab province. In 1861, the district, which Gurugram was a part of was rearranged into five tehsils: Gurgaon, Ferozepur Jhirka, Nuh, Palwal and Rewari and the modern-day city came under the control of Gurgaon tehsil. In 1947, Gurgaon became a part of independent India and fell under the Indian state of Punjab, and by 1966, the city came under the administration of Haryana with the creation of the new state.

Previously a small town, Gurugram has since transformed into a sprawling metropolis marked by glass skyscrapers, gated residential sectors, and segregated land use zones. Its layout relies heavily on planned sectors and large-scale real estate developments, lacking the historical depth seen in Tehran but demonstrating a clear corporate and residential spatial segregation. Just like the mayoral transformation of Tehran, there was a sudden transformation around the 1980s in the city. Led by a conglomerate of real estate companies with DLF and Ansals at the forefront, the city developed with a different approach than the conventional one, guided by a radical approach adopted in the 1982 Notification No. 10 DP-82/6528. Through this notification, zoning regulations were introduced for Gurugram wherein 4,500 hectares of land was identified as residential land\[4\].

This gave companies like DLF, that acquired about 1,416 hectares of land in Gurugram\[5\], to create a walk to work approach – they first built premium office spaces over years, following which housing was introduced to create a captive audience. While it helped develop pockets of affluence, the larger city has languished between rapid land development and greed of developers, with an absolute lack of adequate infrastructure to tackle it. For instance, it has been pointed out repeatedly that The state government’s negligence of or under-investment in public goods such as buses, hospitals, and affordable housing has put a burden on Gurgaon as a whole and most acutely on those who keep the city running\[6\].

#### **Similar Yet Different – How the Two Cities are Set Apart**

Both cities have witnessed rapid peripheral urbanization absorbing previously rural or semi-rural areas. This growth manifests in sprawling suburbs and outgrowths, often inadequately integrated with core urban infrastructure. Socioeconomic zoning marks both cities, creating disparities between affluent and underdeveloped areas. In Tehran, wealth and civic facilities concentrate in the northern districts, while Gurugram’s expensive commercial and residential sectors juxtapose with under-serviced blocks suffering poor infrastructure.

Tehran’s historic neighborhoods maintain sociable, human-scaled spaces fostering communal interaction, supported by traditional Persian concepts of garden and courtyard design. Gurugram’s newer, market-driven urban fabric features high-rise residential towers and business parks that, while modern and well-appointed, often lack organically evolved public spaces conducive to community life. Furthermore, while Tehran’s urban greenery has diminished over time, Gurugram’s master plans aim for green spaces, although implementation has lagged due to competing development pressures.

Despite disparate histories, both cities share critical challenges emblematic of megacity pressures:

**Infrastructure Deficiencies**: Both Tehran and Gurugram struggle with inadequate water supply, sewage, and waste management systems. In Tehran, groundwater pollution and water scarcity are exacerbated by aging infrastructure, while Gurugram faces fragmented sewerage networks and unreliable water services.

**Environmental Degradation**: Poor air quality due to emissions from vehicles and industry plagues both cities, compounded by geographic factors that limit pollutant dispersion\[8\]. This is further compounded in Gurugram by highly inaccurate readings on air quality being reported from the official agencies\[9\].

**Unplanned Growth and Inequality**: Rapid urban sprawl has stressed historic areas and created unequal development zones, leading to significant disparities in service provision.

**Urban Flooding**: Tehran’s southern zones and Gurugram alike are vulnerable to urban flooding due to inadequate drainage infrastructure, encroachment on water bodies, and surface runoff issues.

**Groundwater challenges:** Both Tehran and Gurugram face acute groundwater depletion with severe consequences:

- **Tehran’s Case**: Prolonged over-extraction for domestic and
  agricultural uses, compounded by drought and limited recharge, has led
  to critical depletion of aquifers. This has triggered land
  subsidence\[10\], with some areas sinking up to 30 centimeters
  annually, threatening infrastructure stability and long-term water
  availability.

- **Gurugram’s Situation**: Rapid industrialization and urbanization
  have precipitated groundwater extraction at over 200% of recharge
  capacity\[11\], categorizing the city as a ‘dark zone’ for water
  resources. Declining water tables, illegal extraction, and
  contamination have escalated risks to both supply reliability and
  water quality.

#### **Prospective Risks for Gurugram from Uncontrolled Growth**

Assessment indicates that unless Gurugram substantially reforms its urban water management, infrastructure planning, and environmental regulation, it is poised to replicate many of Tehran’s problems:

> · Continued overuse of groundwater may deepen aquifer depletion,
> threaten water supplies, and cause land subsidence.
>
> · Infrastructure deficits, notably in stormwater management and
> sewage, could amplify urban flooding, congestion, and public health
> crises.
>
> · Environmental degradation from unchecked growth and pollution may
> worsen air and water quality, threatening livability.
>
> · Socioeconomic inequalities might intensify without equitable
> infrastructure and environmental governance.

In recent years, Tehran has initiated a range of interventions aimed at curbing groundwater depletion and mitigating the associated risks of land subsidence. The city has implemented stricter controls on groundwater extraction, enhanced monitoring of aquifer levels, and invested in recharge projects such as managed aquifer recharge zones and artificial wetlands designed to restore hydrological balance. Public awareness campaigns have targeted conservation, while multi-stakeholder collaboration has promoted water-saving technologies in agriculture and domestic use. Although these measures are still evolving and face hurdles in enforcement and coordination, they represent a genuine attempt to address the underlying drivers of water stress in the region. In stark contrast, Gurugram has yet to institutionalize such comprehensive strategies. Despite being classified as a ‘dark zone’ for groundwater and facing chronic depletion, the city continues to lag in enforcing extraction limits, promoting recharge, and integrating community engagement into water resource management. This disparity underscores the urgent need for Gurugram to adopt proven approaches from Tehran and other global cities, lest it confront even graver socioeconomic and environmental consequences as demand continues to outpace supply.

Unless Gurugram substantially reforms its urban water management, infrastructure planning, and environmental regulation, it is poised to replicate many of Tehran’s problems. Continued overuse of groundwater may deepen aquifer depletion, threaten water supplies, and cause land subsidence, as observed in parts of Tehran where excessive extraction has led to alarming rates of subsidence and compromised infrastructure stability. Infrastructure deficits, notably in stormwater management and sewage, could amplify urban flooding, congestion, and public health crises. Further, environmental degradation from unchecked growth and pollution may worsen air and water quality, threatening livability, as seen in Tehran’s persistent struggles with vehicle emissions and industrial pollutants, and mirrored by Gurugram’s rising levels of particulate matter and contaminated water sources. Socioeconomic inequalities might intensify without equitable infrastructure and environmental governance, perpetuating disparities in service provision and quality of life between affluent neighborhoods and marginalized areas.

### **Conclusion**

Tehran and Gurugram offer instructive examples of the dynamics of urban expansion in Asia, where historical legacy and modern corporate development meet common megacity challenges. Both mirror the consequences of water resource overuse, infrastructure strain, and environmental neglect. Gurugram’s future resilience hinges on applying lessons from cities like Tehran—prioritizing sustainable resource management, integrated urban planning, and inclusive governance to safeguard economic vitality and quality of life as it grows.
