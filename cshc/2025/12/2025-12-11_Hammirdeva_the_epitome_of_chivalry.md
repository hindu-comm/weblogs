# Hammirdeva the epitome of chivalry

That is, a lion gives birth to a child only once. Gentlemen say things only once. A banana bears fruit only once. A woman applies oil and ointment only once; that is, she gets married only once. That is the case with Rao Hamir, too. He does not think twice about what he decides. But who was this Hammirdeva? The Hindu hero of medieval India, who is celebrated in numerous historical works like Hammir Mahakavya, Hammir Raso, Hammir Mad Mardan, and Hammir Hath, which are attributed to his chivalry. He was a direct descendant of Prithviraj Chauhan, who, like his glorious ancestor, died fighting for the independence of his motherland against Alauddin Khalji. It’s important to understand how he was connected with Prithviraja before looking in detail about his life and achievements, and his confrontation with Alauddin.

The defeat of the Chahmana king Prithviraj 3 at the second battle of Tarain against Muhammad Ghori in 1192 was a major blow to the Hindu kingdoms of north India, as it opened roads for further expansion for the Turks in the Indian mainland. The glorious dynasty of Chauhan came to an infamous end with this defeat, and Prithviraj was killed immediately after the battle or some time after it at the mere age of 26, sacrificing his life for his motherland. Ghori installed his son Govinddeva on the throne of Ajmer as a vassal, but soon Prithviraj’s brother Hariraja rose in open revolt and forced Govindraja to flee to Ranathambore. This was how a new dynasty called “Chauhans of Ranastambhpura or Ranathambore” was established.

Hammir, son of Jaitrasimha, was the most glorious ruler of this dynasty who made territorial expansion for the first time, adding newly captured territories to the political boundaries of Ranathambore. Before him, his ancestors had witnessed tough times in keeping their freedom intact, as owing to the closer proximity to Delhi, the fort of Ranathambore always proved to be a show of strength for the newly anointed sultan. Ranathambore faced repeated attacks by Qutubudin Aibak in 1209. It was captured by Iltutmish in 1226 after a long siege, but was again recaptured by the gallant chauhans during the reign of Razia Sultan. It successfully repelled attacks in 1248 and 53 by the Delhi sultanate.

With Hammira at the helm of affairs, Ranathambore witnessed days of glory and dominance. Hammira plundered the neighbouring kingdoms of Malwa and forced the territories lying in the south, including Abu and Chittor. Alarmed at the increasing might of Hammira, Jalaluddin Khalji marched against Ranathmabore to capture the fort in 1291. Jalaluddin attacked the city of Jhain, which was lying in the vicinity of Ranathambore. Hammir sent his battle-tested general, Gurdan Saini, to counter Jalaluddin. However, after a severe engagement, he was killed, and the Hindu chieftain of Jhain took shelter in the hills of Ranathambore. Jalaluddin entered the city triumphantly and plundered the city, destroying many beautiful temples with rich carvings. Hammir decided to prepare for a siege and shut his forces in the impregnable fortress, Jalaluddin marched for Ranathambore after looting Jhain to his content. However, his heart failed him seeing the mighty walls of the fort, and he plainly told his officers that he would not be able to capture it. The sultan said that he was not prepared to waste so many lives nor dared to see face-to-face the widows and orphans of the dead. This baffled his general, who tried to convince him by telling him that, “If you return without capturing this citadel, the Rai of this place will become proud and your dignity will be lowered”. Jalaluddin got frustrated and replied that “He did not value even ten such forts above a single hair of a Musalman”. Thus, the siege was abandoned and the Muslim army returned to Delhi. However, Jhain was soon recaptured by the forces of Hammir, and Ranathambore managed to ward off the enemies and save its independence. Jalaluddin led his forces once again against this fort in 1292 without any success.

The greatest test for the line of Prithviraja was to come in the face of Alauddin a few years later. Returning after plundering the kingdom of Gujarat in 1299 and ransacking the temple of Somnath, carrying immense wealth with themselves, the two able generals of Alauddin, Ulugh Khan and Nusrat Khan, camped at Jalore in southern Rajasthan. Here, a dispute arose between the Royal generals and the Mongol generals of Alauddin, who were newly converted to Islam. Royal generals wanted to extract the wealth that Neo Muslims had gained during their raid on Gujrat, and they tried to extract it in the most cruel ways. This prompted consternation among the neo Muslims and raised the banner of revolt under Muhammad Shah and Kahbru. They killed the brother of Nusrat Khan and tried to kill Ulugh Khan, who escaped, but Alauddin’s nephew fell to their hands and was murdered. However, the imperial army managed to organize themselves under Ulugh Khan to face the rebels. Muhammad shah fled to Ranathambore and he was granted asylum by Hammir who according to the chivalry of his race and lineage realized it’s his duty to protect the lives of Muhammad shah and his followers who were certain to be killed if Hammir refused to grant them shelter. Hammir was bound by the ancient code of the protection of “Sharnagat” at any cost which guided the spirit of Kshatriyas from time immemorial.

This event took place in the winters of 1299-1300 AD, Alauddin couldn’t tolerate this open defiance of his authority by Hammir. He ordered his two generals Ulugh Khan, governor of Bayana and Nusrat Khan, governor of Kara, to invade Ranthambhor with the forces at their command. The joint armies marched for the Rajput stronghold and on their way captured and plundered Jhain without encountering any resistance. Before arriving in Ranthambhor Ulugh Khan sent a message to the Chouhan prince that his master bore no grudge against the Rai and if the latter either put the fugitives to death or surrendered them to him, the imperial armies would return to Delhi. He further argued that when his supplicants had not proved faithful and loyal to those who had given them life and honour, how could they be expected to behave otherwise towards their new patron? In the end Hammira was asked to be ready for consequences in case he refused to act in accordance with the instructions contained in the letter.

However, Hammir was not to be cowed down with these threats. He realized the magnitude of the enmity he had established with the Sultan by granting asylum to these rebel Mongols but he was not frightened by the consequences. He declined to betray his guests into the hands of those who were determined to kill them. He must have been aware of the fate received by the families of Muhammad shah and his followers at Delhi. The wives of these neo muslims were sold as prostitutes to the scavengers and their children were ordered to be cut into pieces in front of their mothers. For the young Chauhan prince it was a disgrace to hand over those who had surrendered to him knowing the cruel fate which awaits them in Delhi. He conveyed that while he did not seek conflict with the Sultan, he did not fear it either. For the valiant Chauhan, the duty of offering refuge to the Mughal nobles, along with the proud legacy of his lineage, provided the strongest reason to confront the challenge posed by the Sultan’s generals.

Therefore, the battle preparation began by both sides in full swing. During the time when Ulugh Khan and Nusrat Khan marched against Hammira, he was engaged in some religious rites and could not send an offensive force to check them. That’s why Khalji’s forces marched easily to river Banas. Here, however, his further progress was barred by chauhan forces led by Hammira’s general Bhimasimha. He gained some initial advantages but mistook it for his victory and got down from the mountain pass along with his troops in order to return to the capital thinking that he had beaten the muslim forces. However, his enemies were hiding and they soon surrounded the Chauhan forces. Bhimasimha fought bravely but being outnumbered he was soon overpowered and slain. Hammira was fuming at the loss of one of his ablest generals and he blamed his counsellor Dharmsimha for this blunder and got him blinded and installed his brother Bhoja on his post.

Bhoja was a brave general but not a good financier, he failed to cope up with the growing monetary needs for the war. Dharmasimha saw this as an opportunity to impress Hammira and offered that he can arrange for as many cavalry as Hammira demanded. Hammira accepted the offer as he was in dire need of fresh troops for the upcoming war. However, he didn’t realize that his blind minister made his sovereign extremely unpopular by raising heavy taxes on the people for funding his war. He also instigated Hammira against his brother Bhoja who couldn’t tolerate the disrespect and joined Alauddin’s camp being a traitor to his clan and motherland. At Bhoja’s suggestion, Alauddin dispatched a large army to attack Ranathambore before the season of harvest. The Muslim forces proceeded up to the Hinduvāṭa pass where they were unexpectedly attacked by Hammīra’s army directed by Hammīra’s brother Vīrama, the generals Ratipāla, Jājadeva and Ranamalla, and the neo-Muslim leaders Muhammad Shāh, his brother Kahbru, and two others called Tichara and Vaichara. The Khaljīs sustained a heavy defeat. Much of their equipment fell into the Hindu hands. A number of Muslim women were captured and forced to sell sour butter-milk in the villages of the Ranthambhor kingdom to make known everywhere the great victory gained by the Chauhāns.

This was a great blow to the prestige of Alauddin and he ordered reinforcements in large numbers to be sent to Ranathambore. On the pretext of carrying out a peace settlement, Ulugh Khan marched up to the fort. His messengers offered two alternatives to Hammira. He could either offer four lacs of mohurs, present four elephants, and give his daughter in marriage to Alā-ud-dīn; or he could surrender the four Mongol chieftains who had flouted the authority of Alā-ud-dīn and taken refuge at Ranṭhambhor. The gallant chauhan who was undaunted by these threats replied proudly that Al-ud-dīn could have from him as many sword-cuts as the number of the gold mohurs, elephants and horses that he had demanded.

The muslims started preparation for besieging the fort by carrying mines to the foot of the fort and scaling its walls while the Hindu side was constantly throwing stones and fire weapons from the ramparts of the fort on the Muslims. It was while inspecting the construction of a raised platform in front of the fort that Nusrat Khan was struck by a ball from the fort and died in three or four days. This was a great loss for the muslim army as Nusrat was one of the bravest generals of Alauddin. However, Hammira had no time to lose, he opened the gates of his forts leading his cavalry and brought havoc on the grieving muslim army forcing them to retreat to Jhain once again with everything they managed to take with them. This was the greatest victory of Hammira because the army of Khaljis who had plundered the land till Devagiri was now faced with imminent defeat by the hand of a petty Hindu state.

However, Alauddin was baffled with the news of the death of Nusrat but he decided to press the siege and marched himself from Delhi to Ranathambore. The omen didn’t look good as there was an assassination attempt on his life at Tilpat near Delhi. However, he continued the march defying every sign and reached Ranathambore. With the arrival of Alauddin, a new vigour was installed in the Sultan’s army and they started filling the sacks with sand and throwing them into the moat of the fort. Piling one sand bag on another, Muslims constructed a Pashib and when it had reached high enough to touch the western tower of the fort, they started shooting large earthen balls from those towers into the fort. A mine was also constructed to reach the foot of the fort.

However the gallant Chauhans were not demotivated by these developments, they destroyed the towers by throwing stones from the fort and killed the muslim soldiers engaged in the construction of mine by throwing on them oil mixed with resin. Alauddin’s big army was now getting tired of the siege as they couldn’t witness losing their comrades from the fire projectiles sent by Hammira’s forces from the fort. While Alauddin was busy in Ranathambore, there was a revolt against him in Awadh and an insurrection in Delhi. However, he managed to quell both of them but the omens were bad and it looked like the obstinacy to capture the fort would lead to the fall of his kingship. While the open country around had altogether been ruined, a spirit of despair was overtaking the Imperial troops. Cut off from the capital, the soldiery was certain in its belief that they were destined to perish under the impregnable walls of Ranthambhor. In secret Alauddin would deliberate with his confidential nobles about the causes of the successive revolts and the seriousness of the situation, but outwardly he showed as if nothing of importance had happened, and pressed the siege with great vigour.

The siege which began in January was now entering the rainy season which increased the difficulties for the imperial army and there was no sign of defeat on the proud chauhans who were defiant in their attitude. Allaudin asked Hammira to send Ratipala, his general, to discuss preliminary negotiations and for once it appeared that he was making a genuine offer for peace. Ratipala was received with lavish gifts by the Sultans and he convinced them that if Alauddin managed to capture the fort, he would install Ratipala as the new King. Ratipala fell to the temptations and failed his master, turning a traitor to his race and deserting his king. He reported the false condition of the Alauddin army to Hammira informing him that the sultan cared little for the losses in his army and demanded his daughter for marriage. Ratipala managed to convince Ranmalla, another general of Hammir to join him and a little after the sunset both these traitors fled out of the fort joining the imperial camp sealing the fate of the glorious lineage of Prithviraja. The muslim side was pumped up from these new developments. Even if their condition was not good, the condition of those inside the fort was worse. Hammira had till then put up a brave show worthy of his race but with traitors from Ranathambore in the hostile camp, he could not put up the false appearance for long.

At last provisions fell short in the fortress and soon famine raged to such an extent that one “grain” of rice could be purchased only for two ‘’grains’’ of gold. ‘‘Man can bear every affliction but that of a starving stomach,’’ says Amir Khusrau, and the valiant Rajputs could not bear the pangs of hunger. In the imperial camp, on the other hand, gold was distributed freely among the troops to give them fresh incentive to fight. When nothing remained in the fort except hardship, despair and starvation, Hammira decided to die fighting in the battlefield better enough to die because of starvation.

A huge flame started burning in the fort signalling the inauguration of Jauhar which was led by his queen Ranga devi and daughter Devalladevi. Both of them ascended the funeral pyre with courage and sacrificed their lives to protect the honour. The Mongol guests of Hammira, Muhammad shah and his followers were amazed at this sight and filled with devotion towards their protector who was sacrificing everything he had for their safety. Hammira offered safe passage to the Mongol chiefs but they preferred fighting along with their king to death.

With the culmination of Jauhar, the gates of the forts were opened and the remnant of the Rajput soldiery, their nobles and king donned saffron garments and dashed forth to engage the enemy in a last combat. They fought desperately for there was not the least desire to save a single life. The battle raged fiercely and blood flowed on every side. Muhammad Shah and Kehbru, grateful to the last to the Rajput king for his hospitality and sacrifice, fought side by side with their patron. At last the great Rana Hammir fell, fighting gallantly on the field of battle, as yet in the prime of his life. Thus ended the glorious lineage of the great Prithiviraja as muslim sources inform us that none of the Hammira’s family members were captured alive.

Alauddin entered the fort and he saw Muhammad shah lying with his wounds, he asked him, “What would you do If I order for your wounds to be treated and save you from this imminent death, how would you behave towards me then?” The grateful Mongol replied gallantly, “If I recover, I will slain you and raise the son of Hammir to the throne”. Alauddin was enraged at this reply and ordered him to be trampled under the feet of an elephant but soon he remembered about the loyalty and bravery of the fallen general and ordered a decent burial for him. However he executed Ratipal and Ranmala who had defected to his side saying that, “Those who have betrayed their natural sovereign will never remain true to another”.

The impregnable fort of Ranathambore fell on 10th July 1301 followed by a usual plundering of temples inside the fort by Sultan’s troops. It marks an end to the glorious story of defence led by Hammira who never negotiated on freedom and fought for the independence of his motherland. The fall of the fort was accelerated by traitors changing sides but Hammira showed no sign of fear and defeat. Some people ascribe this great tragedy to Hammira’s obstinacy but Hammira was a man who fought against hope because he thought it was his duty to do so, he scorned to bow his neck before the oppressor, because he thought such a course opposed to the way of his ancient house, he deserves our sympathy and our admiration. Hammīra is such a character.

Born in the noble house of the Chohāns, to whom, as Tod observes, “the palm of bravery amongst the Rājput races must be assigned,”. Hammīra tried to uphold the independence of his race and to make its usages respected, and was for a time preeminently successful in his wars against his enemies. He fell in a war undertaken to protect the Mongol noblemen who had fled from the tyranny of Alauddin to him. Hammira lived these couplets composed by the eminent poet Chandrashekhar:

**शरणागत पालन करे, अरु बरते सुचि नीति।**

**समर सहस्र सन्मुख सहै, यही छत्रिन की रीति।**

The one who grants asylum to the refugee and follows good policy, faces a thousand battles. These alone are the virtues followed by a Kshatriya.
