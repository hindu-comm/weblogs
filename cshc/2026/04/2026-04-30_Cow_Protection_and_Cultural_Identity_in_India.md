Cow serves as a revered symbol in the Indian subcontinent, scholars have pointed out the worship of cow in the Harappan civilization as well as several injunctions in the Vedas associated with its worship. The bovine gained absolute importance with the emergence of Puranic Hinduism as several Hindu scriptures maintained that all Gods and goddesses reside in the body of the cow and all pilgrimages have their abode in the cow’s body. According to the Brahmvaivart Puran,

> “All the gods reside in the body of the cow and all the holy places
> reside in the feet of the cow. The person who applies the tilak of the
> mud from the feet of the cow on his forehead immediately gets the
> virtue of bathing in holy water. The place where the cow lives is
> called the holy land and if a person dies in such a place, then his
> salvation is certain”

In almost all the rituals and worship of the Hindus, it is mandated to have the “Panchgavya” which translates to Five cow’s products. Service to cows is considered the biggest means to accumulate spiritual merit among Hindus. Bhisma in Mahabharat states that “Cow, land and Saraswati, all three have similar names, they should always be donated. The cow is called the mother of all humans, she gives happiness to everyone”. According to Hindu theology, God always takes incarnation to ensure the protection of cows and Brahmins on the earth whenever they are threatened. Explaining the cause of Lord Rama’s birth, Ramcharitmanas says,

“For the benefit of Brahmanas, cows, gods, and saints, the Lord took the human form. His body is created by His own will, beyond maya and its qualities”

The greatness of the cow and its glory is spread over almost all the major scriptures of Hindus mandating service to cows for the devotee. Cow is believed to be a giver of any wish because the “Kaamdhenu”, a divine cow that sprang up from the legendary churning of the ocean according to Hindu theology is a possessor of magical powers who can grant her owner whatever they desire. She is believed to be the mother of all cows and resides with sage Vasishtha the revered teacher of Lord Rama.

The holiest river for the Hindus, the Ganga originates from the Gomukh glacier symbolizing the shape of the mouth of a cow. The imagery of Lord Krishna is always associated with a cow and he is referred to as “Gopaal” or “Govradhan” The Hindu temple architecture generally has the “Gopuram” which serves as the entrance tower for a temple specifically in southern India.

In almost all pagan cultures, cows and the bull commanded deep veneration in nearly all ancient agrarian cultures, where they symbolized fertility, strength, and divine power. In ancient Egypt, the cow-goddess Isis-Hathor was believed to incarnate in a living calf, and harming such sacred animals could result in death or heavy fines. In Babylonia, the ox represented Ramman, the storm god, and deities like Sin and Athtar were associated with bulls. In Tyre, goddesses Ishtar and Ashtart were revered as cows. The Hittites and Minoans worshipped the bull as a sacred link between man and god, with Minoan rituals involving youths performing daring feats with bulls. In Palestine, Baal, a fertility god, was often depicted as a bull, similar to the Greek Dionysus. In Phrygia, killing an ox or damaging a farm tool was punishable by death, underscoring the cow’s role in agrarian life. Even in Rome, as Pliny records, a man was executed for killing an ox merely to satisfy a concubine’s craving showing how the cow was more than livestock; it was life itself symbolizing the divinity.

However, a few Marxist scholars have tried to downplay the religious significance of the cow in Hinduism by pointing out discrepancies about its sanctity in early Vedic literature. Prominent among them is Dwijendra Narayan Jha, who led a campaign against the cow, denying its pious position for Hindus by drawing out biased evidence from the Vedic literature and alleging that the entire cow protection movement is based on hatred toward Muslims. Religion is an evolutionary phenomenon, and Hinduism gradually changed over time, transitioning from the Vedas to the Puranas, during which several rituals were transformed, some even disappeared, and many new ones were introduced. Jha tried to prove that cattle sacrifice was an important part of Vedic rituals and that cows were even consumed for domestic purposes. He based his argument on the word “Goghana,” which appears in the Vedic pantheon and is also mentioned in the *Ashtadhyayi* of Panini. According to Jha, the word means “someone for whom the cow is killed.” He concluded from this that guests were called “Goghana” because feasts involving cow beef were a normal thing in those days. However, eminent Sanskrit grammarians challenged his understanding of the word and refuted his claims, arguing that the word can have varied meanings and that even Panini differed from the interpretation Jha had deduced. Scriptures, especially the Vedas, can only be properly understood under a Guru who has mastered all the Vedangas, especially grammar. For someone like Jha, who was intensely prejudiced against the Hindu faith and insensitive to the religious persecution it underwent during the medieval period, the unilateral interpretation of the word “Goghana,” which supported his conclusion, is not surprising. However, it is important to understand the word properly in its context and through nuanced grammatical analysis before arriving at any conclusion.

It was this significant relevance of a cow in Hinduism that made this animal a target for persecution by the hordes of Islamic invaders who raided the subcontinent from the 8th century onwards. Muslim armies realized the importance and sacredness of a cow for Hindus so they used cow as a means to instill terror in the subject’s mind. The cow was used as a tool to desecrate temples as Hindus will not worship in a temple ever again if a cow is slaughtered there. One of the earliest depictions of cow slaughter for desecration comes from the Sindh campaign of Muhammad Qasim in the 8th century. According to Al-Bruni, “After capturing Multan he heard of the great temple of the city which was the cause of all its wealth. Therefore he thought it best to have the idol where it was but he hung a piece of cow’s flesh on its neck by way of mockery and in the same place a mosque was built”.

The same modus operandi continued in almost all the desecration of the holy sites of Hindus as Cow slaughter was used as a fear-mongering tactic to force the Kaafirs to abandon their sacred sites. It’s Interesting to note that the Indian subcontinent was invaded by several invaders even before the advent of Islam in the 7th century. Hunas, Kushans, Greeks, Parthians, and Scythians raided India several times, often causing havoc in the country. However, we see an attempt in the Indian texts to incorporate them into the ever-assimilating Hindu fold. In both Ramayana and Mahabharata, there is a story related to the origin of “Malechchas” a term denoted to the foreigners where it is told that the “Malechcha army” was created by Nandini, the divine cow of sage Vashishtha to counter the army of Viswamitra.

This changed when Muslim armies started making inroads deep in the subcontinent because they had a firm agenda of expanding the horizons of Islam by hook or by crook and cow slaughter was one of their regular tools. Prithviraj Vijaya, a 12th-century Sanskrit text on the life of Prithviraj 3rd makes this demarcation explicit as the text calls the “Ghurids”(Muslims) as beef eaters. The text further mentioned that the name Gori is given to them because they ate cows as the word can be broken into “Go+Ari” meaning the enemy of cows. Since they ate beef they were considered enemies of the cow. In another 15th-century text, Kanhade prabandha uses the same attribute of beef eaters for the army of Alauddin Khalji. These accounts make it clear that the author and audience of these texts had a clear understanding of the food habits of the Muslims and how they are different from them. Beef eating and temple desecration were the primary markers that made them distinguished from the Indigenous population and these attributes made the Muslims impure in the mind of Hindus.

An inscription dated 1347 CE from Bennur, Karnataka emphasizes the importance of cows, it stated that the divine cow, Kamadhenu complained to Shiva that it was very difficult to walk on one leg, as it no longer had all four. That was a proclamation that only a semblance of dharma remained. Shiva recognized the gravity of the situation and said he would send King Sangama, and Dharma would stand firmly again. The inscription was a declaration that Vijayanagara was founded to reinstate dharma.

There are several episodes where the Muslim armies have slaughtered a cow in the temple followed by its complete desecration and it went unchecked throughout the medieval period. However, there were instances when Muslim rulers also recognized the significance of cows among Hindus. Vitthalnath, a 16th-century Hindu philosopher and son of the famous Vallabhacharya who founded the Pushtimarg sect of Hinduism managed to secure a royal grant from Akbar regarding the protection of cows. In a farmaan dated 9th March 1581, Akbar ordered, “At this time we have ordered that the cows of the indisputable prayer-offerer (well-wisher) Vithalrai, wearer of the sacred thread (Brahmin), wherever they are, should be allowed to graze. In Khalsa(Crown) or Jagir (lands), no one should injure or obstruct them in the least...”. In another rare farmaan issued by the emperor’s mother, Hamida Banu ordered, “They must permit his cows to graze(wherever they are)”. These two royal grants underscore the importance of cows for Hindus which made the emperor grant this concession. However such tolerance was rare and bigotry was often the norm.

In the reign of Shahjahan, his son and soon-to-be emperor, Aurangzeb displayed another instance of bigotry when he converted the Jain temple of Chintamani built by an influential Jain merchant Shantidas Jawahari in Ahmedabad into a mosque. He once again used the tested method of slaughtering a cow in that mosque knowing well that Hindus will not use this structure ever again to worship. When Shantidas complained about this to Shahjahan, he recalled Aurangzeb and appointed Dara Shukoh as the governor of Gujarat and also ordered a wall to be erected to separate the newly built mosque by Aurangzeb from the original temple. However, the Jains never used the temple again; for them, it had been defiled forever, as a cow had been slaughtered in its boundaries.

In Hindu polity, an ideal king is always eulogized as a protector of cows and brahmins. We can hear its echoes even when the Bijapur general Afzal Khan destroyed the sacred city of Tuljapur and marched towards Pandharpur, the head priest of Pandharpur sent a message to Shivaji invoking him for help by mentioning, “It is your fond ambition to establish the Hindu faith; but the king of the Yavanas has sent a general to punish you. He has oppressed in various manners the Hindus, Brahmanas, and cows of Tuljapur and Pandharpur. If you can protect us from his tyranny, then alone will Hinduism prosper.”

Coming into the modern period, as the British gradually brought large parts of India under their control, the issue of cow slaughter gained renewed urgency. The Kuka and Gaurakshini movements were among the earliest and most heartfelt responses to the growing concern over cow slaughter in colonial India, reflecting the deep emotional and spiritual bond that Hindus and Sikhs shared with the cow which was regarded as the symbol of divinity. The Kukas, a devout Sikh sect in Punjab, led a bold campaign against cow slaughter in the mid-19th century. Their approach was militant and confrontational, involving direct attacks on slaughterhouses. Though their bravery generated widespread sympathy for the cause, it also provoked harsh repression from the British authorities. The outcome of this violence led later cow protection activists to adopt a more measured and strategic path.

This new approach found leadership under Swami Dayanand Saraswati, the founder of the Arya Samaj in 1875. While Arya Samaj had a reformist orientation in many areas, on the issue of cow protection it stood united with traditionalist Hindu sentiment. Dayanand was instrumental in shaping a nationwide, organized, and peaceful movement. In 1882, along with some of his former orthodox opponents, he helped establish the first Gaurakshini Sabha (cow protection society) in Punjab. These sabhas worked by rescuing wandering cows, providing them shelter in gaushalas, and collecting public signatures to petition the government for a ban on cow slaughter.

The Arya Samaj branches across India took up the cause with unmatched enthusiasm, using the printing press effectively to distribute pamphlets and spread the message. Their campaign turned cow protection into an all-India movement, especially in regions like Punjab, the Northwestern Provinces, Oudh, Bihar, and the Central Provinces. Swami Dayanand’s ideological defense of the cow combined Vedic authority with economic rationale. In his pamphlet *Gaukarunanidhi*, he argued that the cow was essential for both spiritual well-being and material prosperity.

Another notable leader was Gopalrao Hari Bhide, a lawyer and reformer from Nagpur, who emphasized the economic benefits of cow protection. His Nagpur Gaurakshini Sabha, equipped with its own press and newspaper, achieved remarkable success in reducing cow slaughter. Over time, this sentiment found echo in various parts of India, turning cow protection into one of the most emotionally charged and widespread social-religious movements of the 19th century.

The issue of cow slaughter kept resurfacing in various forms throughout the British period, and it was also a major cause of the 1857 Sepoy Mutiny, when Indian soldiers in the British army revolted over the suspicion that rifle cartridges were greased with tallow derived from beef, something that would violate their religious beliefs. During the rebellion, sepoys issued several decrees prohibiting cow slaughter in the areas that came under their control.

It was against this background of thousand years of persecution of the holy mother of Hindus which kindled the spirit of cow protection in the National movement. Many freedom fighters promised that the first goal of the Swadeshi government would be to impose a ban on cow slaughter. This ignited hope among thousands of Hindus who believed that Cows would once again gaze without fear on the holy land of Bharat. Mahatma Gandhi like any other Hindu held the cow as mother and he remarked, “I would not kill a human being for the protection of a cow, as I will not kill a cow for saving a human life, be it ever so precious”. He believed that cow protection was the greatest gift of Hinduism to the world. He even declared that the prohibition on cow slaughter was more important to him than Swaraj itself.

When eminent lawmakers from across all parts of the country sat together to frame the Indian constitution, the issue of cow slaughter again rose in the debate. Several leaders of the congress party like Seth Govind Das, Pandit Thakurdas, Shibban Lal Saxena, Ram Sahai, and Raghu Vira among others demanded a ban on cow slaughter in independent India. They demanded it to be included in Fundamental Rights, however, Dr Ambedkar opposed its inclusion in Fundamental Rights as a Cow was not an individual. While supporting the resolution of banning cow slaughter, Seth Govind Das remarked, “Our Hindu society, or our Indian society, has included the cow in our fold. It is just like our mother. It is more than our mother. I can declare from this platform that there are thousands of persons who will not run at a man to kill that man for their mother or wife or children, but they will run at a man if that man does not want to protect the cow or wants to kill her.” After an intense debate, it was decided to include the provision against cow slaughter in Directive principles of state policy where it was not enforceable but it guides the state in the formation of policies.

It is enshrined in Part 4 of the Indian constitution as Article 48 which mentions that the state shall prohibit the slaughter of cows and calves and other milch and draught cattle. Based on this, several states have imposed a ban on the slaughter of cows and punishment for those engaging in this activity. In a landmark judgment given in 2005, the Supreme Court of India upheld the ban imposed by the state government on the slaughter of cows, opening the way for further such legislation.

Reverence for the cow is one among the very few things on which all Hindus can agree without any sectarian differences. In an interview, Swami Vivekananda once remarked, “Hindus from North to South are only agreed on one point, viz. on not eating beef.”

The cow has been at the forefront of the civilizational battle ever since our land faced the hordes of barbarians imposing their religion and customs on native Hindus of this land. The cow is used as a tool to mock Hindus even to this day, the prime accused terrorist in the deadly 2019 Pulwama attack, Adil Ahmad Dar referred to the people of Hindustan as “drinkers of cow urine” and warned that more such attacks will be unleashed shortly. The trope that Hindus are cow worshippers has been used by Islamist around the world to degrade and mock our beliefs and customs.

The cow is a foundational symbol of Hinduism and thus serves as a fundamental symbol of the cultural wealth of the nation. Several freedom fighters including stalwarts like Mahatma Gandhi, and Rajendra Prasad dreamt of an India where Cow slaughter would be completely prohibited. Vinoba Bhave, Swami Karpatri Maharaj, and several others dedicated their lives and led several struggles to demand a ban on cow slaughter. However, the deaf state blind with the policy of Nehruvian secularism paid no heed to their demands and forsaken its cultural obligation as a civilizational state.

According to the constitution, the state is bound to protect cows which is not only a boon to our agriculture-based economy but she is a revered figure for billions across the world. Though several states in the country have put forward stringent regulations preventing its slaughter, there is a pressing need to recognize the historical persecution faced by the animal as well as its religious and cultural importance for the majority community of the country. There is a need to put an end to the slaughter of cows as no worship can be successful on the land where the cow is slaughtered.
