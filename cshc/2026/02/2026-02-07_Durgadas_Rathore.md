# Durgadas Rathore

### A Great Symbol of Rajput Chivalry

Durgadas Rathore was the son of Askaran Rathore, a minister in the court of Jaswant Singh Rathore, king of Marwar. In the battle of succession which began soon after the death of Shahjahan among his various sons, Jaswant Singh sided with Dara Shikoh. Jaswant Singh was one of the most trusted nobles of Shahjahan and like his master he championed for the cause of Dara, eldest son of the king. However, Aurangzeb defeated the other sons and crowned himself as an emperor in 1658.

At the time of his accession, Jai Singh of Amber and Jaswant Singh Rathore of Jodhpur were the leading Hindu nobles in the Mughal court. Aurangzeb who couldn’t wait to initiate his bigoted policies against the Non-Muslim subjects of his empire waited patiently. Raja Jai Singh died in 1667 at Burhanpur while returning from Deccan and Jaswant Singh was recalled from Deccan and posted at the mouth of Khyber Pass in Jamrud, Peshawar.

He died at Jamrud on 28th November, 1678 and with his death 5 of his wives and 7 concubines burnt themselves to death. He died leaving no heir to the throne but at the time of his death two of his queens were pregnant but they too were insistent on committing Sati. On this occasion, Durgadas Rathore arrived on the scene for the first time. He along with several other leading rathore nobles persuaded the queens to prevent the extinguishment of the Marwar kingdom. The queens yielded to their demands and the Rathore Party left for Delhi.

On the other hand, Aurangzeb who was waiting for this opportunity decided to seize Marwar and place it directly under his crown. Rathores who had served the Mughal empire from Afghanistan to Bengal and deep south in the mountains of Deccan were being rewarded a betrayal in return for their service as their new bigot master was hell bent on increasing the size of his territory by any means. On 9th January, 1679, the emperor himself set out for Ajmer, in order to be close enough to Jodhpur to overpower any opposition and direct military operations if necessary. Leading nobles like Shaista Khan, Asad Khan and Prince Akbar were summoned to reinforce him.

The campaign went into full swing and Marwar was easily brought under imperial control with virtually no resistance. The Rathores of the Marwar were leaderless and the best among them were in the service of their king Jaswant Singh who were returning to Delhi. The mughal forces under Aurangzeb went on a rampage and many ancient temples were destroyed and anyone who resisted the Mughal advance was slain.

According to “Maasir-i-alamgiri” a contemporary Persian source: “Cartloads of idols were taken from Jodhpur to Delhi and flung down in the cavalry square of the Imperial court and under the steps of Jama Masjid to be trodden on by the muslims”.

After successfully conducting the operation, Aurangzeb returned to Delhi on 2nd April, 1679 when any resistance seemed impossible. He reintroduced the “Jaziya” tax on the same day which was abolished by Akbar in 1564. It was a tax levied on Non-muslims in return of the state permitting them to follow their religion. Eminent historian Jadunath Sarkar believes that Aurangzeb expected some resistance from the part of Jaswant singh rathore if he announced Jaziya while he was alive. Therefore, he waited for his demise as now there was no leading Hindu noble in the court, he could plan out his bigoted schemes without any hindrance.

Aurangzeb appointed “Indra Singh Rathore” as the puppet king of Marwar who was a grand nephew of Jaswant singh and sent mughal officials who were posted in different parts of the province to help the new king. The Rajput party led by Durgadas Rathore has reached Lahore where both the queens gave birth to sons, One of them was Ajit Singh who went on to become the next king of Marwar while other was Dalthamban who died in infancy.

On the reaching of Rathore band in Delhi, they were stationed in the haveli of Rup Singh of Kishangarh. The Rathore nobles wanted the legitimate throne of Ajit singh should be restored to him but Aurangzeb who was an imperialist by nature didn’t wish to lose such an important opportunity to place Marwar directly under his control.

Aurangzeb agreed to grant an interview to the Rathore nobles led by Durgadas. The Rathore nationalist pressed for the rights of his master but Aurangzeb put forth a demand which filled the Rajput party with hate and disgust. According to a contemporary source, “Tarikh-i-Dilkasha”: Aurangzeb agreed to grant the throne of Jodhpur to Ajit singh if he was brought up in Mughal harem and accepted Islam. There is no surprise in this offer of Aurangzeb as he has previously favoured those Zamindars who accepted Islamic creed in family disputes and granted them royal Sanads. However, the Rajputs who couldn’t compromise on their pride felt consternated at this indignant proposal but the cunning Durgadas pleaded that the prince is too young to be parted with his mother. Once he is grown up, I’ll send him to the king myself.

Aurangzeb couldn’t be fooled with these proposals. He ordered Faulad khan to storm the haveli and capture the queens and the infant at once. The rathores decided to sacrifice their lives for the honour of their motherland and for the glory of their clans. Raghunath Bhati decided to lead the charge in front of the Haveli while queens dressed in male attire and Ajit Singh in a basket with Mukund Das Khinchi who was dressed as a snake charmer fled from the haveli towards Marwar. Raghunath Bhati died heroically after stopping the Mughal forces for more than an hour. Now it was the turn of Ranchordas Jodha who led the charge and accepted the same fate. The Mughal party kept pursuing the Rathores and now Durgadas himself decided to check the Mughal advance with a band of 50 soldiers. He was severely wounded in the conflict but the Mughals were tired and they didn’t pursue the chase any further. Durgadas returned with 7 of his men still alive and rejoined his prince. Durgadas reached Marwar safely with the infant who was the hope of Rajputs.

The Rathore Sardars were pumped with joy and courage at the news that Ajit Singh is still alive. Durgadas entrusted Ajit Singh with Mukunddas and he grew up in the mountains of Abu faraway from the Mughal eye. Aurangzeb was frustrated with the escape of Ajit singh but to delegitimize the Rajput claim, he announced that real Ajit singh is still with him. He brought up a milkman’s son and named him Muhammadi Raj and gave it to her daughter Zeb-un-nissa to raise him. It proves that Aurangzeb had a clear intention of converting Ajit Singh into a Musalman.

A grand force was sent for the invasion of Marwar under Sarbuland Khan and reinforcements were called from every province. Aurangzeb called his sons, Muazzam and Akbar to join him in the conquest. He himself reached Ajmer again in September, 1679. Marwar was directly placed under the crown and Mughal officers were appointed in each district of Marwar. The Rathores were harassing the Mughal garrisons and launched surprise attacks on the imperial party. Rathore strongholds were South and eastern Marwar due to its geographical terrain.

Marwar shares its eastern boundary with Mewar which is separated by Aravalli from the Rathore province. The districts of Pali, Sirohi, Rajsamand are on the borders of the two kingdoms which are thickly forested and house low mountains. Rathores made these areas their base and from there launched several attacks on the Mughals. Maharana Raj Singh who was the Maharana of Mewar was following these disturbances happening in his neighbourhood. The mother of Ajit Singh was a Sisodia princess and the Rathores had kinship ties in Mewar too. Raj Singh realised that if Aurangzeb succeeded in capturing Marwar, It would prove to be a preliminary to the conquest of Mewar.

Durgadas Rathore requested Maharana Raj singh to save the honour of Rajputs, Maharana granted the Jagir of Kelwa to Ajit singh and started preparation for the war. He deserted the city of Udaipur and retired in the Kumbhalgarh area to offer resistance. Aurangzeb ordered Hasan Khan to invade Mewar. The rathores were now emboldened in their struggle and launched surprise attacks at night to harass the imperial troops.

Aurangzeb himself visited Udaipur and the fort of Chittor towards the end of 1679 and hundreds of the ancient temples were desecrated by the Mughal army to please their orthodox king. Aurangzeb posted his son, Akbar for leading the attack against Mewar. However, Akbar and his general Tahawwur Khan were slack in conducting operations and Rajput soldiers regularly harassed them. The rajputs advancing from the forests looted supplies of the Mughals and launched an attack at night , making the stay of Prince Akbar awful. Once the Rajputs even attacked Chittor fort, Mughal base at night creating a sense of Panic among the Mughal garrison.

Durgadas Rathore was tactfully coordinating all these operations and was in no mood to lay down their arms. Aurangzeb got frustrated with the slow advance of his son, Akbar and removed him from Mewar. He appointed Muazzam at his place while he was sent to Marwar. A grand scheme was planned to invade the Kumbhalmer area from two directions. Akbar led the charge from the Marwar side while Muazzam advanced from the Mewar territory.

However, Akbar again proved to be a sloth and advanced very slowly. Aurangzeb was highly anxious with this behaviour of his son but little did he know that Akbar had joined the Rajputs and was planning a coup to become the next king. Durgadas was a combination of the reckless valour of a Rajput and a shrewd diplomacy of the high Mughal official. He convinced Prince Akbar to rebel against his father who was sitting at Ajmer with little protection. Akbar who was already frustrated with this endless campaign in a hostile territory accepted the proposal.

On 2nd January, 1681 Akbar rebelled against his father and with a combined Mughal and Rajput force he marched towards Ajmer. Aurangzeb’s camp was alarmed and everyone was frightened as it seemed an easy victory for Akbar. Aurangzeb issued letters to his son Muazzam and other nobles to reach Ajmer quickly with reinforcements. Prince Akbar who was overjoyed with his success engaged in indolence and marched very slowly. He reached the outskirts of Ajmer after 2 weeks. By then, Aurangzeb was emboldened by the reinforcements of Muazzam, Shibab-ud-din and other generals. The fate of the battle was sealed at night by Aurangzeb’s treacherous letter to Durgadas where he praised Akbar for fooling the Rajputs by bringing them here. Rajputs were alarmed and they fled from the scene at night. Akbar was surprised to find his camp deserted and he ran for his life towards Marwar.

Upon realizing the treachery of Aurangzeb, Durgadas decided to protect the life of Prince Akbar at any cost. Aurangzeb tried to offer gold for seducing Durgadas but the Rathore sardar couldn’t be swayed away from his ideals. He organized a band of 500 Rathore cavalrymen and escorted Prince Akbar safely to the protection of Shambhaji Maharaj in Konkan. The Rathore party dodged the Mughal outpost and the party led by Prince Muazzam was sent to Aurangzeb to capture the fugitive prince. It’s important to note that Durgadas gained nothing from this daring enterprise of escorting Akbar other than the fulfillment of his promise. He couldn’t sustain the dishonour of refusing to protect a “Sharnagat”. He could’ve gained immense favors from the emperor if he surrendered Akbar to him but the Rathore leader chose chivalry.

Durgadas spent the next 6 years of his life in Deccan and managed to reach Marwar again in 1687. In these years of his absence the revolt continued unabated but it was a leaderless revolt with no concrete plan of action. Aurangzeb had now shifted his priority to deccan which removed some pressure from the Marwar. Aurangzeb was in no position to send reinforcements to Jodhpur as all his resources were concentrated against the Marathas which relieved the Rajputs.

In 1687, Shujaat Khan was appointed the Subedar of Marwar. He was a good administrator and he tried to win over the Rathore Sardars by granting them different Jagirs and concessions. By this time, Ajit Singh too was out of concealment and he was meeting with his different Sardars who were overjoyed to see their master. The war continued like before and Rathores kept looting the Mughal outposts even levying taxes from them as a security guarantee for traders who crossed through the roads in Marwar.

After some time, Ajit Singh too started joining Durgadas Rathore on his expeditions against Mughals. Aurangzeb was reaching his old age and he was anxious because 2 of his grandchildren were in custody of Durgadas. While leaving for Deccan, Prince Akbar left his daughter, “Saifiyat-un-nissa” and his son “Buland Akhtar” with Durgadas. He entrusted them to a Brahmin named Giridhar Joshi where they grew in concealment. Saifiyat was reaching puberty and Aurangzeb wanted her back at the earliest. Negotiations for her return were started and Ishwardas Nagar, Amin of Jodhpur began talks with Durgadas.

Durgadas agreed to return Saifiyat-un-nissa to the emperor but the princess requested Durgadas to accompany him. He agreed and Ishwardas took both of them to the Emperor’s court in Deccan. Upon receiving his granddaughter, Aurangzeb gave instructions to begin her religious education but the princess replied that She had already memorized the Quran. The king was astonished and he got to know that Durgadas had arranged for a female teacher from Nagaur who gave lessons of Islamic law and religion to the princess. Aurangzeb was highly pleased and surprised. This shows the character of Durgadas who kept Akbar’s children in an honorable condition and made no efforts to change their religion. Even in a condition of Guerilla warfare he arranged for their religious education. He was not even frightened when visiting Aurangzeb at his court.

In 1698, he again came to Deccan with Prince Buland Akhtar. Aurangzeb bestowed Durgadas with 3000 Zat and 2500 Sawar(Cavalry) along with a jeweled dagger for this chivalrous deed. Durgadas also managed to get the Jagir of Siwana, Jalore and Sanchor for his master Ajit Singh. Durgadas was appointed as Faujdar of Patan in Gujarat as Aurangzeb wanted to keep him away from Marwar. Peace persisted for some time between him and the Mughals but with the death of Shujaat Khan in 1701 the tensions renewed.

Prince Azam was appointed the new Subedar of Gujrat and Marwar. He tried to kill Durgadas treacherously but he managed to escape and joined Ajit singh in Marwar. The rebellion resumed again with full vigour. Durgadas was almost 65 years of age by now but that had not reduced his passion to oust the Mughals from his motherland.

Aurangzeb died in March, 1707 and with his death the triumphant Rathore army led by Ajit Singh and Durgadas captured the fort of Jodhpur easily. The fort was washed with Gangajal and Tulsidal as it was occupied by Malechchas for a long period of time. Ajit singh started tearing down mosques and Muslims were being killed in Jodhpur. This forced Bahadur Shah I, the Mughal emperor to launch an invasion of Jodhpur. Jodhpur was taken back again from Rathores and Ajit singh was asked to join the Mughal forces which were marching for Deccan.

Ajit Singh along with Sawai Jai Singh of Amber were going with Bahadur Shah I to Deccan but they were visited by Durgadas Rathore at a place called Mandaleshwar in Malwa. He convinced them to return. They left the Mughal camp and reached Udaipur. A Rajput alliance was formed here between the three kingdoms of Mewar, Marwar and Amber. The combined force attacked Jodhpur and the Mughal garrison fled saving their lives from there towards Ajmer. The Rajput army then attacked Jaipur and placed Sawai Jai singh on the throne.

The Mughal army started arriving at Ajmer to check the Rajput advance and a great battle was fought between the two forces which is known as the Battle of Sambhar. The Mughal army was routed and this decisive Rajput victory in 1708 forced Bahadur Shah I to adopt a conciliatory attitude with the Rajput rulers. Unfortunately some differences started brewing between Durgadas and Ajit Singh by this time resulting from the efforts of nobles who were jealous of the fame enjoyed by Durgadas.

As a result, Ajit Singh banished Durgadas from Marwar. This ungrateful act on the part of Ajit singh deserves condemnation. He was not able to show the respect which Durgadas reserved and acted on the advice of jealous ministers. This act tainted the legacy of Ajit Singh forever. Durgadas suddenly vanishes from the Marwar chronicles after the battle of Sambhar. The heroic Durgadas, the preserver of his(Ajit Singh) infancy, the instructor of his youth, the guide of his manhood, lived to confirm the proverb, “Put not thy faith in princes.”

Durgadas reached the court of Mewar where he was received with due respect and granted the Jagir of Vijapur. He also received an allowance of 15000 per month from the Mahrana. It’s said that Bahadur Shah I demanded the surrender of Durgadas from the king of Mewar to which the Mahrana Sangram Singh II magnanimously refused.

Durgadas spent the next 10 years of his life in Mewar faraway from his motherland while Ajit Singh enjoyed his growing fame. Later on, Durgadas started spending most of his time in Ujjain where he died in 1718 at the age of 80 years at the banks of Shipra. The cenotaph of Durgadas at Ujjain is still a sight of beauty for the visitors who visit the city. Hardly they know the story of this great Rathore hero who spent all his life in hardship to protect his motherland from the Mughal yoke only to be cremated faraway from the same motherland at the end.

Fortunately enough, Durgadas remained to see the Mughal empire tottering towards its end after the death of Aurangzeb. He fulfilled his promise of making his motherland free, the same motherland from which he was banished by the king whose life he protected at all cost.

Durgadas Rathore was the fidelity personified, he was brave and diplomatic at the same time. He refused every favour for his master even if he got betrayed by the same master at the end. Jadunath Sarkar sums up his character beautifully, “A soul of honour, he kept the deserted daughter of Akbar free from every stain and provided her with every facility for Islamic religious training in the wilderness of Marwar. Fighting against terrible odds and a host of enemies on every side, with distrust and wavering among his own country-men, he kept the cause of his chieftain triumphant. Mughal gold could not seduce, Mughal arms could not daunt that constant heart. Almost alone among the Rathors he displayed the rare combination of the dash and reckless valour of a Rajput soldier with the tact, diplomacy and organizing power of a Mughal minister of State. No wonder that the Rathor bard should pray that every Rajput mother should have a son like Durgadas”.

The heroic deeds of Durgadas Rathore are celebrated widely in the Rajput Bards and there are many popular couplets on his character. One of my favourite among these is:

“**आठ पहर चौसठ घड़ी, घुड़ले ऊपर वास;**

**सेल अणि सूँ सेकतो, बाटी दुर्गादास।**”

Durgadas Rathore was a warrior who spent day and night on horseback, constantly fighting and battling difficulties to protect his homeland and his honor.
