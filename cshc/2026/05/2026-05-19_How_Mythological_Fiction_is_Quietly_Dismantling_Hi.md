A wave of bestselling retellings systematically reinterpret the Hindu *Itihāsa*, referred here as epics, through Marxist and left-liberal frameworks fundamentally incompatible with the ontological assumptions and Dharmic reasoning that govern these texts. These are not reinterpretations, but ideological replacements. Whether the vehicle is Marxist class analysis, Radical feminism, or Dravidian identity politics, these frameworks share a common assumption: that the epics are instruments of social power rather than living theological systems. All of them are justified under the academic premise of the ‘death of the author,’ which means that the texts mean whatever readers make of them, and the result is the same: a civilisation’s sacred inheritance strip-mined for ideology, without engaging with it on its own terms. The influence of such works now permeates popular culture through theatres, streaming series, and cinema and thus shapes the popular perception of Hinduism itself. This article examines the literary corpus of retellings that constitute the problem, traces the pattern of distortions produced, and outlines the path to correction.

#### **The Core Problem: Why Left-Liberal Interpretations Destroy Theology**

When Marx called religion “the opium of the masses,” he meant religious ideas sedate the working class, making them accept suffering as divine will rather than recognizing exploitation. This interpretation comes from Marxist analysis which increasingly influences the contemporary Left-Liberal worldview, where religion is always a tool for elites to justify power. Hence, when interpreted through this lens, all religious texts are ideological weapons. The Vedas become a Brahminical tool of oppression. The Bible becomes Roman Imperialist propaganda. The Quran becomes a tool for merchant control by the Arabs.

Can someone who is biased against allopathy be trusted to offer a fair and credible analysis of medicines? Clearly not. So why should religion be treated any differently? Consider this: when the *Srimad Bhagavad Gita* discusses *Karma Yoga* (selfless action), the Marxist reading sees this as propaganda to make workers accept exploitation without questioning. When the *Srimad Valmiki Ramayana* presents Shri Rama’s adherence to truth at personal cost, the Leftist reading sees rigid social hierarchy enforcement. When you start from the premise that every religious concept exists only to justify power, you cannot engage with the text’s layered reflections on moral wisdom and ethical responsibility.

A crucial clarification is that this article is not an attack on creative liberty. There are several contemporary works on Hindu theology that engage with these traditions from within the framework of Dharma itself. For instance, Amish Tripathi’s Shiva Trilogy reimagines Shiva as a mortal and writes on his journey of becoming deified. This is imaginative fiction that does not claim to correct theology. Left-Liberal worldview, however, views religion as something evil and interprets it accordingly.

This article is particularly significant at a time when mythological fiction has become one of the most commercially dominant genres in the Indian publishing market. For a readership that rarely engages the original epics, these retellings become the primary interpretive gateway, allowing them to shape the popular understanding of Hinduism. Although this article analyzes only the top-selling books from this genre, it is important to note that emerging authors continue to enter this expanding genre using the same conceptual framework, due to its commercial profitability.

### **Decoding Thematic Patterns and Literary Examples**

Before examining specific books, it is worth understanding the patterns these retellings follow. These patterns are active in books you may have read, recommended, or gifted. Once you recognize these patterns of distortion, you will see them everywhere: in book clubs, in university syllabi, in theatre adaptations, in streaming series. The goal here is not to tell you what to think but to equip you to recognize when a retelling is engaging with Hindu theology on its own terms versus when it is systematically dismantling it. These recurring mechanisms include manufacturing psychological motivations absent in the original texts, and imposing modern ideological categories onto metaphysical narratives.

#### **Villains as Heroes through Ideological Inversion**

**Ranganayakamma’s three-volume Telugu book series “Ramayana Vishavruksham”** (1974–76) offers a Marxist critique of the Ramayana. It portrays *Rāmarājya* as a feudal and casteist socio-economic regime, and Rama as a monarch who exercises Kshatriya privilege and patriarchal control. Essentially, Rama’s acceptance of exile to honour his father’s vow, his fourteen years of hardship, and his willingness to sacrifice personal happiness for Dharma are somehow reduced to class-exploitation. On the other hand, **Robert P. Goldman**, an American translator of the *Vālmīki Rāmāyaṇa* observes, the text consistently foregrounds restraint on authority and accountability to justice and serves as a model for equity and compassion in governance.

**Anand Neelakantan’s “Asura series”** (2013-2014) applies this inversion across both epics. In **Ajaya**, Krishna’s theological status is dismantled, the Bhagavad Gita becomes battlefield propaganda, and his interventions become Machiavellian manipulation. Once the divine is removed, Dharma stops being cosmic law and becomes arbitrary social rule, and the moral architecture of both epics collapses into a story about who holds power and who does not. In **Asura**, Ravana becomes a learned scholar whose Lanka represents a merit-based society oppressed by a Brahminical Ayodhya, and Sita’s abduction is romanticised as tragic love. This misrepresents a theme that runs across both epics: that the heroines carry an intrinsic divine protection, due to their unwavering commitment to Dharma, which makes their violation impossible. Rāvaṇa cannot touch Sītā for the same reason Dushasana cannot disrobe Draupadi. To attribute this to Rāvaṇa’s personal honour is to strip the heroines of their spiritual power and hand it to their captor.

The rehabilitation of Ravana as a wronged hero is not new to contemporary fiction. It has its roots in **Periyar’s ‘The Ramayana: A True Reading** (1930), which declared the epic an instrument of upper-caste domination, recast Rama as an Aryan invader, and Ravana as the Dravidian king whose defeat represented the subjugation of an entire people. The logic is simple: if the Devas are Aryan oppressors and the Asuras are their Dravidian victims, then the villains of the tradition were always its true heroes, and the heroes its oppressors. Contemporary retellings like Neelakantan’s Asura map the same framework onto the fabricated Deva-Asura binary, with Devas as Brahminical oppressors and Asuras as the wronged, inverting the moral hierarchy of the tradition entirely.

#### **Adharma as Virtue through the Rehabilitation of Wrong Choices**

**Shivaji Sawant’s “Mrityunjaya”** (1967) transforms Karna into a caste justice hero. Karna demonstrates the most dangerous moral configuration; genuine virtue deployed for adharma. During Draupadi’s disrobing, he equates her with a prostitute. His tragedy is wrong choices despite Dharmic knowledge. He knew Duryodhana was wrong. Shri Krishna offered him the chance to come to the side of Dharma, yet he chose adharma. **Kavita Kane’s bestselling debut “Karna’s Wife: The Outcast’s Queen”** (2013) carries this rehabilitation further through a fictional invention. Uruvi, a Kshatriya princess who chooses Karna at her swayamvara, exists in no scripture. Her perspective reframes Draupadi’s rejection of Karna as caste prejudice rather than a lawful exercise of choice, and reduces his participation in her disrobing to a forgivable lapse. Both books attempt to rehabilitate him by asking the reader to sympathise with a man who knew the difference between Dharma and adharma, and chose the latter anyway.

**Vaishnavi Patel’s New-York Times Bestseller “Kaikeyi”** (2022) applies a similar reframing to the politics of Ayodhya. Kaikeyi’s invocation of her boons is presented as the assertion of a voice constrained by courtly structures, and succession becomes a question of denied recognition. In Hindu theology, however, Kaikeyi acts knowingly within a web of vows and destiny that ultimately enables Rama’s exile and the fulfilment of a larger Dharmic purpose. The book completely neglects that in the epic, kingship is not portrayed as privilege but as a burden sustained through sacrifice and restraint.

**Anand Neelakantan’s “The Asura Way”** (2024), uses theological episodes to give self-help lessons. The book’s examples are drawn almost entirely from the actions of the Devas: Shiva, Vishnu, Brahma, Krishna- but it calls itself the Asura way. Even if we ignore this blatant contradiction, the central premise of the book itself is dubious. The *arishadvargas* or the six enemies of the mind (anger, greed, pride, lust, attachment, and jealousy), are repackaged as tools for success in Kaliyuga, with no acknowledgment that these are precisely the forces Dharma exists to overcome. One of the stories cited in the book is the episode in which Shiva beheads Ganesha in a fit of rage, which the author proclaims as evidence that anger is a natural and even useful emotion to be embraced. What it strips out entirely is that Lord Shiva was struck with deep remorse and immediate regret due to this action, and the act is immediately redeemed through divine compassion, which is impossible for us mortals. Hence, to use Shiva’s anger which functions within a cosmic framework of destruction and renewal, to justify an emotion that any rational person would agree should be kept under check, is honestly absurd. More significantly, while the writing fixates on the ideas of *arishadvargas*- there has been explicit negligence of the four *purusharthas* (Dharma, Artha, Kama, and Moksha), which are an inalienable constituent of the tradition that birthed the *arishadvargas*. The blatant disregard of the contemporaneous existence of such ideas, where the former was considered not only the ideal way of life but also a tool in advancing the attainment of all gains, functions as an amputation of Hindu theology.

#### **Women’s “Liberation” through Radical Feminism**

**Chitra Banerjee Divakaruni’s “The Palace of Illusions”** (2008) is presented as Mahabharata from Drauapadi’s perspective, which is quite appealing. However, the manufactured romance between Draupadi and Karna is questionable because it reframes what Draupadi’s disrobing means. Consider an excerpt from the beginning of the book, setting the imaginative romantic subplot: “His eyes were filled with an ancient sadness. They pulled me into them. My impatience evaporated. I no longer cared to see Arjun’s portrait. Instead, I wanted to know how those eyes would look if the man smiled.” This angle recasts Draupadi’s story through psychological grievance and romantic longing, displacing a theological inquiry into Dharma and justice with a narrative of personal desire and dissatisfaction.

Contrasting to the fictional realism of the author, Karna actually challenges Draupadi’s character and virtue. Any fictional rendering that opposes the ethics of conduct between the sexual predator and the victim of sexualised insults is diabolical. Consider this excerpt said by Karna during the disrobing incident, from Krishna Ganguly’s “The Mahabharata of Vyasa”:

> “”O Sun of the Kuru race, the gods have ordained only one husband for
> one woman. This Draupadi, however, hath many husbands. Therefore, it
> is certain that she is an unchaste woman. To bring her, therefore, in
> this assembly attired though she be in one piece of cloth - even to
> uncover her is not at all an act that may cause surprise. Take off the
> robes of the Pandavas as also the attire of Draupadi”  
> -Karna, Section 67

A recurring strategy in **Volga (Popuri Lalitha Kumari)’s “The Liberation of Sita”** (2016) and **Chitra Banerjee Divakaruni’s “The Forest of Enchantments”** (2019) is to reinterpret the Ramayana through the lens of female emancipation from inherited roles. In Kumari’s retelling, Sita encounters ‘marginalized women’ such as Surpanakha, Ahalya, Renuka, and Urmila, who model “breaking free,” and liberation is defined as detachment from husband, throne, and inherited duty. In Divakaruni’s narrative, emotional interiority and personal injury become the organising centre, and episodes such as the Agnipariksha and the abandonment are framed primarily as humiliation and betrayal. Yet in Hindu theology, Sita’s choices are deliberate and conscious. She insists on accompanying Rama into exile. She undergoes agnipariksha voluntarily as a demonstration of spiritual integrity so complete that even fire cannot harm her. Her strength lies in intentional adherence to Dharma even when it is difficult. By redefining agency as exit from sacred obligation, these retellings transform marriage and queenship from chosen vows within a moral cosmos into sites of confinement.

Across these works, marriage and kingship are treated as inherently suspect institutions from which liberation must occur. In Hindu theology, however, they are sacred responsibilities entered into consciously and sustained. Agency is not something recovered from structure; it operates within structure and generates karma. The greatness of our protagonists (irrespective of gender) is in choosing to uphold Dharma even at personal cost, because Dharma itself is the catalyst for the greater good and the restoration of moral order: *dharmo rakshit rakshitaha*. It is this voluntary commitment to Dharma that makes these characters worthy of reverence and worship. When they are instead portrayed primarily as victims of circumstance, their moral stature is diminished, and the very source of their greatness is stripped away.

#### **The Popular Culture Pipeline: How Literature Shapes Mass Perception**

The pipeline from novel to popular culture is well established, and with each adaptation, the ideological framework travels further from the bookshelf and deeper into mass perception. These literary inversions have metastasized into popular culture, fundamentally altering how an entire generation understands Hindu theology.

In film, the most consequential development is the adaptation of Divakaruni’s The Palace of Illusions, with Deepika Padukone as the lead. The production has been confirmed to be based directly on the novel, meaning the invented Draupadi-Karna romance, which has no basis in any manuscript tradition, is on its way to becoming a mainstream Bollywood retelling of the Mahabharata. The screenplay itself is being developed with Anand Neelakantan, who has also been a scriptwriter for shows including Siya Ke Ram on Star TV and Srimad Ramayan on Sony TV, shaping the narrative for television audiences. The Palace of Illusions has also been staged as a theatre production titled Fire and Ice: Draupadi’s Story, performed in India, bringing the same invented narrative to live audiences. The two frameworks, Divakaruni’s radical feminist inversion and Neelakantan’s Dravidian identity politics, are thus converging in a single mainstream film.

In academia, institutionalisation is already underway. The College of the Atlantic runs a course titled ‘Epic Heroines: Feminist Retellings of Indian Mythology’ which assigns Divakaruni, Volga, Kane, and Patel as the primary texts for understanding the Hindu epics, stating in its course description that the epics have ‘set the standard for what an ideal Hindu woman should be, bolstering the patriarchal system.’ These novels are not supplementary reading, they are the primary texts. The University of Hyderabad’s Indian Writing in English resource positions Divakaruni’s feminist retellings as the standard scholarly lens for approaching the epics. A PhD thesis submitted to NIT Rourkela’s Department of Humanities and Social Sciences applies western feminist theory to Kavita Kane’s retellings as authoritative interpretations of the Mahabharata. Most significantly, a peer-reviewed paper published in an Indian academic journal explicitly advocates incorporating these retellings into school and college syllabi under NEP 2020, arguing they foster gender equity.

The effect is measurable. Most people today believe Karna was a hero wronged by circumstance, despite his role in Draupadi’s disrobing. People praise Ravana for not touching Sita while failing to understand the divine protection her Dharma afforded her. People believe Rama abandoned Sita, though the Uttara Kanda is contended to be a part of the original Valmiki Ramayana, as explained aptly in Ami Ganatra’s Ramayana Unravelled. For an entire generation, mythological fiction has become the primary text, and theology has been quietly replaced.

#### **What Is Lost and What Must Be Recovered**

At the foundation of Dharma lies Ṛta, cosmic order governing everything from nuclear fusion to human character. Dharma is alignment with Ṛta, not a social contract invented by authorities but a reflection of cosmic reality itself, the natural inclination to give back to the world that sustains us.

Consider how this operates in the Rāmāyaṇa. By accepting exile to fulfill his father’s vow, Rama enacts Tapasyā, the cultivation of willpower through hardship, and Loka-saṅgraha, acting for the welfare of the world rather than personal happiness, since subjects follow the moral example of their king. His sacrifice is Yajña, the recognition that the cosmos is sustained through giving. This integrated arc is why Rama becomes Maryādā Puruṣottama. Draupadi operates through the same framework. When she is dragged into court and both the Pandavas and Bhishma fail her, she does not abandon Dharma. Her public questioning is not a personal grievance. It is violated Ṛta demanding restoration through the only avenue left to her. This is why she is worshipped as a goddess across South India. Deification, for men and women alike, comes from upholding Dharma, especially when it is difficult and others do not.

When Marxist or Left-Liberal interpretation flattens this into oppressor versus oppressed, Tapasyā becomes repression, Yajña becomes violence, and Krishna’s interventions become manipulation. A generation loses not merely the correct reading, but the conceptual apparatus needed to read these texts itself.

Hindu theology understands existence as cyclical, moving through four Yugas, repeating endlessly as Dharma progressively declines at the end of every Yuga. Destruction is not cruelty or failure but a necessary function for renewal, and it is within this framework that divine action must be understood. It is for this reason that the epics were never encountered in isolation but within a living ecosystem of oral tradition and ritual practice, which together ensured stabilised meaning and disciplined interpretation. As this ecosystem has weakened, texts have been severed from their doctrinal contexts, and into that vacuum step interpretive models drawn from Western hermeneutical and Left-Liberal traditions, frameworks fundamentally alien to the civilisational grammar of Dharma, yet increasingly treated as authoritative.

The big question remains- how do we correct this? The correction will begin from recognising a more uncomfortable truth: these books are bestsellers because readers buy them. Every purchase, every recommendation, every university course that assigns them over the original texts funds the next ideological retelling. Both the authors, and the readers who made them famous are responsible for the displacement of Dharma from popular culture. There are works that engage with these same narratives from within the dharmic tradition, works that deserve the readership and attention currently flowing to these retellings. The fact that they still choose authenticity and honesty over potential profitability is commendable, and deserves to be rewarded. The bottomline is that what we choose to read, recommend, and celebrate is not a trivial act. When theology is severed from its philosophical roots and handed to external frameworks, distortion is not a risk. It is a certainty. But that severance does not happen without an audience.
