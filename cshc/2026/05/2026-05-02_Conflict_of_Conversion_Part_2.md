# **Different Meanings**

### **Conversio**

I can claim no expertise in matters of Christian theology. To demonstrate the Christian perspective on Conversion, however, here I attempt to briefly describe the same, relying primarily on the study done by Roover and Clarehout.

Based on the insights of Dr. S.N. Balgangadhara, they present a twofold understanding of the means by which Christianity exists and propagates (“universalises itself”)- *proselytisation*: the explicit form. It propels religion within individuals and communities. This can be called the way it exists in a ‘religious’ milieu- tenets, rituals etc.; and *secularisation*: the implicit form. It means the propagation in disguised form, through institutions and ideas that are firmly rooted in Christian theology, but are a separate category within the religious world itself.

**Note**: I have discussed this twofold dynamic, in the characteristics and implications of secularism and secularisation in my previous articles.

Back to conversion, or conversio as it is called in Latin. The word refers to a complete turnaround in views, a change of heart. A transformation through which the believer becomes a true Christian. The Bible exhorts to “go and make disciples of all nations, baptising them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, and teaching them to obey everything” commanded by Jesus. \[Matthew 28\].

The original sin of eating the forbidden fruit by Adam amounts to disobeying and leaving the Creator, the burden of which still rests heavy on the shoulders of man. God did mercy by sending His son, Jesus, whose ‘sacrifice’ delivered humanity from sin. The way out is to repent the inherently sinful nature of humanity by following the faith and surrendering to the ‘divine’ ‘saviour’ Jesus. It must be noted that salvation is beyond human capability alone and can be achieved only by ‘God’s grace’ and working of the ‘Holy’ ‘Spirit’ within all. To ‘meditate’ is to ponder upon and realise one’s sins and negligences. Finding sufficient cause for mourning and weeping, reading the punishments of hells etc to realise the merciful nature of God and one’s destitution.\[11\]

> “Core elements are obeying God’s commandments, refraining from loving
> one’s own will, obediently submitting to one’s superior in the
> imitation of Christ and the perfect obedience He showed towards God
> the Father, discovering in the depths of his heart that one is but ‘an
> inferior wretch’ and ‘worm’, and yet never despairing of God’s mercy.
> While climbing this ladder, the monk underwent the process of
> conversion or the soul’s turning towards God.” \[12\]

The twofold (and inherently sinful) nature of humanity was categorised into Carnal: the physical, flesh, outward ‘man’; and Spiritual: the inward, ‘soul’, nature of man. Both of these are forever in conflict which causes all the turmoil. The soul longs for the fulfilment of the (Christian) spiritual desires and the body for worldly ones (sinful). Conversion therefore is the process of transforming oneself to completely subdue the sinful desires. The monk’s life was supposed to be as perfect an imitation of Christ as possible, conversion being a lifelong process of striving to achieve it. It would, however, not be limited to just monks. The Protestant reformation added new dimensions to what it meant to be a Christian; what truly constituted a Christian life. These ideals came to be regarded as the basis of Christian lifestyle for everyone. All practices and activities of humans would be aimed at modulating their lives w.r.t. those core values. The Church and clergy came to be regarded as corrupting agents. Roover argues that through

> “these assaults on the Roman Catholic Church would result in a generic
> model for describing and denouncing the ‘false religion’ and ‘heathen
> idolatry’ allegedly discovered in other parts of the world. That
> generic template of false religion would shape the way in which
> British missionaries and officials approached and criticised the
> traditions they encountered in colonial India.”

It is crucial to note here that the grave sin of idolatry now was not only the worship of images and figures, but also the false human additions (corruptions) in the Gospel. The believers were being misled into thinking that these human works could assist them towards salvation. Luther is quoted by Roover as saying,

“truly the entire ministry of the papacy, even at its best, does nothing but mangle and murder conscience.” \[13\]

Thus great effort was to be made to discern the ‘true’ scripture from human ‘fabrications’, to ensure the believers were on the right path to salvation. This would later be extended to Indian religion, where attempts would be made to prove that the shastras’ human authorship, thus exposing their falsity. The Church was called ‘the arch-whore of the devil’, because it was believed to have “abandoned the ancient church and its ancient bridegroom and have not only become apostate and heretical…but Antichrist and “anti- god”, indeed, the last and most shameless bride of the devil, setting herself up even above God”. It was proclaimed that there is one baptism, one faith and one gospel- the uniting forces of Christendom, thus meaning all Christians were one people- materially and spiritually. All tasks of life were now means of serving God (and thus potential conversion grounds). Path to salvation lied in serving God in all capacity of the occupation and the active realisation of the ‘sins’ incurred meanwhile.

Our point here being, that for various eras of history,“‘conversion’ has referred to a specific phenomenon and process in the world: that of becoming a Christian and turning towards God”.\[14\] This whole system included a set of interrelated notions and narratives, such as God’s intervention, the soul’s inherent yearning, divine grace, limited role of human efforts, repentance and penance etc. Therefore, to classify conversion merely as a decision to adopt a new set of beliefs and customs would not be fair to it’s distinct nature. It would not take into account peculiarly Christian concepts such as God’s role and humans’ ‘conscience’, thus posing problems in an honest analysis.

This distinct nature should have led to problems in the conversation of conversion, as Roover calls it, with their Indian counterparts. This might also partially explain the discomfort and incomprehension displayed by Hindus regarding conversion. In the next part, we attempt to study precisely this.

### **Gandhi and the Hindu Perspective**

Gandhi wrote in detail on the issue of conversion and was visibly troubled by what he perceived as a betrayal of all morality by the missionaries who engaged in needless and generally fraudulent practices to convert people. He presents an excellent view that displays the Hindu response to this clash of systems. He interacted with the subject time to time and his grievances regarding the same present a valuable window. Writing in *Harijan* “If I had power and could legislate, I should certainly stop all proselytising.” And in *Young India* that “I cannot understand a man changing the religion of his forefathers at the instance of another.” \[1\]. He also supported individuals’ right to convert to the faith of their choice. Then how does it make sense? Let us try to understand.

> “As I wander about throughout the length and breadth of India I see
> many Christian Indians almost ashamed of their birth, certainly of
> their ancestral religion, and of their ancestral dress. The aping of
> Europeans on the part of Anglo-Indians is bad enough, but the aping of
> them by Indian converts is a violence done to their country and, shall
> I say, even to their new religion…Conversion should mean a definite
> giving up of the evil of the old, adoption of all the good of the new
> and a scrupulous avoidance of everything evil in the new. Conversion,
> therefore, should mean a life of greater dedication to one’s own
> country, greater surrender to God, greater self-purification.” \[2\]

This shows Gandhi’s ambiguity to the actual nature of Conversion, as Roover writes:

> “Gandhi’s ‘change of heart’ is described **as an achievement of the**
> ***satyagrahi*** **or ‘truth-seeker’ as a human being, rather than a
> process initiated and sustained by God.** Satyagraha is understood as
> a ‘powerful’ process of conversion, which can fundamentally change a
> human being. It is not a process of turning away from the earthly
> material realm but one of transforming oneself and other people in
> society. For Gandhi, the leaders of India must go through this process
> in order to bring about the necessary change in others. He similarly
> describes non-cooperation as a process of conversion, whereby Indians
> should convert through their own model conduct even the staunchest of
> British colonials.Crucially, according to him, ‘**a new birth, a
> change of heart, is perfectly possible in every one of the great
> faiths’, not just in one ‘true’ religion. Ideally, conversion should
> take place within the ‘religion’ or tradition in which one is born and
> raised**.” \[3\]

The reason this is pointed out is, that while at a glance Gandhi sounds just like Christians in his definition (change of heart, inner purification etc.), but his usage of the words is incompatible and seemingly incoherent with the actual context these terms exist in.

Gandhi thus widens the scope of conversion as an activity of purification of oneself and one’s own tradition. The direct implication, obviously is that:

> “The real *shuddhi* movement should consist in each one trying to
> arrive at perfection in his or her own faith. In such a plan character
> would be the only test. What is the use of crossing from one
> compartment to another, if it does not mean a moral rise?” \[4\]

This interpretation indicates that there are three facets to how Gandhi views conversion:

1.  Conversion is fine but only as a process of spiritual growth within
    one’s present tradition. Conversion should serve to make the person
    a better human being, and since Gandhi values all ethical-moral
    systems equally, it is unnecessary.

2.  If the tradition has faults then the adherents have a duty to
    ‘reform’. {Roover in-fact mentions multiple instances of Gandhi
    echoing that “‘the campaign against untouchability is not one of
    compulsion, but of conversion”} \[5\]

3.  The right method of propagating a faith is not proselytisation, but
    by lived example. (Hence the realest form of conversion is ridding
    oneself of faults etc) \[6\]

For the missionary, the activity thus means the propagation of the Gospel’s word, of Christ’s message of salvation from sin. Gandhi on the other hand, is definitely discussing something else but using the same vocabulary as the other side. This poses an obstacle as the terms used are not free from the contexts they are drawn from.

Gandhi also presents a rather different interpretation of truth:

“By a true faith I mean one the sum total of whose energy is for the good of its adherents, by a false I mean that which is predominantly false” \[7\]

Roover quotes an instance of a follower asking Gandhi a question about how advocating religious ideas is not similar to economic or healthcare opinions; to which he gives a revealing answer. He says that the latter are subject to methodological establishment of what may be called scientific truths. In religious, or rather, spiritual ‘truths’ there is no such criterion.

From the perspective of the missionary however, the mission to convert is precisely this- an effort to preach the by nature universal truth of Christianity, opposing which amounts to stopping them from sharing the universal with the rest of the world. The missionary has criteria that determine if a religion is true, rendering Gandhi’s idea irrelevant.

“When the Christian missionary speaks of truth, he or she does not only mean the truth of propositions but the truth of the biblical God’s revelation, which is viewed as independent from any human knowledge.” \[8\]

So, what Gandhi fails to understand is that the Christians’ truth is that which is stated by the Gospel. The only way to redeem oneself from sin is that shown by Jesus. It is not a matter of moral goodness or ancient nature of a tradition as Gandhi theories, but adherence to the doctrine of the Biblical God’s will.

To make sense of this apparent incongruence, Roover and Clarehout discuss two terms- *conceptual distortion* and *topoi.*

**Topoi** are the clusters of interconnected ideas whose meanings depend of the presence of general context. For example ideas about religion in the western world are understood in a Christian background and used to develop newer ideas in the same. **Conceptual distortion** is the process that occurs when topoi originating from one culture are discussed (and interpreted) in another, different cultural landscape.

This theory explains Gandhi’s use of English terms as adoption of ideas and phrases from the west, but simultaneously mapping them into Indian topoi- which causes the apparent incoherence.

When he says faith and religion, he means traditions of communities, ancestral practices etc. which are not rooted in theological doctrines and thus need no justifications in the same. The process of refining and self-correction by a living tradition is what Gandhi refers to when discussing ‘reform’ as a way of conversion. This also explains why Gandhi disregards people who convert without properly understanding their own. If religion is understood as tradition, the logical predicated of ‘truth’ and ‘false’ do not really apply; no religion can be false (hence worthy to be left). Gandhi’s message thus is a spiritual-moral conversion within the confines of one’s own tradition.

> “Hence I cling to my religion, as a child to its mother. But I have no
> dislike for other religions, as a child has none for other mothers.

> \[…\] I therefore prefer to retain the label of my forefathers so long
> as it does not cramp my growth and does not debar me from assimilating
> all that is good anywhere else.”

All these traditions contain moral-ethical heuristics that assist as instructions to the followers in becoming better humans. These too bear no truth claims; meaning that they can migrate from one tradition to other without any obstacle.

The equality of all religions thus comes to mean that they are equal in the sense that they fulfil the aspirations of their respective communities. (At one place he compares it to how every climate on the globe has a best suited clothing that will not be so good for others)

It is this interpretation of religion as tradition and ancestral practices that makes proselytisation attempts cause great discomfort to Gandhi’s Hindu mind. An unwelcome disruptive intrusion. \[9\]

# **Conflict, Neutrality and Pluralism**

As seen earlier, this whole debate manifests into various streams of thought in our times. While some argue for complete ban on conversion, some prefer permitting it to uphold freedom of religion, while some say that curbing it is a violation of their own religious liberties. All these parties appeal to the state, styling their arguments in the tone of tolerance and neutrality. We must however, ask the question, can the state remain truly neutral? If yes then how and what does neutrality even mean? If no then do we have a proposal on how it can?

My articles on secularism in April and June this year dealt with the meaning and form of Indian secularism and how it fails to accommodate India’s unique circumstances. This final part of this article may be seen as a continuation of the same study with religious conversion as the highlight.

Coming back to the subject of conversion and state neutrality, we may define it as symmetrical attitude of the state towards all- favouring no type of conversion over the other. This leads us to the question of whether Hinduism and the Semitic religions are phenomena of the *same kind*. A religiously neutral state would have to assume a yes to this, but that assumption would not be based on reality. While it is beyond the scope of this article to dwell in details, we can say that there exists plenty of literature on the matter to disapprove it and show that Hindu and Semitic religions are phenomena of different kinds. Thus the state is left with to options, either to proceed with the unfair assumption or treat the two as different kinds of phenomena.

As has been quoted numerous times in the first part, Hindus refused to accept theirs or the Christians’ as false religions. They strongly held that there was co-existence was possible with no competition among faiths. The Semitic religions on the other hand clearly perceive themselves as rival movements mandated to convert others. The state either chooses the premise of no-competition, or the one with competition of faiths. There is no middle ground.

We have seen how while Hindus say all religions can be ‘equally true’, missionaries are very clear in their belief in the unique right of the ideas of their scripture. This self description implies that other faiths are corruptions and heresies and definitely not equal to theirs. Conversion is possible from a false to a true religion only when one subscribes to the Semitic dogma. Any secular state that allows or bans conversion will have to choose whether both Hindu and Semitic traditions are candidates to truth and falsity, or they are not.

Proselytisation is an inherent obligation to the Semitic faiths because of their conception of true-false religions. Hindus, as we saw, do not subscribe to this worldview. The state will have to either accept that no religion is false, or that some religions could be false.

The above choices present before the state are logically exclusive and represent different points of view (Hindu and Semitic). The state has to choose either to uphold neutrality but it *cannot* as choosing any one would obviously compromise the very idea of state neutrality! \[The state can not play the agnostic, because claim of ignorance will negate the very capability to legislate on the matter.\]

The secular liberal state can perhaps remain neutral with respect to the ‘truth’ claims of various religions, but this pre-supposes that religion is based on truth claims. In simple words, while it can manage being neutral about the truth of religions, it is forced to “decide wether religion itself a matter of truth” \[10\] The liberal state has thus accepted a Semitic meta-claim as its factual assumption!

Balgangadhara, in the above quoted article, puts forward the argument that it is this acceptance of a Semitic value which generates religious conflict:

> “the state creates religious rivalry where there is none (if viewed
> from the majority perspective). As a matter of state policy, it
> creates and sustains opposition between religions and traditions.
> Consequently, it transforms the conflict between different groups into
> a religious conflict
>
> \[…\] The exacerbation of religious violence does not tell us that
> secularism failed in India. Its intensity tells us that secularism has
> been entirely successful in India. The secular state, which the
> secularists continue to wish for, does not prevent religious
> conflicts: **it actively promotes them.**”

### **Conclusion**

The liberal-secular model of a state superimposes a notion of all cultural traditions bing variants of a single phenomenon while actively subscribing to Semitic theology. Hindu traditions cannot be variants of the same phenomenon as Islam or Christianity, and here possibly lies the scope for the development of a pluralistic and suited alternative to the biased liberal secular model. The Indian landscape, where Indian and Semitic religions co-exist creates an insurmountable barrier for the secular state which threatens to collapse the very basis of it. It’s failure to decide upon an issue as major as religious conversion ought to serve as a sign of warning, and a call of re-examining it’s utility while interacting with other issues of Indian society.
