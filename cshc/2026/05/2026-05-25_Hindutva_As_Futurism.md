*The Dark Knight Rises has one of the best lines in the entire trilogy, and it doesn’t come from Batman; it comes from Bane: **“Victory has defeated you.”** That is remarkable wisdom coming from a villain. Look around, and you’ll see many people who have achieved victory, yet the common pattern is that once success is attained, it becomes easy to let off the gas. Victory creates space for mistakes, pride, and weakness to creep in. The film is powerful because it teaches the value of direction over position. At the beginning, Bruce Wayne occupies a victorious position: he relaxes in his mansion, surrounded by servants, rarely leaving his room. His position is excellent, but his direction is backward. His victory allows the enemy to creep in. Now, contrast this with his time in the pit. His body is broken, he is trapped in a prison from which no one escapes, and his position could not be worse. Yet his direction is forward, as his mind hardens into iron, because the absolute clarity of the enemy and the strict distillation of purpose eradicate all ideological ambiguity. He knows his purpose, and because of this clarity, he climbs out of the impossible pit. This ruthless clarity provides the precise velocity required to scale an impossible vertical limit of escape. This underscores the central message of Nolan’s trilogy: why do we fall? So that we can learn to pick ourselves back up. Hindutva provides ruthless clarity and the velocity needed to scale an impossible vertical limit. The overarching calculus of a catastrophic fall is never the end, but what must happen before you can rebuild yourself from the ruins.*

### **I. Introduction**

Savarkar’s vision of the state has been buried under the weight of his reputation. Those who revere him treat Hindutva as an identity to inhabit rather than an architecture to build. Those who condemn him treat it as a slogan to refute rather than a political technology to analyse. Both responses share the same evasion for us. They engage the symbol while abandoning the structure. When one studies Hindutva as an ideological blueprint for the state. His thought was actually trying to produce, through what mechanisms, on what foundations, and at what cost. These are not comfortable questions because the answers do not resolve neatly into either celebration or condemnation. What his deep reading reveals is a political vision of genuine sophistication, one that diagnosed the civilisational crisis of Hindu society with surgical precision and engineered responses that remain either unexecuted or misunderstood a century later.

While we can clearly see how the contemporary resurrection of Savarkar’s legacy is characterised by structural asymmetry, in which the organisations that revere him most operate in absolute amnesia of his actual architectural vision, which remains, to this date, hardly articulated. This failure is aesthetic before it is political. The Hindu inability to interpret the ruthless mechanics of the present age and their inevitable surrender to its currents represents a terminal crisis of political navigation that leaves the Hindus utterly defenceless. Yet the exact structural solution to this civilisational drift was meticulously engineered a century ago by Savarkar, whose specific formulation of Hindutva is designed precisely to serve as the absolute steering worldview for political modernity. A built that provides structural rigidity and the closed-circuit language of intelligibility required to survive a brutal era that punishes all ideological ambiguity with total extinction.

Nietzsche identifies two species-types in their relation to pain. The first reduces sail as the storm approaches, conserving energy and surviving through withdrawal. The second grows more martial as the pressure mounts — these are his great pain-bringers, the rare ones whose supreme moments arrive precisely when danger is most severe. They are not pathological. They are, in Nietzsche’s precise formulation, forces of the greatest importance for preserving and advancing the species, opposed to smug ease, unable to conceal their disgust at the happiness of comfortable decline. Savarkar belongs to this type entirely. So does Achilles. Achilles knew his fate. He could return home and live long, or remain at Troy and die young with glory permanently inscribed in collective memory. He stayed because long life was the lesser existence — survival purchased at the cost of the only thing that made survival worth anything. This is the transcendence escape hatch out of a clear-eyed assessment that kleos, glory that outlasts biological death, is the only form of permanence available to a mortal who refuses consolation. Grief alone cannot discharge it. Mourning is insufficient because the transaction remains open. Only action closes it. The dead creditor cannot be honoured through tears — only through the completion of the mission of what death interrupted.

This essay reads Savarkar as a state-builder rather than a symbol of history, thereby tracing the architecture of his vision from its foundations in his revolutionary movement to its logic of consolidation and its theory of violence, which he seeks to apply as an aesthetic technology of propagation. The goal is neither rehabilitation nor prosecution, but the harder task of understanding what was actually built, what was left unfinished and what the unfinished portions demand of anyone who seriously inherits the project.

### **II. The Framework Hindus Operate In**

The peculiar phenomenon of embracing humanitarian values without a parallel mechanism for self-protection has left many observers puzzled about what drives Hindus to mass mobilise in pursuit of their own survival. What produces this moral amnesia of virtue? Where does this inertia operate from? Hindus came to mirror the very collapse of the culture to which *Matthew Arnold’s* conception of culture once applied. For Arnold, culture, understood as the pursuit of perfection and a devotion to “sweetness and light,” stood as the most resolute enemy of anarchy and as a means of disciplining the unruly mob. Yet the attempt to view Hindu culture as a stabilising force ultimately failed because it rested on a fundamental misrecognition of the fact that every document of civilisation is, at the same time, a document of barbarism. The paradox for Hindus lies in the fact that their reverence for tolerance, openness, and transcendence precisely inhibits their mobilisation in political spaces. This reversal manifests in the adoption of increasingly humanitarian values without any corresponding capacity for self-protection. Culture comes to be treated as a picturesque ruin, admired for its aesthetic nostalgia rather than recognised as a site of violence. The ruin thus becomes the sole link between the splendours of the past and the degradations of the present, serving as a way of identifying a nation’s golden age. Yet even when such an age is invoked, it fails to safeguard the very self that enables these values, a failure that remains deeply problematic. One can be profoundly religious and ritually observant and yet remain entirely politically inert, oriented upward toward moksha rather than outward toward political consolidation. The process of translating this ethical and spiritual awareness into politics and radical force simply did not occur.

Savarkar’s ideology was forged in the Cellular Jail in the Andamans, a space that served as a laboratory where “virtue” itself became the primary vector of infection. In The Story of *My Transportation for Life*, we witness the steady collapse of his physical condition under continuous torture and abject living conditions, circumstances against which no traditional Hindu immune system could offer protection. The Andaman Cellular Jail exposed Savarkar to the inertness of traditional Hinduism in the face of radical ruptures of survival. Vulnerability is not treated as an exception. It becomes the way forward, the space in which survival slowly undoes the traditional Hindu self, the culmination of these feelings and then a reaction to the threats and destructive conservatism of his birth Hindutva.

The constitutive practices of the past included caste and its dining taboos, as well as the horror of food prohibitions. Prisoners never observed these outside their regional periphery. Prisoners who attempted these practices under survival conditions, then attached moral guilt to their failure, became vulnerable to exploitation by Muslim warders.

> ***“Muslim warders and jamadars forbade Hindu prisoners from reading
> their scriptures. They would look at the pictures in some of the
> books, including the Ramayana of Tulsidas, and comment that it was
> utterly indecent, and that it was their religious duty to disperse the
> gathering that read such books. Hindus received few or no religious
> holidays, but the same provision was readily made for Muslim
> prisoners. Several Hindu prisoners who were deported to the Andamans
> were being converted to Islam and began assuming Muslim names. As the
> ‘chalans’ began to reach the jail, simple and young Hindu prisoners
> would be segregated and subjected to extreme physical torture and
> labour by Muslim jamadars”***

Savarkar operated strictly within the momentum of kinetic reality, where small acts of courage are forcibly generated by love inextricably entangled with relentless misery. This absolute reality permits zero prolonged abstraction because suffering systematically erodes all metaphysical distance by stripping life of the sterile calm that allows one to linger over the immaterial. He did not abandon his poetry within the cellular laboratory. Still, he fundamentally transformed its function for himself by relinquishing the traditional poetic agency that thrives on passive paradox to forge a ruthless *Poetics of Action*. His writing captures the kinetic force required to violently rouse beauty from its historical slumber, thereby absolutely rejecting the preservation of beauty in cowardly isolation from historical trauma. In this violent awakening, Hindutva emerges as a strictly operational text, engineered by a poet’s absolute refusal to aestheticise civilisational collapse. His engagement with the subaltern was born entirely from the geopolitical demand for suffering, converting misery into a diagnostic tool rather than a moral spectacle, where it was never transfigured into a moral spectacle but also weaponised as a diagnostic tool.

His work concerns the material plight of the subaltern and the vision of the abstract as unidimensional and mutually necessary components, where misery becomes the exact thermodynamic ground on which abstraction must shatter and thought must harden into unbreakable resolve. For Savarkar, beauty which is detached from kinetic action becomes total civilisational complicity because love for the nation expresses itself through strictly absolute willingness to endanger oneself, where the risk of annihilation serves as the only mathematically valid proof of devotion. His work inhabits a space where the poetic becomes strictly incendiary, functioning as a command architecture where language ceases to soothe and actively compels the demographic mass toward its historical climax. This was a move away from passive curation and an act of contemplation.

This revolutionary incarnation is necessitated by the confrontation with a biopolitical technology of domination where minimal acts of filth, nudity and humiliation within the jailed premises functioned as weapons of destruction. A single contamination of a plate with beef, or the forced proximity to the forbidden, operated as a precision instrument specifically designed to trigger the catastrophic collapse of the ritual self. The supreme power of such acts lay precisely in their mathematical disproportionality because the symbolic infrastructure of orthodox identity was fatally engineered to fail through the mere physicality of sinning. The brutal demand to swallow beef operated as a precision instrument of psychological violation, enforcing obedience by collapsing the ritual self from within. What is explicitly revealed here is a pristine technology of imperial domination in which total control is achieved by inducing irreversible civilisational collapse through minimal symbolic intervention: the fragile ritual system, when confronted with coercive impurity, instantly converts applied pressure into unconditional surrender. He points to desecration as a highly efficient mechanism of sovereign control.

These moments of disdain were sufficient to undermine the Hindu’s sense of self and leave it in ruins. The ritual self was fragile, and once it broke down, it did not return. What followed was a thinning of moral confidence so complete that conversion came to seem like the only path left open. Savarkar stepped into this breach by shaping what can be understood as strategic impurity. He saw that for the Hindu nation to endure within the modern panopticon, it had to learn how to remain intact while passing through defilement. He compelled prisoners to abandon *pankti-bhed* (dining segregation). He championed shuddhi through reconversion to deliberately harden the psyche against moral transgression, stripping away its ritual character entirely, one capable of bearing moral transgression without collapse. He fought a three-front psychological war for his fellow prisoners. The first front was against the jailers, who sought to break the Hindu self through coercion and humiliation. The second was against the prisoners themselves, many of whom lacked the resolve to withstand physical pressure and preserve their inner strength. The third was against the inertia that prevented these tactics of coercion from being recognised, analysed, and transmitted to the wider community. His objective was to preserve the momentum of the revolutionary movement without dissolving its consciousness. The revolutionary moment, which he understood as Hindu in character, had to be protected. Once the Hinduness of the revolution was stripped away, the revolutionaries themselves would disintegrate. In response, he sought to mould Hindu subjects who were unafraid of kala pani and willing to violate ritual purity when political survival demanded it. Savarkar recognised that in an age of total war, an obsession with ritual purity constituted a critical security flaw. Political survival required the deliberate hardening of the self, even at the cost of inherited religious taboos.

For Savarkar, this **Strategic impurity** can be understood as a response to a biopolitical crisis produced by colonial modernity, where British carceral power targeted the symbolic infrastructure through which Hindu subjectivity was constituted and tried to discipline Hindu bodies. He reframed impurity as a tactical experience rather than an existential collapse. In this sense, impurity becomes a controlled exposure of means of immunisation. The subject (Prisoners of Port Blair) learns to endure defilement without surrendering their political identity. Sovereignty is not an output of a body of ritual commonality but a continuum, a sort of unyielding thread where identity persists despite violation. Savarkar’s Hindu is thus closer to a sovereign subject who can survive the state of exception. The body may be broken, polluted or coerced, but political will remains intact. What is preserved is continuity. Therefore, traditional Hindu morality is not treated as false but as structurally unfit for modern conditions of power. His intervention does not abolish *dharma; rather, it converts* a fragile glass structure into a shock-resistant core. In this framework, Hindu identity is no longer defined negatively by the exclusion of beef, caste, or the seven *bandis* he sought to dismantle, but rather through a new approach of positive attitudes, grounded in a minimal, irreducible political will. This is the “quanta” of identity: the smallest unit that must survive for the collective to persist. Strategic impurity is a sovereign recalibration under biopolitical pressure. It is an attempt to ensure that the Hindu subject remains governable in its own right rather than through its vulnerabilities.

### **III. Savarkar’s Rupture**

To survive the shattering of the ritual self, Savarkar recognised that the metaphysical umbilical cord to the past had to be severed. This cord bound Hindus to history and destiny while offering transcendence as an escape from both. The unresolved problems of translation, communalism, and the Hindu relationship with the modern state have been profoundly rupturous, leaving Hindus unable to integrate themselves into the body politic of Enlightenment rationality, or rendering them elements that such a body politic cannot fully absorb within a rational, democratic form of government. Savarkar’s writings must be read as an attempt to establish the foundations of a new kind of state. They contain latent seeds for a decisive break with the past and for a movement beyond history toward the construction of a new nation-state. This inquiry, therefore, begins by analysing Savarkar’s response to the imperative to sever the metaphysical umbilical cord and to bind Hindus to history and destiny without recourse to transcendence.

***A Hindu is most intensely so when he ceases to be a Hindu***

***-*** **Savarkar.**

Savarkar’s severance of the metaphysical umbilical cord relocates transcendence rather than destroying it. The collapse is a transformation of the path. Where traditions offered escape upward through renunciation. Hindutva demands transcendence *through* historical destruction. The Nation itself becomes the vehicle for immortality, embodying a metaphorical civilizational essence. To die for one’s nation is a heroic dissolution that achieves permanence in collective memory. This is why Savarkar could simultaneously advocate the closure of the route to moksha as an escape while constructing a framework in which revolutionaries and their sacrifice remain metaphysically potent. The abyss he confronts is not the abandonment of the sacred, but the recognition of modernity and its forces. For Hindus, the sacred can be attained only through total commitment to historical struggle.

> *tirī sevā meñ ai bhārat agar sar jaa.e to jaa.e* *to maiñ samjhūñ ki
> hai marnā hayāt-e-jāvidāñ merā*
>
> *In your service, O Bharat, if my head is lost, let it be lost. I will
> consider that death to be my eternal life.*

Savarkar’s hostility toward low political mobilisation, unfounded in the physics of Hinduism, was integral to the project of Hindutva. The aim was to make political inertness culturally illegitimate, viscerally extirpating it rather than simply encouraging its opposite. Elevation and achievement could no longer be sought through fragmented pieties, nor through the servitude of the self to private salvation while the collective burned.

*Let then the rest of our flowers too be plucked thus*

*And offered at the feet of Sri Rama*

*Let this mortal body be put to good use*

*Immortal is the family tree that has extinguished itself for the nation*

*Its fragrance of human welfare spreads all around*

*O Mother, weave a garland of all in bloom for the……Behold! On one side stand watching the past souls of sages and saints*

*Of our race gone before and on the other side the*

*Future generations yet unborn!*

*May we be able to acquit ourselves today in a manner*

*As to evoke universal approval from these godly spectators*

In Savarkar’s reframing, moksha arrives through total immersion in the world’s most violent demand. The family tree that extinguishes itself for the nation becomes immortal precisely through its extinction. The historical struggle has a ritual duration, in that the suffering of the present is a traversal of a sacred time-structure after which transformation becomes inevitable. This is distinct from both linear futurism and cyclical mythology. It is a theory of historical ripening that civilisational victory requires completing a full ritual arc of sacrifice before the transformative force becomes available. This gives his futurism a theological patience that machine logic lacks.

Civilisation itself became the sole vehicle of meaning. Any form of self-subordination that impeded the historical progress of Hindus had to be rejected. Political self-protection became the precondition for the continued existence of Hindu values, the act that made every other virtue possible. At the cosmological level, Savarkar collapsed the vertical axis entirely. *No escape upward. No transcendence. No renunciation.* Consolations that drained life of urgency were to be eliminated. The worthy, in this schema, were denied exit. This world was all that remained, and glory or shame within it was final. In effect, Savarkar abolished the doctrine of rebirth, which had long sustained a deferred optimism. His role as an agent underscores the deliberate nature of this intervention. There is no next life that matters, only the historical fate of civilisation. The individual is welded to that fate without remainder.

Simply put, this is a call to stay. It urges us to confront history rather than run from it. It asks us to view struggle as a constant part of life for those in the Hindu nation who want to connect with its civilizational past. This break needed a new foundation, something for identity to cling to once the option of transcendence was shut off. Savarkar supplies this anchor through the trinity of Pitribhu, Punyabhu, and blood, producing a form of belonging far more difficult to abandon than religion alone. Once transcendence was cut off, it could no longer function as an escape. Identity became inescapable, embedded within the nation, its territory, and its cultural markers. The metaphysical umbilical cord was replaced by chains of a different kind: chains that bound not the soul to sacred memory or topophilia, but the body to land, blood to collective fate and cultural memory to political destiny. Savarkar’s trinity functioned as a technology of capture, absorbing the foreign, the indigenous, and the assimilated into a single category from which beliefs could defect birthplace and ancestry could not. It is, ultimately, a project of remaining Hindu in the most literal sense.

#### **Violence and Its Ethics**

For Savarkar, violence is a tool. Sometimes it is the right tool. It must be used efficiently and without guilt when it serves Hindu interests. His position is unmistakably Machiavellian in the way this political realism is transplanted into the Indian context. While this stance is explicit in his writings, Hindutva operates at a deeper level than mere instrumentalism. For the traditional Hindu, violence functioned as a *pharmakon*, a term that denotes remedy and poison at once. The *Gita* articulates this ambivalence precisely through Arjuna’s hesitation to kill and Krishna’s effort to resolve it, not by encouraging desire but by disciplining action. Savarkar radicalises this move by eliminating ambivalence. Violence is recast as a form of medicine without side effects when correctly administered. It no longer carries moral residue. It no longer accrues karmic weight. Violence is transformed into a rational procedure. It acquires a surgical character. Threats to national sovereignty are rationalised. Their removal is presupposed so that the body politic may function.

> *Alas! When strength I had, how virtuous was I---Looting these
> Foreigners I deemed to be a sin. Once I overcome my stupor, O Mother,
> this very righteousness will curse me!*  

In this framework, violence generates no elemental recoil upon the Hindu psyche. Karma is neutralised through the method. Savarkar’s methodology demanded the cultivation of contempt for specific modes of being, deploying it as a rapid technology of delegitimisation that rendered the pacifist and the secularist structurally unintelligible. The task was pedagogical as much as political. It involved teaching the masses how to feel about specific positions and deploying contempt as a rapid technology of delegitimisation and dismissal.

A critical translation problem addressed in his framework is articulating an ethical structure that renders violence non-traumatic. It removes the moral paralysis that historically allowed Hindus to be outcompeted by Colonial and Islamic forces, which operated without such hesitation. For violence to become a tool, it must be oriented toward something. He doesn’t rely on the past to reach the value of revenge, which looks backwards, as it is the violence of mourning. But a new kind of violence which looks forward. It is instrumental, cold and purposive. It clears the ground for construction.

***Savarkar himself distinguishes between the morally beautiful but politically inferior technology and the morally uncomfortable but politically effective one. In his poetry, he highlights how beauty is detached from kinetic action, becoming a form of civilisational complicity. The pyre is that beauty and the dagger is the Poetics of Action. By choosing the dagger over the fire, he forces reorganisation of Kamlini as a lethal, self-activating mechanism of systemic civilisational defence.***

> *This dagger may not probably help to save the chastity of a young
> girl! But it surely will avenge the violated chastity. The prowess of
> this revenge will also save the chastity of other hundreds of virgins.
> The evil violator will be afraid to keep in his harem such women who
> are carrying such secret daggers, just like a man who is afraid of
> carrying fire in his sleeve.*
>
> ***KAMALINI - (Samagra Savarkar Vangmay Vol. 7 page 531)***

Within this ruthless ethical framework, violence ceases to be arbitrated by abstract morality and mutates instantly into a highly efficient biopolitical deterrent. Kamalini distinguishes between two modes of resistance: the sacred fire of Chittor, which is defensive, self-consuming, and ultimately denies the aggressor while destroying the innocent, and the dagger of revenge, which is offensive, targeted, and capable of deterring an entire population. This is a political philosophy of force. The pyre disappoints the aggressor but cannot eliminate him. The dagger changes his calculus entirely by introducing permanent uncertainty into his dominance. Savarkar argues that the aestheticisation of self-sacrifice, however noble, is a structurally inferior technology of resistance compared to the threat of reciprocal violence. The pyre is beautiful and morally legible. The dagger is ugly and politically effective.

This is why his relationship to the past is so peculiar for us, as he reveres certain figures and texts, but only as promissory notes for a future yet to be built in which no threat to civilisation remains. In his golden age, a nation yet to be built and fought for across all dimensions of space, which will lead to the creation of a modern, industrialised, scientifically advanced, and militarily powerful nation. He rejects nostalgia as a breed of passivity, since it points to a past that *existed* and can therefore be measured, found wanting, and demystified.

In this light, Savarkar demanded that individual energy be absorbed into a more effective collective body, the nation-state. This explains his insistence on scientific education, industrial development, and military modernisation, all directed toward the construction of a powerful Hindu body politic. These were tools for producing a future that had never existed. Hindutva, as both a life project and a political vision, sought to alter the very “quanta” of Hindu character permanently. Savarkar’s futurism abolishes the traditional escape hatch. Time becomes irreversible. What is lost in the present is lost forever. Every defeat leaves a permanent scar rather than remaining a passing phase. This psychological architecture renders the present the only moment that matters. It is in this context that his declaration, “Hinduise all politics and militarise Hindudom,” must be understood as a programmatic demand for total mobilisation. Most reactionary movements are animated by nostalgia for a lost golden age. Savarkar is unusual in being genuinely futurist. They are valued only insofar as they function as precursors of what is to come. This forward orientation is psychologically sophisticated. Savarkar sought to permanently embed what Theodore Roosevelt called the “*hard fighting virtues*” into the biological and cultural life of the Hindu population. Strength, endurance, and readiness for conflict were to be normalised as civilisational traits rather than exceptional responses to crisis. As Savarkar insisted in his later exhortation, “*You help no one else more than you help yourselves if you utilise these facilities and opportunities to militarise Hindudom. This done, everything else shall follow. If you miss this, nothing else shall avail.*”

The aim was to transform Hindudom irreversibly into a civilisation that could no longer retreat from history, delay struggle, or outsource survival to moral consolation.

### **IV. Why Travel To The Future**

Savarkar rejected the picturesque cult that dominated the Romantic imagination and elevated ruins into objects of reverent mourning. Within this tradition, ruins are rendered safe. They are framed as remnants of a once-complete world, softened into shadows of their former selves. The picturesque aesthetic converts loss into sentiment and decay into beauty. It allows one to admire collapse without being compelled to respond to it. In doing so, it domesticates the sublime by enclosing it within a pleasing visual and moral frame. Savarkar understands that the sublime cannot be contained this way. To remain within the picturesque is to linger in an aesthetic of decay rather than to confront the demands of history. Ruins that have been aestheticised through a glorious immobility and a moral upheaval for Hindus have become excuses for inertia. This permits spectatorship in place of responsibility. Futurism operates in the opposite direction. It does not derive its force from what has been lost, but from what has not yet come into being. Its power lies in projection rather than recollection. It mobilises by denying the comfort of nostalgia and by orienting desire toward an unrealised horizon. Futurism seduces through its absence in process, creating energy by insisting that the present is intolerable and unfinished.

This forward orientation stems from the articulation of Hindutva, which fundamentally involves a tension between a shattered present and an imagined future. The present is experienced as rupture, while the future appears as a task. Walter Benjamin’s angel of history looks backwards with sorrow, not only at the accumulation of past atrocities, but at the catastrophe of history itself, propelled forward by the violent storm named progress. Savarkar departs from this melancholic posture. He refuses to remain suspended between ruin and regret. The tension between present collapse and future possibility cannot be resolved through contemplation, mourning or aesthetic reflection. It demands resolution through struggle. Action, rather than interpretation, becomes the means by which history is answered. The ruin is no longer to be admired. It is to be overcome. History is something to be seized, endured to redirect toward a future that does not yet exist violently but still must be made to exist.

### **V. Citizenship to a Gravity Well**

*Once Hindutva closes the escape route to transcendence, the energy that leaks into the attainment of moksha is trapped within the world’s closed system. This creates an accumulation of force. This surplus energy had to be redirected and repurposed. It dissipates outward and compresses inward, transforming the nation from a diffuse civilisational expanse into an inert cultural substrate into a political gravity well. To achieve this density, Hindutva recognises a need to appropriate and reengineer the instruments of the British and Muslims who were being used to fragment Hindu society. This is the point at which his horizontal consolidation project takes shape. Savarkar was prepared to reform, discard, or invent traditions solely based on their effects on consolidating or fracturing the nation further. In this world, it was to be treated as a field of war and awareness of its landmines had to be taught, since such a worldview does not arise organically among Hindus and therefore requires deliberate political education. He began by recognising the slow processes of caste consolidation, which, as a principle of communal unity, was too slow and resting on fragile ground to sustain mobilisation in the freedom movement.*

#### **Shuddhi as a Technology of Citizenship**

For him, shuddhi was originally a ritual to regain lost caste status through pride; he transformed it into a technology of citizenship. The objective was to render an individual ritually pure enough to enter a temple through practices such as consuming tulsi or reciting the Gayatri mantra. But then the ritual was repurposed to confer the mental and political capacity to enter the identity of being a Hindu, thereby granting citizenship to the Hindu nation itself. It shifts from a rite of ritual cleansing to a protocol of political naturalisation, granting the subject entry into the nation’s sovereignty.

In this recalibration, Shuddhi is secularised. Shuddhi was reengineered into a technology of demographic recruitment, bypassing Shastric sanction entirely to grant entry into the sovereignty of the nation rather than the sanctity of a caste, thereby bypassing the requirement of Shastric sanction. It is within this rupture that Ambedkar mounted his interrogation of Hindu society by demanding the structural rationale for the depressed classes to remain within the fold. Savarkar’s Hindutva provides the internal counter-manoeuvre, which was structurally possible only because Savarkar’s atheism emancipated him from theological constraints. He stripped religious authority of its divine origin and weaponised it as a rational instrument for political consolidation, aiming at civic sovereignty rather than spiritual purity. Herein lies potential genius, which diverges in methodology. Ambedkar sought to dismantle the hierarchy through relentless textual combat, yet remained constrained within the hermeneutic architecture of Brahmanical interpretation. He fought a theological war to claim political sovereignty. Savarkar abandoned the text for the territory because he recognised that sovereignty is seized by forging a political majority. While Ambedkar’s project ultimately sought to establish a sovereign identity independent of the Hindu civilisational framework, Savarkar engineered a mechanism to absorb those fractures into a unified political subject. This formulation of a secularised and politicised Hindu identity inherently threatened the trajectory of those who rejected Hindu consolidation. Savarkar’s approach was fundamentally problematic for factions that sought leverage through separation rather than integration, that is to Ambedkarism and Islamists, to secure their position outside the Hindu core, these separatist forces bypassed civilisational unity and allied themselves directly with the colonial state’s apparatus, where they utilised the classificatory, disciplinary, and technological rationalities of colonial modernity to cement their distinct political identities.

#### **Condensation of Time**

In this sense, if the ancient Indian spirit was one of expansion, the Savarkarite spirit is one of condensation. He takes the expansive energy of the past and turns it inward to burn away regionalism, caste, and cowardice. The political *yajna* of Hindutva functions as a pressure chamber. It is designed to forge a unified political subject that is compressed, disciplined, and capable of enduring the brutal thermodynamics of modernity within the closed geometry of modern power. His rhetorical method produces axiomatic identity. Akhand Bharat and Hindu Rashtra function as axioms within Hindutva’s architecture. Marinetti’s futurism was purely destructive, a howl against the past with no civilisational substrate to protect, no demographic to consolidate, no wound to transmit, no enemy whose specific techniques required reverse-engineering. Acceleration was its own object. This is why Italian Futurism burned itself out as an aesthetic movement and collapsed into fascism by default, inheriting a state apparatus it had done nothing to construct. Savarkar’s futurism is oriented toward survival rather than speed. The machine is a means. The difference is architectural: where Marinetti produced an aesthetic, Savarkar produced a political technology that moved from diagnosis through consolidation to the institutional question, however incompletely he answered it. Kemal presents a sharper comparison. He built the machine directly, which is why he developed a constitutional theory immediately. Savarkar was working one stage earlier, producing subjects capable of building it. This is a structural difference in historical position, not a vision deficiency. Zionism operated within a largely secular European intellectual framework and could draw on existing nationalist templates forged by movements whose problems it closely resembled. The demographic was relatively bounded, the religious framework singular enough to either weaponise or dismantle, and the theoretical vocabulary already available.

Savarkar faced a problem: there was no real parallel across any of these comparisons. Every comparable nationalist or futurist movement of equivalent ambition either operated within a relatively homogeneous demographic, had access to state machinery, or worked within a single dominant religious framework. Savarkar faced a civilisation of radical internal plurality, under foreign occupation, fragmented by a colonial classification system specifically engineered to prevent consolidation, with a philosophical tradition whose deepest instincts actively resisted the political mobilisation he required. His solution—the move from religion to culture as the binding substrate—was a genuinely unprecedented political technology. No comparable movement had to construct the category of the nation’s people from scratch using anthropological rather than theological material while simultaneously fighting the colonial power that controlled the anthropological apparatus. That specific problem, and the specific solution he engineered for it, place him outside any clean comparative frame.

### **XIV. The Embryonic Hinduness**

What exists today under the name Hindutva is a crude biological precursor. An embryo of possibilities rather than a completed organism that exists now as the membrane that holds open the space within which higher functions may later develop. This crude instinct can generate energy, but it cannot yet direct it.

What we are witnessing is something more primitive and, for that reason, more consequential. It is the first instinct that precedes philosophy and systematisation. There is only refusal. The refusal to disappear. An organism that does not flinch from its own dissolution requires no ideology. An organism that recoils, struggles and gasps for breath without fully understanding why it must do so is life recognising itself as life. Hindutva, in its present form, occupies this register. This present form is the precondition for any worldview; the body remembers, before articulation, that it wishes to continue. Now, this Identity must be translated into administration, memory into law, mobilisation into logistics and sacrifice into institutional continuity. Without this translation, energy dissipates. With it, survival becomes governance.

When self-preservation is labelled ‘extremism’ and refusal is recorded as ‘fanaticism’, language itself collapses. Death is renamed life. Surrender is elevated to wisdom. The hospice is mistaken for home. Those who have confused exhaustion with enlightenment speak in this register. They attribute paralysis to peace and gradual erasure to tolerance. Yet the drowning man who thrashes toward the surface is not extreme. He is alive. The one who finds struggle distasteful is not serene. He is dying, having aestheticised his own extinction. Hindutva’s present formlessness is therefore a phase. Savarkar supplied the schema for maturation. What exists now is the membrane that holds open the space in which higher functions may later develop. It can secure electoral victories, but it cannot translate them into the structural transformations required to make such victories permanent. For that, instinct must be stabilised, routineised and embedded into institutions that outlast emotion.

Those who have lost this instinct, or been educated out of it, experience its presence in others as violence. They are not wrong to sense danger. The survival instinct is dangerous. It threatens those who had already counted on the other’s quiet disappearance, who had divided the inheritance in advance, written the obituary, and scheduled the funeral. To such observers, the corpse sitting up is an obscenity. The assigning agents are scandalised that it refused the role for which it was prepared. It was not dead. It was sleeping. Drugged by centuries of defeat. Narcotised by philosophies that transformed disappearance into virtue. Hypnotised by colonisers and collaborators into mistaking self-erasure for enlightenment. This moment, in its most primitive form, is simply the moment of waking. The crude formlessness of contemporary Hindutva is misread as pathology. It is instead the necessary dissolution that precedes transformation.

Transformation always needs the destruction of previous forms. The caterpillar must completely dissolve before the butterfly can appear. Those who expect the chrysalis to prove its worth through beauty, who want clear ideology during this breaking-down phase, do not grasp metamorphosis. Formlessness is not failure; it’s necessary destruction. Freedom is not granted. It must be willed. The apparent chaos of contemporary Hindutva is the sound of vessels shattering so that their contents can assume a new configuration. Savarkar’s vision orients the movement across an abyss whose meaning lies in crossing it and, in the act, generating force rather than the promise of what lies beyond. This orientation transforms time itself. Transcendence as cyclical escape is replaced by irreversible progression. Defeats acquire permanence, but so do acts of courage. The abyss becomes a proving ground where Transcendence is possible precisely because the demand is total.

For me, futurism is a method grounded in the recognition that civilisational survival requires the willingness to risk annihilation. The nation must move toward horizons that may not yet exist, building toward forms that have never been realised. Renewal is the disciplined acceptance of uncertainty as the price of continuity. Hindutva relocates transcendence from the metaphysical realm into the historical process itself. Individual death is transformed into civilisational permanence. Sacrifice secures meaning rather than negating it. Those who act in the name of the nation attain transcendence through the only path modernity has left open. This path promises survival in a future where consolation has no place and only continuation remains. The question for us is how long Hindus wish to continue, and how they will organise and constrain themselves so that it does not exhaust itself. The machine Savarkar gestured toward begins with concrete arrangements that make survival independent of mood and belief, institutions designed to function when conviction fades, when leaders fail and when moral certainty fractures. This transition demands a state architecture capable of absorbing intensity without being deformed by it. At this point, Hindutva needs the capacity to endure, the cold intelligence that outlasts the founding heat. The future of this project depends on whether the energy generated over the past decade can be crystallised into institutions that govern impersonally, endure across generations, and protect civilisation even when no one is watching. The question is not whether Hindus will survive; we must ask ourselves: can they learn to build what will survive them?
