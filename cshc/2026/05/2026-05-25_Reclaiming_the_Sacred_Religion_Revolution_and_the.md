In the decades following Independence, much of the Indian intelligentsia strove to craft a secular national narrative. This project, born out of a noble desire for unity and communal harmony, often downplayed or even dismissed the powerful role that religion, particularly Hindu revivalism played in shaping India’s early revolutionary movement.

While academic honesty occasionally forced acknowledgment of the enormous religious and cultural energy driving the movement, the broader public imagination was gradually stripped of this dimension. With the politicisation of history, figures such as Veer Savarkar and Shaheed Bhagat Singh have been pulled between extreme ideological interpretations.

Nowhere, however, is the intertwining of politics and faith more evident than in Bengal, the epicentre of the revolutionary movement, where the fight for freedom was not merely an act of rebellion, but a sacred duty.

### **The Sacred Oath: Where Religion Met Revolution**

The religious tone of Bengal’s revolutionary organisations revealed itself at the very moment of initiation. The Calcutta Anushilan Samiti, one of the earliest and most influential secret societies, required initiates to take their vows with a revolver in one hand and the Gita in the other. Some of these oaths invoked the ideal of establishing a *Dharma-rajya* and were conducted before a fierce image of Goddess Kali, with sword and Holy Gita as witnesses. The Atmonnati Samiti began its vows after ritual *puja* ceremonies involving sacred elements like copper, Tulsi, Ganga water, fire (*Agni*), and the *Gita,* with the ceremony unfolding before a stone image of Shivaji.

A sessions judge’s description of the recruitment of approver Rajani Kanta Das in the Barisal Conspiracy case makes this clear:

> “Fega asked Rajani what was his object in life. Rajani said he wished
> to get on in life. Fega asked him if he did not wish to uphold the
> Hindu religion, and Rajani said he did. Fega then said that for the
> purpose of upholding the Hindu religion it was necessary to establish
> a Hindu kingdom. This sort of conversation passed between Fega and
> Rajani on two or three occasions, and one day Fega asked Rajani if he
> would join a Samiti that had for its purpose the establishment of
> Hindu rule and the expulsion of the British from India. Rajani agreed
> to join the society. Shortly after the Durga Puja, Upendra one day
> took Rajani to the Manasha Mandir”

Such ceremonies were sacred pledges, binding young men not only to a political ideal but to a religious mission. Another revolutionary, Priya Nath Acharji, recalled his initiation before the goddess Kali at Ramna Siddheswati Kalibari:

> “We took Adya, Antya and the Special Vows… before the goddess Kali.
> Pulin Das performed ‘Jajna’ before the goddess… The special vow was
> taken… with a sword and Gita on the head and kneeling on the left
> knee. This is called the ‘Prattalirha’ position and is supposed to
> represent a lion about to spring on his prey.”

A parallel can be found in Abhinav Bharat’s initiation ceremony, whose founder VD Savarkar himself began his revolutionary journey after taking a vow to free India in front of his family deity, the Ashtabhuja Bhavani.

These acts of worship, however, were not mere theatrics. Before going on raids, the Comilla Anushilan Samiti worshipped Goddess Kali while members of the Dacca Anushilan Samiti identified themselves as Vajris or “the Thunderers,” drawing inspiration from the Rigveda and the sacrifice of Sage Dadhichi. Their headquarters was named Bajripuri or “The Abode of the Thunderers” while their crest bore the image of Shri Krishna.

The Samitis also used war cries of “Bande Mataram” and “Har Har Mahadev”during their mock fights and even when they went into action. “Bande Mataram” itself was considered a sacred invocation which had on occasion provoked riots, with Maulana Abu Baqr even issuing a *fatwa* against it.

While describing the daily routine of the Samiti, a revolutionary says:

“After performing the ablutions the members stand in a line. One of them reads the hymns named Bhagabati Stotra and other hymns, and all the members then repeat the hymns. Then they bow down. Then they chant aloud the following portion of the Bande Mataram. Then they shout aloud Hara, Hara; Bom, Bom”

Any doubts left to the nature of the Samiti are clarified by Jogesh Chandra Chatterji in his work *In Search of Freedom*:

> “As the basis of the Samiti had a religious orientation, every member
> looked upon himself as a servant of God. It may be mentioned here that
> Pulin Behari Das, the leader of the Dacca Anushilan Samiti, was
> intensely religious; and so was Barrister P. Mitra, the Founder of the
> Anushilan Samiti.”

### **The Justification of Violence**

To these young men and women, revolution was not rebellion for its own sake—it was *Dharma Yuddha*, a righteous war. The Jugantar, in its articles, frequently quoted the Gita, Chandi Purana as well as other religious texts while continually using terms like Mlecchas and Yavanas to refer to the English. Revolutionary organisations recommended and taught various patriotic literature to their cadre including religious texts like the Gita, Krishna Charitra, Jiban Pariksha, Kathamrita, Lila Prashanga among many others.

The timetable of one of the Samitis advocated the singing of hymns and remembrance of deities not only at the time of waking up and going to sleep, but also while exercising and in a special Sangkirtan session of one and a half hour, where devotional songs were to be sung and Hindu scriptures discussed.

The 1908 tract *Bartaman Rana Niti (Modern Warfare)* laid out the philosophical foundation for their convictions:

> “War is inevitable when oppression cannot be stopped by any means
> whatever, when the leprosy of slavery corrupts the blood of the body
> of the nation…..It is for this reason that Sri Krishna, God himself,
> acted as charioteer, holding the reins in his hand at the battle of
> Kurukshetra. It is for this that Chandi, inspired with the powers of
> 33 crores of gods, appeared to kill the Daityas, and for this reason
> the incarnation of Kali in this Kali-age Kali holding two sword to
> destroy the Mlechhas has passed into a saying of the Shastras.”

The role of Hindu revivalism is further elucidated in the literature composed by Aswini Kumar Rai of Surhid Samiti

> “How the Hindus will join the worship of that Governor to seek his
> favour by whose devilish administration the Hindu religion was
> insulted, the Hindu images were broken to pieces and the wealth of the
> Hindu women’s chastity was taken away. Shame! Shame! Are the Aryans,
> who are the descendants of Arya Rishis (Saints ), Arya warriors and
> the sons of Sita, Sabitri, and Padmini, so mean? Look upward , there
> is fire of anger of the insulted gods, and on all sides there is fire
> of course of the stained chaste women. Be careful if you want
> blessing.”
>
> “As you are born to establish liberty, to improve religion, to kill
> the Mlechhas and to improve the happiness of the pious, so you need
> not sleep. Your mother is standing by you and is calling you . Don’t
> fear, work and work . Liberty can bring real religion and religion is
> the source of personal and national happiness .”

A pamphlet found at the residence of Amulya Sarkar, a member of the Pabna Jugantar party, declared the foremost objective of “the Indian Liberating League” was the “revival of ancient Hindu spiritual culture,” or Sanatan Dharma, with political independence deemed essential, achievable only through the expulsion of “greedy and selfish foreigners.”

The sword was exalted as a sacred instrument of liberation. Revolutionary manuals and *lathi* training songs invoked Krishna himself as a liberator through arms:

> “Leaving aside the flute, Krishna freed his mother Debaki in chains in
> prison with your help, O Sword, the destroyer of sin.”

For these revolutionaries, religion was not a retreat into mysticism but a call to arms. As Jogesh Chandra Chatterji put it, “To serve the country was the religious duty of the members of the Samiti.” The Samiti’s task was “the formation of character of high order,” which required faith as its foundation. Thus politics infused with religion was natural and “independence was translated as *Mukti*—a term signifying blessedness.”

The Gita occupied a central place in the life of a revolutionary from the moment of his initiation to the justification of violence. It is with right reason that Ramsay Macdonald, in his book “The Awakening of India” states

> “The Indian assassin quotes his Bhagavad Gita just as the Scottish
> Covenanter quoted his Old Testament, but the Bhagavad-Gita is more
> cruel in the devotion and self - sacrifice it requires than the most
> awful of the Old Testament passages which have been brooded over by
> the austere fanatics of our own history……It is this inspiration ,
> dazzling human reason into blindness, that leads astray the youths who
> have cast constitutionalism to the four winds, and who have entered
> upon the dark ways of assassination, hoping thereby to reach
> emancipation.”

As revolutionary Jibantara Haldar stated in an oral interview, “We imbibed the ideal of selfless devotion and service to humanity from the Gita.

According to H.L. Salkeld’s Report on the Dacca Anushilan Samiti, seventeen copies of the *Bhagavad Gita* were recovered from the Samiti premises, ten of which were listed in its library catalogue. Records showed that within just three months, the *Gita* had been issued eight times—chiefly to senior members such as Ashutosh Dasgupta, Akshay Kumar Datta, and Pramod Behari Das (brother of Pulin Behari Das). The verandah on the eastern end of the first floor was even known as the “Gita Class” accessible only through Pulin Behari Das’s adjoining private chamber, signifying its exclusivity.

A similar emphasis on spiritual discipline appeared in the Alipur Bomb Case, where Mr. Norton testified that the secret society at the Manicktola Garden House in Calcutta maintained three departments—one of them explicitly spiritual. Under Upendranath Banerjee, this department instructed members in the *Gita*, for their revolutionary mission demanded “immense strength of spirit,” drawn largely from the practice of Vedanta philosophy. Three copies of the *Gita* were also discovered in the garden house, confirming its central role in the revolutionaries’ moral and ideological training.

Books of a historical nature were also popular and were even awarded as prizes in mock fights. A few prominent works included Jaliat Clive, Pratab Singh (on Maharana Pratap), “Maharaj Nand Kumar,” “Sikher Balidan” (Martyrdom of the Sikhs) and “Chhatrapati Sivaji”. Imagery of Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj played an important role in Samiti life whether it be in paintings, events organised to commemorate him, in songs or in literature like “Dharmmarajya and Maharaja Sivaji”.

Texts and revolutionary propaganda seeking to legitimize killing the colonisers frequently resorted to the use of religious iconography as seen in excerpts of Jugantar pamphlets

> “You insignificant English! You are but the eternally prostrate Shiva
> lying at the feet of the santans who are aspiring towards
> independence.”
>
> “The creator, preserver and destroyer, the wielder of great strength,
> Mahakali knows better than you do – Your role is to help in her
> work–the results are up to her – rest easy in sacrificing yourself at
> the altar of your mother’s sacrificial ceremony” (16)
>
> “King Surath secured the freedom of this holy land by offering a lakh
> of victims. We, too, have made a solemn vow touching copper, fire and
> the sacred thread to offer to the goddess skulls full of the warm
> blood of lakhs of white beasts for her propitiation, to establish our
> own kingdom and our own religion.”
>
> The article “One’s own Country and one’s own Religion” states “If thou
> dost desire to be in this world , prepare thyself for the holy war - a
> war that will establish thy religion and thy nation. “ followed by the
> command from the Gita “Fight” enforced by the doctrine “ If thou dost
> meet with death in carrying out the virtue I have taught thee, thou
> wilt enter heaven ; and if thou art victorious, thou shalt enjoy the
> universe.”

Revolutionary songs like “Kabya Bisharad” contain the following lyrics:

> “Come, О mother Bhairabi; соme, О mother, adorned with your skulls to
> this earth, making the earth tremble with a dreadful sound to kill the
> hosts of wicked demons.”

Another directly coaxes Hindus into action through the following lyrics:

> “O you Hindu Santans be of one mind, draw out the sword from the
> scabbard and advance forward riotous in the sports of war to extirpate
> the enemies root and branch. With the blood of the enemy fertilise the
> earth. Tinge your flag of victory with the crimson blood of the
> formidable foe.”

### **The Battlefield as a Sacred Space**

This frequent use of Hindu scriptures by the cadres and training within the revolutionary circles brought within them a sense of both national and religious duty to the land of their forefathers.

Madan Lal Dhingra proudly declared after assassinating the head of the secret police Curzon Wyllie on British soil:

> “As a Hindu, I feel that a wrong done to my country is an insult to
> God. Poor in health and intellect, a son like myself has nothing to
> offer to the Mother but his own blood, and so I have sacrificed the
> same on her altar. Her cause is the cause of Shri Rama. Her services
> are the services of Shri Krishna. This War of Independence will
> continue between India and England so long as the Hindu and the
> English races last”.

He was not the only one to be inspired by religion. Suniti Chowdhury declared the assassination of the District Magistrate of Cornilla by herself and Shanti Ghosh as “their Dharmayuddha”. She further wrote in her memoir,

> “Freedom is the birth right of everyone. Fight(ing) for the sake of
> that right should be the religion of every one….No other better
> alternative is there than religious fight”

Pritilata Waddedar’s body after her martyrdom in the raid on the European Officer’s club at Chittagong was found with a photograph of Shri Krishna and a statement “I sacrifice myself in the name of the God whom I have adored for many years”. An ardent devotee, she prayed for Krishna’s return to restore Dharma and put an end to the land of adharma that British India had become.

Bina Das gave the following statement after her attempt on the life of Governor Sir Stanley Jackson

> “Now I stand alone before the judgment seat of God and open myself
> before Him and pray for His all-forgiving love to wash me clean so
> that I may be a worthy offering to Him. May I see the benignant
> countenance of the Mother Divine and feel Her loving embrace for me -
> even for - at this most solemn moment of my life. If it be Her will
> that I should die, then let it be so. If She wills that I live, let me
> consecrate my life to the service of suffering humanity, which is the
> fondest longing of my heart, if She out of Her infinite mercy spares
> it to be used by Her as Her instrument. May God fulfil Himself through
> my death or my life, if it so pleases Him. Thy will be done. Oh Lord.”

The revolutionary press echoed the same justification. The *Sandhya* wrote:

> “The teachings of the Gita should not be lost sight of. As humble
> agents of God, we should kill those whom he had already destroyed.”

The *Jugantar* exhorted:

> “Kill the destroyer of your religion, kill him who throws obstacles in
> the path of your duty or religion, no matter whether he is rich or
> poor, a zamindar or raja, officer of government or an emperor.”

The diary of Sushil Kumar Sen echoed this sacred resolve:

> “Taking up sword -I bow down to Thee, sword ; Thou crown of all
> weapons, the symbol of death. With much respect I take Thee from the
> hands of Adya Sakti.
>
> In this vicious Kali Yug when Vistu ( religion Dharma) has been so
> much attenuated , when weapons have been the order of the day, weapon
> incarnation, Thou an (the only) arbiter (or upholder) of truth.”

Dinesh Chandra Sen Gupta, another revolutionary writer frequently filled his poetry with calls to the Goddess:

> “Where are you, O mother Chamunda, wearing a garland of human heads?
> Come. O mother Sarada; come, O mother, of a terrible form , the
> destroyer of fear in this dire calamity. Your afflicted children are
> calling you... Devils are committing oppression on us.”

### **Communal Boundaries and the Decline of Inclusivity**

While the strong Hindu revivalist streak hardly implied hostility towards Non Hindus/Sikhs at the movement’s onset, the partition of Bengal, the Lal Ishtihar pamphlet, and riots sponsored by the Nawab of Dhaka (one of the founders of the Muslim League) gave a “communal” colour to the movement.

Revolutionaries like Pulin Behari Das and his followers found themselves increasingly at odds with local Muslims. When Muslim mobs attacked their training houses, they retaliated fiercely. In one instance, six to seven revolutionaries fought off a mob numbering 700, wounding dozens. In another instance, a riot subsided after revolutionaries opened fire and killed a Muslim in Comilla. A violent clash erupted in Dacca when Nawab Salimullah’s followers, opposing anti-partition festoons put up by Hindu volunteers of the Mukti Sangha, tore them down on the eve of Lord Curzon’s visit. The Atmonnati Samiti in 1907 dispatched armed men “for the protection of the hearth and home of the Hindus” during communal disturbances in Jamalpore (Mymensingh district).

When the Inspector of Police Ahsanullah was assassinated in August 1931 by revolutionaries for the terror he brought on Chittagong in the aftermath of the Armoury raid, serious rioting took place. Preetilata had a firsthand experience of communal riots in Dacca while she was a student at Dacca Eden College. When Muslim rioters threatened the Hindu girls, the District Magistrate refused police protection, citing the Indians’ demand for independence.

Witnessing the violence against Hindus and British police indifference, Preetilata became convinced of the necessity to expel them. A similar trend was observed in the revolutionary psyche as highlighted by **Jogesh Chandra Chatterji:**

“As the result of these communal riots and Government repressions the Hindu youths became very much excited and the more zealous among them added to the number and strength of the secret activities of the revolutionaries. These riots like the anti-Partition movement indirectly helped to accentuate the underground anti-British, activities. Thus the motive behind the Partition as well as the riots miserably failed”

Many of the Samitis, therefore, refused to admit Muslims, with the Dacca Anushilan Samitti (one of the most successful organisations with over 500 branches) clearly declaring in its programme “None who is not a Hindu or a hater of Hindus will be admitted”. Its leader Pulin Behari Das declared:

> “It will not be particularly profitable to us if one or two Musalmans
> join our hands: on the other hand. those Musalmans who are
> antagonistically disposed towards us will be all the more envious
> because some of their own men have joined our hand. Besides, it has to
> be seen that if two or four Musalmans are admitted because they are
> found trustworthy after selection. then other crafty Musalmans will
> want to be admitted. There will then be no means of excluding them.”

The distinctly religious orientation of Samiti life precluded most Muslims of Bengal from participating in their activities though instances of cooperation do emerge as evidenced in the European Club attack. Nevertheless, the result was stark. Daly’s Note containing all prominent revolutionaries of the period doesn’t contain a single Muslim name. A similar pattern is seen in Kerr’s “Political Trouble in India”, where out of the 500 or so names of revolutionaries, only a dozen are Muslim. The Sedition Committee Report which gives the caste composition of 186 persons convicted or killed in Bengal while indulging in revolutionary crimes during 1907-17, again shows no Muslims among them.

Even figures collected from Punjab in 1931 reveal that out of 163 individuals charged with revolutionary crimes, “with the exception of forty-five Sikhs and five Mohammedans, all were Hindus.”

### **Conclusion**

The story of Bengal’s revolutionaries reminds us that Hindu revivalism was not merely a backdrop but a vital moral force in India’s struggle for freedom particularly before the advent of Marxist/Socialist streak in later decades. It gave the movement its discipline, symbols, and sense of sacred duty. To dismiss this spiritual energy is to miss the emotional core of Bengal’s revolutionary fervour.
