Unsparing and unrelenting in detailing her love affairs,the books of Annie Ernaux, nobel laureate in literature (2022) is the continuation of the great French tradition where truth of one's life is presented in its raw version. For Ernaux, personal life is the centre stage of writing. Her art is created through multiple experiences of living. It is from the element of self in its most uninhibited, some will say intimate form, where her books emerge. Her stories exhibit an inner world, a domain that is most sheltered from the intruding eyes of the public. This inner world as one would imagine is full of decisions and choices that are flawed, questionable and yet remain human. The inner world is situated underneath the surface of the body in Ernaux's universe. It is most often confined to the bedroom.

The bedroom subtly imposes itself over the public space when the unusual couple appear together outside of it as in her book ‘The Young Man.’ Here a woman in her 50s is having an affair with an young man of early 20s who's attending university. The theme of difference of age between the couple seems to dominate this slim memoir. It is no wonder as society sees relationship of an old woman with a young man quite differently from the case where the roles are reversed as our author reminds us boldly. However the intimidating question of how a menopausal woman being in a relationship with this young man will be received among his friends doesn't escape her mind. Its outlying nature needs to be explained, she unhesitantly admits. It is an anomaly, an accident in the order of the universe out of one's own. It arouses suspicions of incest for the dynamics of age it inhabits. The image resemblance to mother-son duo that is invoked by their presence in the public needs to be explained away. The public considers it outside the purview of normal.

The author knows that the act of lovemaking, the physical intimacy between them within the boundary of a room has become a ritual for her, in her words; “the present was only a duplicate of the past.” She has known it, lived it her whole life and still feels closely the need for the reenactment of the ritual. This experience of intimacy for the young man is quite new. It is for him just a beginning while for her it has become an elaborate web of deception, solemnised as a ritual that invokes love for the other. Love making for her has no concrete meaning anymore. She knows that it is an illusion, yet she is willing and an enthusiastic participant of this pleasant illusion. The need to justify the act, to ascribe this act to something that transcends the flesh of the bodies is constantly felt. The bodies can be enjoyed mutually but it also needs to furnish itself an ideology for its justification.The phrase ‘lovemaking’(tirelessly repeated in her books) has become vaguely contrived. Love itself is only an instrument to justify the desire that is either forbidden or frowned upon by the society. Her books are an object to refrain society from pronouncing harsh judgements over unconventional couples and to record the memory of her affair with some man. The man has not disappeared from her memory. Every imprint of the space they inhabited once, stood to remind her of this desolate present. The shared past is not a distant memory but something that still felt surreal. The surrender to it would destroy her senses. She wants to get away from it. She has maintained a diary to record this affair. She could never contemplate tearing those pages of the diary as she knows that the destruction of records doesn't make memories disappear. This is manifested with singular vehemence in her book ‘Simple Passion.' We are observing here an affair between a divorced woman (author) and a married man from Russia. In the aftermath of another doomed affair, she recounts her story. As the man is married, the affair is illicit. The forbidden aspect of this desire is not stultifying to her but increases its appeal to the himalayan heights. The desire is ignited with vigor because of the impossibility of possessing the fullest part of man. It is the presence of the Other; invisible and yet lurking around that fuels her passion. She is obsessed with him. Indeed, we hear from her the outbursts of passion reminding us of Goethe's Werther. Like Werther sees the things of the world “only in relation to her", Annie is indifferent to everything except “those related to this man.” If Goethe's happiness ceases when he has to tear himself from her, Annie's sole happiness consists in the time spent with the lover.

However, there's a significant difference. For Werther, the beloved is an unattainable object since she's engaged to someone else while for Annie, the beloved is attainable, or atleast a part of him is. But a lack there is. As Socrates says Eros can exist only in lackness. It is the aim for wholeness that love desires. Even though here physical desire has been consummated, its scorching heat has not been quenched. The passion is still breathing. There is a faint recognition that intimacy of physical kind alone is unfulfilling. She knows that her fate is tied to physical intimacy alone. She harbors no illusion regarding her position of mistress or at worst, a ‘whore.’ She recounts that she is the mother of two kids. The kids are an obstacle to her sexual union with her lover. She feels loathing towards them, as she's interrupted by their arrivals. Her world seems to be threatened by such rapture, she searches ways to remove this obstacle. If the lover is going to come, they're sent away. The kids have to take permission to come to their mother's home. Except “the barest practicalities that enabled” her to conduct this affair, nothing is revealed to them. There's no sense of obligation to kids. It proceeds from the breakdown of marriage,which provides a space where such relations can flourish without any need of concealment.

The haunting spectre of love making being scheduled, neatly arranged in accord with the constraints of clock wanders in Ernaux's universe. The appointments and schedules which resurrect erotic desire to be performed is a chilling portrait of modernity. The woman has only one recourse available to ascertain the passion of the other. As she writes: “jealousy... the only proof of his love in my eyes.” If passion is judged by jealousy, the depth of desire could only be “glimpsed by looking at his penis.” Love is measured by the stiffness of the erection, she's unsure of anything else. The inner logic of love has long been destroyed. Pure bodily passion spurting out in the bedroom which helps her to achieve orgasm in the bedroom is the only way desire can be apprehended.

No one wants to be bored by all those lofty, elevating claims of two souls being merged. There is not even a squeak of it. Such antiquated notions have no place in Ernaux's universe, not even in denunciations. Relationships are viewed here only through “the perspective of mutual gain.” For Annie, even if relationships are purely of sexual nature, she nevertheless wants to be reassured of other's affirmation of love. The need for such affirmation of love has not yet vanished, even if it has been mutilated beyond recognition. In the poem ‘The twelve properties or conditions of a lover’ Thomas More says “The first point is to love but one alone/And for that one all other to forsake:/For whoso loveth many loveth none.” More's property is utterly absent in Ernaux's life as her books show a glimpse of the series of love affairs consisting of a marriage, divorce and renewal of the act of “lovemaking” with different persons, where rituals of love are invoked with no definite meaning!

The physical ecstasies of desire and the achievement of orgasms are the symbols of this modern love. Indeed, the repetitive nature of ‘lovemaking’ with different partners at different phases( what Scruton wittily dubbed as seriel polygamy) is an attempt to rescue oneself from emptiness which nevertheless never disappears. The murmuring of the phrase ‘I love you’ from author, itself meaningless, is given a cold expression of gratitude( Thank you) by her Russian lover. The pronouncement of the phrase itself is not genuine and serves rather as a shallow justification of sex. In a moment of candid honesty, she admits the word ‘F\*ck’ would be more appropriate than that camouflaging phrase of ‘lovemaking.'

Her diaries reveal that the absence of real, noble sentiments constitute the core of their affair. These sentiments are understood as constraints, which bind and are understood as hindrance from the realization of freedom. The belief that ‘love will bind you and sex will free you’ has become sacrosanct. It is only the “perfection of pleasure” that is sought in love, not the higher values of truth or fidelity. Love and pleasure have become synonymous. The subterfuge of sentimental aspects in the love affair is not even maintained for decorative purposes but discarded with a disdainful disregard. The relationships in this universe start from indifference and end at indifference. In the middle it's filled with sex.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

In Annie's universe, we find the grotesque portrait of the pursuit of individualism dressed in fancy liberalism. Liberal individualism offers us such sexual freedom which disguises itself as a form of alienation, uprooting humans from their true emotions and desires. What is gradually concealed from humans is that they make a sense of themselves only through association with others. The Other is not an abstract, merely a faint shade but equipped with emotions that need to be understood. The nihilistic portrait of lovemaking where it is barely distinguishable from animals is present throughout her works. So is the attempt to conquer that nihilistic impulse. The defeat is, however, certain. This certain defeat stares not only at her, but at everyone who is plagued by the modern mores where widespread meaninglessness of intimacy threatens to sever us from our nature and conditions of selfhood itself.
