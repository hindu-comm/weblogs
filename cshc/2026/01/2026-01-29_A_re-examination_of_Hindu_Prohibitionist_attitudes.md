#### This article is merely an exploration of an alternative way to approach alcoholic beverages in a Hindu worldview. Readers are expected to exercise discretion in the matters of alcohol consumption.

Hindu conservative thought holds, and has continued to hold, the view that alcohol is to be eschewed by the noble, and the aspirational. Despite this, it is not the pernicious substance that it is made out to be, but as part of the human society since man learned to cultivate, an enjoyable beverage, provided it is utilized in a guided manner.

For such a guidance, many have spoken from antiquity until the present, like the Hindu ancients, who have written two very wonderful texts purely on the drinking of wine, along with other texts which touch on social, culinary and medical aspects of alcohol, and so has the modern conservative thinker Roger Scruton, in his book I drink, therefore I am.

But despite such guidance being available, Hindu conservative thought so far has shied away from approaching the question of alcohol consumption in a measured manner, and instead relied on prohibitionist attitudes, whether direct, like the perpetual post-Independence ban on liquor in Gujarat, or by proxy, like the treatment of alcohol as the greatest of sins, even for those who are not obligated to abstain. Although there are precedents for this in the Dharmaśāstra literature, where consumption of alcohol is discouraged despite being considered natural to man, and there being no safe alternative that is encouraged at all social levels, it is pertinent to take recourse to outside assistance, and the ancients, to have a system where consumption of alcohol is done safely, for not all can abstain in full. When a disease arrives, so does the cure. It is impossible to prevent a disease fully, but to cure it is something one must move on to. To allow a degree of freedom is not contradictory to order, since freedom itself is a form of order, and to even enjoy a freedom, one has rules. The alternative, that happens when repression is removed, is disorder, which often disguises itself as freedom but is always a prelude to a greater slavery.

For this purpose, I shall speak primarily of wine, since it is the drink allowed to a majority of the Hindu populace, with no restriction on its consumption, and because Roger Scruton treats only of this drink in his work.

# A Philosophy Of Wine

The ancients who treated of wine, spoke of it in terms of its social, sexual and religious aspects. While it is treated with great praise, in poetry, rites, and treatises, one is also warned of overindulgence, and in many cases, encouraged to abstain fully. Yet such abstinence is not absolute, since even the most cultured of śiṣṭa-s drank wine in their feasts, like Arjuna and Śrīkṛṣṇa, despite the exhortation to abstinence. Such may thus be explained as being due to wine being allowed to them, as is the opinion of scholars like Medhātithi.

But to return to our subject, wine is unlike other alcoholic drinks, for it is not merely an object of pleasure, but also an object of knowledge. Like the Śikhara of a temple that symbolizes the journey from Earth to Heaven, it leads to a meaning if we have the culture and faith to provide it. When wine falls on a full stomach, it rises back as discourse. It is cultural achievement, which is not available to atheists, believers in progress, and Protestants, for it depends on the local Gods.

Wine is civilization, and the distinction of places where wine is drunk and places where it is not drunk, is the distinction between the civilized and the uncivilized.

It is a channel of communication between the Gods and men. Through it, the distilled essence of the soil awakens the body, and having swamped it, wine invades the soul. It sets your thoughts and feelings free of bounds. Wine helps to meditate on the difficult things.

> “as the cups go swiftly round, a sweet subduing power warms the heart,
> and, blending with the gifts of Dionysus, a presage of the Cyprian
> goddess flutters the mind. That power sends a man’s thoughts soaring
> ;—straightway he is stripping cities of their diadem of towers,—he
> dreams that he shall be monarch of the world ;— his halls gleam with
> gold and ivory ;—over the sunlit sea his wheat-ships bring wealth
> untold from Egypt :—such are the raptures of the reveller’s soul.”
>
> -Bacchylides, Fragment 16

It is the only thing which lays bare the paradox of being both bound and free, for one can not choose to enter the heightened state that wine induces, but in the induced state, one is free to pursue upāsanam, or direct himself to creation of a work, or plan something. It brings forth choices which we are unwilling to confront, for our conscious life is always subject to forces beyond us.

Wine is the distillation of Aidos (Αἰδώς), the virtue which is a recognition of the other as more important than yourself, of reverence and humility. It is associated with practice of a religion, not mere belief. In drinking wine, we also taste the landscape where the Gods were invited and found a home. By refusing to drink this, the message people send is that they do not belong to the earth.

## In Vino Veritas

Other drugs and intoxicants are epistemologically culpable, in that they only tell lies about a transcendental reality beyond ours where ours pales into significance or is considered inferior. Wine reveals the world as a truth which we have failed to know, for we failed to belong to it, and it is the only thing which will overcome it. Under the effects of wine, one can even rejoice in success of one’s rivals, which is impossible without it. It allows us to see good in people, no matter who has it.

Truth in wine is not what the drinker perceives, but rather, what he reveals. It is truth for others, not for self.

Wine helps us to look inward. Neither does it mask reality, nor tells lies, nor makes us escape from reality. Wine’s effect is not merely of intoxication, but of opening out our self to another. In tasting wine, you pour the self into it, and by this, the wine also recalls the self into it. This is just like how ancient Greeks understood Dionysus as being in the wine, and not merely the cause of it.

Wine is not just any alcohol, but a transformation of the grape. This is again stimulated in the soul when we drink it. It is the transformation of grape to wine by Dionysus that enters us. Not merely an object, but almost a living being, which gives life to us. The whole being rushes to meet the sip of wine, just like the whole being of a lover rising to the lips in a kiss.

That which can not be grasped intellectually, may be grasped sensorily, and wine is a great means to such an end. Wine is what heightens the doubled up nature of man, for it enhances his bodily experience, and also radiates the sense of self.

Wine, properly drunk, transfigures the world, illuminating the mysterious in things that surround you, of what they are and what they might not have been. Contingency of each thing glows, and one realizes that identity and individuality are outward forms taken by the same fire, and that fire is also the one observing it.

We shall see further, how this aspect plays out in the Kaula rites of Tantra practitioners.

## The Taste and Epistemology of Wine

To truly participate in wine drinking, one must not merely look at labels and reputation. Every wine is a result of its grapes, the maturation process, the region where it is grown, the soil in which it is grown, and the customs of the land where it is grown.

When one drinks wine, it is not merely tasting the liquid, but also taking part in the culture of the land to which it belongs.

Hence, wine is not merely to be judged by taste and aroma alone, but has to be combined with knowledge. To do otherwise is like appreciating a poem on the basis of sound alone without knowing the meaning of the words. It tastes differently to someone who can locate it in a place and time.

Tastes can linger long after the cause is gone, and have a life of their own. Tastes are not inherent, but only associative, hence they can launch trains of association. Tastes have no spectrum, but are in orders of continua. Hence they must be only described via association.

Because wine’s epistemology is related to the immediate reality, in tasting a wine, one also tastes the background of the wine’s creation: The soil, grape, customs of the region and so on.

Planting vines is not merely about commerce, but also an expression of belonging to a place and identity shaped by it. Doing this is to express your divine right to stay on the land and to invoke the protection of the Gods.

Wine is thus associated with practice of a religion, not mere belief. In drinking wine, we also taste the landscape where the Gods were invited and found a home. By refusing to drink this, the message people send is that they do not belong to the earth. We shall see further, how wine has a similar place in the Hindu traditions, and how deluded the prohibition advocates are, when they rally against consumption of such a sanctified beverage.

## Intoxication in Wine

Intoxication in wine is sensory, and not aesthetic. Yet it is a vehicle of reflection and object of thought. Intoxication is either purely a result of its cause, or the experience of the quality of the cause. So one may be intoxicated by the wine, and also intoxicated towards the wine.

Only rational beings can appreciate wine, since they can direct the intoxication. If you make a non rational being like a horse drink wine, then wine is only a source of pleasure, not an object. It is pleasure which becomes dominant, not any faculty which may say “I am tasting this”. There is no focus on the taste of the wine in such beings. Such a way then leads not to *appreciation*, but to *addiction*, which negates all the good that wine may bring.

## Alteration of the Wind

Wine is a mind and even identity changing beverage. Wine is good and bad, and the self welcomes one and fights the other. This battle must be won by the good. Thus, there is a lot more at stake in wine than taste.

There are things which please but do not alter the mind, things which alter the mind but are not pleasing, and things which are pleasing and alter the mind. The last is one whose mind altering is related to the act of consumption.

Addiction to cannabis leads to a loss in mental function, but in liquor, addiction is to the drink, and not to the taste. Alcohol content of liquor escapes from the flavor, but that of wine does not. Hence liquor is like a medicinal drink, where flavor and effect detach from each other.

But in wine, the taste of wine is mental transformation, and it outlasts the pleasure. In drinking it, the mind is altered, and the taste is imbued with an altered consciousness, while the consciousness is in return directed at the taste.

Wine alters the mind but not permanently; The effect is ephemeral. It increases sociability like liquor, provided it is drunk moderately. Hence wine, is a substance that can be enjoyed very differently compared to other intoxicating substances, which are in vogue and not attacked as vigorously, while wine is attacked by health fanatics, religious and secular prohibitionists, despite being the most benign of intoxicants, as has been shown.

# Wine and Intercourse

Although such an elaborate philosophy on wine, as with Scruton may have been missing in the Hindu ancients, wine was still, outside of this, present in application. Beyond Āyurveda, and the Kāmasūtra, we have two texts which speak highly of how wine aids in sexual function.

The Kādambarīsvīkaraṇasūtram and the Kādambarīsvīkaraṇakārikā are, until now, the only two Sanskrit texts dealing exclusively with the use of wine in sex.

The **Kādambarīsvīkaraṇasūtram** begins with the proclamation that in the Yajña of Kāmadeva, the drinking of wine is a must. The use of Yajña is figurative, and is to be understood as an euphemism for intercourse. Wine is not merely a substance, but the daughter of Varuṇa, who maintains Ṛta. One obtains indescribable pleasure in the act, only by drinking wine, and not otherwise. It is the prime cause of this high pleasure, and to partake in the act without it causes the libido to die out. Only in consumption of this before the act, can there be an inclination towards the act.

The text then describes the pleasure of sex as the seventh taste, beyond the six Āyurveda tastes, and this taste is one which is something that is worthy of offering to the Gods. To achieve this, drinking wine is the way. It gives rise to amorous sport, beyond mere sexual desire. Yet the text warns that it can not be drunk with a maiden who has passed beyond youth, for it does not give rise to pleasure and drinking with such a maiden is like filling an already filled pot of water.

It is described as being a climax delaying substance, a way to dull sensual pain, a way to increase the attractiveness of the face and body.

At the end, the text says that when one’s wife drinks it, it brings forth the voice of the Gods, and when a young girl drinks it, it brings forth the root cause, the Ādiśakti in front of the eyes, with nothing concealing her from you, or you attain unity with her.

In drinking it this way, one thus attains sexual pleasure along with his wife, and progeny too, and after death, attains the realms of Urvaśī.

## Social Aspects of Wine

In the Kādambarīsvīkaraṇasūtram, one is advised to drink wine on the birth of someone in the same gotra, according to the opinion of Mārkaṇḍeya. In addition, the commoners are allowed to drink only on Fridays, while Kṣatriya-s, petty kings and the rich can drink every day.

But beyond it too, there is an aspect, of the Symposium of the Greeks, and of the Hindus, the Samāpānaka and the Goṣṭhī. While in Greece, the Symposium was restricted to men, the Samāpānaka included women as well, and just like in the Symposium, wine flowed freely.

The Kāmasūtra tells us that these were either done in the confines of one’s house, or in a location outside, like a picnic.

And although the Samāpānaka is a purely pleasure oriented act, it is nevertheless not impossible for Hindus to indulge in a similar act by combining Samāpānaka and Goṣṭhī but excluding things of religion, for there is a different way that the Hindu ancients approached wine in that context.

## Religious aspects of wine

As has been spoken, the transfiguration of wine as being imbued with Dionysus by the ancient Greeks, sacralized wine. It is no surprise, that the vineyards were all cultivated in sacred precincts, like the temples of Roman Gods.

In a different way, we see wine as an essential offering in the Kula rites. The Kulapūjanacandrikā, quoting the core Tāntrika texts, tells us that a Kula ritual without wine is just like a body without life. His merits across many births are rendered null and void. In drinking such sanctified wine, he attains Śiva and is not reborn.

And there are many methods of offering wine, and the literature for such rites is vast, hence it shall suffice to show that wine is offered in libation, with a ceremony, and not in a haphazard manner.

Let that be enough thus, for describing the greatness of wine.

## The prohibitionist victory, and how it has only worsened the problem of alcohol addiction

> “Lo! the poor toper whose untutored sense, Sees bliss in ale, and can
> with wine dispense; Whose head proud fancy never taught to steer,
> Beyond the muddy ecstasies of beer.”
>
> — George Crabbe

Prohibition was a result of transferring sexual morality to alcohol drinking. In the former, it is the object which defines whether it is innocent or guilty, but in drinking, it is not the act but its excess which defines the guilty. Puritanism is jealousy and also an overblown concern with what others are doing. Once something is no longer taboo, they indulge in prohibiting that which makes someone profit monetarily.

If there is a sin in drinking, it is only because pleasure and virtue are divorced. The Puritan, who gave rise to prohibition, has a mind of absolutes, which sees something as a sin in full, or as something sanctioned for indulgence without limits.

Hence the attitudes towards sex, which saw pleasure in it as a sin, and chastity was not seen as a preparation for enjoyment, but as an obligation with no motivation behind it except for being a custom. Thus, when the taboos broke down, there was no way to maintain sexual discipline. Thus one sees in the reaction to such things, that the discerning faculty is also missing in the opposing force. That force, even though being open to indulgence, knows not how to partake properly. This itself is something that has led to these forces play a game of all or nothing, and in this, pleasure suffers.

The idea of the golden mean, in this regard, is pertinent, for instead of total restriction or unrestrained indulgence, a middle path, of disciplined indulgence, makes enjoyment of something real. In the Indian way, Dharma, which can be considered virtue in this context is the regulator of indulgence. But to go further, we shall say that there is a right and a wrong way to indulge, as opposed to the two extremes, and the material by our precursors tells us exactly how we must partake in wine, and also other liquors in as far as the Śāstra permits us.

## The Way Forward

While the words of Manu encourage eschewing liquor, as has been seen, it is nevertheless a great punishment to bring this about by force, with edicts of government, or by fraud, promoting abstinence as virtue and drinking of all kinds as vice.

But as has been discoursed of above, the way for a Hindu becomes clear. It is not alone that he must drink, but in company. This company is with the Gods, with a lover, a wife and friends as well as relations.

This drinking itself in every case, must then be sanctified as a part of a ritual. With the Gods and the lover as well as wife, it is a path to liberation and unity with the mūlaprakṛti. Outside of sexual and religious functions, it is something that can be taken in celebrations too, as is seen in the sūtra which enjoins drinking at the birthday of a gotra relation in the Kādambarīsvīkaraṇasūtram, and allows the rich, petty kings and Kṣatriya-s to drink every day, while Śakti worshippers are allowed to drink on Tuesdays and Fridays.

But let us restrict our discourse to the sensuous and the profane, for sacred drinking is by its very nature, regulated in the rites of the sects.

Now as the Kāmasūtra suggests, one can hold drinking parties at the homes of friends by turns. These drinking parties can also be held in forests and places outside urban areas.

In our times, one may have these parties in an establishment serving liquor and wine, or at houses whether one’s own or of a friend’s, or take wines with them to a picnic with food appropriate to the wine.

In such a place, offer the wine or liquor to Devī, hailing her, with a small amount spilt on the floor as the offering. From this point, the wine is no longer a mere indulgence, but the Gods are now within the Goṣṭhī as witnesses.

As the Kāmasūtra recommends, consume pungent, bitter and sour food items with the wine, or the liquor. (Kāmasūtra Book 1, Chapter 4, verses 22-25)

With these done, one can pour a drink no more than a standard pour, which may be two pāla-s or three pāla-s (`~`100-150ml), and having taken your cup, endeavour not to reveal under the influence, what you wouldn’t reveal in sobriety. The drink that you have thus taken, you must not endeavour to drink it rapidly, but let it reach out to the deepest recesses of the being, before taking another sip. As that effect takes over, discourse shall start flowing. When discourse takes place, let only one person speak at a time, and involve everyone in the discourse. As the cups empty out, one may take up another round, and continue it until the discourse is satisfactorily done. In this process, one must exercise restraint in behaviour, and in the amount of wine that one indulges in, for the aim is not intoxication, but the stimulation of the entire being.

The alcohol that one drinks, is the Devī herself, and she leads to liberation. Hence one must seek to avoid any behaviour in his drinking that may lead him towards delusion, for if one does not practice the propriety that is warranted in the explicit rite, ruin is certain.

In this regard, we must also look to the Chinese and the Greeks, with their concepts of 禮, 道, εὐδαιμονία and αἰδώς . Each of these is tied together to form an entire whole. 禮 (Lǐ), translated as ritual, propriety and so on, is that which establishes harmony. If one tries to achieve harmony without propriety, things will still go amiss, as Youzi says in the Analects. 道 (Dào) is the source of everything, and its nature is like water, which benefits everyone, and occupies places which men avoid. Εὐδαιμονία is happiness, or sukhaḥ, which is achieved not by suppression of Kāma, but by subordinating it to Dharma. Αἰδώς is the recognition of the other as more important, of reverence and modesty.

> -Analects 1.12
>
> 上善若水。水善利萬物而不爭，處衆人之所惡，故幾於道
>
> The highest excellence is like water. The excellence of water is in
> benefiting all things without being in conflict with them. It occupies
> the low lying places, which other men dislike.
>
> -Dao De Jing, Chapter 8

In combining these, the propriety thus, in drinking, encompasses relishing and savouring the wine, as opposed to aiming for intoxication. It is to consider that even the Samāpānaka is a Yajña, and in a Yajña, one is always careful about the entire vidhi, and in this, every drinker is a Yajamāna, who aims to achieve sukha. But such a Sukha, shall not be achieved merely by observing this propriety, but in facilitating the achievement of the Sukha of another. And when everyone mutually facilitates such an achievement, there is none who shall be left deprived of sukha, and such a ceremony of drinking of wine, like that of tea ceremonies of the Chinese and Japanese shall become a mark of excellence.

> A cup of wine, under the flowering trees;  
> I drink alone, for no friend is near.  
> Raising my cup I beckon the bright moon,  
> For he, with my shadow, will make three men.
>
> -Li Bai
