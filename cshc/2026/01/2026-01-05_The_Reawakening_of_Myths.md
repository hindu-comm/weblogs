``` text The crisis of modern mythology is not a crisis of faith but a crisis of categories. By forcing myth into the binary of historical fact or fictional lies, modern thought has disabled its capacity for understanding. The grammar required to read reality as meaningful has been stripped. What remains is a divided world: brute physical objects on one side, private mental fantasies on the other, with no ontological bridge between them. Recovering this lost dimension requires more than correcting historical errors or defending belief. It demands a new metaphysics. We must postulate a mode of existence that is neither physical, nor mental, nor abstract, yet irreducible to all three. As long as we remain confined within a framework that recognizes only history as the arbiter of the real, mythic figures will remain exiles in their own cultures, reduced to literature, symbolism, or personal belief. The solution, however, is neither to force myths back into historical fact nor to retreat into relativism. It is to recognize that Western philosophy has miscounted the categories of existence. Alongside the physical, the mental, and the abstract, a fourth ontological mode exists. In this mode, myths neither report facts nor fabricate illusions but manifest a distinct form of reality. To grasp the crisis of modern mythology, we must recognize this fourth mode. We must name it: the Figural. The loss of this mode is not merely theoretical. It is palpable in lived experience. ```

### **I. Basics of Argument**

The figural must be understood in a precise sense. It is not merely a fourth category added alongside the physical, the mental, and the abstract. The figural is both a distinct mode of being and the medium through which beings become intelligible at all. It names not only what exists, but how existence acquires form, coherence, and meaning. Figures are not objects placed within reality; they are the patterned structures through which reality becomes readable, inhabitable, and normatively charged. The article is placed to establish that the existing tradition of myths is less precise about governing its structure and therefore proceeds in four movements.

1\. It reframes ontology not as an inventory of what exists but as a grammar of world-disclosure.

2\. It establishes the figural as a distinct ontological mode irreducible to the physical, mental, or abstract.

3\. It develops the temporal, epistemic and participatory structure of figural beings.

4\. It examines the political and cultural consequences of the collapse of figural consciousness in modernity.

### **II. Ontology as Grammar**

Figural ontology rests on a fundamental recognition that, in recent decades, both philosophy and anthropology have moved toward: different cultures do not merely hold varying beliefs about a single shared world; they actually inhabit differently constituted worlds. What used to be seen as different belief systems are now understood as different worlds. To begin understanding figural ontology within this wider philosophical landscape, we must abandon the assumption that there is only one ontology waiting to be correctly described. By ‘world’ I mean not merely the physical universe but the structured field of intelligibility within which entities can appear, relations can obtain, and meanings can emerge. A world is not a collection of objects but an organised totality that determines what can count as an object, what forms of causation are possible, and what modes of agency exist. *In this sense, the figural is not only one ontology among others, but the ontological medium through which worlds disclose themselves. It is simultaneously a mode of being and the grammar that renders beings intelligible within a world.*

Typically, normative metaphysics assumes that metaphysics provides the authoritative blueprint of reality, and cultures only supply interpretations rather than participate in the constitution of worlds. Moving towards this approach suggests that ontology is not a universal inventory of what exists but a culturally inflected mode of world-making.

Ontology acts as a background schema of intelligibility, the conditions of possibility that make a world show up in a particular way. It’s not only about what exists, but how existence is configured. That is what counts as real: appearance, causality, agency, personhood, or truth. Simply put, ontology is a form of metaphysical, abstract structure shaping our worldview. I suggest that ontology is the deep logic through which a culture discloses reality to itself. We must call this “world-making grammar. Let me explain this in depth to understand it as a category. Like linguistic grammar, ontological grammar operates largely unconsciously. Native speakers don’t explicitly know grammatical rules, yet speak fluently. Similarly, participants in an ontological framework don’t typically articulate its structures, yet navigate the world it constitutes with tacit understanding. And like grammar, ontological structures can be learned, enabling movement between worlds—though such learning requires more than intellectual grasp. It requires reshaping the tacit know-how through which reality discloses itself.

In this sense, ontology isn’t a neutral science of being, but rather a tacit pattern of sense-making embodied in rituals, which are the language-forming habits. From this perspective, to follow tradition is to live in a differently constituted world, which most people confuse with the fact that traditionalists see the same world differently. The underlying ontological grammar of a culture determines what constitutes causation and what constitutes truth. Consider how different grammars constitute different worlds. Indigenous frameworks often constitute worlds not divided into subjects and objects but organised as fields of relations where agency circulates rather than originating in bounded subjects. The claim is not that naturalist ontology is false—within worlds it constitutes, it operates successfully. The claim is that naturalist grammar forecloses dimensions of reality by configuring worlds where such dimensions cannot exist, rendering them not false but literally unintelligible within that world-structure. This is ontological violence when imposed: not correcting false beliefs but destroying worlds where different realities were possible. A figural ontology positions itself within this plural landscape as one such grammar of being. It understands the world not as composed of discrete objects or mental representations, but as a living weave of figures—symbolic and narrative patterns that reveal meaning through repetition and participation. Where modern ontologies prize the measurable and the mental, the figural privileges the expressive and the relational. It asks how reality manifests through story, image, and ritual form, rather than through matter or idea alone.

In this sense, figural ontology does not reject pluralism, but rather deepens it. It provides us with a vocabulary for understanding how myths and ritual full worlds constitute genuine ontological orders rather than metaphorical overlays. By treating figures as real modes of manifestation, it allows us to see that different cultures are not mistaken about a single world but are participants in distinct, symbolically mediated worlds. Naturalist ontology constitutes an impoverished world where its grammatical structure forecloses entire dimensions of reality. Other ontologies might constitute richer worlds where more is possible. The task for us is not proving if figures “really exist” in some neutral sense, but showing how different ontological grammars constitute different worlds and making the case that some worlds are more adequate to the fullness of reality than others.

### **III. Myth as Figural Being**

Once ontology is understood as grammar rather than inventory, a previously invisible problem appears. Certain entities persist, act, and command allegiance, yet remain illegible within the dominant ontological grammar of modernity. The following section introduces the figural as a mode of being required to account for this phenomenon.

Now that we have established that ontologies are grammars, and that the figural is one such grammar. Let us answer what a figure is. What kind of entity possesses the qualities we have been attributing to it, that is, the capacity to make normative demands, to require metabolic sustenance, to enter into relation with consciousness? To answer this, we must establish figural being as a distinct ontological mode irreducible to the three modes Western philosophy has traditionally recognised. We typically recognise three modes of existence. Physical objects, such as stone, trees, and the human body, possess extension and location. Abstract objects exist formally, such as numbers, geometrical relations, and logical structures, and have reality independent of material instantiation. Mental states exist experientially; for example, thoughts, feelings, and perceptions constitute the private interiority of consciousness. But in all this, where do myths belong? Myths are not material objects; rather, they resist these categories completely. You cannot touch Rama or locate him spatially. Yet they are not abstractions like numbers either. Numbers have no agency, personality or narrative structure. A triangle cannot choose or suffer, or teach. And myths are not merely mental states, not private fantasies confined to individual minds. They persist across generations, shape collective behaviour, and carry authority that private thoughts never possess. What if myths represent a fourth ontological category that Western philosophy has failed to theorise properly? Call it the figural mode of being. A figure is not physical, not abstract, not mental, but something else entirely.

Although Figural Ontology has been studied in some parts of the West, they approached figural reality through different approaches, be it Cassirer’s symbolic forms, Jung’s archetypes, Ricoeur’s hermeneutics, and Eliade’s sacred phenomenology. Indeed, Western thought has not entirely ignored the figural; they have been very much aware of this, but Western thinkers have approached this dimension through other modes of explanation, such as symbolic forms, archetypes and sacred phenomenology. However, they ultimately psychologised or symbolised the figure, locating myth within the human mind (as an archetype) or human culture (as a symbol). They stopped short of the radical ontological claim I am making here, which is that figures are **real entities** independent of our private psychology, yet distinct from physical matter. They recognised the *meaning* of myth, but they miscounted the *categories of reality* required to sustain it.

I also try to take a different approach in terms of ontologies, that is, they don’t offer competing beliefs about one shared world but constitute genuinely different worlds. Naturalist ontology doesn’t just deny figures exist—it configures reality such that figural being is impossible within its grammar. We recognise ontological plurality where Western thought assumes a universal framework. Making my position metaphysically more radical than the others, figures are real but participant-dependent, requiring instantiation yet maintaining trans-temporal identity. We integrate non-Western frameworks not as examples but as more sophisticated understandings than Western philosophy has developed. The claim isn’t that myths are meaningful but that entire dimensions of reality become inaccessible when ontological grammars that sustain figural beings are destroyed.

### **IV. The Structure of Figural Being**

Having argued that the figural constitutes a distinct ontological mode, we must now specify its internal structure. Without such specification, the figure risks collapsing into metaphor or mysticism. The following account is therefore not illustrative but metaphysical.

We must then define the Figural by what it exceeds mainly. We must step out of the binary of ‘objective matter’ and ‘subjective thought.’ Figures are a fourth mode of being with their own reality that is neither physical, abstract, nor mental. They exist as trans-temporal patterns that achieve presence through instantiation. This is a metaphysical assertion, not merely an epistemological observation about when myths are accessible. Let me explain this assertion. A figure has internal coherence through narrative structure rather than through material continuity or logical consistency. We must investigate the structure of figural beings, or rather, suggest that accessing myth is to access a distinct form of being. The pattern is what persists, and the pattern is irreducible to any of its instantiations. More crucially, figures achieve reality through instantiation rather than through independent existence. Physical objects exist whether anything perceives them. Abstract structures exist whether anything exemplifies them. But figures become real precisely through their enactment. When repetition penetrates the unconscious, the actor’s movement does not become automatic like a habit; instead, consciousness clarifies into a kind of transparent alignment with the pattern. Without such instantiation, the figure remains merely potential. This means figures carry normative weight inherently rather than derivatively. When we say “act like Ram(or some figure)”, it’s not because we first establish that Rama exists and then extract moral lessons from his behaviour. The figure itself is a normative structure. Description and prescription are unified in the figural beings themselves. To grasp the figure is already to feel its ethical demand without any of the empiricism of its behavioural authenticity. A way to put this is that figures operate trans-temporally. Not by existing outside of time, but by maintaining a peculiar availability across time. The figure is simultaneously narrated as past, instantiated in present, and held up as a future ideal. This is not an atemporal existence but rather an *omni-temporal* availability.

I use the term “Omnitemporality” not for rhetorical muscle flexing, but to precisely describe their emergence across time. The figure is always accessible for activation, regardless of when you encounter it. A better way to understand myth is to say that it has a **dual mode of being**. It appears in time whenever it is told, performed, sung, or enacted—this is the **performative exercise or ritualistic**. In other words, a myth is not outside time, but it is **available across time**. It has a structure that allows it to be reactivated, re-embodied, and re-interpreted in new historical settings, without ceasing to be *that* myth.

We must distinguish this mode carefully from what Mikhail Bakhtin called the “Absolute Past” of the Epic. The vocabulary for this distinction was provided by **Mikhail Bakhtin(1895–1975), a seminal Russian literary theorist known for his concepts of** ***dialogism*** **and the** ***chronotope*****.** Who argued that different genres constitute different worlds. For Bakhtin, the ancient Epic was defined by its distance from an ‘absolute past’ and the mythic world is “walled off” from the present, a completed time of heroes that we can revere but never touch. But I must contest that the Figural is not merely this. It would be nothing but nostalgia if interpreted this way. The Figural operates differently. It is not a closed past but a **Vertical Present**. It is a trans-temporal pattern that cuts perpendicularly through the linear flow of time. It offers the coherence of the Epic without requiring a retreat from the immediate ‘now.’ Have tried explaining how this works in Article 1 itself.

While writing of this article I was confused as to how to think of these ideas as **Platonism and Constructivism** respectively establishes that the Forms are eternal and pre-exist us, while the latter says we build these gods, but to my findings, I see that once a Figure is articulated in history, it reveals a pattern that is *then* recognised as having always been true. The reason I have used a Mandelbrot image is to show that Benoit Mandelbrot “*discovered*“ or formed the set in 1980 using a computer. It has a specific birthdate in history, but once discovered, we realise that this mathematical structure describes the nature of reality *forever*. It didn’t exist as a “picture” before 1980, but the *potentiality* for it was always woven into the fabric of mathematics. I think of Figural in these terms. We see a similar trajectory in the history of physics. The force we call “gravity” has always acted upon the universe. Christiaan Huygens, Robert Hooke, and Edmund Halley all groped toward an understanding of this force, sensing its presence and working on them. But it was only when one of them reached Isaac Newton to ask him to publish this in a coherent form, *Principia Mathematica,* that the “Law of Gravity” was fully articulated. Newton did not invent gravity, but rather gave it a grammar( Einstein certainly shakes this up). He took a diffuse phenomenon and crystallised it into a coherent form that could thereafter be recognised, calculated, and relied upon by everyone. While I can be criticised and dismiss this as simple recognition as the **Baader-Meinhof phenomenon,** which is a mere trick of selective attention, where we suddenly notice what was always there, only because we learned a new name. But Figural is not a cognitive bias. When a culture articulates a figure like deities or gods, they are not projecting a fantasy onto the world as the Baader-Meinhof phenomenon contends. Rather, they are rendering visible a ‘standing wave’ of moral and cosmic reality that was waiting for a grammar. Like Newton with gravity, *Mythopoet* (used by *J.R.R. Tolkien* in his poem “*Mythopoeia”* for one who crafts myths) does not create the force, but rather performs the making of its form, giving it the coherence that allows us to inhabit it. Myths are not eternal (formed in history), while not being merely historical (capable of renewal). In simple words, myths occupy a paradoxical temporal status for us. They are not eternal in the Platonic sense, for they were born within specific human histories. Yet, they are not merely historical, for they escape the past through ritual renewal. They are formed *in* time but remain available *across* time. That is, they are formed in history, carried through history, yet capable of renewal in any moment where human consciousness and ritual practice meet the narrative structure again.

This leads to the creation of a ‘standing wave’ in the river of time, that is, a structure that subsequent generations can enter and inhabit regardless of their historical distance from the origin. To resolve the paradox we stated above about temporal status, we must start by viewing Figures as **Participant-Dependent Autonomous Entities**.

Let’s take an example: consider the game of Chess. The rules of Chess are not physical objects, nor are they private hallucinations. They are strict, autonomous patterns—you cannot move a Rook diagonally simply because you ‘believe’ you can. It has a ‘will-independent’ reality. Yet, without players to enact it, Chess sleeps in mere potentiality. Figures operate similarly, that is, they require the ‘computational power’ of our ritual and attention to manifest, but once instantiated, they run on their own logic, often defying the very wills that summoned them. To put a caveat to this, I must clarify that when we summon a Figural for a situation or the consequentiality of it, the figural doesn’t act as a tool, but rather opens a door. We grant the Figure entry into history, but we do not dictate its conduct once it arrives (I want result X), but the logic of the Figure is ontological (I am pattern Y). When X and Y conflict, the Figure does not bend; it overrules this, depending on the ethicality of the myth. This is why tradition emphasises *safety*, *purity*, *priests* and *containment* in ritual. This is not to protect them from us, but to protect our finite wills from the overwhelming autonomous logic of the pattern we have invited in.

### **V. The Relational Fabric**

We must go further than merely adding figures as a fourth category alongside the other three. The figural is not a separate region of reality but the relational fabric through which reality acquires form and significance. It is the ontological medium, the field where meaning coheres through patterned manifestation. Consider what happens in a direct encounter. The physical world appears as brute materiality until figures organise it into a meaningful world. Abstract structures remain sterile formalism until figures give them existential weight. The figural is the rhythm that renders these dimensions intelligible to one another. It structures possibilities of human existence in ways that abstract principles cannot. When we participate in Rasa Lila, we aren’t commemorating a past event or symbolically representing an idea. We inhabit a figural being directly. Such acts rhythmically coincide with mythological existence and the ontology of the natural world, without any contradiction. Nor are the figural archetypes in Jung’s psychological sense, nor are they structures of the psyche projected outward. They are ontological patterns that have their own reality, and human consciousness develops in ways that enable participation in them. Psyche itself is structured by its participation in figural reality, not the other way around.

### **VI. Recognition as Figural Knowledge**

If figures are real yet non-material and participant-dependent, we are to provide a corresponding epistemology. The question is no longer whether figures exist but how they are known without being reduced to belief, imagination, or inference.

How do we know figures? Like what makes us access them well, I would in future like to establish that traditions with their coherence help us access the figure. Not through introspection or imagination, as we do not fantasise about them in arbitrary scenarios. We know figures through recognition. Not recognition in the ordinary sense, as we say “I have seen this before”, but more on the lines of recognition as re-cognition, as to thinking alongside the figure. We do not access figures through introspection or imagination. We access them through recognition, but not in the ordinary sense of identifying something previously seen. It is re-cognition, and I think of it as a pattern that tradition has already disclosed. The tradition supplies grammar while consciousness aligns itself with this grammar. To know a figure is to join a trans-generational structure of meaning that one does not invent but inherits.

When you encounter a mythic figure, what happens is not the acquisition of new information but the activation of understanding that was always potentially available. This explains the strange phenomenology of mythic encounters. You do not learn something entirely new so much as suddenly understand something you somehow already knew.

The figure does not inform you in the manner of propositional knowledge; it crystallises patterns of understanding that were implicit in your existence but remained unarticulated until the figure gave them form. This is why myths cannot be transmitted as mere information or remembered as private mental content. Their force lies in *activation*: an alignment rather than a recollection. The figure does not arise from within the psyche it comes *to* the subject, yet it requires a distinct structural capacity to receive it. This explains the phenomenology of cognition without reducing it to psychology. One feels that one “already knew” not because the content was hidden in the brain, but because figural reality is the ontological atmosphere one has always been breathing without noticing. The figure simply renders that atmospheric pressure visible, condensing the background conditions of reality into a perceptible form.

*What enables this activation remains mysterious for both the practitioner of tradition and the social scientist attempting to study it. I can describe certain conditions theoretically. It seems to require existential readiness, meaning being in a life situation that the figure addresses. It requires cultural preparation and having conceptual resources that make the figure intelligible. It requires participatory engagement through ritual. In simpler terms, experience without locative modes might occur but remain surreal or uninterpretable. Intelligibility requires self-orientation to the locative modes of the figural, an understanding of where and how figures exist. Yet none of these conditions guarantees recognition. The figure has autonomy in this regard. I cannot deduce when revelation will occur, nor can I guarantee it will happen at all, nor can I fully explain why it reveals itself in some moments and not others. Meeting conditions does not produce recognition. It is granted, and the granting isn’t human-controlled.*

Yet this recognition is never disembodied or free-floating. It is inseparable from the affective geography that makes a world livable. Here, **topophilia** becomes the decisive force. It is the deep attachment to particular landscapes, temples, rivers and ritual grounds. Figural presence is not encountered in abstract space but within environments saturated with memory. Conversely, when these environments collapse through displacement or rupture, communities undergo **cultural bereavement**. As *Maurice Eisenbruch* notes, this is a grief arising not merely from losing physical territory but from losing the cultural grammar that made figural reality accessible. Topophilia reveals how figures are anchored in place. Cultural bereavement reveals that the violence of separation creates “un-locatedness”. Together, they show that figural ontology is intrinsically locomotive, that is, a form of **Locative Memory**. In simple words, it is the understanding that human identity is not stored solely within the private mind rather also anchored in specific geography of a lived world and its coordinates, such as a temple, river sides or the soil of a ritual ground, you can see as we describe this, that meaning is “emplaced” with the geometry of such places and the landscape itself acts as a mnemonic device that sustains a community’s mythic history. When this connection is severed through secularisation or other such mental or physical processes, we lose a physical coordinate and external scaffolding of the self simultaneously. The loss of locative memory results in a specific kind of modern amnesia where the individual, stripped of spatial references, becomes a ghost haunting a generic, interchangeable world, unable to recall the patterns that once made his world existence intelligible. We should, in brief, look at the collapse of such figures in culture.

### **VII. The Collapse of Figural Consciousness**

Christianity in its early mystical forms understood figurality profoundly. Typological readings of Scripture saw Old Testament figures as living patterns that found fulfilment in Christ without being abolished. The symbolism of liturgy created spaces where figural reality could manifest. The theology of incarnation insisted that divine reality genuinely appears in material form without being captured by its limited physical form to which it is born. In the Bhagwat Gita, Krishna explicitly says he *appears* in material form (sambhavāmi) yet remains *unborn, imperishable and sovereign,* not confined by the physical form he assumes.

> **अजोऽपि सन्नव्ययात्मा भूतानामीश्वरोऽपि सन् । प्रकृतिं स्वामधिष्ठाय
> सम्भवाम्यात्ममायया ॥ ४.६ ॥  
> यद्यपि मैं अजन्मा और अविनाशी स्वरूप हूँ और भूतमात्र का ईश्वर हूँ (तथापि) अपनी प्रकृति
> को अपने अधीन रखकर (अधिष्ठाय) मैं अपनी माया से जन्म लेता हूँ।।**

The divine does not merely represent itself through material form or approximate itself in diminished fashion. It becomes present in materiality while retaining its transcendent nature intact. A figure in this framework operates as a living mediation between two dimensions. There are two sides, respectively

1. **Transcendent**—*that which exceeds all temporal and spatial limitations, which cannot be grasped through ordinary perception or contained within finite categories*.

2. **Immanent**—*the immediate material world of bodies, objects, and temporal sequence where human existence unfolds*.

Without mediation, these dimensions would remain forever divided, with no possibility of contact or mutual participation. The figure prevents this collapse into separation. It refuses to treat transcendent reality as pure abstraction, forever removed from material engagement. Equally, it refuses to reduce divine presence to merely a physical phenomenon that could be exhaustively explained through natural categories. The figure maintains both dimensions in tension.

It allows the eternal to be encountered within a specific spacetime, showing within a particular material form. Insisting that what is encountered exceeds and outlives any particular manifestation, living on after the moment passes. This is the double movement that makes the experience of the ‘figural’ meditation possible. The transcendent genuinely appears in what’s beyond all of us; it can actually show up in our lives, without ever losing its sense of mystery or vastness, an immanence without ceasing to be transcendent. The material genuinely contains the divine without becoming reducible to divine essence. Krishna, as a figure, is fully human—born, loving, fighting and dying—yet his full divinity can’t be contained in humanity, cannot be contained or exhausted by any of our natural methods. The ritual genuinely instantiates a sacred pattern without reducing sacredness to bodily gesture. What makes this possible is that figures are not objects in either transcendent or immanent realms but the ontological structure through which these realms interpenetrate.

Figures aren’t just objects in some spiritual realm but modes of being. There are ways that what’s beyond time enters the flow of time, where what cannot be grasped becomes tangible, where the infinite becomes accessible to our finite selves without losing infinity. The figure represents the grammar through which transcendence and humans participate in a common reality, a medium through which rituals, patterns, and stories act as a means for the divine and human worlds to connect despite their radical differences. But when we lose touch with this figural consciousness, something happens to the texture of lived experience that we rarely articulate. The present becomes vanishingly thin, just the instantaneous now with no depth or dimension. Theology became increasingly juridical and rationalised; these living figures were subordinated to dogma or displaced into pure concept. The rhythm of figural being was replaced by the linear history of revelation (as explained in article 1) and the conceptual theology of essences. Figures became either historical facts requiring verification or abstract symbols pointing beyond themselves to doctrinal truths. When this structure secularised through modernity philosophy inherited these same suspicions of figural autonomy, thereby closing the whole dimension of figural reality. What remained was only physical fact, abstract concept, and private experience. The mediating fabric where meaning coheres was gone.

I must also shed light on why death is such a hurtful experience in modernity, in brief. You only exist at a knife-edge between a dead past and an empty future. Nothing opens onto anything beyond itself. When one of your relatives dies, like your grandmother, she is simply gone. Not dwelling in patterns that continue her essence, not accessible through ritual or memory in any thinning wire of nostalgic reminiscence. Just gone, matter burning and eventually you will be gone too, as if neither of you ever existed. The felt reality of life is collapsed permanently temporally. Such a collapse and her findings are searching for meaning, which, once it fails to find, can be understood without the help of religion as a base. This is not depression exactly. At least depressed people know something is wrong with them; there is nothing wrong with them, just a part of them, but the self in the West is looked at as a whole and not in parts. For example, we aren’t our feelings; we have them upon us. We aren’t sad, we aren’t happy, we have sadness on us, we have happiness on us. I think this makes us more cognisant of the fact that feelings are fleeting, you wear them and then take them off and put another one on. Just as we put on a feeling, we put on a myth.

I must also shed light on why death is such a dislocating experience in modernity. It is not necessarily that the modern secularist lacks deep feeling or love for things. It is that they lack the container for these events. In a world of figural acknowledgement, an ancestor’s death is a transition into a different mode of presence, that is, the death moves from the local proximity square(Village) to the ritualistic circle. But in the flattened present of modernity, the dead do not move; they simply cease. The pain is not just emotional loss, which is universally present for enormous events like death; we should call this **narrative severance,** the reason is that the dead is not ‘elsewhere’ in the pattern; it is nowhere. The modern self is left holding a thread that has snapped, with no loom to re-weave it. This creates a specific kind of ‘thin’ grief, a grief where one has nowhere to go but inward, looping endlessly in the private psyche because it has no figural address to escape in the external world.

This phenomenon is normal in modernity. It is the background hum of meaninglessness so constant that we stop hearing it. While one tries to escape these through busyness and material consumption, achieving goals within the flattened present, which has been flattened without any idiosyncrasies. We just cope with things. But the sense that existence might have depth, that particular experiences might participate in eternal patterns, that life might be about something beyond biological survival and social success, becomes nearly inaccessible. This is why I have tried emphasising figural consciousness as recognition that the human self is a vessel, not the source. The mythic figure operates like a “divine emotion” — the Figure is a garment on us. But let’s not dwell too deeply into this as of now.

### **VIII. Alterations of Figural**

If figural beings possess autonomy, normativity, and temporal depth, then their suppression is not accidental but structurally engineered by regimes that demand total control over time, meaning, and authority. This destruction of figural consciousness was not merely intellectual development or theological evolution. It was politically necessary for particular forms of domination. Figures have autonomy. They cannot be commanded or controlled, even by God, because they are not the kind of thing that obeys commands. They are patterns that manifest according to their own logic. A theology premised on absolute divine sovereignty cannot accommodate this, the hallmark of Abrahamic monotheism. In them, figural figures must be reduced either to divine creations subject to God’s will or to human inventions subject to our manipulation. Modernity secularised this but preserved the structure. Now, human reason replaced Divine Will as the absolute sovereign. So, every phenomenon can be explained by human understanding or is looked at as subject to human manipulation. The intolerability of them comes to the surface when figures that reveal themselves according to their own logic cannot be fully grasped or controlled either by logic/rationality or through the subordination of the divine that makes demands on consciousness rather than being available for instrumental use. Colonial domination required the dismantling of indigenous figural systems because they provided alternative sources of authority that resisted European power. You cannot efficiently extract resources from a people whose time is organised by ritual cycles rather than industrial schedules, as one cannot impose foreign legal codes on a community whose concept of justice is structured by mythic paradigms of “Right Relation” (Dharma). Therefore, the figures had to be delegitimised. The figures had to be delegitimised as superstition, as primitive holdovers destined for extinction before enlightened modernity. This was not an accidental byproduct but a necessary precondition. The modern regime of power requires consciousness that is trapped in the present moment, disconnected from ancestral patterns, and unable to access dimensions of reality that might ground resistance. Figural autonomy is a political threat because it provides a standpoint outside the system of power. If your identity is grounded in participation in eternal patterns, losing your job is not an existential catastrophe. If your sense of right action comes from figural exemplars dwelling in sacred time, legal-rational authority loses its absolute grip.

> ***The empire’s architects understood this mechanism. Lord Macaulay,
> in his infamous Minute on Education, explicitly sought to create a
> class of persons. He recognised that true colonisation required
> severing the native consciousness from its linguistic and mythic
> roots. Similarly, Indologists like Max Müller recognised that the
> “soul” of India was encoded in Sanskrit. To break the continuity of
> the civilisation, one had to break the continuity of the language that
> carried its figural ontology. The colonial project was not just a land
> grab; it was an ontological siege.***

### **IX. The Question of Plurality**

If different traditions access figural reality through incompatible frameworks, what does this imply? Christian incarnational theology understands figures through the logic of divine descent into history, while Hindu līlā perceives them through a cosmic play that exceeds all frameworks as it captures the self-manifesting spontaneity of figural being better than any Western term. Buddhist emptiness understands them as arising dependently without essence. Indian traditions thus offer an already articulated ontology of the figural. For them, reality is a patterned play which can be known through ritual participation rather than analytic definition.

This leaves us with an open-ended ontological question to ponder upon, and that is, are we dealing with irreducible plurality or a single reality viewed through different prisms? **I urge readers to resist the urge to prematurely read whether these realities are genuinely plural or merely refracted. The figural dimension may be more primordial than the theological distinctions of any single tradition, suggesting that these divergent frameworks are distinct apertures opening onto the same underlying strata of being.** Yet, we cannot rule out that figural realities are genuinely multiple, created through different participatory practices that become real through enactment. What seems clear is that figural beings cannot be reduced to universal archetypes that appear identically across cultures. However, it is equally possible that Prometheus and Krishna are not the same figure wearing different costumes, but inhabitants of genuinely different ontological coordinates. We must be rigorously precise here to avoid the colonial trap of Universalism. We are not asserting that there is a single ‘Hero Archetype’ that wears a Greek costume in Athens and a Hindu costume in Mathura. That is a structuralist reduction that erases difference. In a Figural Ontology, the ‘Hero’ is not a generic mental mould, but a specific mode of engagement with the cosmos. The figural reality of Krishna constitutes a world of *Lila* (Play), whereas the figural reality of Prometheus constitutes a world of *Agon* (Struggle). These are not two views of the same thing; they are two different ways that reality itself can be folded. To collapse them into a single ‘trans-cultural archetype’ is to destroy their specific ontological grammar.

They are culturally specific but not merely conventional. They have autonomy but not absolute independence from human participation. The truth of a figure is not that it corresponds to some fact but that it genuinely discloses patterns of existence, and different figures disclose different patterns in ways that can be complementary, conflicting, or partially overlapping. It is a bridge that allows one to reconcile Pluralism with Coherence. I will explain the ethics of figural in my next and final part of my article, where I delve deep into **Monomaniacal Figures.** There is a figurative limit to how different traditions (even within India) access reality differently. Shankara represents this “Zero Point” where the Figure dissolves. An acknowledgement concerning the limit case of this ontology. Just as the animator breathes a humanized soul into images to evoke emotion, Śaṅkara perceived existence through a radically different aesthetic. He saw reality not as populated by figures but as dissolving into the unbroken oneness of Being. To him, every form is a fleeting ripple upon the ocean of the Atman. Where the figural consciousness forges connection by projecting narrative into the world, Śaṅkara’s vision strips the world of personhood. His spiritual aesthetic is **trans-figurative**. **He seeks not to animate reality but to de-animate it, piercing the illusion of multiplicity to reveal the sole reality of Brahman.** For the strict Advaitin, the world is not a field of figures available for recognition. It is a veil to be lifted. This makes Śaṅkara a crucial contrast case. He represents an ontology in which the figural is deliberately dissolved rather than enacted. This proves that the Figural is a specific mode of engagement and not the only mode of spiritual life, even within the Indic fold.

### **X. Conclusion**

This article has argued that mythic figures are neither historical fictions nor psychological projections, but manifestations of a distinct ontological mode, “the figural”. By reconceiving ontology as grammar, it has shown how figures structure time, normativity, knowledge, and political life and their suppression produces a flattened consciousness characteristic of modernity. Through this article, we see that metaphysical shifting has hardened the human self. We reinterpreted “possession” as hysteria and “inspiration” as brain chemistry. We locked the doors of the self from the inside. In the end, recovering figural consciousness is not an exercise in antiquarian nostalgia. It is the necessary antidote to a civilizational malaise that has reduced existence to data, politics to management consulting and imagination to consumption. To recover this faculty is to restore the sense that being speaks—that the world bears patterns worthy of reverence.

We must understand that these figures emerge in three distinct registers. They appear as transcultural archetypal patterns, as localized cultural embodiments, and as historical reactivations that answer the needs of a particular age. They are neither merely discovered nor invented. Myths cannot be treated as another region of being added to the familiar triad of matter, mind, and abstraction. To speak of the figural is to speak of a condition that allows reality to take on shape and meaning at all. This understanding depends on living traditions that maintain unbroken streams of practice through which figural knowledge continues to be transmitted. These traditions require cultural frameworks that make figures intelligible and communities that sustain them. To live figuratively is to inhabit the interval where meaning discloses itself. It is important to remember that truth is not what corresponds to fact, but what calls forth transformation. And to be transformed today means learning again how to recognise the figures that make the world intelligible, and to serve them without surrendering to them. To recover figural beings is not to return to ancient belief but to reopen the dimension where meaning, body, and cosmos speak to one another. In that reawakening, the human is no longer master or spectator but participant in a larger choreography of forms. When a myth truly lives again, whether through art, ritual or any other method of engagement, it is remaking the world. This leaves us a task of philosophy that is neither analysis nor nostalgia, but participation in this ongoing work of figuration of the creation of worlds.

This article has now established the Figural as a genuine mode of being for us. However, granting reality to these patterns raises questions that cannot be avoided. If figures have agency, can they be malevolent or destructive? If they require our participation, what constrains their influence over consciousness? Having established what figures are, we must now address the practical challenge of discernment. For if the modern world suffers from the silence of the gods, it risks perishing at the hands of their dark distortions. Looking forward, my research aims to explore this discernment and the ethical challenges in depth. In the next parts, I will try to investigate methodologies for establishing criteria to distinguish between beneficial and harmful figural influences. Furthermore, I shall delve into the ethical implications of engaging with figural realities, developing guidelines for responsible interaction and participation in this complex ontological landscape.
