## **1. Origins and State Policy of the Gulf States**

The Gulf States of Saudi Arabia and Qatar have had very different origin stories. Saudi Arabia is ruled by the Al-Saud family, it is this family’s name from which the country derives its name. Qatar has been ruled by the Al-Thani family.

### **Saudi State**

The Modern State of Saudi Arabia came into being in 1903, under the leadership of Abdulaziz, also known as Ibn Saud. This was the third Saudi State\[1\]. **The first Saudi State was formed under Muhammad bin Saud, with the support of the religious scholar Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab, who is the founder of the Wahhabi-Salafi movement. It was known as the Emirate of Diriyah\[2\].** This ensured that the Saudi State would have a Wahhabi religious orientation, as the Saudi State gained legitimacy from religion/god, given the nature of monarchies, and this ensured a perpetual support of religious Wahhabi Muslims in the region of Riyadh, where the State was formed. The State was crushed by Mohammed Ali Pasha, the Ottoman governor of Egypt, in 1818, as it had conquered large parts of Najd, which contained the towns of Mecca and Medina, the holiest Muslim religious sites. This conquest led to a decline of Ottoman prestige, as they saw themselves as a caliphate. The region then came under the suzerainty of the Ottomans\[3\]. A short-lived 2nd Saudi State came into existence as the Emirate of Najd, but it was quickly wiped out by another ruling Arab family, the Al Rashid\[4\]. These changes did lead to some advantages for the Saudis, as they were going to reap the rewards of their partnership with the Wahhabis, as the religious Wahhabi tribal militia, known as the Ikhwan, would help them in the formation of the Modern Saudi State. **It would be amiss of me not to remind the readers that the existence of Saudi Arabia became a reality due to the partnership of the Al Saud family and the Wahhabis, making this partnership the bedrock of the modern Saudi State.**

The Ottomans, by the late 19th and early 20th century, had severely weakened due to Russian aggression and Balkan Nationalism, of the Christians. In 1913, they withdrew from Hasa, and the Saudis swooped in. The Ottomans did not contest this, as they were more interested in the region of Hejaz that has the two holy Muslim sites. During the next twelve years, Ibn Saud defeated al-Rashid, based in Hail, then the Sharif of Mecca. In January 1926, he proclaimed himself King of Hijaz and Sultan of Najd. Further expanding his domain, he annexed the Asir region in southern Hijaz in 1930. Two years later, he united all of his domains into one kingdom, which he called the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia\[5\].

### **Religious Scholarship**

The Wahhabis had been successful in creating a native class of religious scholars for the Saudi State with whom this Salafi sect has good relations\[6\]\[7\]. Ibn Abd al-Wahhab settled in Diriyah in 1744, where he found a loyal disciple and ally in Muhammad bin Saud, the ruler of the town. Declaring the populations of the Arabian Desert to be polytheists, the two Muhammads embarked on a religious mission to re-Islamise the Arabian Peninsula. As stated before, until the 1930s, the Saudi State was a tribal state with no bureaucracy or system of governance or a modern economy; it was Wahhabism that kept it united\[8\]. The Wahhabis ensured this support remained throughout the 19th century by continuing proselytization and ensuring the Sufis stayed out of the Najd\[9\], the power base of the third Saudi State. As the scholar, Birol Başkan states\[10\]:

> *Having no military power of their own, Wahhabi religious scholars
> could not bring political unity back to the region. But they could
> provide critical help to a potential candidate in unifying and
> consolidating a realm. This was their contribution to the unification
> campaign launched by the future King of Saudi Arabia, Abd al-Aziz Al
> Saud, in 1902. His first act after capturing Riyadh sent a powerful
> message to Wahhabi religious scholars across Najd: he married Tarfa
> bint Abdullah al-Sheikh, the daughter of the religious judge of
> Riyadh, Abdullah bin Abd al-Latif al-Sheikh, a descendant of Muhammad
> ibn Abd al-Wahhab and by then the leader of the Wahhabi religious
> scholars.*

Due to this, even to this day, the Saudi State has the most religiously layered law within the Gulf States\[11\]. The Wahhabis were successful in creating a native class of religious scholars as well as bureaucrats by the 1950s when the oil boom took place\[12\].

### **Qatari State**

The modern Qatari State came into being in the year 1971 after it gained complete Independence from the British as a protectorate, with the states of Bahrain and the UAE. During the mid 1820s, the Al Thani family had been on the rise in Qatar. In 1868, the British imposed an agreement on Qatar, obliging it to maintain maritime peace in the Gulf and refer any disputes to Britain. Even though the agreement was imposed on Qatar, Britain clearly recognized Al Thani as the ruling family; it became an independent chiefdom by 1870\[13\]. Ottoman rule confirmed Qatar’s independence from Bahrain and consolidated Al Thani’s position, as the Ottomans acknowledged it as the ruling family of Qatar. Qatar remained under Ottoman rule until 1913, the year the Ottoman Empire signed a treaty with Britain and left Qatar\[14\]. The discovery of oil in the region in the 1930s changed the British attitude. After losing concessions to an American oil company in Bahrain and Saudi Arabia in 1932 and 1933, respectively, the British began to offer full maritime, land, and air defense to entice the other Gulf rulers to grant concessions to a British company. Qatar was the first chiefdom to grant an oil concession to a British oil company and, in return, began to receive full British protection\[15\]. It remained a protectorate until 1971, as mentioned before.

*The Qatari State adheres to a form of Wahhabism but does not have a native class of scholars. By the end of the 19th century, even Kuwait had developed a native religious scholarship and scholars, but Qatar had failed like the other Trucial States. Qatar even had to import judges from other places for both religious and civil matters\[18\]. The religious judges were appointed by the Ottomans until 1913, who were usually Iraqi Sunnis\[16\].*

Arable Land

These differences took place due to the population sizes of Qatar and Saudi Arabia. Qatar does not have a lot of arable land, and neither does Saudi Arabia, but it has the Najd, which has a lot of oases. This is the region where their native religious scholarship took root. During the 1900s, the population of the region of Saudi Arabia was 2 million, for Qatar, it was 37,000\[17\].

**The above stated factors had an adverse effect on the formation of the modern bureaucracy and the religious bureaucracy within the two nation-states. Due to a low population base, both the Gulf Arab States had to import millions of educated Arabs from other Arabs states with higher population bases and literacy rates. This resulted in certain ideologies creeping into the Gulf States, Arab Nationalism and Islamism being the prominent ones, and both were doctrinally anti-monarchy, while the Gulf States were monarchies, which resulted in extreme societal tension as most of the Arab nationalists and the Islamists became part of the educational system and state bureaucracy. This will be covered in detail in Chapter 3.**

## **2. Islamism, Muslim Brotherhood & the Gulf**

Islamism’s origin lies in the decline of the Islamic world’s might and prestige, vis-à-vis the rise of Europe and colonization of historically Muslim lands at the hands of the British, the Russians, and the French. It originated as ideas from people like Jamal Al Din Afghani and Muhammad Abduh. It was a bunch of ideas rather than a socio-political movement\[18\]. The ideas came as a defense of Islam and Muslims against the assault of Europeans and their ideas. Jamal Al Din Afghani, the luminary of Islamism, believed that Muslims could progress like Christians if they reformed their religion like Christians\[19\]. They would do this by going back to “**true Islam**”. It held that Islam was not responsible for the political, economic, cultural and military weakness of Muslims with respect to Europeans. It was the opposite. Muslims were in a state of total weakness because they were not true Muslims. This is why most Islamists are Salafis, as they want to revive a time period in the present Muslim world\[23\]. Salaf, as a word, refers to the pious ancestors, the Rashiduns.

*Hasan Al-Banna*

Hasan Al-Banna is the man who created a movement out of these ideas\[20\]. He was exposed to the Islamist literature that had been developing since the late nineteenth century in the writings of such as Jamal al-Din Afghani, Muhammad Abduh and Rashid Rida.

He viewed Islam as ‘**a perfect system of social organization which encompasses all the affairs of life**’. Islam was a comprehensive religion (al-din al-shamil). . Islam set forth the fundamentals of belief and prescribed in detail the duties of humans to their creator and sustainer, Allah. **However, Islam was, in al-Banna’s view, beyond this. It also set forth guidelines for Muslims to follow while regulating all other social, economic, political and even international affairs.**\[21\]

In the Gulf, the Islamists started gaining prominence through the merchants from Egypt and other Arab countries who went there for trade\[22\]. Qatar had a much weaker merchant class and therefore did not have a modern school until the state founded one in the early 1950s. Yet Qatar had already been exposed to the Islamist literature of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. Wahhabism, Saudi Arabia’s official religious understanding, shared striking similarities with Islamist ideas. Both viewed their Muslim contemporaries as seriously deviating from the true Islam, an Islam that could only be founded in the original sources of Islam and the practices of the Prophet and his immediate successors, or Salaf al-Salihin. Both were Salafi , therefore, and preached a return to Islam’s original sources and the practices of the early Muslims\[23\]. Hasan al-Banna visited almost annually to perform pilgrimage, making his first visit in 1936. During one of these visits, he even met Abd al-Aziz Al Saud, the King of Saudi Arabia; a photograph taken at this meeting shows al-Banna kissing Abd al-Aziz’s hand. It is claimed that al-Banna asked Abd al-Aziz to establish a branch of the movement in the Kingdom, but he was gently refused\[24\]. The reason Al-Banna was rejected is because while both the Saudi State and Banna were Salafis, they were not Wahhabis, and this small difference in the view of Salafism is bound to sow distrust and fuel resentment as both the ideologies claim sovereignty from and for god.

### Expansion in the Gulf

The Gulf expansion occurred due to the existing geopolitical situation and regional context, as the Arab Cold War had begun, and Pan-Arab Socialist regimes were toppling monarchies, a form of government prevalent in the Gulf. \[25\] This made the Gulf monarchies and the Muslim Brotherhood willing allies to combat the threat posed by the secular, republican, and socialist forces. Saudi Arabia had a Free Princes movement based on the Free Officers movement of Egypt and Libya. The author Birol Başkan notes\[26\]:

The Brotherhood’s expansion to the Gulf took place at a time when the Gulf states were expanding their state institutions. Because they had no prior experience, the states relied heavily on expatriate professionals from other Arab countries such as Egypt, Syria, Iraq and Palestine. Most of these professionals were inclined towards pan-Arab socialism. This approach would prove troubling because the Gulf states were facing not only a geopolitical challenge posed by pan-Arab socialist regimes that had come to power in other Arab countries, particularly Egypt, but also a domestic opposition that had adopted a discourse blended with the same ideology. In this domestic and regional context, the Muslim Brotherhood proved to be an acquiescent and loyal ally of the Gulf states. Most of the Brotherhood figures in the Gulf were foreigners who escaped persecution in their home countries at the hands of pan-Arab socialist regimes, not having the necessary social and cultural capital to be oppositional.

In the school year 1960–1, for example, only 4.45 per cent of primary school teachers in Qatar were Qataris. Saudi Arabia also fared better than all the others in supplying post-primary school teachers in the school year 1969–70, 29.5 per cent of intermediate and secondary school teachers, and 27.7 per cent of higher education teachers were Saudi citizens\[27\]. In order to staff their rapidly expanding state institutions, Gulf states brought in foreign labor in large numbers, naturally from other Arab countries, such as Egypt, Syria, Palestine, Lebanon, and Iraq. Heavy reliance on these more educated professionals from other Arab countries would not have been troubling if the geopolitical context of the period, the 1950s and 1960s, were more friendly to the ruling Gulf families. But it was not. It was in this period that successive pan-Arab socialist regimes came to power in other Arab countries. It started in Egypt in 1952 when a group of officers calling themselves the Free Officers overthrew the monarchy and founded a republic in its stead. Egypt’s Free Officers became a model for others, inspiring radical-revolutionary figures/parties elsewhere in the Arab world to take political power. In Syria, the Ba’ath Party, a pan-Arab socialist party, came to dominate the government in 1957 and forged a union with Egypt; thus was founded the United Arab Republic in 1958\[28\]. They were allowed to spread their tentacles by the Gulf monarchies to counter the Pan Arab socialists in the 1950s-60s through youth clubs, schools as teachers, universities as professors, bureaucracy as bureaucrats, and the people through labor unions and other avenues of mass contact like the media, especially Al-Jazeera which was started with Qatari State funding and was filled with Pan Arab socialists and Muslim Brotherhood members, they marginalized the former and the latter gained more power which has led to the present conditions\[29\]. The situation was to change drastically in the coming decades. We shall discuss this in the next chapter.

## 3. Divergence

After the 1967 Arab-Israeli 6 day war, it was a massive propaganda and military defeat for the Pan-Arab republican socialist regimes. The Suez crisis was a military loss for Egypt, but a diplomatic and propaganda victory. By 1973, the oil embargo instituted by the OPEC nations(the de facto leader is Saudi Arabia), most of whom were Gulf monarchies, against the actions of the Israelis in the West Bank led to the Arab people seeing the Gulf monarchies as the true warriors for Palestine, leaving the Pan-Arab socialists with nothing to show for themselves. This meant that the alliance between the Gulf monarchies and the Muslim Brotherhood was useless, as the threat they helped combat became essentially non-existent.

### Saudi Arabia

In 1979, Iran became a Shia republican theocracy which led to a wave of protest within the Shiite population of Saudi Arabia. That year, Islamic militants also took over the Grand Mosque in Mecca, known as the Juhayman al-Utaybi incident. This led to the questioning of the religious legitimacy of the rule of the Al Saud family. The leader of the Iranian revolution, Ruhullah Khomeini, was particularly harsh about Saudi Arabia and often criticized its ruling family, Al Saud. For Khomeini, the US was the ‘great Satan’ and Saudi Arabia was its puppet. Juhayman al-Utaybi was the leader of a group that took over the Grand Mosque in Mecca and held it for a number of weeks with the pilgrims inside. Al-Utaybi questioned Al Saud’s fitness to rule on several grounds, such as the family’s failure to wage religious wars and its alliance with foreigners. He also criticized Wahhabi religious scholars for supporting the family\[30\]. This led to Saudi Arabia enabling the Muslim Brotherhood supported Sahwa Movement for a little while, until 1990. It was difficult to guess whether the Sahwa movement would eventually direct its criticisms against Al Saud’s regime and the Wahhabi religious scholars. It certainly criticized the latter but its criticisms either remained implicit or were made in private circles. The triggering event was Saudi Arabia’s invitation to the US to defend its territories against a possible Iraqi attack and to expel Iraq from Kuwait. Within six months, half a million American soldiers were stationed in the territories of Saudi Arabia. Even though the Saudi regime secured the support of high-ranking Wahhabi religious scholars, it could not escape the criticism raised both abroad and at home.

After Iraq was driven out of Kuwait in the Gulf war, the Sahwa movement members wrote a letter and memorandum which demanded\[31\]:

total Islamisation in Saudi Arabia, from laws to the legal system, from the economy to international relations. As such, they reflected the Brotherhood’s view of Islam as a total religion. Both documents were critical of the place accorded to religion in the political system of Saudi Arabia.

The Kingdom’s reliance on the US was just one problem. For example, religious scholars had a minimal role in the running of the state and had no independence from the regime. Second, not all the laws of Saudi Arabia conformed to the Islamic law, which should have regulated all sorts of relations, individual, societal and international. Accordingly, the country had a dual court system, religious and secular, which needed to be corrected. Both documents also asked for a more Islamic foreign policy\[32\]:

Saudi Arabia had ‘to support all Islamic states and groups and to cut off the kingdom’s relations with states hostile to Islam’, such as the US.

The Saudi Monarchy clamped down severely and with an iron fist with the help of their native religious and Wahhabi Salafist scholarship. They increased the religiousness of the laws and also ensured that the members of the Sahwa movement are marginalized completely.

The 9/11 incident and the killing of American soldiers in 1995 and ’96 on Saudi land by Al Qaeda were also a big stain on the reputation of the Saudi monarchy. Most of the 9/11 terrorists were also Saudis. These attacks were now being blamed on the Wahhabi sect of Salafism, which was detrimental to the Saudi State, as Wahhabism is the foundational cement of the State, which ensures its existence, vitality, and longevity. To counter this, the Saudis attacked the Muslim Brotherhood as a radical organization that sowed this poison within the Saudi public, and not Wahhabism. They also took the help of Sahwa movement leaders by rehabilitating them and taking their help against the Muslim Brotherhood\[33\].

### Qatar

Qatar had and has a good relationship with the even though even they were modernizing at a more rapid pace than the Saudis and equal to that of the UAE. This is due to the Muslim Brotherhood being stamped out of the neighboring gulf States of Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and the UAE. The other reason was that Qatar was wanting to gain a place for itself within the Gulf States and the final reason was that the relatively less literate Qataris helped the Muslim Brotherhood members gain employment within organs of the State. The case of Yusuf al-Qaradawi is again illustrative. He was given a precious opportunity when the state established the Al Jazeera network in 1995. Al-Qaradawi became a regular guest on a weekly religious program, al-Shariah wa’l-Hayat \[Shariah and the Life\], in which he expressed his views and issued his religious opinions, fatwas, on a variety of matters, including those most of interest to Arabs\[34\]. Qatar had become a mini ‘Ikhwanistan’ according to Andrew Hammond.

## Conclusion

The divergence between Saudi Arabia and Qatar took place due to primarily three reasons:

- Demography

- Literacy levels

- State Building and Origins

The Saudi State, as a Wahhabi nation, had a native class of religious scholarship and a big enough population with enough literacy to run the Saudi institutions of State, while Qatar as a Wahhabi nation did not have enough population, a nonexistent native scholarship and not enough literate class of people to run their institutions, which made them more dependent on foreign Arab nationals. They would either be Pan Arab republican socialists or Muslim brotherhood affiliated Islamists, and the Qatari regime chose the latter over the former, given it was a monarchy. The actions in the 1990s initiated against the Brotherhood ensured their dependence on the Qatari State, which gave them immunity against the Arab Spring, initiated by the brotherhood. This symbiotic relationship between the Qataris and the Brotherhood has led to the quagmire and hostility of the Gulf States to the Qatari State and has made the Qatari State a broader regional and international security threat.
