Dharma is the all encompassing aspect of Indian(read Hindu) thought that influences, or we may even say governs, all aspects of life. Be it means of livelihood, administration, morality or any other field. Education is no exception. The shastras have espoused a system of education the aim of which “***is chitta-vriddhi-nirodha, the inhibition of those activities of the mind by which it gets connected with the world of matter or objects.”*** (RK Mukherjee).

He further comments,

> “Of all the peoples of the world the Hindu is the most impressed and
> affected but he fact of death as a central fact of life. He can not
> get away from the fact that while Man proposes, God disposes.
> Therefore he feels he can not take life seriously and scheme for it,
> without the knowledge of the whole scheme of creation. He takes the
> biological vital process in the context of the total life situation,
> comprising of the inner self into the depts of which he can descend by
> means of contemplation, the ideal self that he can discover through
> intellection and the social self into the laws of which tradition
> initiates him. Thus he devotes himself into a study of the fundamental
> truths of life and does not care for half truths and intermediate
> truth. His one aim in life is to solve the problem of death by
> achieving a knowledge of the whole truth of which life and death are
> phases. He perceives that it is the individual that dies, and not the
> whole or the absolute. Thus the individual must merge himself in the
> universal to escape from the sense of change, decay and dissolution.
> The absolute is not subject to change. Individuation is death, a lapse
> from the knowledge, and this has to be stopped.”

The great scholar and visionary Shri Ram Swarup also observed the same and put it very succinctly:

To see and hear and desire better, purer, finer and deeper is the first aim of Indian education. According to Plato, there is a higher soul and a lower soul. The process of education consists in bringing the lower mind increasingly under the control of the higher. The ancient Indian **education helped to conquer what are called the vikshepas of the mind,** like inattention, distraction, confusion, perplexity, lassitude,restlessness.

The Hindu seers also believed that fellow-men are brothers, that the world and nature around are friendly. Therefore, they also founded their education on this vision. They taught for happiness, for integrity, for service. They **taught harmony with oneself, harmony with our neighbours, and fellowmen, harmony with our environment.**

The life of student was a consecrated one, indicating that the attainment of truth being possible only through the path of unwavering devotion to the guru and a sense of duty towards the ancestors. Proper habits like waking up in the brahma-muhuruta, regular nityakarma etc were encouraged so that the student would benefit them throughout his life. The daily activities were carefully planned and scheduled by the ācharyas to ensure that the students built excellent habits and mastered their subjects throughly. Students observed strict celibacy, were required to worship gods daily, to conduct yajñas, to abstain from activities (that would reduce spiritual merit) like meat eating , too much focus on external appearance, causing hurting other creatures, being in contact of women, gambling, lying, engaging in useless disputes(Manu 2.177-9). They were taught to control their sensual desires, anger, covetousness and other vices.

The system emphasised the development of noble character. As writes AS Altekar,

> “The tree of education must flower in wisdom as well as in virtue, in
> knowledge as well as in manners.”

By inculcating self-restraint, self respect it endowed the student with a powerful personality. All this was done to make the student worthy enough to carry forward the rich legacy of his ancestors and ensure that the sacred fire of Vedic Dharma is never extinguished. The rules and guidelines around svādhyaya ensured that a portion of what was learnt to be revised daily, even in later stages of life. This was necessary because the knowledge taught by the guru was not mere rote leaning. Instead it had to be imbibed and lived to experience it.

While the default choice of study was dependent upon the family tradition, it is however notable that the fields of study were diverse, involving logic, science, philosophy(darshana), martial arts, medicine, itihasa etc which attracted exceptional talent.

The system of three **ṛṇas**(debts) laid further emphasis on the learning, preservation and transmission of knowledge. The guru’s role was the most important, so much that he was elevated to the posting of being equivalent to god. Hindu perspective on education has always sought development of an individual on spiritual and physical levels, both being in tandem with each other.

The Gurukula (guru’s family) was the prime institution of education. Students would stay in the teacher’s āśrama where they would learn from their guru the ways of life in a multi-faceted way. The close association with elderly scholars induced the junior students to imitate their example, and identify himself with them.

Elucidating the importance of gurukulas vis-a-vis being trained at one’s own house, the Mahabharata says: (13.36.15, KMG translation)

> Undergoing the discomforts of a residence in the abode of his
> preceptor, the Brahmana should study the Vedas or at least the
> Pranava. Divesting himself of wrath and renouncing earthly
> attachments, he should become a Yati, viewing all things and all
> creatures with equal eyes. If remaining in the abode of his sire he
> masters all the Vedas and acquiring great knowledge attains to a
> position that should command respect people still condemn him as
> untravelled or home keeping. Like a snake swallowing mice, the earth
> swallows up these two, *viz*., a king that is unwilling to fight and a
> Brahmana that is unwilling to leave home for acquiring knowledge.

The thematic presence of **gurukulas** in forests was one of the the finest spectacles of India, however they were also present in villages, sometimes even in cities that were centres of learning. As seen in in the above quote from MBh, the ones situated in far and isolated lands were considered somewhat superior due to their perception of being away from worldly chaos.

(Girls being sent outside for education was an exceptional case, that too only in affluent families. Generally they were tutored at home by their parents and elders.)

The question of ‘nature vs nurture’ (that do children have innate qualities or they depend on the training provided) is of great interest in our study.

The Hindu system was more focused on nurturing and training subject experts. Special care was to be taken to make the students have sharp memory, so that they could preserve and transmit vedic knowledge orally.

While some hints favouring the ‘nurture’ view (that training is the single influence) can be found (Sathapatha Brahmana 10.4.1.10, for example), there is significant weight on the ‘nature’ side of the question; owing to the fact that karma and rebirth are the central founding principles of Hindu culture, and hence education. The view that the capacities of a person are influenced to a significant extent by the conditions of his birth (wealth, lineage etc). To put it concisely, Hindu education system considered education not as an act of giving sight to the blind eye but to turn the eye to the light. Often a balance of both approaches was made for all practical purposes. Example can be the initiation of Satyakāma Jābāla by maharishi Gautama on the sheer merit of him speaking the truth without fear(see Chandogya Upanishad, 4.4.5).

The varṅa-jāti system had a great influence on the education system. Brahmins’ education was primarily religious. However, a significant portion of them would devote to learning disciplines like astronomy, mathematics and medicine. Significant contributions were made in these subjects, some of which are held in high respect even today. In the Mahabharata and Ramayana, we find that kshatriyas also were well versed in vedic religious knowledge. They were taught governance, policy and martial arts additionally. The eligibility of vaishyas varied from time to time. For women and shudras religious knowledge was transmitted through the puranas, epics and other literature. In shudras especially, the transmission and preservation of vocational skills like trade, carpentry, pottery, smithery etc were taught by father to son. The profession of teaching was almost always reserved for brahmins, with exceptions being made for certain disciplines or exceptional talent. No special training or courses were required for becoming a teacher. One could simply complete own education properly and then take up the work of teaching students in a relevant discipline.

To conclude, education was partially rigid (religious study, Vedas etc) and partially flexible (martial arts, crafts, trades etc).

Special was the relationship between the guru and the sishya, which was the central aspect of the Hindu system. In this regard, we turn to Manusmriti where the process of education has being called the ‘second birth’ and the teacher, the ‘father’.

> तत्र यद् ब्रह्मजन्मास्य मौञ्जीबन्धनचिह्नितम् ।   
> तत्रास्य माता सावित्री पिता त्वाचार्य उच्यते ॥ १७० ॥
>
> Among these, at that Brahmic birth which is. marked by the tying of
> the girdle, the Sāvitrī has been declared to be his “Mother,” and the
> Teacher his “Father.”—(2.170)

The guru was elevated to a status that was of being equal to gods. Apastamba says (1.2.6.13), “

> One shall approach his guru with the same reverence as a deity,
> without telling idle stories, attentive and listening eagerly to his
> words.”

The students were advised to “do what is helpful to the teacher”, “always put for their exertion to study”, “stand with folded hands before him”, “remain well behaved and sit facing the teacher”, “rise before him and go to sleep after him”, “not converse with the teacher while lying down, eating, sitting or face turned away”, “not pronounce his name or mimic his ways” etc. (Manusmriti 2.190 onwards). All these injunctions indicate the high place of the guru in the educational system and the society (स्वदेशे पूज्यते राजा विद्वान सर्वत्र पूज्यते - king is respected in the kingdom alone but a scholar, everywhere).

Similar is the view of the Buddha in the Vinaya Pitika (Mahavogga, 1.32):

> (The Buddha said..) “'The âkariya, O Bhikkhus, ought to consider the
> antevâsika (i.e. disciple) as a son; the antevâsika ought to consider
> the âkariya as a father. Thus these two, united by mutual reverence,
> confidence, and communion of life, will progress, advance, and reach a
> high stage in this doctrine and discipline.”

Since the teacher was seen with such high regard, therefore he was expected to have the ideal conduct which the students would emulate. Patience, impartiality, pious lifestyle in accordance with the shastras, excellent character, being proficient in the subject were the important qualities expected in a teacher.

Education was available to students irrespective of fee considerations. The honorarium given by students (the guru dakṣiṇā) would be dependent upon the financial status of the guardians. In his works AS Altekar has observed that historically, sometimes students would compensate the lack of money by manual work in helping the teacher.

As said Ram Swarup,

“the old educational system was economical and democratic. It was open to all irrespective of caste or creed. Pupils belonging to widely different conditions shared a common life under a common teacher. Princes rubbed shoulders with plebians.”

Another amazing feature of the gurukula system was the begging of alms by the students being elevated as a sacred duty. It is glorified in an hymn from the Atharvaveda as:

> “The Brahmachāri first of all brought hither this ample earth as alms,
> and heaven above it.” (11.5.9)

Manusmriti 2.188 declares “the subsistence of a student on begged food is to be equal (in merit) to fasting.” Alms were to be taken only from the “houses of men who are not deficient in (the knowledge of) the Veda and in (performing) sacrifices, and who are famous for (following their lawful) occupations.” (Manu 2.183) This was meant teach the students humility and gratitude, while also making them closely acquainted of the society whose heritage they would preserve. Begging was elevated to a duty for the brahmacharin, and giving alms a responsibility for the householder.

It must be noted here that despite the austere lifestyle prescribed by the seers, they also stressed on the importance of physical strength and fitness (शरीरमाद्यं खलु धर्मसाधनम् - this body verily, is the instrument of dharma). Students’ health and proper nutrition were taken care of.

Refractory students were generally corrected by imposing fasts, banishing them temporarily etc. Manu 2.159 onwards endorses gentle persuasion, sweet and soft words to keep pupils in discipline but later permits mild physical punishment.

We have now had a brief overview of the ideals, practices, roles and ways of Indian/Hindu education systems. Here is a traditional classification of the fields-disciplines that were prominent:-

Four Vedas-

1.  Ṛgveda

2.  Sāmaveda

3.  Yajurveda

4.  Atharvaveda

Six Aṅgas-

1.  Śikṣā : the discipline of pronunciation

2.  Chandas : the prosody

3.  Vyākraṇa : grammar

4.  Nirukta : philology, etmyology

5.  Kalpa : ritualism

6.  Jyotiṣa : astronomy, time keeping

Daśa granthas- one’s own veda, it’s brāhmaṅa, ārayaṅaka, 4 nighaṅṭu and the above six aṅgas.

Fourteen vidyas- the four vedas, six aṅgas, dharmaśāstras, purāṅas, mīmānsā and tarka

Eighteen śilpas-

1.  Vocal music

2.  Instrumental music

3.  Dancing

4.  Painting

5.  Mathematics

6.  Accountancy

7.  Engineering

8.  Sculpture

9.  Agriculture

10. Cattle-breeding

11. Commerce

12. Medicine

13. Conveyancing and Law

14. Administrative training

15. Archery and military art

16. Magic

17. Snake charming and poison antidotes

18. The Art of finding hidden treasures

(This list is from the appendix of a book by AS Altekar mentioned in the sources section)

The 64 kalās included singing, music, dancing, spinning, carpentry, architecture, metallurgy, conversation, making toys and similar skills and arts.

In the end, I thankyou for your patient reading. This was the first part of a series of articles that will span into several parts as the topic is of great value and expansive in nature. The importance of this article and the need of understanding the Dharmic perspective of education is extremely important in an era where we are facing a crisis in terms of India’s academic capabilities. We have harsh questions staring at us that are of critical importance in the modern age. India’s own education systems are now becoming a thing of the past. What we have now are resource-scarce, poorly implemented imitation of the western model. The disastrous nature of this tragedy in education in india can be understood by the fact that we stand nowhere when compared to the international competition. The sad state of affairs is furthered by the rot in our current academia which is plagued by false narratives that cater ideological interests of certain groups at the expense of scholarly honesty and India’s future. Therefore, through this article I make a comprehensive attempt to discuss the problems with our approach to and application of education systems.

My aims to write this article series are:

1\. Presenting the Dharmic perspective on the objectives, methods and the utility of education.

2\. Taking a look at the state of education in India through the ages.

3\. Presenting a critique of the present western education system.
