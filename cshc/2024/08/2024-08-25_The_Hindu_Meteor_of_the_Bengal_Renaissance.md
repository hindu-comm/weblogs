मातरम्।।

Being one of those ubiquitous things of general knowledge which we learn at school, we all have heard of this gentleman sometime or the other - Bankim Chandra Chattopadhyay. Yes, o dear reader?

Bankim is most well-known today as the writer of India's national song - 'Vande Mataram', and as a litterateur. But what else is he known for? Not much else, in the popular imagination, unfortunately; but let us explore more!

It is regrettable that the great man is not known for much beyond what we have just stated; but in truth, Bankim was so much more and above. And today, he is even more relevant in an age when the Hindu is becoming aware of his civilization's greatness, and desires to resurrect and revitalize the same.

Bankim remains a dormant diamond - one who has the explosive potential to once again rise from the ashes, just as effulgent as in times of yore, like a divine phoenix.

This introductory essay on Bankim, is the premier of a series of essays, in whom we shall investigate the historic and far-reaching role of Bankim Chandra Chatterji in the modern Hindu renaissance.

Bankim Chandra Chattopadhyay

According to Sri Aurobindo **\[1\]**-

Bankim, the greatest of novelists, had the versatility developed to its highest expression. Scholar, poet, essayist, novelist, philosopher, lawyer, critic, official, philologian and religious innovator, — the whole world seemed to be shut up in his single brain.

Bankim Chandra Chattopadhyay appeared in the epic of Indian history at that stage when the sceptre of the Islamic crescent had crumbled, and was becoming largely inconsequential. The reason? The British East India Company's military brush which had drenched the Indian map in red, and had established a new authority.

This new rule was as foreign as that of the predecessor, but provided considerable relaxation to the Hindu - whose religion and culture was now in a much better position under the English than under the Islamic colonizer.

English rule led to English education - and Bengal was the first province to drink from that formidable fountain. This produced a new intellectual stream among Hindus - patriotic and sympathetic to Indian religion and culture, but who had their view of things through Western glasses.

Foremost among them, most famously, was Raja Rammohun Roy. Others included Dwarkanath Tagore, Ishwarchandra Vidyasagar, Keshab Chandra Sen and others. But simultaneously, a stream arose which was more Indianized and less Westernized - which included Swami Dayananda Saraswati, Ramakrishna Paramahamsa, and the Theosophical Society.

Let us designate the first as Hindu reformist, and the latter as Hindu revivalist. Bankim takes his worthy position as a formidable giant in the Hindu revivalist stream.

Bankim Chandra was born on June 26, in 1838, at his native place of Kanthalpara. **\[2\]** His upbringing and family background played a vital role in the trajectory of his life.

His religious and spiritual convictions were rooted in those early experiences. His father, Jadav Chandra, had been resurrected (so it seems) at teenage by the potency of a sannyasi, **\[3\]** and the mystical well at Kanthalpara formed the inspiration of one of the scenes of Bankim's novels - 'Krishnakanter Will'. His ancestral diety was Shri Radhavallabh, and Bankim remained a staunch Vaishnava and devotee of Shri Krishna till his last breath.

Bankim's family was a family of Deputy Magistrates - his father, and all his brothers were Deputy Magistrates, and so was he to become one in the future. They might have pushed further, but practically, Deputy Magistrate was the highest position available to Indians in that age.

Bankim started his English education at Midnapore, in 1844. Highly gifted from a young age, he used to read voraciously, and spend all his time in the school library - only reading his prescribed books at the eleventh hour - and then achieving greatly high marks. **\[4\]** In 1856, at Hooghly College, Bankim first studied Sanskrit.

He was soon appointed as Deputy Collector, in 1858. An extremely able and independent administrator; he dealt with wicked indigo planters, handled various cases, and provided good relief in cyclone ravaged areas. He retired because of failing health in 1891, four years prior to his demise at a relatively young age of 56, on April 8, 1894.

But Bankim was foremostly a man of ideas, and not necessarily a man of action. His ideas, his thoughts, his words - that is the true treasure that Bankim created.

The reader may be provided a small glimpse into Bankim Chandra's prolific writings, here.

Bankim is famous as a novelist - specifically as the man who penned 'Vande Mataram', and 'Anandamath' - the novel which contains that dazzling ballad of patriotism. But he wrote many other works of importance.

Firstly, he wrote many more historical novels, including the celebrated 'Raj Sinha', based on the historical but romantic tale of Maharana Raj Sinha of Udaipur who defied the might of Aurangzeb. His other such works include Durgeshnandini, Mrinalini, Chandrasekhar, Sita Ram and Devi Chaudharani. His other novels, which are not historical ones; are Kapalakundala, Bish Briksha, Krishnakanter Will, Rajani and Indira. He also wrote a rather peculiar and unusual work called Kamalakanter Daptar, which follows the life of Kamalakanta - a strange man who lives on opium and borrowed milk, and records his musings on sociology, philosophy, politics and patriotism.

Secondly, Bankim also established the high quality Bengali journal called 'Bangadarshan', in 1872. The purpose of the journal was to make it at once a mouthpiece of the intelligentsia, and a carrier of knowledge to the people, in general; through their mother tongue. **\[5\]** Its impact on the social and intellectual field cannot be overestimated.

Nationalist leader Bipin Chandra Pal remarked **\[6\]** -

Bangadarshan did for the contemporary Bengali thought what the French Encyclopaedists did for the 18th century European thought and French literature.

Bankim wrote conscientiously and competently many insightful essays on a variety of topics - history, sociology, religion, archaeology, literature and even science. He also had many works in process and preparation at the hour of his death, and those works never saw the light; one cannot but pronounce it a major loss to the Indian and Hindu people.

Towards the last quarter of his life, Bankim turned his mind increasingly away from art and fiction, and deemed the study and investigation of religious truths a more important endeavour.

So describes Sri Aurobindo **\[7\]** -

But now in that valley of the shadow of death his soul longed for the sustaining air of religion. More and more the philosophic bias made its way into his later novels, until at last the thinker in him proved too strong for the artist.

At this crucial time of his life, Bankim played the role of a calm and poised paladin of the Hindu faith, so to speak, and wielded his pen like a deadly sabre against all those who dared slander the sacred religion of the Aryans.

This form of Bankim gave its first prominent expression in the Hastie controversy. A Christian reverend, Hastie, had published public letters slandering Hinduism, especially on account of "idolatry", and calling upon Hindus to accept Christianity. Bankim gave blow for blow, and gave a crushing counter to Hastie's arguments. **\[8\]**

Further, Bankim wrote two important books - Krishnacharitra ('Life of Shri Krishna') and Dharmatattva-Anushilan ('The Principles of Dharma'). He has also left behind an incomplete commentary on the holy Gita. He had planned to pen a commentary on the Vedas as well, but sadly, his premature end took away that chance. **\[9\]**

Demolishing the malicious ramblings of Hinduphobes point by point with unquestionable logic and biting sarcasm, the triumphant Bankim thundered **\[10\]** -

If ever I entertained any doubts that it is possible to be exceedingly eloquent on subjects of which our own conceptions are dim and shadowy, the critics of Hinduism have certainly dispelled them.

Thus and in this manner, we conclude our very short introduction to the life of a most remarkable son of Mother India. He was the man who arose like a fiery meteor in the late 19th century Indian and Bengali landscape - and whose initial flaming spark of the sacred chant of 'Vande Mataram' lit up into a devastating conflagration which, in the coming decades after he had passed away, shook the very foundations of the British empire in India.
