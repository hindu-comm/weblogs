Rao Bahadur Sitaram Keshav Bole

**Sitaram Keshav Bole** (1869–1961) was born into a well-off Bhandari (toddy tapper) caste family in Mumbai. Eventually, he became a follower of Satyashodhak’s ideology. Bole first met B.R. Ambedkar in 1907, when Ambedkar was honored for being the first Mahar to pass matriculation. Between 1921 and 1927, council members of lower castes and Dalits, known as Kulas, united against the landowners, called Khotis, who were upper caste and Muslim. There was a political alliance between the Dalits and the lower castes, symbolized by Rao Bahadur Bole and Dr. Ambedkar. In 1923, Rao Bahadur Bole passed a bill stating that “the untouchable classes be allowed to use all public watering places, wells, and dharmashalas which are built and maintained by the Government or created by statutes, as well as public schools, courts, offices, and dispensaries.” In 1926, even the Mahad City Council endorsed this resolution. However, certain orthodox elements of Hindu society did not allow the Dalits to exercise their rights regarding the use of public water bodies. This led to the famous Mahad Satyagraha, witnessed firsthand by the author’s maternal grandmother on 20th March 1927. During that time, the Hindu Mahasabha considered a Hindu Orthodox body, was not expected to support the satyagraha. However, under the leadership of **Swatantryaveer Vinayak Damodar Savarkar**, who issued the following statement, “Untouchability must be condemned and abolished not only as the need of the hour but also as the command of true religion; not only as a policy or as an act of expediency but also as a matter of justice, not only as a matter of obligation but also as a service to humanity,” the Hindu Mahasabha came out in support of Dalit Hindus and many members of the Hindu Mahasabha supported this movement. Veer Savarkar’s brother, **Dr. Narayan Damodar Savarkar** supported the cause of the Dalits in place of his brother, who still was not allowed to leave Ratnagiri**.** Finally the Mahad Satyagraha achieved legal victory. On 17 March 1937, Justice Wadia upheld the rights of the so-called ‘untouchable’ Hindus to use the water at the Mahad water tank.

**Rao Bahadur Bole** observed these developments, which led him to **join the Hindu Mahasabhain1938**. He noticed that the Pan-Hindu movement appeared to address the aspirations of all Hindus, not just historically privileged caste groups. He never held any animus towards Hinduism or Hindu society as a whole unlike his associates who would like it destroyed, but the lumpen orthodox and regressive elements within them, and when he saw even the most conservative strata of society wanting to change for the better and accommodate historically discriminated groups within them with respect, he joined the Hindutvavadi Hindu Mahasabha and became the chairman of the Pratnik Hindu Mahasabha.

Many, to this day, speculate whether his turn towards Hindutva was genuine or was due to political reasons. British documents show us that his turn was a genuine ideological shift towards Hindutva and there seemed to be a feeling of anguish within the British ranks due to this ideological shift towards the Hindu Mahasabha as his previous ideological affiliations seemed to be with Ambedkarism, which as an ideology could be used by the British to influence certain sections of the Indian populace to have a pro-British stand. Anti-British boycott

British officer R.M. Maxwell was an officer of the Home department who was displeased by the actions of S.K. Bole ji, he showcased his displeasure in the following words, “**I am surprised at Rao Bahadur S.K.Bole having a hand in this resolution. He and the depressed classes whom he represented (as a Govt. nominee, I think) in the Bombay Legislative Council owe every consideration they have received to the British Govt. which has stood between them and the Hindu Mahasabhaites with whom he has now apparently allied himself. His change of attitude is so remarkable that it would be interesting to have the comments of the C.I.O. Bombay on Bole’s real part in this affair**.”

Not only were the British displeased with his ideological shift, but even his previous ideological cohorts seemed to be livid and wrathful due to his change of heart and ideological shift towards **Hindutva** from Ambedkarism as on 10 July 1947, Rao Bahadur Sitaram Keshav Bole, along with the delegation of Hindu activists, met Dr. Ambedkar at the Bombay airport. They demanded that the **Bhagwa Dhwaj** become the national flag of India. Dr Ambedkar accepted their demand and asked them to organize and conduct a people’s movement for the same.

Babasaheb Bole with Dr. Ambedkar

Dr. Ambedkar discussed his ideological shift by mentioning, “**S K Bole spent his life criticizing the Brahmins. But the same Bole has now become a cat under the hands of Barrister Savarkar.**” Although they continued to collaborate on certain causes, Dr. Ambedkar felt a sense of frustration due to his ideological turn. Dr. Ambedkar must have felt that Babasaheb Bole was a **turncoat**.

This shows us that his ideological turn towards Hindutva was genuine as both the British and his past ideological cohorts were startled by his now association with **Hindu Mahasabha and Veer Savarkar**.

I wrote this article to prove two things to two groups of people, both groups of people who seem ideologically opposed but show the same amount of patronization and disdain for the individuals who belong to the OBCs and SCs. The Casteists and Ambedkarite Hindutvavadis, one who think that lower castes are biologically inferior and the latter who believe that the only way to win over the lower castes is to roll over for them and accept some sort of ancestral sin and spend your life rectifying it. Veer Savarkar showed us the path decades ago. You don’t need to roll over to win people from the lower castes, accept your mistake, and make the lower castes accept theirs, as even the lower castes have a bitter history of discriminating against one another. We still have time to rectify this mistake and win over the lower castes, but not in this cowardly way, but the Savarkar way.
