Knowing without knowing: what tune deafness and face blindness have in common

[![](https://i0.wp.com/1.bp.blogspot.com/_MyFAOEj9GKk/TTWqr1v6XDI/AAAAAAAAAIM/As5pHqnK-pQ/s320/tune%2Bdeafness.png?w=640)![](https://i0.wp.com/1.bp.blogspot.com/_MyFAOEj9GKk/TTWqr1v6XDI/AAAAAAAAAIM/As5pHqnK-pQ/s320/tune%2Bdeafness.png?w=640)](https://i0.wp.com/1.bp.blogspot.com/_MyFAOEj9GKk/TTWqr1v6XDI/AAAAAAAAAIM/As5pHqnK-pQ/s1600/tune%2Bdeafness.png)  
I have a [new post](http://www.scientificamerican.com/article.cfm?id=the-neuroscience-of-tone) over on the [Scientific American Mind Matters](http://www.scientificamerican.com/section.cfm?id=mindmatters) website. It describes new research which suggests that tune deafness and [face blindness](https://wiringthebrain.blogspot.com/2010/04/hello-stranger.html) – two examples of conditions known as agnosias, both of which can be genetic – are caused not by a failure of the brain to recognise previously seen faces or detect incongruous musical notes, but a failure to communicate these events to frontal brain regions where conscious awareness is triggered. In essence, your brain knows something but can’t tell you. [Read more…](http://www.scientificamerican.com/article.cfm?id=the-neuroscience-of-tone)
