Meanderings through the virial theorem, globular clusters, galaxies and dark matter

For an introduction, read [this.](https://manasataramgini.wordpress.com/2017/12/21/astronomical-experiments-a-preliminary-look-at-globular-clusters/)

In the mid-eighteen hundreds, even as Hindu civilization was at its lowest point, science in Germania was reaching a high point that would lay the foundations of modern physics. The great Carl Gauss was in his last days, but he was now surrounded by a veritable constellation of bright stars who would form the bedrock of the successor generation that came after him. There was his own student, Bernhard Riemann, the ship-physician Julius Mayer, the polymath Hermann von Helmholtz, Rudolf Clausius and Gustav Kirchhoff, who spanned the undergirdings of modern physics and chemistry, to name some. Of these, the “forgotten” Mayer rather remarkably laid the founding stone of thermodynamics, approaching it via biology, through his observations on what today would be understood as the degree of oxygenation of hemoglobin in venous blood. He thus went on to publish the mechanical equivalent of heat in 1842 – the basis of the first law of thermodynamics. However, facing a hard life with many deaths in his family and no recognition for his glorious discovery, he almost ended up committing suicide by jumping off a building later in life (indeed, the path to modern thermodynamics was beset with both attempts and actual suicides – Ludwig Boltzmann and Paul Ehrenfest thereafter). But, at least as a saving grace, he was rehabilitated in his final years and conferred the highest scientific awards of his age, along with promotion to nobility. The man who overshadowed him was none other than the great von Helmholtz, who provided the first rigorous mathematical formulation of the first law (the law of conservation of energy), a few years after Mayer’s publication, in 1847.

One of the men chiefly responsible for the rehabilitation of Mayer was Clausius, who in 1850 laid the groundwork for the discovery of the second law of thermodynamics, which in the following decades he developed into the principle of entropy (1865). But the story of Clausius did not end there. Five years later, as the Franco-Prussian War broke out, the noted physicist served his kingdom as a member of the medical ambulance corps. Wounded in the conflict, as he lay in recovery, he was awarded the Iron Cross. However, his mind kept working through these trying times and discovered the remarkable virial theorem that was to change the course of our understanding of the universe. If the first law of thermodynamics told us that the energy of a system was conserved, the virial theorem told us how that energy is partitioned between kinetic and potential energy. To us, these momentous events marked the birth of modern physics – the transition from the astronomy-driven phase of Newtonian mechanics to the discovery of the fundamental laws and the ultimate realization of a universe dominated by the mysterious black matter. While Clausius originally proposed the virial theorem in the context of thermodynamics, Lord Rayleigh and Henri Poincaré generalized it, eventually making it applicable to astronomy and cosmology.

We first learnt of the virial theorem in the summer of 1990. Shortly thereafter, we ran into our acquaintance, the vaisya-jyotiṣa, who told us of the excellent telescope he had acquired. With some excitement, he told us that with it, he was able to measure the diameters of a few globular clusters with considerable accuracy. Fresh from our study of the virial theorem, we suggested to him that we use his measurements and some other data to estimate the mass of globular clusters. Below, we provide a simple derivation of the theorem in an astronomical context and how this exercise would play out, followed by some related astronomical meanderings.

[![](https://manasataramgini.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/m5_globular-cluster.jpg?w=640)](https://manasataramgini.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/m5_globular-cluster.jpg)Figure 1. The globular cluster M5 in the constellation of Serpens.

1\. For a system of ![N](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=N&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) bodies with mass ![m_i](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=m_i&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) and position vector ![r_i](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=r_i&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002), the moment of inertia ![I](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=I&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is:

![\displaystyle I = \sum\_{i=1}^{N} m_i \|r_i\|^2](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdisplaystyle+I+%3D+%5Csum_%7Bi%3D1%7D%5E%7BN%7D+m_i+%7Cr_i%7C%5E2&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002)

2\. Calculating the “virial” of the system: Take the time derivative of \\I\\:

![\displaystyle \dfrac{dI}{dt} = 2 \sum\_{i=1}^{N} m_i (r_i \cdot v_i) = 2 \sum\_{i=1}^{N} p_i \cdot r_i](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdisplaystyle+%5Cdfrac%7BdI%7D%7Bdt%7D+%3D+2+%5Csum_%7Bi%3D1%7D%5E%7BN%7D+m_i+%28r_i+%5Ccdot+v_i%29+%3D+2+%5Csum_%7Bi%3D1%7D%5E%7BN%7D+p_i+%5Ccdot+r_i&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002)

For body ![i](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=i&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) in the system ![\tfrac{dr_i}{dt} =v_i](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctfrac%7Bdr_i%7D%7Bdt%7D+%3Dv_i&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002), its velocity;  
The momentum of a body ![i](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=i&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) in the system is ![p_i = m_i \cdot v_i](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=p_i+%3D+m_i+%5Ccdot+v_i&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) and ![\sum r_i \cdot p_i](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Csum+r_i+%5Ccdot+p_i&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is the “Virial” of the system.

3\. The dynamics of this system is determined by its second derivative: taking the second derivative with respect to time gives:

![\displaystyle \dfrac{d^2I}{dt^2} = 2 \sum\_{i=1}^{N} m_i (v_i \cdot v_i + r_i \cdot a_i)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdisplaystyle+%5Cdfrac%7Bd%5E2I%7D%7Bdt%5E2%7D+%3D+2+%5Csum_%7Bi%3D1%7D%5E%7BN%7D+m_i+%28v_i+%5Ccdot+v_i+%2B+r_i+%5Ccdot+a_i%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002)

In the above, we have written the acceleration of a body ![i](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=i&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) in the system as ![\tfrac{dv_i}{dt} = a_i](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctfrac%7Bdv_i%7D%7Bdt%7D+%3D+a_i&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002)  
The Kinetic energy of a body ![i](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=i&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) in the system is ![K= \tfrac{1}{2}m_i v_i^2](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=K%3D+%5Ctfrac%7B1%7D%7B2%7Dm_i+v_i%5E2&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002);  
The force acting on a body ![i](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=i&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) in the system is ![F_i=m_i \cdot a_i](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=F_i%3Dm_i+%5Ccdot+a_i&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002)

Plugging these into the above, we get:

![\displaystyle\dfrac{1}{2} \dfrac{d^2I}{dt^2} = 2K + \sum\_{i=1}^{N} (F_i \cdot r_i)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdisplaystyle%5Cdfrac%7B1%7D%7B2%7D+%5Cdfrac%7Bd%5E2I%7D%7Bdt%5E2%7D+%3D+2K+%2B+%5Csum_%7Bi%3D1%7D%5E%7BN%7D+%28F_i+%5Ccdot+r_i%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002)

4\. For a gravitationally-bound astronomical body, the force ![F_i](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=F_i&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is the gravitational pull from all other bodies. For the bodies in the system, the sum of the products of the respective forces and position vectors is the gravitational potential energy ![U](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=U&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002):

![\displaystyle \sum\_{i=1}^{N} (F_i \cdot r_i) = U](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdisplaystyle+%5Csum_%7Bi%3D1%7D%5E%7BN%7D+%28F_i+%5Ccdot+r_i%29+%3D+U&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002), thus: ![\dfrac{1}{2} \dfrac{d^2I}{dt^2} = 2K + U](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdfrac%7B1%7D%7B2%7D+%5Cdfrac%7Bd%5E2I%7D%7Bdt%5E2%7D+%3D+2K+%2B+U&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002)

5\. An astronomical system in steady state (like an isolated galaxy or an old globular cluster) is not notably expanding or contracting. Therefore, the average value of the second derivative of its moment of inertia over a prolonged period of time is zero:

![\left\langle \dfrac{d^2I}{dt^2} \right\rangle = 0](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cleft%5Clangle+%5Cdfrac%7Bd%5E2I%7D%7Bdt%5E2%7D+%5Cright%5Crangle+%3D+0&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002)

Thus, we get the virial theorem:  
![\mathbf{2K + U = 0}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cmathbf%7B2K+%2B+U+%3D+0%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002)

**The relationship between mass and radius of a globular cluster**  
Using the virial theorem, one can derive a relationship between the total mass and the radius of a globular cluster.

***Kinetic energy:***  
![K = \dfrac{1}{2} M \sigma^2](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=K+%3D+%5Cdfrac%7B1%7D%7B2%7D+M+%5Csigma%5E2&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002),

Where ![M](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is the total mass of the globular cluster; ![\sigma](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Csigma&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is the velocity dispersion of the stars relative to the center. It can be inferred from the speed distribution that can be approximated by a Maxwellian distribution:

![f(v) = 4\pi n \left(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\right)^{3/2} v^2 e^{\left(-\dfrac{v^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=f%28v%29+%3D+4%5Cpi+n+%5Cleft%28%5Cdfrac%7B1%7D%7B%5Csqrt%7B2%5Cpi%5Csigma%5E2%7D%7D%5Cright%29%5E%7B3%2F2%7D+v%5E2+e%5E%7B%5Cleft%28-%5Cdfrac%7Bv%5E2%7D%7B2%5Csigma%5E2%7D%5Cright%29%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002),  
where ![f(v)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=f%28v%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is the probability density for speed ![v](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=v&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) and ![n](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=n&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is the number density of stars.

If the Root Mean Squared speed of this distribution is ![V\_{RMS}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=V_%7BRMS%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002), then,  
![\sigma = \dfrac{V\_{RMS}}{\sqrt{3}}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Csigma+%3D+%5Cdfrac%7BV_%7BRMS%7D%7D%7B%5Csqrt%7B3%7D%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002)  
The ![\sqrt{3}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Csqrt%7B3%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) comes from ![\sigma](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Csigma&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) being the one-dimensional RMS speed in a single direction, and the distribution of speed is isotropic in all directions ![V_x, V_y, V_z](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=V_x%2C+V_y%2C+V_z&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002), giving the factor of 3. Observations with modern telescopes suggest that it is in the range of 5-20 km/s relative to the cluster center.

***Potential energy:***  
![U = -\alpha \dfrac{G M^2}{R_v}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=U+%3D+-%5Calpha+%5Cdfrac%7BG+M%5E2%7D%7BR_v%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002),  
where ![\alpha](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Calpha&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is a numerical constant — the structure constant that tells us how concentrated the mass is toward the center of a globular cluster (around 0.5 for typical clusters); ![R_v](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=R_v&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is the radius of the cluster (virial radius) of the cluster; ![G](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=G&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) the gravitational constant

***Virial theorem*** (Assuming the globular cluster to be a stable, self-gravitating system in equilibrium):  
![2K =
-U](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=2K+%3D+-U&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002)

![\therefore 2 \left( \dfrac{1}{2} M \sigma^2 \right) = \alpha \dfrac{G M^2}{R_v}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctherefore+2+%5Cleft%28+%5Cdfrac%7B1%7D%7B2%7D+M+%5Csigma%5E2+%5Cright%29+%3D+%5Calpha+%5Cdfrac%7BG+M%5E2%7D%7BR_v%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002)

![M \sigma^2 = \alpha \dfrac{G M^2}{R_v}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M+%5Csigma%5E2+%3D+%5Calpha+%5Cdfrac%7BG+M%5E2%7D%7BR_v%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002)

![R_v = \dfrac{\alpha G M}{\sigma^2}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=R_v+%3D+%5Cdfrac%7B%5Calpha+G+M%7D%7B%5Csigma%5E2%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002)

If ![R_v, \sigma, \alpha](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=R_v%2C+%5Csigma%2C+%5Calpha&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) can be inferred using astronomical observations, we can compute the mass of a globular cluster contained within its virial radius. It turns out a typical globular cluster has ![M \approx 5 \times 10^5 \\ M\_{\odot}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M+%5Capprox+5+%5Ctimes+10%5E5+%5C%2C+M_%7B%5Codot%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002), where ![M\_{\odot}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M_%7B%5Codot%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is solar mass.

[![](https://manasataramgini.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/globular_cluster_virial.jpg?w=640)](https://manasataramgini.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/globular_cluster_virial.jpg)Figure 2. Deriving the virial radius-mass relationship.

**Relationship between galactic mass and matter in globular clusters**  
There is a remarkable relationship between total galactic mass ![M_h](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M_h&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) and the matter contained in globular clusters. ![M_h = M_b + M_d](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M_h+%3D+M_b+%2B+M_d&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002), where ![M_b](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M_b&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is the total mass in baryonic matter (remember this is not just baryons but includes electrons, though not neutrinos. However, the baryons proper make up 99.95% of this mass; hence, baryonic) and ![M_d](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M_d&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is the total mass in Dark Matter. Then we have:  
![\eta = \dfrac{N\_{GC}}{M_h}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ceta+%3D+%5Cdfrac%7BN_%7BGC%7D%7D%7BM_h%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002),

![N\_{GC}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=N_%7BGC%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is the number of globular clusters in a galaxy. Strikingly, it turns out that ![\eta](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ceta&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is nearly constant.

As we can telescopically count the number of globular clusters and infer their mass from the virial radius as above, we get the total mass of the globular clusters in a galaxy. Its ratio to the total galactic mass is a constant:

![\dfrac{M\_{GC}}{M_h} \approx 5 \times 10^{-5}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdfrac%7BM_%7BGC%7D%7D%7BM_h%7D+%5Capprox+5+%5Ctimes+10%5E%7B-5%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002)

Remarkably, this relationship holds over five orders of magnitude, from tiny dwarf galaxies with just a few clusters to massive galaxy clusters with tens of thousands of them. Thus, the count of globular clusters allows us to estimate the total mass of a galaxy. Interestingly, while globular clusters almost entirely lack Dark Matter. If ![\Upsilon](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5CUpsilon&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) denotes the ratio of “light” (stellar matter) to mass, then for a typical globular cluster ![\Upsilon \approx 1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5CUpsilon+%5Capprox+1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002). However, for the rest of the galaxy ![\Upsilon \approx 10 \cdots 100](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5CUpsilon+%5Capprox+10+%5Ccdots+100&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002), i.e., the majority of its mass is in the halo in the form of Dark Matter.

**Relationship of number of stars and Dark Matter in a galaxy**  
The above leads us to ask whether there is a relationship between the number of stars or total stellar matter and dark matter in a galaxy. Unlike the tight ![\tfrac{M\_{GC}}{M_h}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctfrac%7BM_%7BGC%7D%7D%7BM_h%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) relationship, that between stellar matter and Dark Matter is not a clean linear relationship. Instead, we have Moster et al.’s formula from a little over a decade ago:

![{M\_\*} = \dfrac{2N \cdot M_h}{\left( \frac{M_h}{M_1} \right)^{-\beta} + \left( \frac{M_h}{M_1} \right)^{\gamma}}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%7BM_%2A%7D+%3D+%5Cdfrac%7B2N+%5Ccdot+M_h%7D%7B%5Cleft%28+%5Cfrac%7BM_h%7D%7BM_1%7D+%5Cright%29%5E%7B-%5Cbeta%7D+%2B+%5Cleft%28+%5Cfrac%7BM_h%7D%7BM_1%7D+%5Cright%29%5E%7B%5Cgamma%7D%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002)

Here,  
![M\_\*](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M_%2A&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is the total stellar mass, the mass of the galaxy in stars.  
![M_h](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M_h&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is the total mass of the galaxy, including the dark matter halo.  
![M_1](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M_1&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is a parameter, the specific value of ![M_h](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M_h&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) where star-formation efficiency is highest ![\approx 10^{12} M\_{\odot}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Capprox+10%5E%7B12%7D+M_%7B%5Codot%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) (solar masses).  
![\beta](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cbeta&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is a parameter capturing the situation in low-mass galaxies where supernovae blow away the gas to suppress star growth.  
![\gamma](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cgamma&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is a parameter capturing the situation in high-mass galaxies where superheated gas and black holes suppress star growth.  
![N](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=N&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is the maximum fraction of a galaxy’s total mass that can be converted into stars. The process of star formation from the available baryonic matter is inefficient: ![\le 20\\](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cle+20%5C%25&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) of this matter can maximally be converted to stars. In turn, baryonic matter is ![\approx 16\\ M_h](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Capprox+16%5C%25+M_h&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002). Hence, currently, ![N \approx 0.03](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=N+%5Capprox+0.03&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002). However, ![N](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=N&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) changes with time. In the early universe, it was lower than today because the hot gas reduced star-forming efficiency. The middle period was the peak age of ![N](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=N&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) as there was a balance of gas density and temperature. It will drop from there as galaxies run out of cold gas to make stars. We appear to be early in the dropping phase.

[![](https://manasataramgini.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/moster_function.jpg?w=640)](https://manasataramgini.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/moster_function.jpg)Figure 3. A normalized version of the Moster et al. function.

Thus, it captures three basic regimes:  
1. Low ![M_h](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M_h&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) galaxies (Left Side): Here, gravity is too weak; hence, supernovae blow away the galaxy’s gas into space. Thus, enough gas is not left behind to make more stars. Hence, the number of stars stays low compared to the dark matter.

2\. The central hump: Gravity is strong enough to keep gas in the galaxy, but not so strong that the gas becomes too hot to clump together. This allows maximal star formation. The Milky Way lies in this central hump.

3\. High ![M_h](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M_h&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) (Right Side): Gravity is so intense that gas falling in is super-heated to millions of degrees. Additionally, giant black holes in the galactic nucleus pump energy into the galaxy. This makes the gas too hot to collapse into stars again, making it star-poor.

The Moster et al. function can be converted into a formula to give the number of stars in a galaxy ![N\_\*](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=N_%2A&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) as a function of ![M_h](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=M_h&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) by using average stellar mass: ![N\_\* = \tfrac{M\_\*}{\mu\_\*}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=N_%2A+%3D+%5Ctfrac%7BM_%2A%7D%7B%5Cmu_%2A%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002), where ![\mu\_\*](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cmu_%2A&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is the average stellar mass. Due to the dominance of low mass red dwarfs as the most common type of star, the ![\mu \approx 0.4 M\_{\odot}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cmu+%5Capprox+0.4+M_%7B%5Codot%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002). By plugging this into the above, one gets the relationship of the number of stars in a galaxy to its total mass.
