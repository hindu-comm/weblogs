Notes on the Kiñcita tantra and the Guhyakālī tradition

[![](https://manasataramgini.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/guhyakali_rp_sreshtha.jpg?w=640)](https://manasataramgini.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/guhyakali_rp_sreshtha.jpg)

**Prolog**  
In 1994, when we were visiting R1’s house, we saw, for the first time, an actual picture of the deca-zoocephalous Guhyakālī in her father’s devagṛha. We were aware of her mūla-mantra as part of the “Para-Atharvan” tāntrika tradition, but this was the first time her iconography came alive, and we found an instant connection to the deity. R1’s father subsequently revealed to us the following vidhi for the practice of the mūla (oṃ hrīṃ phreṃ guhyakālike phaṭ phreṃ hūṃ namaḥ ॥) with the divine ogdoad and Bhairava:  
One takes a deep in-breath, expanding one’s belly with  
oṃ  
One holds the breath, imagining the following syllables settling into one’s interior:  
hrīṃ phreṃ guhyakālike phaṭ  
One then exhales with the syllables, pushing one’s belly dorsally towards one’s spine:  
phreṃ hūṃ namaḥ ॥  
One performs this cycle a fixed number of times. Thereafter, one offers a triple arghya followed by a prāyaścittārghya with the following mantra:  
phreṃ sāvitryai vidmahe gāyatryai dhīmahi । tan naḥ kālī pracodayāt । svāhā ॥  
Then, one makes water offerings in the directions and the center, thus:  
hrīṁ hūṁ phreṁ + x + śrīpādukāṃ pūjayāmi namaḥ ॥  
x= 1. indra-guhyakāli; 2. agni-guhyakālī; 3. yama-guhyakālī; 4. nairṛtya-guhyakālī; 5. varuṇā-guhyakālī; 6. vāyu-guhyakālī; 7. kūbera-guhyakālī; 8. iśāna-guhyakālī; 9; brahma-guhyakālī; 10. bhairava-guhyakālī.  
He also brought to our notice the multiple tantra-s pertaining to the worship of Guhyakālī.

**The Guhyakālī system**  
The system of Guhyakālī is presented in a rather complete form in the sprawling eponymous khaṇḍa of the even more massive Mahākāla-saṃhitā. While its practice is today mostly seen in the Mithila-Nepal region, there is textual evidence for its once wider prevalence:  
1. The well-known Kashmirian smārta manual for fire rituals (Agni-kārya-paddhati) gives the Guhyakālī mantra as: oṃ aiṃ klīṃ sauḥ oṃ juṃ saḥ aiṃ klīṃ guhyakālike hrīṃ svāhā ॥. Here, it includes the Mṛtyuñjaya mantra of the Netra tantra system – an intersection not seen in the Mithila-Nepal branch of the tradition.  
2. Kṛṣṇānanda Āgamavāgīśa’s provides the mantra-s for her worship in his voluminous tantra compendium, the Bṛhat-Tantrasāra, indicating that she was once worshiped in Bengal.  
3. The surviving Atharvan traditions further south in the subcontinent have some knowledge of the Guhyakālī mantra, even if they lack the detailed iconography and vidhi-s seen in the Mithila-Nepal zone.

We also find evidence for intersection with the other surviving kaula traditions in the regions where the Guhyakālī system flourished:  
1. Not surprisingly, the survival of Paścimāmnāya (the Kubjikā tradition) in Nepal has resulted in some intersection with the Guhyakālī tradition. A relatively early evidence for this intersection is seen in the mention of Guhyakālī in the Kubjikā tantra, and the offering of a bali to her within the 28-fold arcana of Kubjikā indicates the persistence of this connection.  
2. This her worship was incorporated into the Sarvāmnāya-krama of Navamīsiṃha (as seen in the Kula-mukti-kallolinī), the Mahāmātya of Bhūpalendra Malla (around 1695 CE), and Bhāskara Malla.  
3. Her worship is also specified in the relatively recent (~1775 CE) compendium, the Puraścaryārṇava of king Pratāpa-siṃha, alongside several other kaula traditions.

The standard iconography of Guhyakālī depicts her in a deca-zoocephalous form with the heads arranged in four rows with 5, 3, 1, 1 in each. The first pentad has elephant, eagle, human, crocodile and horse heads. The second triad has ape, tiger and boar heads. Above those is a lion head, and above that is the final anthropomorphic divine head. However, there is a 13-headed form specified in the Nirvāṇa-guhyakālī-pūjā manual. These heads of the goddess are worshipped with the mukha-tarpaṇa-mantra-s, which are as below:  
vphreṃ vhkṣmlvryūṃ nirvāṇa-śrī-guhyakāli+ n + tṛpyantu ॥  
n= 1. siṃha-mukha-maṇḍalaṃ; 2. pheru-mukha-maṇḍalaṃ; 3. daiva-mukha-maṇḍalaṃ; 4. śarabha-mukha-maṇḍalaṃ; 5. vyāghra-mukha-maṇḍalaṃ; 6. makara-mukha-maṇḍalaṃ; 7. ṛkṣa-mukha-maṇḍalaṃ; 8. kapi-mukha-maṇḍalaṃ; 9. tārkṣya-mukha-maṇḍalaṃ; 10. varāha-mukha-maṇḍalaṃ; 11. haya-mukha-maṇḍalaṃ; 12. gaja-mukha-maṇḍalaṃ; 13. nara-mukha-maṇḍalaṃ  
In the order of their mention, the heads are of a lion, a jackal, a divine anthropomorph, a śarabha, a tiger, a crocodile, a bear, an ape, an eagle, a boar, a horse, an elephant and a human.

While her consort is typically the Bhairava, sometimes matchingly termed Guhyabhairava, in the Mahākāla-saṃhitā system, he is sometimes presented as a “Bhairavaized” Nṛsiṃha. This is also hinted in her praise from the Hāhārāva-tantra: mahā-nṛsiṃham-ārūḍhāṃ kālasaṃkarṣiṇīṃ numaḥ ॥  
She is described as being mounted on the great Nṛsiṃha (ungrammatical), suggesting that he could be the consort, paralleling the goddesses mounted on their consorts in other kaula systems.

The Kālī-s of the different Kālī-centric systems are often presented as being part of a series (krama) or a polyad (cakra) – e.g., the 12/13 Kālī-s of the Bhānavī-kaulinī system of the Ciñciṇī-mata-sāra-samuccaya. In the Guhyakālī system too, she is presented as a multiplicity of Kālī-s, namely the Pañcaguhyakālī-s who are worshiped at the corners of the pentagram maṇḍala along with the corresponding Guhyabhairava consorts:  
1. Sṛṣti-guhya-bhairava sahita Sṛṣti-guhyakālī  
2. Sthiti-guhya-bhairava-sahita Sthiti-guhyakālī  
3. Saṃhāra-guhya-bhairava-sahita Saṃhāra-guhyakālī  
4. Anākhyā-guhya-bhairava sahita Anākhyā-guhyakālī  
5. Bhāsā-guhya-bhairava-sahita Bhāsā-guhyakālī

**The“Kiñcita-tantra.”**  
While the Guhyakālī system can technically be placed in the Uttarāmnāya (as explicitly stated in the Parātantra) – the Kālī-centric systems (Kālī-kula) – its origin is poorly known. It does not show any specific affinities to the classical Uttarāmnāya of the Bhānavī-kaulinī-based system or the ancient teachings such as the sūtra-s of Vātūlanātha. Among the fragmentary tantric material from Nepal (NGMPP manuscript 4-1867) is a text titled the “Kiñcita-tantra.” It remains unclear if this was its original name. Apart from the Apāṇinīya register of the language, this text is both fragmentary and corrupt. Despite these, we can see in it rather original material with clear connections to the Guhyakālī tradition. We suggest that this is hence an earlier text of this tradition than the Guhyakālī khaṇḍa. It probably lies closer to the root of this tradition, alongside the similar but more comprehensive and better-preserved Hāhārāva-tantra than the more mature material of the Mahākāla-saṃhitā. While a proper treatment of the “Kiñcita-tantra” would require a detailed comparison with the Hāhārāva-tantra, that would require more work. Hence, here we present a few notes on the former without a systematic presentation of the parallels in the Hāhārāva-tantra.

1\. It has a clear association with the cremation ground worship of the Bhairava and his consort (Siddhi)Bhairavī, who is identified with Guhyakālī. The word Bhairava is mentioned 31 times in the surviving fragment along with a Bhairava-śikhā mantra: aiṃ śrīṃ phreṃ bhairavāya haiṃ hūṃ phaṭ namaḥ ॥ The Bhairava teaches, and sanctions the practice of the lore, and the goddess is invoked with the Bhairava as Trailokyamātṛ in an incantation known as the yoginīnāṃ samaya-vidyā (An equivalent Samaya-vidyā is found in the Hāhārāva-tantra and it is also related to a similar Samaya-vidyā found in the Guhyakālī khaṇḍa): bhairava-sahita-trailokya-mātre hreṃ ceṃ cheṃ namaḥ svāhā ॥

2\. Foreshadowing the classic polyzoocephalous form of the goddess Guhyakālī, in a long mantra for the protection of self-marman points and penetration of non-self marman-s, the Mahābhairavī is addressed as: 1. siṃha-śārdūla-vyāghrādinī; 2. mṛgeśvarī; 3. sarva-pakṣīrājā-rājñī; 4. makara-kumbhīra-macchakā (crocodile and gharial); 5. kacchapa-macchādiṣu rājñī (the queen among the tortoises and fishes). A close parallel is seen in the Hāhārāva-tantra.

3\. The surviving fragment mentions the pīṭha-s of Jālandhara, Kāmarūpa, Karṇakubje (=Kānyakubja) and Devīkoṭa but not the others (Oḍḍiyāna is missing!) in the Samaya-vidyā mantra. The same list is found in the equivalent mantra of the Hāhārāva-tantra. In contrast, the Samaya-vidyā of the Mahākāla-saṃhitā lists Oḍḍiyāna. However, the Hāhārāva gives the dhyāna of the five-headed goddess at Oḍḍiyāna further down. This suggests that the omission of Oḍḍiyāna in the Samaya-vidyā of the “Kiñcita-tantra” and the Hāhārāva-tantra is deliberate.

4\. There is a clear connection to the old cult of Camuṇḍā with her companion group of Aṣṭamātṛkā-s. The surviving fragment identifies the goddess of the system with Cāmuṇḍā and gives at least 10 Cāmuṇḍā mantra-s. She is termed Siddhicāmuṇḍā, Siddhikālī and Mahāvajreśvarī. In the Samaya-vidyā, the goddess as Cāmuṇḍā is mentioned as being worshiped at the Jālandhara pīṭha. In one place, the goddess is also identified with Pratyaṅgirā. The Hāhārāva has a whole chapter on Pratyaṅgirā and a parallel set of Cāmuṇḍā vidyā-s.

5\. The Yoginī-s are mentioned at least 20 times in the surviving fragment. Of these, there are multiple references to the conference of human Yoginī-s (yoginī-melāpaṃ karoti ।). However, the Bhairavī/Kālī is invoked in the midst of the cluster of Yoginī-s in multiple mantra-s. One of these is the above-mentioned Sarvayoginīnāṃ samaya-vidyā. Whereas Ḍākinī-s are never mentioned, Śākinī-s are mentioned a few times. In contrast, Ḍākinī are mentioned multiple times in the Hāhārāva and all the other Dhātunāthā-s are also named therein.

6\. A Guhyakālī yantra is mentioned as having 1. A trikoṇā; 2. A pañcāra (pentagram); 3. ṣaṭkoṇa (hexagram); 4. aṣṭāra (8 star); 5. daśapatrāṇi (10-petalled lotus). Same yantra is also specified in the Hāhārāva.

6\. The text specifies an āveśa ritual (attributed to a certain Atharvaṇa-saṃhitā), wherein the deployment of a mantra to the goddess Guhyakālī identified with Siddhikarālī results in goddess possession (evidently of a young woman). The full mantra is better preserved in the Hāhārāva. The possessed individual displays various indications of possession, including signs of the Aṣṭamātṛkā-s: e.g., “mā bhaiṣī vadati । ekāṅgulaṃ tarjanīṃ darśayati । … rudrāṇī aṅguṣṭhena । vaiṣṇavī vāma-madhyamayā । indrāṇī anāmikayā ।…”

7\. Both the Hāhārāva and “Kiñcita-tantra” allude to an Atharvaṇa-saṃhitā and a Kālīkulārṇava-tantra. It is not clear what exactly is meant by the former of these texts; however, it suggests a connection to the “Para-Atharvan” tantric literature. This is notable in light of the knowledge of Guhyakālī among practitioners of the Atharvaveda. Other texts, like the Dakṣiṇakālī-hṛdaya, are attributed to the Kālīkulārṇava. However, it is not clear if the same text is implied here. In any case, this suggests that these were by no means primary tantra-s and potentially presupposed the existence of other Kālīkula texts.

8\. A section from the ki~nchita-tantra. This section is quite corrupt in both the Hāhārāva and “Kiñcita-tantra”. Nevertheless, we present the latter version (corrupt parts in brackets), though a more detailed comparative edition might extract more sense and possibly better readings.  
etad dhi paramaṃ jñānaṃ jarā-maraṇa-nāśanam । guhyam paramakaṃ gopanīyaṃ (nākārambha-stane-pītam) vajra-veṣṭitaṃ । pītaṃ pītaṃ dhārā-varṣantam(?) । stambhane manasīpsitam । hyakāraṃ varuṇā-rūpam । yakārāgama-saṃyutaṃ sarva+ariṣṭa-karī devī (vyaṃgam-āgata-yogataḥ) । uccāṭana+astu hrīṃkāra parama-jñānaṃ (hāhinī) puṃso sama-prabham । āgamāgama-yogena samānayet । āliṅgya-vaśaṃ karoti । noccāṭayati (na-narādhipā\[ḥ\]) ॥  
Indeed, this is the supreme knowledge, the destroyer of old age and death. \[It is\] a secret, most supreme, to be guarded; (drunk from the breast starting with ‘Na’ and ‘A’?) \[it is\] enveloped with Vajra-s. Drunk and drunk \[again\], like a stream being showered. In the act of Paralyzing (stambhane), it is the desired \[object\] of the mind. The syllable Ha(?) of the form of Varuṇa; joined with the arrival of the syllable Ya, the Goddess, the remover of all diseases through the yoga arrives (?). For overthrowing (uccāṭana), there is the Hrīṃ-kāra, the supreme knowledge hāhinī (?), with luster like a masculine. By the yoga of ‘coming and going’ (?), one should lead the \[power?\] together. Having embraced \[it\], one brings \[all\] under control. Neither can one be overthrown nor by the lords of men (kings?).

sarvasvam-apaharati । sva-mukhe cittayati । vadam ānayati vaśam ānayati cittaṃ harati । siṃha-vyāghra-mahiṣa-gaṇḍa-gavaya-tarakṣu-varāha-vāhanā devī cintanīyās tathā śravaṇā kārtikā siṃhinī pūrvā vāhanā sva-guhyātmānaṃ kālikā-yoga-samaṃ dhyāyet । sita-padmāsana-stho ‘pi caritaṃ sapta-dvīpāṃ yogindraiḥ siddhinām-arūpī na-jñāyate ॥  
It takes all away. One meditates \[upon it\] in one’s own mouth. It leads \[subjects\] to speech, it brings subjugation, it steals the mind. The Goddess – whose vehicles are the lion, tiger, buffalo, rhinoceros, gayal, hyena, and boar – is to be meditated upon thus. \[Under the lunar asterism deities\] Shravaṇā, Kārtikā, \[and\] the Lioness-formed one (Siṃhinī), who have the former \[beasts\] as vehicles – one should meditate on one’s own secret-self as equal to the Kālī-yoga. Even if stationed on the white lotus-seat, one roams the seven continents, in formless state of the siddhi-s, unrecognized by the lords of yogin-s.”

yoginī jaṃbhiṇī caiva hāriṇī siddhi-sādhanī । sarvajñā kāmarūpī na-jñāyate । sarva-kāma-svarūpī ca oṣadhi-siddhidā priyā । anena aṣṭa-mahā-siddhiḥ bhakṣaṇe kalpa-jīvinaḥ । vaśīkarāṇādi-sarvāṇy eva kārayet । ye bhaktā te (pajā) aśeṣa tathā koṭi śatāni sarvāṇi kurvanti manasīpsitāni-karmāṇi karoti । ete oṣadhi-siddhā sarva-karma-parā । yoginī pāda-lepena jaṃbhiṇī moha-dāriṇī । ākarṣaṇī devī trailokyam ānayet ॥ tathā siddhi-sādhana-arcanam । sarva-vyādhi-nivāriṇī ॥  
The Yoginī is indeed Jambhiṇī (‘The Devourer’) and Hāriṇī (‘The Stealer’), the instrument of success. She is all-knowing, taking any form at will, yet she is not recognized. She is the essence of all desires and the beloved giver of the success of plant medicine. By the eating of this \[herbal preparation\], one lives an eon with the Eight Great Siddhi-s. One may cause all \[acts\] to be done, such as Subjugation and others. Those who are devotees (corrupt), they perform all \[acts\] without remainder, as well as hundreds of crores \[of acts\]; he performs the works desired by the mind. These (ungrammatical) are the perfected plant medicine \[goddess = the herb goddess Oṣadhi described in the Hāhārāva Tantra\], the \[goddess who is the\] foremost of all ritual actions. By the foot-ointment of the Yoginī, Jambhiṇī, the destroyer of delusion; the ‘Attractor’ Goddess, she can lead the three worlds \[to the practitioner\]. Likewise, there is the worship for the attainment of success. She is the dispeller of all diseases.  
The mantra:  
oṃ hrīṃ bhamba 2 leya 2 sarvajñā kāmarūpinī rasa-rasāyaṇe । añjane mahāpārvatīnāṃ samīpe prāpyate (guṭikaḥ sarve sarvakarmakarāḥ) ॥  
Evidently, the mantra refers to the Yoginī manifesting as rasāyana (chemical preparation) and añjanā (ointment) and leading the votary to obtain a magical pill in the vicinity of a great mountain range that enables the attainment of the above-stated siddhi-s.
