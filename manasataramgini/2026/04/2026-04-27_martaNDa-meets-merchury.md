Mārtāṇḍa meets Mercury

The first reflex of the Mārtāṇḍa myth, viz., the sun as the dead egg, is attested in maṇḍala-10 of the Ṛgveda as part of a famous cosmogonic sūkta (RV 10.72):

aṣṭau putrāso aditer ye jātās tanvas pari \|  
devāṃ upa prait saptabhiḥ parā mārtāṇḍam āsyat \|\|  
Aditi had eight sons,  
who were born from her body.  
With seven, she joined the gods;  
Mārtāṇḍa (the “dead egg”) she cast away.

saptabhiḥ putrair aditir upa prait pūrvyam yugam \|  
prajāyai mṛtyave tvat punar mārtāṇḍam ābharat \|\| RV 10.72.8-9  
With Aditi’s seven sons,  
she joined the ancient generation.  
For progeny as also death  
she once again brought Mārtāṇḍa.

The Ṛgvedic verses are alluded to or quoted in part in all subsequent references to this myth in the Vedic tradition. Exegeses of this myth are provided by multiple Brāhmaṇa passages: three from the Yajurveda (the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa of the Śuklayajurveda; the Taittirīya Āraṇyaka, and the Maitrāyaṇīya Saṃhitā of the Kṛṣṇayajurveda) and one from the Sāmaveda (the Pañcaviṃśa Brāhmaṇa). In summary, the first 7 sons of Aditi (Dakṣa, Aryaman, Mitra, Varuṇa, Bhaga, Aṃśa and Indra) become immortal gods, while the eighth, Mārtāṇḍa (the dead egg; identified with Vivasvān), is the one from which mortals emerge. Indeed, the unusual use of the term mārtāṇḍa in RV 2.38.8 is likely to be a metaphor for a mortal being.

The Mahābhārata preserves a peculiar Brāhmaṇa-like passage that furnishes a further version of the Mārtāṇḍa myth. It shares the motif of Aditi cooking food with the version found in the Maitrāyaṇīya Saṃhitā (see below). However, it peculiarly introduces the archon of the planet Mercury (Budha) into the story, something which is not seen in any of the extant Vedic Brāhmaṇa-s.

aditir vai devānām annam apacad etad bhuktva+asurān haniṣyanti+iti । tatra budho vratacaryā-samāptāv āgacchat । aditiṃ cāvocad bhikṣāṃ dehi+iti । tatra devaiḥ pūrvam etat prāśyaṃ nānyenety aditir bhikṣāṃ nādāt । atha bhikṣāpratyākhyāna-ruṣitena budhena brahmabhūtena vivasvato dvitīye janmany aṇḍa-saṃjñitasyāṇḍaṃ māritam adityāḥ । sa mārtaṇḍo vivasvān abhavac chrāddhadevaḥ । aditiḥ śaptā aditer udare bhaviṣyati vyathā ॥ Mbh 12.329.44

Aditi verily cooked food for the gods, \[thinking\] that by eating this, they would slay the Asuras. At that time, Budha (Mercury), having completed his ritual observance, arrived there. He said to Aditi, “Give me alms.” Aditi did not give him alms, saying, “This is to be eaten first by the gods, and not by anyone else.” Then, angered by the refusal of alms, Budha – who had become identified with Brahman – killed the egg (aṇḍa) of Aditi during the second birth of Vivasvān (the Āditya) from the egg. That \[still birth of\] Vivasvān became Mārtaṇḍa (the dead egg), who is Śrāddhadeva (the deity invoked in Śrāddha-s). Aditi was thus cursed: “There shall be loss in Aditi’s womb.”

Below, we give the exegeses of this myth from various brāhmaṇa texts in which it appears.

**Maitrāyaṇīya Saṃhitā:**  
aditir vai prajākāmaudanaṃ apacat । soñśiṣṭam āśnāt । tasyā dhātā cāryamā cājāyetām । sāparam apacat । soñśiṣṭam āśnāt । tasyā mitraś ca varuṇaś cājāyetām । sāparam apacat । soñśiṣṭam āśnāt । tasyā aṃśaś ca bhagaścājāyetām । sāparaṃ apacat । saikṣatoñśiṣṭaṃ me .aśnatyā dvau dvau jāyete । ito nūnaṃ me śreyaḥ syād yat purastād aśnīyāṃ iti । sā purastād aśitvopāharat । tā antar eva garbhaḥ santā avadatām । āvam idaṃ bhaviṣyāvo yad ādityā iti । tayor ādityā nirhantāraṃ aicchan । tā aṃśaś ca bhagaś ca nirahatām । tasmād etau yajñena yajante । aṃśa-prāśaṃ aṃśasya bhāgadheyam । janaṃ bhago .agacchat । tasmād āhurjano gantavyas tatra bhagena saṃgacchatā iti । sa vā indra ūrdhva eva prāṇam anudaśrayata । mṛtaṃ itaram āṇḍam avāpadyata । sa vāva mārtāṇḍo yasyeme manuṣyāḥ prajā । sā vā aditir ādityān upādhāvat । astv eva ma idaṃ mā ma idaṃ moghe parāpaptad iti । te .abruvan । athaiṣo .asmākaṃ eva bravātai na no .atimanyātā । iti sa vāva vivasvān ādityo yasya manuś ca vaivasvato yamaś ca । manur evāsmiṃl loke yamo .amuṣmin । ete vai devayānān patho gopāyanti yad ādityāsta iyakṣamāṇaṃ pratinudante । yo vā etebhyo .aprocyāgnim ādhatte taṃ ete svargāl lokāt pratinudante । uñśiṣṭabhāgā vā ādityāḥ । yaduñśiṣṭe vivartayitvā samidha ādadhāti tad ādityebhyo .agnyādheyaṃ prāha । nainaṃ svargāl lokāt pratinudante ॥ MS 1.6.12

Aditi, desiring offspring, cooked \[sacrificial\] rice. She consumed the remnant. From her, Dhātṛ and Aryaman were born. She cooked another and consumed the remnant; from her, Mitra and Varuṇa were born. She cooked another and consumed the remnant; from her, Aṃśa and Bhaga were born.

She cooked another and reflected, “By consuming the remnant, two are born each time. Surely, if I were to eat \[the rice offering\] first, something better would be mine.” She ate first and offered the rest. Within her womb, the two embryos spoke, “We shall become this which the Āditya-s are.” The Āditya-s sought a remover for them. Aṃśa and Bhaga removed them. Therefore, people sacrifice to these two with a sacrifice—the Aṃśaprāśa is the share of Aṃśa. Bhaga went to the people, therefore they say, “One should go to the people and meet with Bhaga.”

Now, that Indra breathed forth and remained upright, \[but\] the other egg fell dead. That is Mārtāṇḍa, whose offspring are these humans. Aditi then ran toward the Āditya-s \[saying\], “Let this not be in vain for me, let not this be in vain.” They said, “Let this one also be ours, let him not despise us.” He is Vivasvān Āditya, whose \[sons\] are Manu Vaivasvata and Yama. Manu is in this world, Yama in the other.

These Āditya-s indeed guard the paths of the gods; they push away the one who wishes to perform sacrifice \[if he is unworthy\]. Whoever establishes the sacrificial fire without having informed them, them they push away from the heavenly world. The Āditya-s have the remnant as their share; when one, having stirred the remnant, adds the fuel \[to the fire\], one proclaims the establishment of the fire for the Āditya-s. They do not push him away from the heavenly world.

**Taittirīya Āraṇyaka:**  
aṣṭau putrāso aditeḥ । ye jātās tanvaḥ pari । devāṃ upapraith saptabhiḥ । parā mārtāṇḍam āsyat । saptabhiḥ putrair aditiḥ । upaprait pūrvyaṃ yugam । prajāyai mṛtyave tat । parā mārtāṇḍam ābharad iti । tān anukramiṣyāmaḥ । mitraś ca varuṇaś ca । dhātā cāryamā ca । aṃśaś ca bhagaś ca । indraś ca vivasvāgś cetyete ॥ TA 1.13.62-63

There were eight sons of Aditi who were born from her body. She approached the gods with seven. She cast away Mārtāṇḍa. With seven sons, Aditi approached the first age (of the manifestation of the universe). For the sake of progeny and death (generating the mortals), she brought forth Mārtāṇḍa thereafter. We shall enumerate them: Mitra and Varuṇa, Dhātṛ and Aryaman, Aṃśa and Bhaga, and Indra and Vivasvān—these are they.

**Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa:**  
tad dhaike । ādityebhyaś caruṃ nirvapanti tad asti paryuditam iva+  
aṣṭau putrāso aditer ye jātās tanvas pari ।  
devāṃ upa prait saptabhiḥ parā mārtāṇḍam āsyad iti ॥

aṣṭau ha vai putrā aditeḥ । yāṃs tv etad devā ādityā ity ācakṣate sapta haiva te .avikṛtaṃ hāṣṭamaṃ janayāṃ cakāra mārtāṇḍaṃ saṃdegho haivāsa yāvānevordhvas tāvāṃs tiryaṅ  
puruṣa-sammita ity u haika āhuḥ ॥

ta u haita ūcuḥ । devā ādityā yad asmān anvajanimā tad amuyeva bhūd dhantemaṃ vikaravāmeti । taṃ vicakrur yathāyam puruṣo vikṛtas tasya yāni māṃsāni saṃkṛtya saṃnyāsus tato hastī samabhavat । tasmād āhur na hastinam pratigṛhṇīyāt puruṣājāno hi hastīti । yam u ha tad vicakruḥ sa vivasvān ādityas tasyemāḥ prajāḥ ॥ SB 3.1.3.2-5

Regarding this, some offer a caru (oblation) to the Ādityas. It is as if it were cast off. \[They cite the verse:\]  
‘Eight are the sons of Aditi who were born from her body;  
With seven, she approached the gods, but cast away Mārtāṇḍa (the dead egg).’

Verily, there were eight sons of Aditi. These gods, whom they now call the Ādityas, are only seven. The eighth, Mārtāṇḍa, she brought forth as a conglomerate mass, as tall as it was broad. Some say it was the same measure as a man.

The gods, the Ādityas, said: “That which was born after us, let it be just so. Come, let us reshape him.” They reshaped him. As a man is shaped, so they cut and arranged his flesh. From that \[which remained\], the elephant came into being. Therefore, they say: “One should not accept an elephant as a gift, for the elephant indeed is born from \[the same source as\] a human.” The one whom they thus reshaped is Vivasvān, the Āditya, and these \[mortals\] are his offspring.

**Pañcaviṃśa Brāhmaṇa:**  
ādityānāṃ yamātirātrāḥ । yamevaiṣā śrīr bhavati ya etā upayanti । etābhir vā ādityā dvandvam ārdhnuvan mitraś ca varuṇaś ca dhātā cāryamā cāṃśaś ca bhagaś cendraś ca vivasvā~ś caitāsām eva devatānām ṛddhim ṛdhnuvanti ya etā upayanti । tad abhyanūktā । aṣṭau putrāso aditer ye jātās tanvaṃ pari devā upaprait saptabhiḥ parā mārtāṇḍam āsyad iti । ādityā asmiṃl loka ṛddhā ādityā amuṣminn ubhayor anayor lokayor ṛdhnuvanti devaloke ca manuṣyaloke ca ya etā upayanti ॥ PB 24.12

The twin Atirātra \[rites\] belong to the Āditya-s. That prosperity (śrī) \[from the rite\] indeed becomes twinned who perform these. Through these \[rites\], the Āditya-s achieved \[success\] in pairs – Mitra and Varuṇa, Dhātṛ and Aryaman, Aṃśa and Bhaga, and Indra and Vivasvān. Those who perform these \[rites\] attain the prosperity of these very deities. Regarding this, it is said: “Eight are the sons of Aditi who were born from her body. She approached the gods with seven; she cast away Mārtāṇḍa.” The Āditya-s are prosperous in this world; the Āditya-s \[are prosperous\] in that \[world\]. They prosper in both these worlds—in the divine realm and the human realm—those who perform these \[rites\].

**Mercury in the Mahābhārata and astronomical metaphors**  
The injection of the planetary archon, Budha, is one of the striking differences between the reflex of this myth in the Mahābhārata and the Veda. Certain white Indologists and their Japanese fellow travelers have suggested that the Ārya-s were unaware of planets until contact with the Greco-Macedonians or, in the least, the Mesopotamians. While we disavow this claim (this is not the place for lengthy discussion on planets in the Veda), we do believe the Itihāsa period marked a rising interest in the planets relative to the early Veda. This included the use of the now common names and observations on Mercury, which is difficult to notice due to its closeness to the Sun. Hence, we believe the Mahābhārata reflex of the myth encodes an obscure astronomical allegory that was superimposed onto the original Vedic expression of this ancient myth. Before we try to understand this allegory, we take a detour to see how the National Epic uses similes with astronomical imagery concerning Mercury.

Often, the bright planets Venus and Jupiter are paired in Sanskrit similes that go back to the epics. For example:

tataḥ sumantreṇa guhena chaiva  
samīyatū rājasutāv araṇye ।  
divākarash chaiva nishākarash cha  
yathāmbare śukra-bṛhaspatibhyām ॥ Rāmāyaṇa 2.93.40  
Then, in the forest, the two princes (Rāma and Lakṣmaṇa) met with Sumantra and Guha, just as the Sun and the Moon meet with Venus and Jupiter in the sky.

Their pairing also denotes the clash of the teachers, the asura-aligned Śukra and the deva-aligned Bṛhaspati. However, rather than the celestial pairing of Jupiter and Venus, in the Mahābhārata we encounter multiple military similes involving the conjunctions of Mercury with other planets, all clustered in the Bhīṣma-parvan.

The first involves the two inner planets, Mercury and Venus:  
tato droṇaś cha pārthaś cha sameyātāṃ mahā-mṛdhe ।  
yathā budhaś cha śukraś cha mahārāja nabhastale ॥Mbh 6.097.057  
Then, O great king (Dhṛtarāṣṭra), Droṇa and Pārtha came together in the great battle, just as the \[conjunction of\] Mercury and Venus, the celestial vault.

The greater speed of orbit of these inner planets with respect to the others, reflected in their frequently changing relative positions, might have lent value to this simile involving the clash of Arjuna and Droṇa.

The next is a simile utilizing the conjunction of Mercury and Mars. Describing the battle between the Bāhlika on the Kuru side and Chedi-rāja Dṛṣṭaketu on the Pāṇḍava side, Sañjaya says:

tau tatra samare kruddhau nardantau cha muhur muhuḥ ।  
samIyatuḥ susaṃkruddhāv aṅgāraka-budhāv iva ॥Mbh 6.43.38  
Those two, enraged there in the battle, roaring again and again, came together in great fury, like the \[conjunction\] of Mars and Mercury.

The final one is a simile involving the conjunction of Mercury and Saturn used to describe the clash between Citrasena and Abhimanyu:

samāgatau tau tu raṇe mahāmātrau vyarochatām ।  
yathā divi mahāghorau rājan budhaśanaiścharau ॥Mbh 6.100.20  
Those two of great stature, having met in battle, shone resplendently, just as the two most formidable \[planets\], Mercury and Saturn, do in the heavens, O king (Dhṛtarāṣṭra).

In contrast to the planetary conjunctions of the Mahābhārata, the Rāmāyaṇa instead mentions a conjunction of Rohiṇī (α Tauri) by Mercury:

ity uktvā maithilīṃ vākyaṃ priyārhāṃ priyavādinīm ।  
jagrāha rāvaṇaḥ sītāṃ budhaḥ khe rohiṇīm iva ॥ Rāmāyaṇa 3.47.15  
Having said these words to the Maithilī (Sītā), who was worthy of affection and spoke affectionately, Rāvaṇa seized Sītā, just as Mercury (Budha) seizes Rohiṇī in the sky.

The question that arises from the above is how prominent and common these conjunctions might have been relative to the brightest planetary conjunction, namely that of Venus and Jupiter. For most modern city dwellers, any conjunction involving the elusive Mercury is a difficult proposition because it is low in the sky and is visible only for a brief period before dawn and after sunset. However, in the pre-modern skies over open spaces, these conjunctions might have been quite striking for ancient observers as Mercury is a fairly bright planet (usually around magnitude 0..-1.9 at maximal elongation). In terms of frequency, as inner planets, Venus and Mercury cross paths more frequently (once in 6-8 months) than Venus with Jupiter (approximately once in 13 months). However, most conjunctions of the former two are lost in the glare of the sun. Conjunctions of Mercury with Mars and Saturn, respectively, happen once or more in a year. However, due to being lost in the solar glare, good visible conjunctions of these planets are less frequent than that. The frequency is comparable for the stellar conjunction of Mercury with Rohiṇī mentioned in the Rāmāyaṇa. Hence, in practical terms, the properly visible conjunctions of Mercury are less likely to be seen than those of Jupiter and Venus.

Accordingly, we posit that the national epic chooses similes with these conjunctions for a specific reason beyond their easy observability, frequency of visibility or brightness. Their occurrence only in the Bhīṣma-parvan is also puzzling: were there visible multi-planet conjunctions with a prominent Mercury during the first 10 days of the Great War? Whatever the case, it suggests that at the time of the composition of the National Epic, Mercury was being carefully observed, and its rapid movement probably formed the basis for its frequent use in military similes. This might also underlie the stellar conjunction of Mercury and Rohiṇī used for Rāvaṇa in the Rāmāyaṇa. It is also possible that these Mercurial conjunctions held special significance in early Hindu omenology. Thus, the allusions of the epic author might have been understood by those well-versed in those traditions (c.f., Varāhamihira’s extensive omenology of Mercury, including the statement that when its form is bright and big it is auspicious and if otherwise malefic).

Against this backdrop of the prominence of Mercury in the Mahābhārata, we return to its appearance in the Mārtāṇḍa myth. There, Budha, the archon of Mercury, is said to appear after the completion of his vrata (c.f., vrata as natural laws in the Ṛgveda). We take this to mean that Mercury has completed one revolution, or at least that is seen reemerging after vanishing in the glare of the Sun. At this point, Aditi refuses him alms. Aditi is associated with the solar luster of the first seven Āditya-s. Hence, this refusal of alms could be an allegorical expression of Mercury’s brightness being suppressed by the solar luster upon reemergence, i.e., not being given food to make his brightest form visible. Given the prominence of Mercury in the tradition preserved in the Mahābhārata, the last Āditya being born as the “Dead Egg” was attributed to his archon’s curse.

Finally, we find this major ancient cosmogonic myth to be rather remarkable because it is striking to see that our ancestors instinctively arrived at the understanding that we are all ultimately born of the non-luminous solar material.
