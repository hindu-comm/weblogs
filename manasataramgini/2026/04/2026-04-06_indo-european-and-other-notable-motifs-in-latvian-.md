Indo-European and other notable motifs in Latvian dainas

As we have discussed before, outside the Indo-Iranian and Old Greek worlds, some of the most conservative Indo-European traditions were found among the Baltic peoples. Linguistic and genetic evidence favor a higher-order “satemizing” clade of Balto-Slavic and Indo-Iranian. Among the surviving Baltic tongues, Lithuanian is more conservative than Latvian; however, the Latvian surviving tradition preserves folk material that provides a useful complement to the Lithuanian material. This material is in the form of Dainas, Latvian folk chants (equivalents of the Lithuanian Dainos, which inspired the German poet Johann Wolfgang von Goethe), often rich in motifs from the Baltic heathen past. Unfortunately, their equivalents in another conservative Baltic language, Old Prussian, have been entirely lost.

Before the arrival of the German crusaders, the Latvian lands were divided among several major Baltic groups, each with its own fortified hillforts (*pilskalni*) and ruling chiefs. The major groups were:  
1. The Curonians (Kurši): The fierce sea-faring group known as the “Baltic Vikings,” who inhabited the western coast.  
2. The Latgalians (Latgaļi): They occupied the east and have lent their name to modern Latvians, though they descend from all the groups mentioned here.  
3. The Semigallians (Zemgaļi): Inhabitants of the fertile central plains who engaged in intensive agriculture.  
4. The Selonians (Sēļi): A tribe in the southeast known for their distinct hillfort culture.  
These Indo-European people further interacted with the Livs (Lībieši), who were a Finnic people (related to Estonians and Finns) on the coast of the Gulf of Riga and more inland along river systems.

While little is known of their early history, they were part of the economic network of the “Amber Road” (amber was seen as Baltic Gold) and the iron trade.

Keeping with old IE traditions, the Latvian tribes are said to have had sacred groves with old oaks and lime trees. The German-led Northern Crusades ravaged the old Baltic lands after the German Christian missionaries’ attempts to convert the Latvians through proselytization mostly failed. The Latvian tribes held out for nearly a century (e..g., Semigallian leader Namejs) before being completely overrun by the second Abrahamism in the late 1200s.

Below are a few interesting daina-s from the daina catalog made available by the University of Latvia. We provide notable Sanskrit cognates below the translation in some cases.

***The Perkons and his sons***  
This surviving Latvian daina to the god Perkons (= H: Parjanya) features his sons. One may read potential Marutian motifs in his sons.

Pērkoņam seši dēli,  
Visi seši Vāczemê.  
Lūdziet Dievu arajiņi,  
Lai nāk viens šâi zemê,  
Lai nāk viens šâi zemê,  
Šìs zemites trīcinàt,  
Šìs zemites trīcinàt,  
Šo asniņu līdzinàt.

Perkons (the thunderer) has six sons,  
All six are in Germany.  
Pray to the God, little plowmen,  
That one comes to this land,  
That one comes to this land,  
To make this earth tremble,  
To make this earth tremble,  
To straighten these sprouts.

zemê = jmā: earth; trīcinàt ~ trasati; dievu = deva; sesi = ṣaṭ.

***A daina to the “Mother of the Wind” (Vēja māte = Vāyu-mātṛ):***  
Apsagrieza Vēja māte,  
I vienadi, i otradi,  
Dieva namu slaucidama,  
Mēnestiņa istabiņu.

Mother of Vēja (Wind) turned herself,  
This way and that,  
Sweeping the house of the God,  
The little room of the Moon.

Mēness= Mās: Moon

***The daina of the celestial dogs and the deer-hunter***  
This daina potentially captures an ancient motif of Orion, his dogs and his deer quarry. In the IE world, this goes back to proto-Indo-Hittite times. In the Hindu tradition, its reflex is Rudra as the hunter of Prajapati in the form of the celestial deer/antelope.

Rītâ agri es redzeju  
Ka Saulite medìt gāja:  
Div’ sudraba kuceniņi  
Apzeltitu briedi dzina.

Early in the morning, I saw  
How the Little Sun went hunting:  
Two silver puppies  
Chased a gilded stag.

Saulite, the diminutive Sun, the hunter, evidently represents the sun in the Orion region. The two silver puppies are Canis Major and Canis Minor, denoted by their brightest stars, Sirius and Procyon; the golden Stag is Orion: Mṛgaśiras. kucēns ~ kukkura (also c.f. IA word for dog with Prākṛta attestations, kutra): puppy

***The two divine sons and the solar daughter***  
A Latvian version of the Aśvin-s bearing Sūryā in their chariot. In this daina, they bear her in a boat instead. The boat in Hindu tradition is more commonly associated with Pūṣan and Sūrya (see below).

Ābelkoka laivu daru,  
Abi gali pazeltiti.  
Dieva dēli jīrejiņi,  
Saules meitu vizina.

I build a boat of apple-wood,  
Both ends gilded with gold.  
The God’s sons are the rowers,  
Giving the Sun’s daughter a ride.

meitu is cognate of English maid and German Magd, instead of following duhitṛ from PIE

***The divine smith***  
This daina features the Latvian cognate of the engineer god Tvaṣṭṛ:  
Kalejs kala debesîs,  
Ko tas kala, ko nekala?  
Dieva dēla jostu kala,  
Saules meitas vaiņadziņu

The Smith forged in the heavens,  
What did he forge, what did he not?  
He forged a belt for the Son of the God,  
A crown for the Sun’s daughter.

jostu ~ yoktra: belt; The yoktra is the belt worn by the yajamāna-patnī in the sacrifice. It is her equivalent of the yajñopavīta. The crown of the Sun’s daughter brings to mind Tvaṣṭṛ forging the divine weapons from the solar luster.

***The twin gods (divine sons) as falcons***  
This Latvian daina is notable in featuring the motif of the twin gods (=Aśvin-s, Dioscuri) as falcons.

Celies agri, Saules meita,  
Mazgà baltu liepas galdu:  
Ik rītiņa Dieva dēli  
Kà vanagi lidinaja.

Rise early, Daughter of the Sun,  
Wash the white linden wood table:  
At every rising, the \[twin\] Sons of God  
Circle above like falcons.

A comparable theme is seen in RV 8.35.9 of Śyāvāśva Ātreya in the rare Pariṣṭājjyotis meter:  
śyenāv iva patatho havyadātaye  
somaṃ sutam mahiṣevāva gacchathaḥ ।  
sajoṣasā uṣasā sūryeṇa ca  
trir vartir yātam aśvinā ॥

Like two falcons, you fly to the sacrificial offering  
Like two buffaloes, you descend to the pressed soma.  
In conjunction with with Uṣas and Sūrya,  
Make your circuit three times, O Aśvin-s!

***The solar boat***  
This Latvian daina provides a direct cognate for the golden solar boat metaphor, which is clearly attested in the Ṛgveda.

balta noiedama  
Guļàs zelta laiviņâ;  
No rītiņa celdamàs  
Apģērbjàs sudrabâ.”

The white Sun, while setting,  
Lay down in a golden boat;  
Rising up in the morning,  
She dressed herself in silver.

One may compare this to the nocturnal journey of the sun alluded to in RV 6.58.3  
yās te pūṣan nāvo antaḥ samudre  
hiraṇyayīr antarikṣe caranti ।  
tābhir yāsi dūtyāṃ sūryasya  
kāmena kṛta śrava icchamānaḥ ॥

Those boats of yours, O Puṣan, which in the ocean,  
the golden ones in the mid-region roam,  
on them you ride with the embassy of Sūrya,  
seeking glory, O (god) prompted by desire.

***The deity Ūsiņš***  
Par kalniņu Ūsiņš jāja  
Ar akmiņa kumeliņu;  
Tas atnese kokiem lapas,  
Zemei zaļu āboliņu.

Over the hill Ūsiņš rode  
With a steed of stone;  
He brought the leaves for the trees,  
And green clover for the earth.

akmiņs = aśman: stone.

The etymology of Ūsiņš is debated. One theory connects it to the old Indo-European root associated with dawn, from which the Sanskrit Uṣas (the dawn goddess) is also derived. The alternative theory (less plausible) connects it to horseman – similar to Skt Aśvin. Ūsiņš is seen as a spring deity who reinvigorates the natural world, functions shared with Savitṛ and Pūṣan, with solar symbolism. The idea of a little stone horse (Kumeliņš) appears to be a peculiarly Baltic conception, whose exact significance is unclear to us. A variant of this daina has Dieviņš for Ūsiņš. This is a cognate of Sanskrit deva, an epithet specifically applied to Savitṛ but could simply be used in the sense of “the god”:

Pa zāliti Dieviņš brauca  
Akminaju kumeliņu;  
Tas atveda kokiem lapas,  
Zemei zaļu āboliņu.

Through the grass Dieviņš rode  
With a steed of stone;  
He brought the leaves for the trees,  
And green clover for the earth.

A further variation of this daina indicates that the deity Ūsiņš also called the god Dieviņš was armed:

Atjāj viens bruņots vīrs  
Par lielo tīrumiņu;  
Tas atnesa kokiem lapas,  
Zemei zaļu dābuliņu.

An armed man came riding  
Across the great wide field;  
He brought the leaves for the trees,  
The green clover for the earth.

vīrs=vīra: man; bruņas\>bruņots meaning “armed” or “armored” seems to be an early Germanic borrowing; c.f. Norse: brynja, Old English: byrne, meaning coat of mail.

***The divine tree***  
Sajāja bramaņi  
Augstajâ kalnâ,  
Sakāra zobenus  
Svētajâ kokâ.  
Svētajam kokam  
Deviņi zari,  
Ik zara galâ  
Deviņi ziedi,  
Ik zieda galâ  
Deviņas ogas.

The Bramaņi rode together  
Up onto the high hill;  
They hung their swords  
Upon the sacred tree.  
The sacred tree  
Has nine branches;  
At the tip of each branch  
Are nine blossoms;  
At the tip of each blossom  
Are nine berries.

This daina is remarkable because it preserves the suggestive word Bramaņis (Pl: Bramaņi). This word has generally been understood as a nobleman and is thought by some to be a cognate of brāhmaṇa. Others disagree and believe it is a late loan from the Middle Low German word for a brāhmaṇa from India. If the former were true, it would suggest that the Baltics and Aryan-s might have shared a common name for their ritualist caste. Even if it were a late loan, it is notable that the Baltics adopted and incorporated into a daina referring to a ritual context. In this daina, they clearly appear in the context of an old ritual being performed in what seems to be a sacred grove on a high hill.

***The mothers of nature***  
Krūmu māte, Mežu māte,  
Gani manas avetiņas;  
Kad es iešu tautiņâs,  
Ik pie krūma ziedu metu

Mother of the Bushes, Mother of the Woods,  
Shepherd my dear little ewes;  
When I go away into ‘the people’ \[to marry\],  
At every bush I will strew flowers.

avetiņas ~ avi: little ewes, diminutive of avis = ewe.

This daina invoking the “mother of the woods” brings to mind the forest goddess Araṇyāni invoked in RV 10.146

***The thunderer Perkons at the marriage of the Moon and Sun***  
Mēness ņēma Saules meitu,  
Pērkon’ lūdza panāksņôs.  
Pērkons gāja panāksņôs,  
Visu zemi puškodams:  
Kalniņiemi zīda zāli,  
Ozoliemi zelta lapas,  
Mazajiemi kārkliņiem  
Sudrabiņa gredzentiņus.

The Moon took the Sun’s daughter \[as a bride\],  
He invited Pērkons to the wedding feast.  
Pērkons went to the wedding feast,  
Adorning all the earth:  
For the hills—silk grass,  
For the oaks—golden leaves,  
For the little willow bushes—  
Silver rings.

The growth of vegetation through the activity of Pērkons is reminiscent of that of his Hindu cognate, Parjanya.
