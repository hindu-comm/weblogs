Some further notes on the Mongol religion-4: the ritual to call back the spirit

While far removed from each other in space and time, both the Egyptian and Tibetan Books of the Dead describe a structured journey of consciousness through what seems like a non-ordinary postmortem reality. Notably, the existence of a structure to this postmortem journey is also emphasized by overarching commonalities found in the extensive body of modern reports on Near-Death Experiences (NDEs) and individuals claiming to remember the “intermission” (vide Ian Stevenson) between incarnations. Further, they also relate to key aspects of the shamanic ritual of spirit calling, a practice seen across Eurasian steppe cultures, which is the topic of this note.

Common to the inception of all these phenomena is the separation of the ghost/spirit/consciousness from the physical body, followed by its journey or wandering through a series of apparently non-physical realms. Accounts of this process have been vividly reported in modern NDEs, with the percipients mentioning things like floating above their physical body even as their brain activity has ceased. The Egyptian Book of the Dead describes two souls, the Ba (roughly equivalent to Hindu Ātman) and the Ka soul (roughly equivalent to the sūkṣma-śarīra). The Ba, separating from the physical body, begins a guided journey through the Duat (Underworld). The Tibetan Book of the Dead (Bardo Thödol) comparably maps the experience of consciousness in the Chikai Bardo (the moment of death), where it experiences a separation from the mortal body. The postmortem journey of the consciousness can be perilous, and the Egyptian Book of the Dead provides the spells and knowledge needed to navigate the challenges, such as the crucial Weighing of the Heart ceremony by the god Anubis and the judgment before Osiris. Similarly, in the Tibetan Bardo Thödol, the discarnate consciousness experiences 42 peaceful and 58 wrathful deities of the Vajrayāṇa tradition, including cosmic Buddha-s, ferocious Heruka-s (Bhairava equivalents), Mātṛ-s, zoocephalous Ḍākinī-s, and finally King Yama. Yama judges the discarnate spirit in a striking parallel to the Egyptian situation.

The Central Asian shamanic ritual of spirit calling is attested among the Mongols, the heathen Turks and the Tungus (Manchus); it was also likely performed by the para-Mongols (e.g., the Khitan). The ritual is initiated to cure a grievously ill person. As in NDEs and paralleling the accounts of the Books of the Dead, the spirit of such an ill man is believed to have wandered away. Some of the incantations used in these rituals vividly describe the ill man’s soul wandering close to or into the perilous realm of the underworld (see below). The shaman, through a series of incantations with the aid of the gods (Tengri-s) and other beings, both benign and maleficent, is said to summon the wandering spirit back into the physical body to cure the said individual. Interestingly, a striking parallel to the Altaic shamanic ritual of spirit calling is found in the Ṛgveda, namely the rite of calling back the mind or the Gaupāyana rite (see below for more). Here, as in the Altaic ritual, the mind of the dead man is seen as having wandered away from the body to the realm of Yama Vaivasvata. It is brought back from there to dwell again in the body of the dead individual by a series of incantations (RV 10.57-60). Among the deities invoked is the goddess Anumati Asunīti, who leads one to the afterlife; she is asked to bring back the life and restore it. The same deity is also invoked with one of these incantations in the Hindu rite of Prāṇapratiṣṭha or installation of life in a divine image. Concomitantly, Nirṛti, the goddess of perdition, is asked to go far away. These RV incantations present multiple parallels to the incantations used by the Mongolian shamans in their comparable rite.

While the Books of the Dead provide guidance regarding the onward journey into the non-ordinary realms, in NDEs, there are frequent accounts of guides in the non-ordinary realm leading the spirit back to its abandoned physical body and sometimes also performing cures/repairs on the diseased/damaged physical body. This situation with the NDE guides is also paralleled in the Central Asian spirit calling ritual, wherein the shaman, along with the divinities, help guide the spirit back to the body, thereby effecting a cure from the illness. In the Mongolian tradition, the spirit that has wandered off from the body is often specifically said to be journeying towards the realm of Erlig Nom-un Khan, the Yama-like figure of old Mongol tradition (frequently equated with Yama transferred via the Tibetan tradition). The Hungarian, Sarközi, and the Russian, Sazykin, have collated and translated a remarkable collection of Mongolian texts that provide the incantations deployed by the shaman as part of the spirit calling ritual. These researchers provide a summary of the ritual, which goes thus:

When a person falls gravely ill and their soul is believed to have departed, a shaman is called to perform the ritual of calling the spirit back. The preparations begin with two pieces of pure white felt – both new, unblemished, and perfectly clean. One piece is laid beneath the sick person; the other is placed under the shaman’s feet. Tradition holds that these first ritual objects were originally sent down from heaven, and so the felt is regarded as sacred. It is also said to represent a white cloud, the vehicle by which the shaman ascends to the upper (non-physical) world.

Next, a bucket is prepared with “white food”: milk, sour milk, or similar offerings. At the bottom of this bucket, a silver coin is placed to represent prosperity. Into this bucket, the shaman plants a special ceremonial arrow, the dalangajin nomo, which is used in many sacred rites to request blessings and aid from the divinities. The bucket is then set in the western part of the yurt.

A long red ribbon, tipped with a copper button, is tied to the arrow and carried out through the open door of the yurt. Outside, it is fastened to the branch of a birch tree that has been planted upright in the ground. This bright red ribbon forms the path along which the wandering soul is expected to return. The yurt door remains open for the entire ceremony so that the soul may pass freely.

Near the birch branch, a man sits holding the bridle of the patient’s finest, best-harnessed horse. It is believed that the horse will sense the spirit’s arrival: it will begin to tremble and neigh when the returning soul draws near. The spirit is thought to re-enter the world by first settling upon the man outside, and then traveling along the red ribbon until it reenters the body of the sick person.

Inside the yurt, a table is set with an abundance of offerings — wine, spirits, tobacco, sweets, sugar, confections, cakes, and nuts — all gathered in a large bucket. The shaman purifies and blesses these provisions by fumigating them with smoke from “grass”-like herbs belonging to Ericaceae or Cyperaceae, marking the final stage of the ritual.

Curiously, a version of the ritual is called the “\[The rite\] to weigh the soul on scales”. Here, the soul/spirit of the gravely ill individual is weighed on a scale to usher it back into the body (c.f., the Egyptian Book of the Dead, where the soul is weighed to decide its onward passage into the Aaru, i.e., the Field of Reeds). Typically, the hat or the clothes of the individual are placed on one pan and a weight on the other. If the wandering soul returns, then it goes to the pan with the hat/clothes and makes it go down, thus allowing it to return to the body.

Finally, it is worth noting that the Mongols have a curious belief that an individual has three souls: 1. The first soul resides in the skeleton and is said to usually remain behind with the corpse or in its vicinity and offer protection (likely what Jamuqa implied when he promised to protect Chingiz Khan after his death). It is believed to manifest in a skeletal form. 2. This soul or spirit resides in the blood and can take a station in the heart, lungs or liver. It is likely to be the one that wanders away during illness or death and needs to be brought back by the shaman. It can take the form of a little homunculus or, conversely, a giant. 3. The third soul is said to take the form of the body by default, but can take several forms. Upon death, it is believed to haunt the settlement as a ghost. Some Mongols (especially in Buryatia) believe that this soul becomes a malignant spirit known as an Ada and settles in reptiles. Ada spirits can also appear as a wolf and cause pediatric diseases. A Mongolian manuscript in Tibetan script has been preserved with pictures of Kaumāra mātṛ-like figures for averting pediatric diseases caused by Ada-s. This suggests the incorporation of divinities from ancient Kaumāra lore into a Mongolian context via Tibet.

The texts for the calling back of the spirit, while showing the same broad structure, patterned after the aforementioned ritual process, exhibit considerable diversity in terms of their religious content. They can be broadly divided into two types: 1. The first type is sparse in terms of invocation of any kind of deities and primarily depends on the shaman leading the soul back to the body. Some of these texts display an archaic language and are likely survivals from a pre-Bauddha Mongolian substratum. 2. The second type is heavy on invocation of deities and invariably shows Tibetan influence. This second type can be further subdivided into two types. The first subtype is prominently Bauddha, and the whole ritual is incorporated into a Vajrayāṇa-influenced practice. The second subtype has a minimal Bauddha element proper but extensively melds Pre-Bauddha Indic elements like Lokapāla-s and the mantra of the Hindu deity Kālī with Mongolian and Bon elements. It is this second subtype that we shall pay more attention to in the remainder of this note.

Below, we focus on excerpts from two texts edited and translated by Sazykin, which emphasize the role of the Lokapāla-s in the spirit-calling ritual:

1. The Lokapāla pantheon in these rituals appears to be one which is Prājāpatya, i.e., with Brahmā (Mongolian Esrua) positioned at zenith as their head. This pattern diverges from the earlier Hindu aṣṭa-dikpāla model with Indra in the east as their head. This system likely entered the Vajrayāṇa tantra-s from a Śaiva or smārta source.

4. The incantation melds in several Mongolian figures like Caghan Moritu and the Royal One with a Golden head, whose identities or relationships with Indo-Tibetan deifics remain unclear.  
5. The nakṣatra listing follows the archaic Indic system of starting with Kṛttikā.

3\. In this chant, again only 4 Lokapāla-s are explicitly named: Brahmā, Indra, Īśāna, and Agni. The last one is referred to by his Mongolian name Ghal-Tengri, i.e., the Fire God.  
4. This incantation again mentions several other beings, like the Ada-s, mubdud, the Cidkür devils, the one-footed Teyireng (depicted in a Mongolian manuscript) and Murji Khan. Apart from the Ada-s for whom there is robust Mongol typology, the provenance of the rest remains poorly understood and may have roots in the pre-Buddha Mongolian religion.  
5. This text also mentions the Ḍākinī-s who have obvious roots in Vajrayāṇa. Apart from those from the classic Vajrayāṇa categories (Padmaḍākinī, Buddhaḍākinī etc), the Mongol tradition records several who still retain their zoocephalous iconography hearkening back to their original Śaiva roots, e.g., Arslan terigü-tü eke= Siṃhamukhī; Bars terigü-tü=Vyāghramukhī; Matar toluyai-tu eke= Śiśumāramukhī.

The incantations with Tibetan influences used in the soul-calling rite often incorporate a dhāraṇī in their climactic phase. While some of these are Vajrayāṇa mantra-s, we see surprising variants with a Kālī mantra in place of the usual dhāraṇī-s. A version of the soul-calling rite with this mantra is attributed to the Indian master Padmasambhava, considered the Buddha of his age, who is said to have promulgated it for curing a certain Cakravartin Khan (a likely memory of the early Tibetan emperor Trisong Detsen). At the climax of the rite, when the shaman plants the ritual arrow known as the dalangajin nomo into the ritual bucket, he utters an incantation 108 times, which goes thus:  
OṂ Kālī! Kālīye huṃ huṃ phaṭ phaṭ svāhā ॥  
He then makes food offerings with the incantation:  
oṃ huṃ caṃ caṃ huṃ phaṭ cū phaṭ cing cing huṃ phaṭ phaṭ ॥

With that, the ill man’s spirit is brought back into his body. Thus, rather remarkably, a Kālī mantra has journeyed a long way from its land of origin and has lodged itself right in the heart of an Altaic ritual mostly free of any Bauddha envelope. This raises the question of how this mantra might have reached the Mongolian tradition. We posit that the mantra was likely transmitted in Nepal or Tibet via the interactions between Hindu ascetic groups (e.g., Nātha, Advaitin, and other yogin-s) and Tibetan Vajrayāṇa exponents. While such exchanges continuing to recent times are often ignored, there is a body of evidence for such interactions that has mostly “passed under the radar.” Further, as evidence for a direct Indo-Mongolian contact, one might point to the ācārya “Stone-Finger”, a mysterious Indian teacher who is said to have gone to Mongolia in search of the location of Śambala, settled there and established an aimag. With him was another Indian Acharya who is said to have drowned in a lake. Stone-finger’s finger froze in the course of the journey and broke off, giving him the said moniker. He is said to have transmitted the tales of Emperor Vikramāditya and the vetāla to the Mongols.

Given that the rite of the calling back of the spirit is widespread among all heathen Altaic groups, it is likely that it was an ancient ritual. In light of this, it is striking to find a parallel for it in the Ṛgveda in the form of the Gaupāyana rite. The deployment of the Gaupāyana incantations as a Vedic rite is now otiose, with no specific continuity in the later ritual tradition beyond the reuse of certain incantations in the Prāṇa-pratiṣṭha ritual as noted above. However, one might find a parallel to the ritual enactments in the Śaiva funerary rite, which is best preserved among the Saiddhāntika-s. Here, the deceased consecrated Śaiva’s spirit is brought back by the officiant via incantations to his corpse and temporarily reunited with it. It is then offered a full ritual funeral culminating in a cremation when it is finally released to its onward journey. Thus, the initial step of calling it back after death, temporarily to the corpse, reminds one of the calling back of the “mind” in the Gaupāyana rite.

Tradition offers the following legend regarding the Gaupāyana rite: The Ikṣvāku monarch Asamāti (= Itihāsa-purāṇa Asamañjas) is said to have originally had the four Gaupāyana-s, clansmen of Agastya, as his purohita-s. He dismissed them and replaced them with Asura officiants (Iranics?). The Asura-s killed Subandhu, one of the Gaupāyana-s. Then the remaining three Gaupāyana brothers (along with Agastya’s sister?) performed the rite of Mana āvartanam, “calling back of the mind” to restore the “mind” and “life breath” of Subandhu and bring him back to life. The mantra-s describe Subandhu’s mind as having gone to the realm of Yama Vaivasvata and the four-cornered realm (a euphemism for the śmaśāna-vedi). As in the Mongolian rite, several deities are invoked to aid the return of the mind, including Indra, Agni Narāśaṃsa, Soma, Aśvin-s, Anumati Asunīti, Pūṣan, and Dyāvāpṛthivī. Finally, the rite concludes with a mantra containing a benediction for the disease to go away (RV 10.60.11). This suggests that, indeed, like its Mongolian counterpart, the RV Gaupāyana rite was also used in the context of a man afflicted with a serious illness.

Thus, we come a full circle with this connection between the Altaic soul-calling rite and the Gaupāyana rite of calling back the “mind”. Given its obsolescence in the later śrauta tradition, we believe it is a memory of an ancient Indo-Iranian rite that was performed on the steppe. This suggests that a version of the soul-calling ritual was already present on the steppe, well before it became “Altaic”. Hence, it is possible that such a shamanic rite was either transmitted by the Indo-Iranians to early Altaic people during their contact in Central Asia (directly or via an intermediary) or it was received by both the Indo-Iranians and the Altaics from a common ancient source on the eastern steppe.
