The hare on the Moon: an ancient mytheme

The pervasiveness of didactic animal tales in the Śanti- and Anuśāsana-parvan-s of the Mahābhārata, the Jātaka-s of the Tathāgata, and the Pañcatantra of Viṣṇuśarman suggests that proto-versions of these tales were a prominent feature of Indo-Aryan folkloric narratives. Indeed, we get a hint of their ancient presence in the enigmatic words (which seem to be references to such tales) uttered by the great god Indra to the vipra Vasukra, famed for his cryptic ṛk-s in the Ṛgveda:

śaśaḥ kṣuram pratyañcaṃ jagāra  
adriṃ logena vy abhedam ārāt ।  
bṛhantaṃ cid ṛhate randhayāni  
vayad vatso vṛṣabhaṃ śūśuvānaḥ ॥  
Indra said:  
The hare swallowed the razor\* aimed at him.  
I broke the rock from afar with a lump of earth.  
Even the great one I weaken for the small.  
Growing stronger, the calf will attack the bull.  
\*could mean the kṣurapra, the razor-headed arrow often mentioned in the Epics.

suparṇa itthā nakham ā siṣāya  
avaruddhaḥ paripadaṃ na siṃhaḥ ।  
niruddhaś cin mahiṣas tarṣyāvān  
godhā tasmā ayathaṃ karṣad etat ॥  
The eagle thus became entangled by its claw,  
just like the lion caught in the trap.  
Even as the buffalo was captured when thirsty,  
the Varanus lizard plowed its leg this way.

tebhyo godhā ayathaṃ karṣad etad  
ye brahmaṇaḥ pratipīyanty annaiḥ ।  
sima ukṣṇo ‘vasṛṣṭāṃ adanti  
svayam balāni tanvaḥ śṛṇānāḥ ॥ (RV10.28.9-11)  
The Varanus lizard will snatch his leg,  
whoever resists a brāhmaṇa with food\*.  
Such people eat even released bulls,  
destroying the strength of their own bodies.  
\*i.e., one who is against giving food to a brāhmaṇa

It is possible that the cryptic allusions to (i) the hare swallowing razor; (ii) the lion being trapped; (iii) the thirsty buffalo being captured; (iv) the Varanus lizard were either allegorical references to events (e.g., astronomical) or (not mutually exclusively) have specific animal fables behind them (there are more such references in the said sūkta of Vasukra).

With this background, we posit that the Jātaka-s of the Tathāgata preserve one such old fable featuring a hare and Indra (J 316, Sasa-jātaka):

***The Hare’s Gift***  
*“Seven red fish,”* etc. The Buddha once told this tale while staying at Jetavana.

A wealthy landowner in Sāvatthī had invited the Buddha and his śramaṇa-s to his home. He built a pavilion before his house, set out fine seats, and for seven days offered them the choicest foods. On the seventh day, he gave the Buddha and the five hundred śramaṇa-s all the requisites of a śramaṇa’s life. When the meal was done, the Buddha thanked him, saying:  
“You have done well to give joy through generosity. This is the way of the wise of old, who gave freely, even their own flesh to beggars.”

At the householder’s request, he told the following story of the past.

***The Four Friends***  
Long ago, when King Brahmadatta ruled in Vārāṇasi, the Bodhisatta (Bodhisattva) was born as a hare and lived in a forest by a mountain, a river, and a village. There too lived a monkey, a jackal, and an otter – his close companions. Each sought food in his own ground, and each evening they gathered together.

The hare, being wise, exhorted them:  
“Give alms, keep the dhamma, observe the holy days.”  
And the others listened, and dwelt content.

One day, seeing the moon, the hare knew the morrow was a holy day (likely a parvan day). He told his friends, “Tomorrow, keep the precepts well, and give food to any beggar who comes. For almsgiving brings great fruit to one firm in virtue.” They agreed. At dawn, the otter went to the river. A fisherman had caught seven red fish, strung them, and buried them in the sand. The otter sniffed them out, dug them up, and cried aloud three times: “Who owns these fish?” No one answered. He carried them to his lair, proud of his virtue. The jackal prowled and found in a field-watcher’s hut: two kebabs, a lizard, and a pot of curd. He cried thrice, “Who owns these?” Hearing no reply, he carried them home, pleased at his deed. The monkey climbed a mango grove, gathered a bunch of ripe mangoes, and set them aside, thinking how righteous he was.

But the hare thought: “I eat only grass. What can I give a beggar? I have no rice, no oil, no food. If one comes, I must give my very flesh.” So noble was his thought that Sakka (Śakra = Indra)’s throne in heaven grew hot. Sakka, perceiving the cause, came to test the hare.

***The brāhṃana in disguise***  
Indra came first to the otter in the guise of a brāhṃana. “Sir,” he said, “I have fasted. If I had food, I could fulfill my priestly duties.” The otter gladly offered him his find, reciting:

Seven red fish from Gaṅga’s tide  
I safely brought to shore.  
Eat your fill, O brāhṃana,  
And dwell within this wood.

Sakka said, “I will do so tomorrow,” and went on. Next, he came to the jackal, who offered his prize and said:

A lizard and a jar of curd,  
Two spits to roast the meat –  
Such humble food is all I have;  
Accept it, O brāhṃana, eat.

Again, Sakka replied, “I will do so tomorrow,” and moved on. He came to the monkey, who offered fruit and shade, saying:

A mango ripe, a stream ice-cool,  
And greenwood shade to rest—  
All this I gladly give to you;  
Here may you find it best.

Again, Sakka put him off till the next day, and finally he came to the hare. The hare rejoiced:  
“You have done well to come to me. This day, I will grant a gift I have never given. But you shall not break the law by killing. Instead, light a fire, and I will leap into it. Roasted in the flames, my flesh shall be your food.” He spoke in verse:

No beans, no rice, no sesame—  
No food have I to share.  
But take my body in the fire:  
My gift beyond compare.

Sakka conjured a heap of burning coals by his māyā. The hare shook himself three times, so that no insect might die with him, and leapt joyfully upon the fire. But the flames were cool as frost. “O brāhṃana,” he asked, “why is your fire icy-cold? What does this mean?” Sakka replied: “Wise hare, I am no brahmin. I am Sakka. I came to test your virtue.” The hare said: “Not only you, O Sakka, but all beings in the world could test me, yet none would find me unwilling to give.” He then cried aloud in exultation, like a lion’s roar. Sakka declared: “Let your virtue shine through all the world!” He pressed a mountain, drew forth its essence, and painted upon the moon the image of a hare. Then he laid the Bodhisatta gently back upon the grass and returned to heaven.

The four friends lived happily, keeping the dhamma and observing the holy days until their lives’ end.

***The Buddha’s epilogue***  
The Master ended the story and revealed the Truths. At its close, the generous householder attained the fruit of the First Path (Sotāpanna: such a bauddha aspirant achieves the state of an arihant in no more than 7 reincarnations from that point). “And at that time,” the Buddha said, “Ānanda was the otter, Moggallāna (Maudgalāyana) the jackal, Sāriputta (Śāriputra) the monkey, and I myself was the wise hare.”

Behind the relatively minimal bauddha boosterism, we see that the Tathāgata has adopted an ancient Indo-Aryan fable that was likely prevalent when the mainstream Ārya Aindra religion was still popular. In that context, we see the rather transparent device of the test by Indra in disguise, which is widely used in IE literature. While both the original and the adaptation by the Buddha likely had a didactic element to teach the proper observance of dharma and the special place for selflessness, it also encodes an old astral mytheme, namely the origin of the hare on the moon. Close to the time of the Buddha, we have punch-marked coins from the Saurāṣṭra Janapada in Western India depicting the moon with the hare, capturing the common name for the moon in Sanskrit, Śaśāṅka, from the Epics onward.

We can further trace the deep history of the hare on the moon in Indo-Aryan tradition backward from this narrative of the Tathāgata. The word Śaśāṅka is repeatedly used for the moon both in the Mahābhārata (e.g., aśvāḥ śaśāṅka-sadṛśāś candra-devam udāvaha । Mbh “critical” 7.22.50c) and the Rāmāyaṇa (e.g., tvaṃ sameṣyasi rāmeṇa śaśāṅkeneva rohiṇī । Ram “critical” 5.37.44c). In the Mahābharata, Saṃjaya proposes the unusual theory to Dhṛtarāṣṭra that the image of the hare on the moon is actually the reflection of the geographic features of a continent known as Sudarṣaṇa (Mbh “critical” 6.7). We can trace, this association of the hare with the moon even deeper:  
śaśaś cāndramasa iti (in Śatapatha brāhmaṇa 11.1.5.3)  
It is said: “The hare is the moon.”

We have more extensive exposition of the same in the Jaiminīya brāhmaṇa:  
eṣa vai śaśo ya eṣo ‘ntaś candramasi। eṣa hīdaṃ sarvaṃ śāsti। eṣa vai yamo ya eṣo ‘ntaś candramasi। eṣa hīdaṃ sarvaṃ yamiti। eṣa mṛtyur yad yamo ‘tsyann eva nāma। tam eva tābhir āhutibhiś śamayitvorjaṃ lokānāṃ jayati yamaṃ deva devānām । yamasya devasya sāyujyaṃ salokatāṃ samabhyārohati ya evaṃ vidvān agnihotraṃ juhoti ॥ (in JB 1.28)

That indeed is the hare (śaśa), which is within the Moon (Candramas). For this one (Moon) rules (śāsti) over all this. That indeed is Yama, which is within the Moon. For this one restrains (yam dhatu) all this (the brāhmaṇa connects Yama, the root yam, ‘to restrain’). This is Death (Mṛtyu), for Yama is the name of one who “eats(=consumes)” (an interpretive etymology of Yama as one who consumes, hence Death). Through those offerings, having pacified Yama, the god of the gods, he conquers the strength (ūrja) of the worlds. He who, knowing this, performs the Agnihotra offering, rises up and attains to the union (sāyujyam), and the same world (salokatām) of the god Yama.

Remarkably, a connection between death, the moon and the hare is also seen in a Bushman tale from Southern Africa (see below).

We have noticed an even earlier reference to the Moon-hare in the Vedic tradition. To our knowledge, this is the earliest surviving mention of the hare in the moon in Indo-Aryan tradition. It is seen in a spell against the kidnappers of brāhmaṇa women in the Atharvaveda. This particular incantation is filled with astronomical allusions referring to a comet and the moon rising in the midst of a meteor shower.

yām āhus tārakaiṣā vikeśīti  
duchunāṃ grāmam avapadyamānām ।  
sā brahmajāyā vi dunoti rāṣṭraṃ  
yatra prāpādi śaśa ulkuṣīmān ॥ AV-vulgate 5.17.4  
She, whom they call “a star with disheveled hair” (comet)  
is the misfortune falling upon the village;  
she, the \[kidnapped\] brāhmaṇa ’s wife, burns up the kingdom  
where the hare (=moon) has arrived with meteors.

To our knowledge, no other Indo-European tradition has such a clear mythology of the hare on the moon. While some have tried to connect the hare and the Germanic goddess Eostre (a Germanic “mātṛkā”) with the moon, the evidence is slim. However, remarkably, this motif is prevalent across peoples speaking diverse unrelated language families in East Asia:  
China: The Jade Rabbit (Yùtù), companion of the moon goddess Chang’e, pounds out the elixir of life or medicinal herbs.  
Vietnam: The Jade Rabbit (Thỏ Ngọc), often associated with the Mid-Autumn Festival.  
Korea: The Rabbit (Daltokki) pounding ingredients for tteok (rice cakes).  
Japan: The Rabbit (Tsuki no Usagi) pounding ingredients for mochi (rice cakes).

This motif is seen further afield in the Americas:  
*North American natives –*  
Cree (modern Canada/US): A young Rabbit who wished to ride the Moon was taken there by a Crane. The rabbit’s weight stretched out the Crane’s legs, and the rabbit left a red mark on the Crane’s head. The rabbit’s shadow is still seen riding the Moon (oral folklore).

Mi’kmaq (Northeastern American Woodlands): A great hunter Rabbit caught the thieving “Man in the Moon” (a Moon spirit) in a snare. To ensure the Moon Man never returned to Earth, the Rabbit threw clay balls at his face, creating the dark spots (or image of the rabbit) seen on the Moon (oral folklore).

Algonquin tribes (Ottawa River Valley, modern Canada): The hare (Michabo/Nanabozho) is presented as a powerful, divine trickster figure/hero who is often described as being raised by his grandmother, the moon.

*Meso-American natives –*  
Aztec (Nahua): A rabbit was placed on the moon by the god Quetzalcoatl to reward its selfless sacrifice. We also have an alternative Aztec myth related to the origin mythology of the 5th Sun: To create the new (5th) sun and moon, the gods agreed that two of them should sacrifice themselves into the sacred fire. Nanahuatzin, a humble god, bravely jumped in first to become the sun. Tecciztécatl, an arrogant god, hesitated out of fear before finally leaping in and becoming the moon. Thus, there arose two bright bodies in the sky. Because Tecciztécatl hesitated, the gods decided he did not deserve to shine as brightly as the brave Nanahuatzin. To dim the moon’s luminosity, one of the other gods grabbed a hare/rabbit and threw it at the face of the moon, leaving a dark, permanent silhouette of the rabbit on the Moon’s surface. This reduced its light so it would only shine during the night.

The first Aztec tale and aspects of the second represent a case of striking long-distance convergence with the fable of the Sasa-jātaka.

Maya: The Moon Goddess is frequently depicted holding a rabbit. This presents a long-distance connection to the Chinese myth.

*South American natives –*  
Guaraní (Paraguay; unfortunately, we have only one poorly sourced oral report of this myth): The moon came down to Earth disguised as a woman and was attacked by hunters. A brave rabbit threw itself at the hunters to protect her. Touched by the rabbit’s bravery and selfless act, the moon carried it into the sky, where its silhouette was imprinted on the lunar surface for all time as an honor. Here again, we see a motif paralleling the Sasa-jātaka.

Finally, a myth connecting the Moon, the hare and death appears in the earliest branch of humanity, the Bushmen of Southern Africa (oral folklore). Among the Khoi, the myth is generally reported as follows:  
The Moon intended to send a message of immortality to humankind, saying, “As I die and rise to life again, so shall you die and rise to life again.” The hare, acting as the messenger, deliberately or mistakenly reversed the message, telling humans, “As I die and do not rise to life again, so you shall also die and not rise to life again.” In a fury over the false message, the Moon struck the hare on the mouth, splitting his lip (explaining the hare’s distinguishing feature). The Moon then decreed that, because the correct message was rejected, all mankind must now die altogether and never return.

The alternative version (supposedly from some San people) states that the hare was originally a human who argued with the Moon that his recently deceased mother is “altogether dead” and will not return, despite the Moon’s assurance of reincarnation. For his disbelief, the Moon struck him, turned him into a hare, split his lip, and cursed all humans with permanent death.

If these Bushmen myths have been reported correctly by their European interlocutors, then they represent a really long-range connection to the above-narrated brāhmaṇa passage of the Jaiminīya-s in terms of bringing together the themes of the moon, the hare and death. More generally, we have more than one long-range connection of specific themes in the “hare-on-the-moon” complex that are unlikely to have diffused in premodern times, given the absence of any sustained contact between the cultures in question. While there is the question of recording bias – whether early modern European oral folklore/myth collectors were biased by the tales in old, well-preserved literary traditions – the myths in question seem divergent enough from each other to believe that they were natural versions in those cultures.

Given that the Bushmen are the earliest branching lineage of extant humans, the simple interpretation is that a myth connecting the moon and the hare was already present in their last common ancestor. This would put this mythic complex at least 150,000 years before present, if not older. This would place the moon-rabbit connection as a (quasi)-astral myth of deep provenance. It probably joins the Pleiades as seven sisters with a disappearing member, in that early set of ancestral myths of Homo sapiens.

If we restrict ourselves to myths where the rabbit is explicitly placed on the moon, we have that among the Indo-Aryans, various East Asian groups (where there was clear diffusion from a single source) and various First American groups. Now, the Indo-Aryans are the only Indo-European group with this motif; however, it goes deep in their tradition, raising the possibility that its record was lost in other IE groups with less preserved traditions. Alternatively, the Indo-Aryans could have acquired it from one of the other substratal groups (e.g., BMAC, Harappans, Uralics) they spanned in the course of their peregrinations. In either case, the evidence does not favor it being acquired by the Indo-Aryans from the East Asians, with whom they intersected only later. This suggests that, in the least, the specific myth of the hare on the moon could have emerged in the common ancestor of extant non-African Homo sapiens. If this were the case, this ancestral myth would be at least 47,000 years old – the latest mean estimate of when the non-Africans radiated. Given the split between East Asians and the First Americans around 23-17 Kya, we can posit that the myth was prevalent around this time, prior to the Bering crossing in Northeast Asia. Thus, the innocuous mythic motif of the hare on the moon appears to be one of the best traceable mythemes transmitted from the misty depths of human existence.
