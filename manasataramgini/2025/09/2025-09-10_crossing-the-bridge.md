Crossing the Bridge

We have often wondered what was in the mind of the great mantravādin Bhāskararāya Makhīndra when he titled his commentary on the Nityāṣoḍaśikārṇava section of the Vāmakeśvara-tantra the Setubandha (building the bridge). Where was the bridge to, and what was it bridging? In that work, we see that he departs from his predecessor of the Kashmirian Śrīkula school (the student of Puṇyānandanātha, Amṛtānandanātha, who composed the original commentary on the said text, the Dīpikā) to present a new set of connections. The establishment of these connections could be seen as the building of a bridge. Thus, the name Setubandha could be interpreted in two ways. First, Bhāskararāya was attempting to find connections between the Vaidika and the Kaula mantra-mārga-s to present a unified view of the Mantra-śāstra. For example, right in the beginning, in explaining the name Bhairava, he first presents the Tantrika theory with the quotation: “bhairavaḥ sarva-śakti-bharitaḥ ।’’ and the Śivasūtra: “udyāmo bhairavaḥ ।’’. Thereafter, he builds a bridge to Śruti, stating that the idea of the fear-evoking Bhairava (bhairavatvād eva) might be found in the Veda itself by citing the Kaṭhopaniṣat’s account of the brahman: “mahad bhayaṃ vajram udyatam । (great is the fear, the thunderbolt is raised up).’’ Second, his title might be seen as a description of his effort, viz., the building of a bridge to cross over from ignorance to realization of the Kaulamārga.

Beyond this metaphor, the Śaiva-mantra-śastra also talks more directly about building real bridges. In the Niśvāsamukha 2.30c-2.35 of the Niśvāsa-saṃhitā, a scripture of the Rudrāgama division of the 28 root tantra-s of the Ūrdhva-srotas, we encounter the below:

asaṅkrama-pathe yas tu saṅkramaṃ kārayiṣyati ।  
dharmarāja-pathe so hi supathenaiva gacchati ।  
nadīṃ vaitaraṇīṃ caivam (?) uṣṇa-toyām mahāravām ॥  
gambhīra+avarta-dustārāṃ santaret saṅkrameṇa tu ।  
setubandhaṃ tu yaḥ kuryāt kardame pathi dāruṇe ॥  
dharmarāja-pure so hi durgame sukhayāyy asau ।  
paṅka-lepaś ca narakas tapta-trapu-jatuś ca yaḥ ॥  
saṃtaren narakān ghorān nālīmārga-prayāyinaḥ ।  
maṭhasyāvasthasyaiva maṇḍapasya ca kāriṇaḥ ॥  
dharmarāja-puraṃ gatvā svarge hema-mayaṃ gṛham ।  
taptāṅgāra-śilāvarṣe na bhayaṃ tasya vidyate ॥  
If one makes a causeway across an uncrossable path, then he will verily comfortably traverse the path of the Dharmarāja (Yama) down a comfortable route. He indeed will cross the great roaring boiling water of the river Vaitaraṇī, difficult to cross due to deep eddies, by a bridge. Indeed, if one constructs a bridge across a terrible, muddy path, one will have a pleasant journey to the abode of the Dharmarāja that is difficult to reach. He who makes water flow through an \[unclogged\] pipe will pleasantly cross the hells Paṅka-lepa (Dirt-smeared), Tapta-trapu (Molten-Tin) and Tapta-jatu (Molten-Wax). He who is the maker of a maṭha, a house or a pavilion, having attained the abode of the Dharmarāja, dwells in a golden house in heaven. He knows not the fear of the \[hells known as\] Taptāṅgāra (Hot embers) and Śilā-varṣa (Stone-shower).

When we first studied the above, it struck us that the tantra was reifying an ancient mytheme. While this tantra talks about building a physical bridge to karmically aid the crossing over to the other world, in the Taittirīya Śruti, we repeatedly encounter the following statement in the context of completing various rites, typically within the soma-ritual:

ākramaṇam eva tat setuṃ yajamānaḥ kurute suvargasya lokasya samaṣṭyai \|\|  
Indeed, the ritualist makes himself a ladder and a bridge to attain the world of heaven.

It is implied that, if the ritualist correctly completes the given rite, he is said to avail himself of a ladder to attain the heavenly realm (i.e., that of the gods). While this seems to talk of the heavenly realm, a closer examination reveals that even in the ancient Vedic texts, the bridge was also to Yama’s world, even as in the above Śaiva tantra. For example, we have the following enigmatic verse from the Ṛgveda:

yatra rājā vaivasvato  
yatrāvarodhanaṃ divaḥ ।  
yatrāmūr yahvatīr āpas  
tatra mām amṛtaṃ kṛdhi+  
indrāyendo pari srava ॥ RV 9.113.8 (Paṅkti)  
Where \[Yama\] Vaivasvata is king  
where the path descends from heaven  
where those ever-flowing waters are,  
there, make me immortal!  
For Indra, O Soma drop, flow around!

Interpreting the word avarodhana in this verse has been problematic. One set of students of the śruti sees it to mean an enclosure. However, we take the contrary view that it means a descending path – a legitimate alternative interpretation of the word. This interpretation is strengthened by its juxtaposition with the ever-flowing or roiling waters – the cognate of the Vaitaraṇī of the later text – a motif with deeper Indo-European cognates (see below). Thus, we take the avarodhana to be none other than the bridge taken to the realm of Yama as in the above tantric passage.

Looking deeper into the śruti, we find further references to the divine bridge. The Atharvan-s have an incantation for crossing the bridge over to the realm of the gods. It is potentially an allusion to the journey taken after death, because prior to this one (AV-P 5.6.8), there is a mantra to Agni Vaiśvānara, the path-maker, to lead one along the path of Yama Vaivasvata (AV-P 5.6.7), which seems to be none other than the avarodhana of the above verse.

mahi jyotir nihitaṃ martyeṣu  
yena devāso atarann arātīn ।  
tenemaṃ setum ati geṣma sarve  
vaiśvānaraṃ jyotir amīva devāḥ ॥ (AV-Paippalāda Saṃhitā 5.6.8)  
A great light is placed among the mortals,  
by which the gods overcame the enemies.  
With that (light), we all will cross this bridge  
to the light of Vaiśvānara like the yonder gods.

The connection to Agni Vaiśvānara presented in this verse is further reminiscent of a connection between the bridge and the Agni found in the Taittirīya Brāhmaṇa 2.4.2.6:

tigmaśṛṅgo vṛṣabhaḥ śośucānaḥ ।  
pratnaṃ sadhastham anupaśyamānaḥ ।  
ā tantum agnir divyaṃ tatāna ।  
tvaṃ nas tantur uta setur agne ।  
tvaṃ panthā bhavasi devayānaḥ ।  
tvayā agne pṛṣṭhaṃ vayam āruhema ।  
athā devaiḥ sadhamādaṃ madema ॥  
The bull, with sharp horns, blazing resplendently,  
perceiving the ancient seat,  
Agni stretched out the heavenly thread.  
You, O Agni, are our \[connecting\] thread and also our bridge.  
You become the path leading to the gods.  
by you, O Agni, may we ascend to the \[heavenly\] height,  
and then rejoice in the banquet together with the gods.

The motif of the bridge also reappears in the Sāmaveda, where we have the Setu-sāman:

hā u 3, setūṃs tara 3, dustarān 3, dānenādānaṃ 3,  
hā u 3, aham asmi prathamajā ṛtāsyā ।  
hā u 3, setūṃs tara 3, dustarān 3, akrodhena krodhaṃ 3,  
hā u 3, pūrvaṃ devebhyo amṛtasya nāma ।  
hā u 3, setūṃs tara 3, dustarān 3, śraddhayā aśraddhāṃ 3,  
hā u 3, yo mā dadāti sa id evam āvāt ।  
hā u 3, setūṃs tara 3, dustarān 3, satyenānṛtaṃ 3,  
hā u 3, aham annam annam adantam ādmiḥ ।  
hā u 3 vā, eṣā gatiḥ 3, etad amṛtaṃ 3,  
svar gaccha 3, jyotir gaccha 3,  
setūṃs tīrtvā caturāḥ ॥

Here, we again see the crossing of four difficult-to-cross metaphorical bridges: niggardliness, anger, lack of conviction, and falsehood, which are crossed by their opposites. Each of these crossings is then linked to a realization sung by Bhṛgu in the Bhṛguvalli, the third book of the Upaniṣat of the Taittirīyaka-s (TU3.10.6). There again, that song is said to be sung by the spirit of the realized one who, having departed from this world, wanders through the many worlds assuming any form of his liking. In the Setu-sāman, too, upon crossing the four difficult bridges and experiencing the four realizations of Bhṛgu, one attains immortality and goes to the realm of the gods (svar) and the realm of light (jyotiḥ)

These Hindu incantations present a parallel to the Iranian tradition of the Cinuvant bridge. The bridge is said to have been made by Ahura Mazda (Mazda-δāta) and is called “well-known from afar” (dūraē srūta), suggesting that it was seen as something prominent and clearly visible. The spirit of a deceased Iranian who has performed good deeds is said to cross over it on the third day after his death, aided by the goddess Daena, accompanied by dogs (c.f., the dogs of Yama Vaivasvata):

Then comes the beautiful, well-shapen, strong and well-formed maid (Daena), with the dogs at her sides, one who can distinguish, who has many children, happy, and of high understanding.  
She makes the soul of the righteous one go up above the Hara-berezaiti; above the Cinuvant bridge she places it in the presence of the heavenly gods themselves. Vendidad 19.30  
-translation by Darmesteter.

In contrast, if the Iranian has led a wicked life, an ugly maiden confronts his spirit, and it fails to cross the bridge. It falls from its heights into the abyss of hell. These specific features suggest that the bridge motif goes back to the unified Indo-Iranian tradition. The motif appears farther afield in the North Germanic world in the form of the Bifröst Bridge (= Æsir-bridge: the bridge of the gods). In the Poetic Edda (Grímnismál), we see:

“Körmt and Örmt (the two boiling rivers) and the two Kerlaugar (=cauldrons) / Thor must wade through each day when he goes to judgment by Yggdrasil’s ash; / for the Æsir-bridge burns all with flame, and the sacred waters seethe.’’

While the god Thor wades across the boiling rivers, the Prose Edda mentions that the Bifröst is the daily route for the gods to Urðarbrunnr, the sacred well beneath Yggdrasil (the world tree), where they hold court. It is guarded by the god Heimdallr from the Jötnar (“the giants”). The fieriness of the Germanic bridge is reminiscent of the Hindu version of the bridge being potentially equivalent to the path made by Agni Vaiśvānara. Outside of these, we have seen references to the bridge in Baltic and Slavic folklore. However, we have not ascertained the provenance of these references. In any case, the presence of this bridge in the Vedic, Iranian and Germanic traditions establishes it as a mythic motif going back to at least core PIE.

While the bridge in question might sound like a purely “mythic” element, we believe that its various reflexes across different IE traditions consistently point to an astronomical inspiration. The prose Edda refers to the Bifröst as the rainbow, with the red color representing the fire that prevents the Jötnar from crossing. However, there are features suggesting that the original understanding was something different: Thor and the other gods are said to journey \*daily\* to hold court, and it is associated with the world-tree (Yggdrasil), a metaphor for the world axis. This suggests that it was associated with a diurnal phenomenon potentially related to the daily revolution of the celestial vault. This connection is also seen in the Taittirīya Saṃhitā on the Indo-Aryan side:

dvau samudrau vitatāv ajūryau  
paryāvartete jaṭhareva pādāḥ ।  
tayoḥ paśyanto ati yanty anyam  
apaśyantaḥ setunā (a)ti yanty anyam ॥  
dve dradhasī satatī vasta ekaḥ  
keśī viśvā bhuvanāni vidvān ।  
tirodhāyaity asitaṃ vasānaḥ  
śukram ā datte anuhāya jāryai ॥ (TS 3.2.2.2)  
Two oceans are there extended, unperishing;  
They revolve in turns like beams in the cosmic cavern (jaṭhara);  
Seeing, they pass over one of them,  
Seeing not, they pass over the other with a bridge.  
Two garments continuously the one wears;  
the hairy one, knowing all the worlds;  
He goes hidden, clad in the dark \[robe\];  
He puts on the bright one, abandoning the worn-out one.

While highly metaphorical, this verse, recited by the adhvaryu during the third soma pressing when he places the soma stalk in the upāṃśu-graha, has some clear diurnal and astronomical meanings. First, the two interchanging oceans, the two alternating bright and dark garments worn by the hairy one (the sun), and the ability to see and not see are indicative of the day and the night rotating in the sky. Notably, the bridge is associated with the period of not being able to see, i.e., the night. This suggests that it was a feature that appeared in the night sky. Now, the Iranian account stating that the Cinuvant could be perceived from afar is consistent with it being a feature visible in the sky. Finally, the Iranian account gives us a vital clue of the two dogs being stationed on the Cinuvant or accompanying Daena on it. This immediately points our attention to the region of the sky with two famed dogs – Canis Major and Canis Minor. Hence, the bridge and river, which are associated with these dogs, should be in their vicinity. The Greeks, too, have rivers that are crossed to reach the other world, but rather than a bridge, they have a boat for the crossing, suggesting that the river motif was an ancient IE one.

Hence, we posit that the metaphor of the river/the ever-youthful or roiling waters was based on the Milky Way seen in the vicinity, and the bridge was none other than the ecliptic. Given that the Equinoctial colure passed through this general region in the period of early core Indo-Europeans, we believe that the bridge might have also been used in a specific sense of the “bridge” between the northern and the southern ecliptic (the Deva-nakṣatra-s and the Yama-nakṣatra-s: the realm of the gods and the realm of Yama) that was positioned where the equatorial plane cut the ecliptic. This proposal satisfactorily accounts for all the motifs juxtaposed in traditions surrounding the bridge: 1. The path of light associated with Agni Vaiśvānara: the ecliptic traversed by the sun. 2. The bridge to the realm of the gods: the Deva-nakṣatra section of the ecliptic. 3. The bridge to the realm of Yama Vaivasvata: the Yama-nakṣatra section of the ecliptic. 4. The world axis (Yggdrasil) defined by the equatorial plane, which intersects the ecliptic plane in this region. 5. River or the roiling waters (the Milky Way). 6. The two canine constellations.
