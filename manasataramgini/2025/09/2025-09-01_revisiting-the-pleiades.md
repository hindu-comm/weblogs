Revisiting the Pleiades

The Pleiades (Kṛttikāḥ), probably the visually most dramatic object in the Earthly skies, emerged quite recently in typical astronomical time scales. Multiple independent studies place their origin between 100 and 120 million years ago (Mya). Low-mass stars lose lithium at a predictable rate, making it a potential clock. Applying this to relevant members of the Pleiades, one gets an age window of 107-117 Mya, which is perhaps the best estimate of their age to date. Thus, it is rather amazing to contemplate that the Pleiades first appeared in the skies above the heads of the large predatory carcharodontosaurs, the giant titanosaurian sauropods and iguanodontid herbivores. This is quite a delight for an astronomer, for it gives a snapshot of a young group of stars ( ![\approx \tfrac{1}{41}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Capprox+%5Ctfrac%7B1%7D%7B41%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) the age of the Sun, a middle-aged star at 4.57 billion years) and a window into their evolutionary trajectory.

How many Pleiades are there? Most ordinary people see 6-7 of them. The Taittirīya brāhmaṇa lists 7 of them in the Nakṣatreṣṭi: Ambā, Dulā, Nitatni, Abhrayanti, Meghayanti, Varṣayanti and Cupuṇīkā. The later Kaumāra tradition sees 6, with one being “lost” or separated from the rest (see below). Remarkably, some medieval observers, like Tycho, saw many more of these stars than regular observers. With exceptional skies and exceptional sight, you could potentially see 25 of them. When my eyesight was good, I have managed 9 under exceptional skies in central Texas, USA.

Just as one of the Kṛttikā-s was “left behind” in the Hindu Kaumāra myth, a comparable myth exists in the Greek tradition of a lost Pleiad. This mythic motif is also found farther afield outside the Indo-European world among the Black Africans in the Gold Coast, Australian aborigines, First Americans of the Northern and Southern American continents and Austronesian groups. This has led to the hypothesis that one of the Pleiades faded away in human memory. Whether this really happened remains hotly debated, and it is unclear if it occurred in the remote past and was passed down through myth or if the idea of its fading emerged more recently via independent observations. We favor the idea that the lost Pleiad myth is an ancient one going back deep in human history, probably when Homo sapiens was confined to Africa. If this were the case, the fading event was probably earlier than ![\approx 60](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Capprox+60&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002)K years. The Greek myth names Merope (=23 Tauri), the second star of the “trough” of the Pleiades, as the lost one. However, Merope is clearly seen these days and seems an unlikely candidate, unless the old Greeks reckoned the names differently. Another possible candidate is (Pleione = BU Tauri), the fainter of the stars in the handle (beside the brighter and clearly visible Atlas). There are two, not mutually exclusive, hypotheses that make Merope attractive. First, it is calculated that ![\approx 100](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Capprox+100&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002)K years ago, the separation between Atlas and Merope was much greater, making it more visible to the naked eye. This time horizon fits well with the idea of the myth emerging deep in human history in Africa. Second, Merope, while still on the Main Sequence with the luminosity of about 190 Suns, is a Gamma Cassiopeiae-type variable, which even today fluctuates between 3 states: (i) a regular B star; (ii) a Be star with a ring around it contributing to the emission lines in its spectrum; (iii) a Be shell star with a discharged shell around it which causes a drop in brightness. Indeed, other Gamma Cassiopeiae-type variables are known to show striking brightening and fading events: in the 1930s, the founding member of the group, Gamma Cassiopeiae, erupted to around magnitude 1.6 and faded away to 3.4 in the course of a shell discharge event. Thus, Merope could indeed have been brighter in the past and then faded, triggering the lost Pleiad myth.

[Around 9 years ago, we had taken a look at the Pleiades using the Hipparcos data](https://manasataramgini.wordpress.com/2016/08/08/some-astronomical-experiments/). There, we had discussed a famous issue with the parallaxes of Pleiades obtained by Hipparcos and said the issue would be resolved once its successor, Gaia, completed its mission and released the data. In 2023 and 2024, there have been publications on the Pleiades using the Gaia data (Heyl et al and Chulkov et al) that have finally settled the parallax issue. We recapitulate aspects of these studies to delve into some of the new things we have learned about the Pleiades. The Pleiades were one of my earliest objects of telescopic observation – indeed, the telescope reveals its true glory with many more members becoming visible. That leads back to the question, just how many Pleiades are there? Using the Gaia studies that record parallax and proper motion, one can come up with a candidate list of 1345 members with good confidence. In the map in Figure 1, we have plotted all of them, showing how much of the sky it covers. It also shows that some relatively bright members are quite distant from the core (e.g., star HD 23950 at magnitude 6.04).

For plotting, we used the flux-magnitude relationship ![F \propto 10^{-0.4m}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=F+%5Cpropto+10%5E%7B-0.4m%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002). The area of the circle ![A](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=A&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) representing a star in the map is proportional to the flux ![F](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=F&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002); hence, the stars are plotted with radius ![r \propto \sqrt{A} \implies r \propto 10^{-0.2m}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=r+%5Cpropto+%5Csqrt%7BA%7D+%5Cimplies+r+%5Cpropto+10%5E%7B-0.2m%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002). This yields the formula ![r = k \cdot 10^{-0.2(m-m_0)}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=r+%3D+k+%5Ccdot+10%5E%7B-0.2%28m-m_0%29%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002), where ![k](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=k&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is a proportionality constant and ![m_0](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=m_0&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) is the lowest magnitude (i.e., highest value) in the dataset.

One of the key findings from Gaia is the identification of a whole host of escapees. Numerous stars were gravitationally propelled out of the Pleiades almost all over the sky. Figure 2 plots the escapees. Here, the core density is the region plotted in Figure 1 – the core members. The escapees include multiple bright members, e.g., the 5.1 magnitude GS Tauri (=41 Tauri). Ironically, science has recapitulated myth in identifying Pleiades escapees distributed over much of the sky. In H tradition, it was believed that Arundhati (Alcor) was the “lost Pleiad,” while the others were cast out.

Pleiades and escapes in the Right Ascension range ![(100^o,0^o)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%28100%5Eo%2C0%5Eo%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) and Declension range ![c(-50^o,88^o)](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=c%28-50%5Eo%2C88%5Eo%29&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002). While this covers the vast majority of the escapees, there are a few outside of this range.

One of the recent mysteries pertaining to the Pleiades was the issue of its distance. The first large collection of stellar parallaxes was performed by the Hipparcos satellite. The results of this mission gave a distance of ![\approx 118](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Capprox+118&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) parsecs ![\approx](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Capprox&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) 385 light years for the Pleiades. This was at odds with all previous Earth-based estimates that indicated the open cluster as lying farther away. This issue was finally settled by the Gaia observations. Figure 3 shows the distribution of the parallaxes of the core members, and Figure 4 shows the parallaxes of the escapees. The core members show a mean and median parallax of 7.37 milliarcseconds, with a normal distribution around it. This indicates that the distance of the Pleiades from Earth is ![\approx 135.6](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Capprox+135.6&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) pc ![\approx 442.4](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Capprox+442.4&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002) ly, in line with earlier Earth-based estimates.

[![](https://manasataramgini.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/fig3_pleiades_core_plx.png?w=640)](https://manasataramgini.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/fig3_pleiades_core_plx.png)Figure 3. Parallax distribution of the core members of the Pleiades cluster

The parallax distribution for the escapees is more skewed, consistent with them being pushed out of the cluster (mean = 8.2 mas, median = 7.39). This resolution of the Pleiades parallax issue indicated that the presence of several close-by stars in the same field resulted in systematic correlated errors in the original determination of parallax by Hipparcos.

[![](https://manasataramgini.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/fig4_pleiades_e_plx.png?w=640)](https://manasataramgini.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/fig4_pleiades_e_plx.png)Figure 4. Parallax distribution of the escapees of the Pleiades cluster.

Once we have the distance of the stars, we can calculate their absolute magnitude. Their absolute magnitudes (Figure 5) show an interesting distribution with a peak around 11.75 and a median of 10.42. Thus, the most common type of star in the Pleiades is a faint dwarf, even though bright giants and subgiants visually dominate the cluster. This also indirectly gives a picture of the distribution of stellar masses during the formation of open clusters, with a core comprised of massive stars and a halo of low mass stars. What we do not know for sure is whether the shape of this distribution reflects reality or is merely a consequence of the magnitude depth to which Gaia’s space telescope operated.

[![](https://manasataramgini.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/fig5_pleiades_absolute_mag.png?w=640)](https://manasataramgini.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/fig5_pleiades_absolute_mag.png)Figure 5. Absolute magnitude distribution of the Pleiades.

The Gaia survey used two passbands, Bp (Blue Photometer) in the wavelength range of 330 to 680 nm, and Rp (Red Photometer) in the wavelength range of 640 to 1050 nm. Based on this, it provides a Bp-Rp color index, i.e., the difference between the magnitudes measured by these two photometers: ![m\_{Bp}-m\_{Rp}](https://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=m_%7BBp%7D-m_%7BRp%7D&bg=ffffff&fg=333333&s=0&c=20201002). In this index, O-B stars have a Bp-Rp of −0.5 to 0.0; Sun-like stars (G2V) are around 0.82; M stars have Bp-Rp in the range 2-3.5. Thus, using the Bp-Rp color index as a substitute for spectral type, one can plot the Gaia Hertzsprung-Russell diagram for the Pleiades (data available for 1561 members and escapees; Figure 6).

[![](https://manasataramgini.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/fig6_pleiades_hr.png?w=640)](https://manasataramgini.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/fig6_pleiades_hr.png)Figure 6. The H-R diagram for 1561 Pleiades members and escapees.

It is rather obvious that, while the blue giants and subgiants dominate the cluster visually, the bulk of the stars in the cluster are in the Main Sequence (Luminosity class V): in fact, the Sun-like yellow stars and K and M red dwarfs are numerically vastly more dominant, though generally too faint to be seen through small instruments. Indeed, having lived fast and furious lives, the giants like Alcyone will die young, while the Sun-likes and the red dwarfs will live on for billions of years as the cluster loses its visual prominence in the distant future. One also finds cool subdwarfs (below the Main Sequence ) in the orange-red range. These are low metallicity stars, viz., stars that are low in elements heavier than Helium.
