# Understanding Change

### It's a new year again and there will be resolutions for change. But why do we desire that?

It’s that time of the year again— a beginning. And beginnings make us hopeful that there could be changes in store. We make resolutions of all sorts— to get in shape, to be more outgoing, to be more thoughtful, to spend time with loved ones, to pursue a hobby or a dream, and so on. Yet, most of these resolutions fail and it’s easy to laugh it all off because for most part, life goes on. Life itself is constantly changing and so our personal desire for change gets lost in the chaos. It still shows up every year though. So, in the spirit of a new year resolution, let’s try to understand change.

Change, on a personal level, is often an attempt at getting closer to our ideal realities as oxymoronic as that sounds. Getting in shape, for instance, can be about fitness or health but it can also be about looking better for our peer groups and photos. Pursuing a hobby or a dream like learning to play a musical instrument can be way to get in touch with oneself but it can also be a wild goose chase. In other words, change can be superficial or in-depth. When it’s former, it’s usually short-lived and we revert to a previous status quo. But when it’s latter, it usually leads to transformation and rebirth. Which means, the real question when we desire change is— *do I want to seem or do I want to be?*

The modern society makes the former easier and the latter more difficult than ever before in human history. The two actually feed into each other. While the world has always been a stage, as Shakespeare wrote in 1599, the stage has never been more obvious as it is today. It is the human desire to maintain appearances that led to our social media platforms and their current popularity. True to the form, even infants can have Instagram accounts today, handled by their parents. Parents want to showcase their parenthood and children, like any parent in the history of humanity, and share their joys with the world. But that world is no more limited to one’s immediate family, relatives, and friends around. Today, that can be anyone on the internet. Which means, when we post online, we are not just trying to resonate with our small circle but the entire world in some way or the other. Taken to an extreme, it can lead to young girls and boys being stalked by random strangers online as [this](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/02/22/us/instagram-child-influencers.html) New York Times story details. Yet, it was all supposed to be just good fun. It was supposed to be about the joys of childhood and being a new parent.

But being a child is also about being lost and confused, as is being a new parent. Once again, we turn to the online world and Google searches only to find a million answers which is somehow worse than not having any answer. In an age where technology is rapidly changing the world, the norms through which most communities navigated life are increasingly becoming irrelevant. These norms are being upended, hollowed out, and worse, being warped out of their depth. Because of this, it is becoming more and more difficult for one generation to pass on their wisdom to the next one because what it knew in its context often doesn’t apply anymore. What the new generation needs to know in its context often doesn’t have any ready-made answers. The marketplace of technologies are not just that— stuff that makes an activity easier— but an entirely new reality that must be confronted like an unknown territory, full of dangers and challenges. In other words, the modern society is inflicting in-depth change on us if we are to keep up with its infinite surface-level changes.

In his book, When We Sold God’s Eye (Hachette Grand Central, 2024), the journalist Alex Cuadros details the experience of an Indigenous group, Cinta Larga, as they came out of their Amazonian forest life into the modern civilisation of Brazil’s smaller cities in 1970s. The people who lived an entirely self-contained life, sleeping on hammocks and eating shared meals, all of it without clothes, now had to contend with the idea of money, flip-flops, and man-made legal systems. Once proud warriors and hunters, the Cinta Larga men developed potbellies because of the “civilised” food— sugary soda beverages, processed rice and beans, and a host of other things they had no conception of back when they ate animal meat from the forest without any spices, sugar or salt. In their case, the potbelly is not a sign of bad health but intense upheaval.

Cuadros writes insightfully of their predicament and the Cinta Larga continue to grapple with unfamiliar concepts like a modern government, weekends, Church, and diamonds. Throughout their journey, they also exhibit profound existential crises. They felt caught between their life in the forest which was bare-bones but good enough for them and the modern city life which was alluring but also felt alien. They now wanted expensive cars, watches, modern housing, and the like, only to realise the futility of them. They regret not choosing the path of education and wonder if their desires led them astray?

How we understand reality has a lot to do with our environment, people around us, and how we have been taught to think right from our birth. When that is shattered, it can be difficult to find the ground beneath one’s feet, let alone a new vision for future. Yet, that has been the perennial task of being human. Countless civilisations have come and gone, which means countless ways of being have come and gone. Yet, the humanity continues to survive and thrive. While the Cinta Larga have been tasked with a greater degree of change to deal with, our task as humans living through the age of rapid change remains the same— to let ourselves be transformed at a deeper level so that we don’t get washed away with the strong currents of societal changes.

And yet, what is this deep, transformative change? How can one go through a process of death and rebirth while still being alive? The example of Cinta Larga serves as a helpful indicator again— to persevere even when one’s surface reality crumbles and crawl back to life again in one’s new reality despite the many stumbling blocks. Cinta Larga felt guided by their conscience rather than any authority figure or group idea per se and perhaps, that is what eventually saved them from destroying themselves entirely.

To survive is an innate human tendency but perseverance takes something more. Survival makes life seem urgent where every up has to be capitalised upon and every down has to be minimised right away. Perseverance shows us that this dance of life’s ups and downs is unchanging, which is why there is something else that must anchor us. To persevere is get so deeply in touch with oneself— one’s conscience, values, and deeply held aspirations— that one can be whole again even if one’s reality becomes paradoxical for a while. This is why, true change can come only from perseverance against all odds because it reveals what really needs to be changed. It is what takes us from the state of *seeming* to that of *being*. In being, we become more of ourselves which is really the change we desire every year in form of our resolutions. But for that to happen, the phoenix has to first rise from the ashes of its illusions before it can fly into its wisdom. If that is the change you desire this year, then may this year help you find it. And if not, then may this year bring you joy anyway. Happy New Year!
