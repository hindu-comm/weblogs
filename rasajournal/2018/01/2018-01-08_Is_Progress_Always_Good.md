# Is Progress Always Good?

### If progress is defined in material terms, then is it always better to have more? Or is there more to the idea of progress?

I recently came across the following words by Pope Leo, which I thought were interesting to be coming from the highest quarters of Vatican. Addressing a gathering, he said,   
  
“Today, a flourishing life is often confused with a materially wealthy life or a life of unrestricted individual autonomy. The so-called ideal future presented to us is often one of technological convenience and consumer satisfaction. Yet we know this is not enough. We see this in affluent societies where many people are struggling with loneliness, despair and a sense of meaninglessness. Authentic human flourishing stems from what the Church calls integral human development, or the full development of a person in all dimensions: physical, social, cultural, moral, and spiritual. This vision for the human person is rooted in natural law, the moral order that God has written on the human heart, whose deeper truths are illuminated by the Gospel of Christ.”

Of course, it is his job to pull more and more people into the fold of Christianity but how he chose to do that here is equally noteworthy. As an American, Pope Leo is hailed as a more progressive papal head than most of his predecessors. So, why does he denounce the modern idea of progress? Part of the answer lies in his own explanation that progress cannot be merely material but should be physical, social, cultural, moral, and spiritual too.

Our societies today are organised towards one common aim— more material progress. Every aspect of our lives is now centred towards having more wealth, more consumerism, and more power. Little wonder, then, that this has resulted in a much greater desire for personal autonomy. Progress today has transformed into progressivism, i.e., an unconscious belief that we need more of what we already have. We race ahead, across the world, towards this common goal, all the while battling its consequences such as climactic disturbances, unmanageable waste production, a rapidly deteriorating societal fabric, and more.

We can understand this with an example. Once we started making cars, it did not take much longer to figure out how to make more of them at cheaper prices. That meant that what was a luxury for a select few quickly became a necessity for common people across the world. Where most people travelled on foot, hand driven carriages, buses, and the like, they could now travel by car. But was it progress to require that much more petrol to fuel these cars, factories to make them, dump yards to discard them? We are on the verge of a yet another automobile revolution— electric cars. We are told they are environmentally progressive yet no one bothers to address the question of the monumental wastage that would incur if we were to en masse discard existing petrol and diesel cars around the world.

Is this progress or progressivism, then? True progress should effectively raise our consciousness and conduct, rather than being a mere change of medium through which we live. This progress cannot be solely be external or internal but a dynamic alignment of both. In other words, modern progress, which is more material in nature, would have been more meaningful had it made us better human beings too. Because if being more materially abundant cannot make us more generous, satisfied, and grateful, then what will? Turning to a higher reality may cure us of our self-absorption but ultimately, inner progress too is more complicated than we imagine. It is an active process of meaning-making, acceptance, and upholding our higher values at all costs.

It is this progress that is missing our societies today. In its place, we have a highly individualistic approach to reality where the work of creating meaning is outsourced to mass media, acceptance is performative, and values are often flexible. As much as we seek progress, it is worth our while to understand the true meaning of it for ourselves.
