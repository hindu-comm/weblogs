# Being And Becoming

### How modernity keeps us from being and becoming our true selves despite its emphasis on individualism.

If you are a certain kind of a person, at some point you will begin to realise that it has never been in interest of a functional society for every individual to realise their true self. A healthy society contains truth in its foundations, which is reflected in its ideas, goals, and values. For this reason, it works out well-enough for societies if most people live out their individual lives on surface. As long as they subscribe to the norms of their society in a life-affirming way, the society sustains them and they sustain the society. It is a symbiotic relationship that is in the best-interests of everyone. Without this, both suffer in chaos.

Which is why, those who escape this dynamic do so out of compulsion— artists, sages, warriors, and the like. They exist on the fringes of this society not out of rebellion but because their callings require them to answer to something higher and personal than their societal norms. They can contain their individual egos and emotional complexes for the greater good of the society and for this reason, they are *allowed* to escape its norms. Sometimes (not always), they are celebrated for their efforts because they provide the crucial meaning to a society and how to achieve it. They lay down the roadmap that the society follows and fight the battles that it needs to survive. Yet, without the society, they have no meaning of their own either. This too is a symbiotic relationship, albeit a subtle one.

Yet, something changed with the evolution of the written word, i.e., printing press. While it is not accepted conventionally but a strong argument could be made that printing press built the modern society. I will not get into it here in detail but to summarise the argument, I will describe what the printing press did to the essential dynamic of a healthy society outlined above. The words, insights, and efforts of the outliers of a society were no more contained within them but became immortalised in books first and eventually, all manner of mass media. For most of our history, artists relied on a king’s patronage or became travelling gypsies; sages relied on monasteries for their knowledge, rituals, and meditations; and only warriors had the courage to protect us from malcontents of a society or a rival tribe. But whatever they said or did, belonged to them. No one could lay a claim on it, only participate in it. The hierarchies were harsh but clear.

With books (and ultimately mass media), however, this hierarchy was not only broken but subsequently erased. Everything these outliers did or said became accessible to everyone at their leisure at no cost. This cost was an essential factor because it distinguished the outliers from the masses in order to become good enough a vessel to receive higher wisdom and express it in some way or the other. But with democratisation of these symbiotic relationships, you could artificially embody these realities even if you did not “understand” art or were not inclined towards higher realities or were not physically or emotionally strong.

Books were a new kind of a technology that freed us from the messy work of being a human which was previously outsourced to the outliers of a society. With this, modern societies began to embody the outliers as the norm but only on the surface, as societies always do. At the same time, the actual outlier essence began to hollow out. This is a devastating subterfuge unique to modernity which has severed both of its symbiotic relationships and has essentially shattered the idea of itself as a functional society.

We see this more clearly with the advent of social media and artificial intelligence. When the algorithm decides what kind of art, policies, and tech most people want, it tends to degrade the quality of the best a society can offer to itself. That is because the consensus in such a case rests upon the lowest common denominator in a society as opposed to the highest one in pre-modern and pre-literate societies. It is profoundly paradoxical but it is also factually true.

Because while we still make good art, have good insights, and are willing to fight for goodness, we do not ascribe the same meaning to these anymore as we used to until a few centuries ago. No one expected Beethoven to belt out a hit number every year or demand that Kalidas write an enchanting poem every season or pay Achilles to fight a tough battle every week or look forward to Pyramids in every major city of the world. Yet, that is precisely what we expect today. We want everything all the time everywhere.

So what we get in return is an ocean of garbage that has not only blocked our vision from ever finding the pearls of treasure but is also actively killing us in many ways. Increasing supply does not translate into demands being fulfilled in a meaningful way and neither does an overconsumption of art, wisdom, and emotional depth make us more of a human being. The balance that came with paying the natural costs of being ourselves in a pre-modern era was the balance that sustained not just us but also our societies. With the space for outliers and their essence being shorn of meaning, what we are left with are increasingly confused masses that do not know how to ascend to higher realities while being in an even more desperate need of it.

It is because of this reason that despite modern emphasis on individuality, we struggle to realise our true selves. We see these trends intensifying today and they will likely become more grave as the time goes. What’s required here is a wilful and a determined remembrance of true art, wisdom, and courage so that we may begin to find our true selves. Without this, we cannot bring back the stability, compassion, and trust into our societies in order to rebuild them.

Modernity hooks us all into the same idea— travel the world like a tourist, shop, eat, and drink the best you can and as much as you can, curate your life according to trending aesthetics, replace everything slow and natural with everything fast and “smart tech” because you deserve the best. Because *this is the best*. Yet, what does one’s true self have anything to do with any of this? To be your true self is to live your actual life, however boring, ordinary, and strange it might be. To be your true self is to realise that your fears can teach you something about yourself and your life and at the same time, your dreams are not just yours but belong to everyone and everything around you in some measure. To be your true self is not to impose yourself on the world to fulfil your whims and fancies but to absorb the world in all its imperfections and still offer it fully whatever you can. To be your true self is to be on the journey of being and becoming, rather than a constant compulsion to be *seen* for the sake of applause, rewards, or validation. There’s no better time to do so than in this age of individualism yet how many will?

I’ll leave you with this from Carl Jung as some year-end inspiration, “the privilege of a lifetime is to become who you truly are”. May the new year bring you more truth, depth, and joy! Wishing you a Happy New Year!
