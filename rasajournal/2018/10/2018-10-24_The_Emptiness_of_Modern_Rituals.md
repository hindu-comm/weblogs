# The Emptiness of Modern Rituals

In 1517, a young monk in Germany unwittingly set in motion a turn of events which would lead to unbelievably earth-shattering consequences. Martin Luther, born to a mining trader, was meant to become a lawyer but fate had other plans, as it always does. He found his calling in the path of God and had accepted the monastic life in his youth. Yet, he was no ordinary monk. He had a strong mind and an even stronger way with the words. So when he witnessed something he did not like, he did not shy away from pointing it out even if it meant questioning the highest authority on the planet. Frustrated with the Roman Catholic Church’s indiscriminate printing of indulgences, he published his opinions in a document titled, Ninety-Five Thesis. His criticism of the practice and the Church spread across Germany and eventually Europe like wildfire, leading to significant changes in not just how Christianity was practiced but also how modernity would come to understand reality itself.

Indulgences were essentially a certificate issued by the Church which meant that the recently departed ones may be forgiven for their sins sooner and find their journey to heavens being sped up. This was a comfort to grieving family members who had to say certain prayers or perform a particular pilgrimage to help their loved ones beyond the grave. The practice was so popular that the Roman Catholic Church used it as a major fundraising measure during the construction of St. Peter’s Basilica in Vatican City in early 16th century. This meant that indulgences were being printed left, right, and centre for believers to avail this convenience. Effectively, indulgences might have been the first example of something being commercialised. It was this aspect that bothered Luther and which resonated with his readers the most. Eventually, it led to what is now known as the Protestant Reformation which sought to banish not just rituals, customs, and traditions within Catholicism but also to establish a personal relationship between a human being and the higher realities in all aspects of life.

Globalisation today means we’re all subject to influences that originated in different parts of the world and capitalism ensures that almost all of these influences come to us as a commodity. This is why it’s common to see Westerners spend more money on yoga mats, classes, athleisure, and a host of paraphernalia like scented candles in order to practice yoga whereas Indians, especially older ones, will simply get the most inexpensive floor mat and proceed with their practice free-of-cost in their night-wear in a public park. At the same time, while Italians may not make too much of their lasagnas or French of their pain au chocolats, Indians (and in fact, much of the Global South), may attach an aspirational value to these delicacies even if they come at significantly inflated prices.

However, it’s all very well to exchange our cultures for a monetary value. The problem arises when there is no actual culture being exchanged. Take, for example, the recent launch of Apple’s yet another smartphone. The American company marketed the launch in India much like it does in US. This meant that consumers started lining up outside its flagship store in Mumbai a day before, even sleeping on the pavement during the night. When the doors opened in the morning, Apple employees cheered for the first consumers entering the store, almost as if they had completed a tough pilgrimage. And yet, this indeed is what passes for a ritual and a pilgrimage now— the company is the deity and the product is the religious offering for which we pay out of our pockets. The process becomes a transaction devoid of meaning unlike a pilgrimage where you might have a personal interest in appeasing the gods but so does everyone else. Everyone may be doing the same thing but every person brings in their own level of awareness to the task. The modern pilgrimage, on other hand, gives us a reward that might come in form of a high that lasts maybe a few seconds, a social media post, and bragging rights for a few days. But that’s it. The pilgrimage will have to be repeated the following year when Apple may launch a yet another iPhone.

What’s really happening here? For one, the higher realities have been flattened out. Human civilisations have always sought to constantly deepen and sharpen our attention towards reality. Lighting a lamp in front of a deity will trigger an awareness of the warmth of the flame, the smokiness of the oil, and a slightly acidic smell of the lamp. Offering flowers, or a garland made of them, will give us a hint of their gentle fragrance, softness of their petals, and a playfulness of our emotions. When water comes into the equation, whether in terms of offerings or simply ensuring our cleanliness, we may reflect on its transparency, its flow, and purity. The infinite divine, when shaped into forms that we resonate with, is kept alive day and night by a priest or a priestess through a variety of rituals so that we may have an encounter with it whenever we need or want it.

In all of these processes, we constantly renew our awareness of the divine, nature, and our goodness. When all of these are traded for a direct, personal, and thoughts-only connection with higher realities, it does not always lead to a better relationship as was felt during the Protestant Reformation. In fact, what happens is that their place is taken up by lesser realities of our world— our unwillingness to suffer, an intense focus on our self, and a bluntness of our awareness. The subsequent revolutions of industrialisation, colonialism, and modernism in the last few centuries have done just that to our psyche. This trifecta of influences let us imagine that everything in life can be satiated by the market, never letting us delve into the inner world of our selves as well as the reality around us. They lead us to view nature as a resource to be capitalised infinitely for our comfort, rituals and customs as a weakness of mind, and the higher realities of divine, fate, and Karma a pseudoscience.

Byung-Chul Han, the South-Korean philosopher, has aptly captured the spirit of our world in his book, The Disappearance of Rituals. He writes,

*“Capitalism is often interpreted as a religion. However, if religion is understood in terms of religare, as some-thing that binds, then capitalism is anything but a religion because it lacks any force to assemble, to create community. Money, by itself, has an individualizing and isolating effect. It increases my individual freedom by liberating me from any personal bond with others. I can have someone else work for me by paying her, and this avoids entering into a personal relationship. And what is essential to religion is contemplative rest, but this is the antithesis of capital. Capital never rests. It is its nature that it must always work and continue moving. To the extent that they lose the capacity for contemplative rest, humans conform to capital. The distinction between the sacred and the profane is also an essential characteristic of religion. The sacred unites those things and values that give vitality to a community. The formation of community is its essential trait. Capitalism, by contrast, erases the distinction between the sacred and the profane by totalizing the profane. It makes everything comparable to everything else and thus equal to everything else. Capitalism brings forth a hell of the same.”*

To be sure, capitalism itself is not at fault for money corrupts even the most devout as we can see from the example of Roman Catholic Church and their indulgences (pun intended). The distinguishing factor is awareness, which must be renewed constantly as we go through the journey of our lives. In doing so, we will inevitably be led to rituals and may develop our own because without them, there can be sustained awareness that goes beyond the ordinary. Without this awareness, there can be no meaning to our existence, no matter how many iPhones may come and go.
