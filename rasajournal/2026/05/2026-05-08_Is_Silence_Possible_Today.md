### Can we realise our higher consciousness with advertisements, products, and content constantly vying for our attention in the modernity?

When was the last time you experienced silence?

That is, the zen-like silence where you don’t have even a single thought in your mind. No devices, no overthinking, and no meandering of your conscious awareness from one thing to another. Just pure stillness.

Was it while having a meal, working out, or sleeping? Chances are that it was the last of these possibilities. It is only when we sleep that we lose touch with our conscious awareness and when we might feel such a silence. Yet, even then, the subconscious may be at work in form of dreams.

So I’ll ask again, when was the last time you experienced silence?

The modern life makes it absolutely impossible unless you go after it intentionally. As soon as we wake up, our devices start bringing us the entire world at our fingertips from news, messages and mails, and social media app notifications. They start bombarding us with something to pay attention to, something to buy, or something to desire, and then keep on doing so for the rest of the day until we fall asleep again. After all, it’s not just our devices but the entire world around us. Billboards, newspapers, advertisements plastered on everything from buses to clothes— our conscious awareness is steeped in material reality. In fact, it is drowning in it. That excludes silence as an experience from our lives by its very definition because it cannot be bought or sold by anyone in any shape or form.

Silence belongs to the realm of what we call the spiritual reality. It can be our inner awareness or it can be a byproduct of meditation. But is this silence a mere absence of noise? Is it found in remote places? Do we need to wear certain coloured clothes to experience it? There might be many answers to these questions, depending on who you ask.

Almost a decade ago, I found myself attending a corporate mindfulness class somewhere in Manhattan. The class was aimed at increasing employee productivity and the trainer told its participants to imagine a white light in middle of office chaos. She told them to first imagine it increasing in scope and then decreasing back to the same point. I remember finding it all a bit silly but perhaps, it is one of the ways of getting to that silence. Even if the goal is to earn more money ultimately, it might be a useful exercise in general.

A few years later, I found myself at a busy area in Varanasi in the wee hours of a winter morning. There was chaos all around— someone’s getting a haircut, someone’s taking a bath in the river, someone’s preparing a religious offering, someone’s running about for no reason, someone’s sitting about drinking chai. Yet, it all felt quiet. Not just because there was none of that honking of vehicles or screaming of hawkers but because everyone seemed to be doing precisely what they wanted to do. Silence, here, was not entirely about god but definitely something to do with that higher reality.

Much of Hindu philosophy, from Advaita Vedanta to Samkhya to Yoga, is concerned with achieving this silence. Vedanta, which seeks to achieve self-realisation to understand the ultimate reality, calls it Moksha. Samkhya, which boils down reality to three gunas or traits of Sattva, Rajas, and Tamas, calls it Kaivalya. Yoga, which brings our minds and bodies in alignment, calls it Dhyana. All of these schools of thought have their own ways of getting to their objectives but none of these can be achieved without silence. In other words, then, silence is the root of all consciousness, both human and divine.

But then, the question is, why is modernity so lacking in silence and what does that mean for us? For one, it is because at some point in history (well, 17th century Western Europe), we decided to privilege the material over spiritual. Progress, rationality, and science became the new goals of our conscious awareness over stillness, self-realisation, and spirituality. From manufacturing houses to factories to assembly lines to MNCs to social media behemoths— our silence was stripped away from us, bit by bit. Our surroundings as well as our internal landscapes are prime real estate for modern consumerism to yield hefty profits.

However, it is also because this silence is difficult to ease into, stay with, and understand at all. For most of our history, we had no way of escaping this silence. It was a part of our lives, perhaps often unnoticed. But as our conscious awareness became louder, we became more and more uncomfortable with this silence. Today, we cannot not eat, work out, or even drift off to sleep without drowning out this silence through YouTube videos, music, or social media reels. We do not allow ourselves to feel silence because we do not know what to do with it. That means, in modernity, we have moved further and further away from it, both literally and metaphorically.

“So what”, many might think. Who needs silence? After all, most of us want to live normal lives. Silence is for monks, not us. Well, in modernity, even the [monks are hooked to TikTok](https://restofworld.org/2023/buddhist-monk-tiktok-monastic-code/)? Which is why, there is a deeper question at play here. That is, what does this loss of silence mean for our collective consciousness today and how does it impact the regular human life? Perhaps, the answer to that lies in our own lives. Our discomfort with silence makes it difficult for us to enjoy what we have, let go of what we cannot, and prevents any clarity from emerging about either of these two realities. For the entire world to function like this feels like an acute spiritual crisis at the very least. Who knew silence could be this important?
