*A home somewhere in Switzerland. Photo by Garima Garg.*

Every time I log into my Instagram account, I’m bombarded with dozens of ads from brands I don’t follow to buy something for the upcoming festival of Raksha-Bandhan, or Rakhi. From silver bracelets to premium leather bands being repurposed as a Rakhi, it’s study in how mind-boggling the reach of the market can be on our lives. So far I have seen ads for Rakhis that— feature extinct animals (made by World Wildlife Fund), are made of resin artwork, made of cold-pressed leaves and flowers, feature motifs such as zodiac signs, various gods and religious symbolisms, and so much more. But this is not all. Pretty much everything has also been repurposed as a gift, including a chocolate shell made in shape of a coconut which can be broken to reveal smaller treats inside it. In any case, whatever you buy will be at your doorstep as quickly as possible.

This phenomenon of instant gratification feels innocuous enough, at least at first. It’s just about groceries and an occasional online purchase, you say. But slowly and surely, it solidifies our preference for a market-based solution to every problem because the market is apparently way more efficient than we could ever be. It is quick, costs less, and makes everything look nicer. Tying a simple home-made decorated thread as a Rakhi, as has been done for years, simply doesn’t look as attractive as the ones being advertised to us now. The emphasis, in this case, shifts from the festival or the bond itself, and moves on to a purchase. Bit by bit, every single second of life becomes something to celebrate because that is the only way the modern hydra-headed marketplace can keep on growing.

That this is unsustainable and environmentally harmful in the long-term is something that has already been discussed extensively. Yet, it shows no signs of slowing down. If anything, the market has become way more virulent with the advent of tech-capitalism than it was before it. But what’s less discussed is the impact of such consumerism on our psyche.

The market prefers to speed up everything because the intensity of speed directly translates into better profits. In fact, Amazon has made a practice out of it by listing a discounted price if you were to buy a product within hours, rather than taking a few days to deliberate over it. This means that a consumer will definitely buy more than what they need or even reasonably want because the entire marketplace, including the first buyer to the last seller, is locked in a paradigm of speed. We are all programmed to prefer speed over slowness— eat faster, grow faster, learn faster, earn faster, travel faster, and even heal faster. It seems the modern civilisation is speeding up life in its totality but for what? I’m not sure anyone has the answer to that question.

Because speed can never lead us to that answer, only slowing down can. For only then can we think, understand, and make a decision knowingly. It is only in slowing down that we can comprehend the reality around us and within us. Of course, modernity makes a marketplace of the slowness too. The [Slow Movement](https://bigthink.com/thinking/slow-movement-philosophy/) started in the 1980s and today it manifests in a myriad of ways— from mindfulness colouring books to sustainable fashion wear, from organic produce to exotic wellness retreats. Anything that becomes a part of the market today is susceptible to speed eventually.

Which begs the question— what really does it mean to slow down? For one, it means to go within. To seek the beauty, change, and solutions within, rather than the world outside. Whether one does it through meditation, cooking, or art, it is upto them but the essential idea is to make do with already one has rather than go chasing for something new to buy. It is to trust that there is a world just as wonderful and complex inside of us as the one outside of us. And most importantly, it is to make friends with this inner reality, rather than suspect it or be afraid of it. In a world designed to prevent this, it is the only protection, or Raksha, we have from the overwhelming deluge of market forces.
