In Plato’s Phaedrus (370 B.C), his seminal work about love, logic, and rhetoric, he writes of an Egyptian story which is passed on to the young Phaedrus by the Greek philosopher Socrates. Much like the work itself, the story is also a dialogue between the Egyptian king Thamus and the moon god of geometry, mathematics, astronomy, and writing, Thoth. When the god offers the king the gift of writing so that he may offer it to his subjects, the latter declines. A present day ChatGPT enthusiast might consider it an unwise decision but the king explains it as such—

“It men learn this, it will implant forgetfulness in their souls, they will cease to exercise memory because they rely on that which is written, calling things to remembrance no longer from within themselves, but by means of external marks. It is no true wisdom that you offer your disciples, but only its semblance. Those who rely on reading for their knowledge will seem to know much, while for the most part they know nothing. They will be filled, not with wisdom, but with the conceit of wisdom.”

Socrates and Plato, the Greek philosophers considered the architects of modern Western thought with their emphasis on the objective truth, go back and forth in their exposition about the perks of writing. While they encourage it over the mystical traditions and approaches to life, they do often warn readers against taking their written works too seriously. “It is as though he meant to build into the very body of his writings a caution that they not be given too much weight,”, writes David Abram in his The Spell of The Sensuous (1996). “Not because he was uncertain about the genuine and serious worth of his philosophy, but simply because he had strong reservations about the written word and its ability to convey the full meaning of a philosophy that was as much practice— involving direct, personal interaction and instruction— as it was a set of static formulations and reflections,” Abram concludes.

Writing, then, was a way of apprehending reality that was rooted in the physical. In this way, it is more of a technology for simplyfying reality by creating an order or a structure rather than experiencing and understanding life innately within a context. Abram’s book, as the title suggests, deals with the latter. He calls it the sensuous world because it is the sort of reality that we perceive with our senses— touch, smell, taste, sight, and sounds.

He traces from various cultures to understand how humans have historically co-existed with Nature. He writes, for instance, about how the words we use to communicate to each other didn’t arise in a vacuum but out of an intimate recognition of the world. Even the first letter of the word alphabet, originally aleph-beth, A, was used to symbolise an ox. Aleph, in ancient Hebrew, is the word for ox and if you were to look at one with its horns, the animal’s face resembles an inverted modern A. Similarly, the ancient Hebrew word for water is mem and which was written as a kind of an M to resemble a series of sea waves.

For Socrates and Plato, the act of writing itself was transformative because they lived in a society that was excessively mystical, full of belief, and focused on sensory experiences. And so, even though they recognised its merits, they also saw its demerits and cautioned against them. However, in the last few centuries, the influence of thinkers such as Descartes and Kant began to privilege the mind over the body and soul and finally, the Western “Enlightenment” considered the pursuit of mind as the highest ideal. Because the mind can only perceive the visible, material, and rational, that became our “progress” too. This is why most of our technologies exist to cater to our needs for money, status, and individualism.

This excessiveness of the material side of life is probably something Plato and Socrates might never have imagined. That the arc of humanity probably swings from spiritual to material and vice versa is not difficult to accept but it paves way for an important reality—balance and moderation.

Nature maintains this balance and moderation through an inherent duality that exists within it— sun and moon, light and day, “Heaven” and “Hell”, water and fire, and the like. Human thought has evolved primarily in binaries such as these and the deeper one goes into such binaries, the more one finds them to be parts of a whole. So while water and fire are contrasting elements, they both belong to Nature and carry within themselves both life-threatening fierceness and life-affirming gentleness. Humans can never control either one of these and remain in constant vigil and awe of them.

However, the problem today is that we are becoming more and more hooked to the extremes, whether material or spiritual. In our age of screens, we either have people who seek to maximise their material gains through them or those who seek to escape from them in the spiritual. The ideal of balance and moderation is not being understood, accepted, or sought-after. This is crucial because this is where the average exists, i.e., most of life whether human or non-human takes form.

In the world of extremities, the tech world is unwilling to leave the art to artists and artists seem unwilling to understand that there is a world outside their feelings and thoughts. In the sphere of politics, we are seeing more religiosity coming back to vogue and in religious sphere, we see many people who pass off their personal prejudices and shortcomings as their virtues. A billionaire wants the entire world to look at his son’s wedding in a show of power and status and on the other hand, we see countless people around the world struggling to have basic dignity of life. These extremities may always have existed in human societies but our screens have enabled them to become more and more normalised, visible, and sought-after than ever before.

It’s no wonder that our most spectacular ambition today is to reach out into outer-space and build colonies on other planets. A world that fails to understand that status, power, and prosperity need to be tempered with respect, generosity, and compassion is a world that will inevitable fear the running out of resources, over-crowding, and the growing discontent of masses.

In doing so, our screens are essentially normalising the idea of extreme or the linear rather than moderation or the balance. We live today, as king Thamus warned, not in wisdom but in profound conceit of it.
