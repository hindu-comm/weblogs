# Why We Need Memories

### What a Native American tribe can teach us about the importance of memories in our realities.

In Oregon, the northwestern state of US, there exists Crater Lake which is uncharacteristically deep at almost 2000 ft. It was “discovered” by John W. Hillman in 1853, except the district’s indigenous Klamath Indian tribes had known of it for thousands of years. So, when a national geological survey team went to survey the lake and the area surrounding it, the two Klamath Indian guides of the team refused to go anywhere near the lake. At the same time, William Steel, a man who wanted to build a national park around the lake, found that the Klamath Indians he sought out for a survey as well were also wary of the lake. They were “making all sorts of mysterious signs and staring directly at the ground”, a report describes the incident.   
  
Now the conventional and rational tendency would be to mock them for being superstitious. But turns out, the “lake” used to be a volcano named Mt. Mazama. It is part of 18 volcanic mountains forming the Cascade Range, from Lassen Peak in Northern California (U.S) to Silverthrone Caldera in British Columbia (Canada). This line of volcanic mountains runs parallel to a giant ‘crack’ in the surface of the Earth’s crust in North America. It is the point where the crust underlying the Pacific Ocean is being thrust eastwards. This is the reason why that part of North America often experiences more earthquakes compared to rest of the continent. But what does this have to do with the Klamath Indians?

The tribe has retold a story through countless generations about this “lake”. Within the Mt. Mazama, there resided a god— the Chief of the Below World— who desired to wed the beautiful Loha, a human living in a nearby village, but she didn’t want to be him. Enraged, the Below-World Chief began to rain red-hot rocks and burning ash down on Loha’s people, but they were saved through the intervention of the Above-World Chief, who eventually caused the mountain to collapse inwards on his underworld counterpart. The hollow that formed filled with water and thus Crater Lake came into being.

Patrick Nunn, in his book The Edge of Memory (2018, Bloomsbury Sigma), recounts this episode and the story. He writes of a Klamath Indian elder who explained this in his words in 1865, “Now you understand why my people never visit the lake. Down through the ages we have heard this story. From father to son has come the warning, ‘look not upon the place, for it means death or everlasting sorrow.’”

Mt. Mazama is now characterised as a complex volcano. The U.S geological survey says that even though the volcano remains dormant for now, minor eruptions are still likely. But the Klamath story, Nunn writes, belongs to a time when the “lake” did not yet exist for it implies the awareness of a volcano and the destruction caused by it. The radiometric dating of rocks belonging to Mt. Mazama have shown that the volcano it contained was destroyed some 7,600 years ago. The staggering implication here is that a tribe managed to pass down a story about a volcano for that long. They did so because it was vital to their existence and so it was their duty to one another and their community. Yet, what does this story, this duty, and these memories mean when they meet with modern rationality that is devoid of context, a bias towards scientism that views such stories as superstition, and rapid resettlement of people across the globe where such memories are either being lost or becoming irrelevant?

For one, this reminds us again of that timeless wisdom— reality is in a constant flux. Where there used to be a volcano raining down ash and rocks on people around, today there is a national park that serves as a picnic spot for countless families around the area. And yet, tomorrow the volcano could erupt again. Such constant (and difficult) change is challenging for human beings to live with and evokes all sorts of insecurities, fears, and anxieties within us. It nags at the inadequateness of our perceptions, forever needling us about that which is unknown. But then, it shows us why humans create and tell stories over and over again. If it wasn’t for the modern bias towards classroom education, perhaps we could better appreciate this simple yet effective method of educating one another across generations about lived realities. Which is why, the most important takeaway here is about human memory. In a way, it would seem like the memory is all we truly have. We cannot control the nature of life, our natural environment, or human character but we always have our stories, songs, and dances that teach us about these realities. In doing so, the human memory weaves our past, present, and future in a way that ensures a continuity between all of our timelines.

However, this is precisely what we find fractured in the modern society. Today, our appetite for data, recording our experiences, and timekeeping are more precise and abundant than ever before. Anyone with a smartphone can keep a track of how many hours they slept every night or how many steps they walked. We can constantly take photos of every experience we have, every meal we eat, every person we meet, and every flower we glimpse. We can note down each and every thought we have and carry with ourselves a thousand encyclopadieas in our smartphones at all times. We can measure time down to every single second and organise our lives accordingly. Yet, if tomorrow there were to be a sort of a digital catastrophe which wipes out our digital data, would we be any less than zombies?

Memory is so intrinsically necessary for our survival— emotional and physical— that it feels catastrophic that we should have transferred the function entirely to our technologies in the modern rapidly changing world. To the extent that we retain our memories, especially the ones that fuel us in our present and take us to our future, is the extent to which we manage to come alive. For whatever we carry within ourselves as memory is what we are and what we become. A memory from childhood about blowing bubbles or watching your kids enjoy while on a vacation is more than just that— to our minds and bodies, it is a symbol of happiness, warmth, and togetherness. In tough times, it can steer us towards better times again. But if the memory only remained a photo in an album of thousands of photos in a hard drive somewhere, then it is lost to us. And in losing that part of our past, we lose that much of our future as well.
