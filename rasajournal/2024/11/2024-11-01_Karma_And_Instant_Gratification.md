Here are some scenarios and let’s see how many of these hold true for you.

Scenario 1: After a long day, you’re finally sitting down for dinner. You’d like some entertainment too to go with it, so you start scrolling through your Netflix or related app to find an appropriate show or movie that can keep you company as you eat. However, before you know it you’ve already finished your meal, eating bits and pieces of it while scrolling, and you still haven’t found the correct visual side-dish. Cue a minor sense of dissatisfaction.

Scenario 2: You saw an online post of a friend eating a nice meal of hummus and pita bread. Immediately, you develop a craving and fire up your food delivery app to get yourself the same. It arrives in 40 minutes even though the preparation time of the meal itself exceeds that time-frame, let alone the time it takes to pack it and deliver it to you.

Scenario 3: In a conversation with colleagues discussing their holiday plans, you realise you are the only one with no plans. Someone is going to Turkey, someone else to Maldives, and yet another to a quaint weekend resort outside the city. You can already sense the incoming barrage of their photos on your WhatsApp groups, Instagram, and LinkedIn. Perhaps, you too will book yourself a solo trip to the mountains. Perhaps, you’ll finally “find yourself” too.

Scenario 4: Life is perfect but you’re bored. So maybe, it’s time to pick up a new hobby. Pilates? Becoming a barista? Or becoming a Bookstagrammer? Whatever you may choose, it means building yourself a new personality and not just for yourself but for the world to see. For the world to see this new dimension to who you are as a person. Cue new purchases and expressions of self.

Scenario 5: Everyone seems to be enjoying life with a significant other. From vacations to cute little dates, everyone seems to be getting the best out of relationships. Except you, of course. Time to download a dating app or plan something fun with a partner that you can then romanticise for your social media feeds?

Scenario 6: What used to seem luxurious a decade ago seems quite affordable now. Everyone around you seems to have the latest high-end gadgets, expensive and attractive clothes, five-star staycations, and the like. Perhaps, it’s time to take that 5% salary hike or learn about cryptocurrencies?

Scenario 7: There’s been a raging public controversy. You want to express yourself. You fire up your Twitter because you want to be a part of the conversation too. Even if the conversation seems to be simultaenously taking place through your TV news, WhatApp groups, in-person chats, three social media platforms, where no one listens but everyone is talking.

All of these scenarios reflect a particular aspect of our lives— entertainment, food, individualism, relationships, wealth and status, and societal participation. In every single one of them, there is an element of instant gratification which wasn’t a part of our lives a decade ago.

Human desire acts as a catalyst for all life. But putting an over-emphasis on desire, as the materialist worldview is designed to, only instigates more desire. The modern culture, especially the kind enabled by technology, seeks to call it by different names— comfort, convenience, and choice— at the end of the day, it is nothing but desire on steroids. Living in an age of high-speed internet and cheap resources, it leads to what we know as instant gratification.

Where the materialist seeks to make a god out of desire, the spiritualist seeks to banish it altogether for it is seen as the root of all suffering. That is, the suffering of being a human in the world at any given point. However, both seem incorrect as far as most of human life is concerned. Where desire helps us propel forward into our lives, participating in them fully helps as see the complete reality of the said desire. So, maybe, something looked fun when someone else made it seem attractive and easy but doing it yourself made you see sides of it that weren’t as…attractive and easy.

What the modernity makes near impossible is this extremely necessary stage of reality. The reality of the unpleasantness, discomfort, and incomprehensibility of life is repeatedly shunned by replacing it with more and more desire. In doing so, we shun a vital part of our Karma because we remain locked in the state of desire rather than the state of acceptance of the reality. The problem with instant gratification, then, is not one of desires or even the the insatiability of them but of never leaning into one’s desires fully because we’re programmed to experience desires as only attractive and easy.
