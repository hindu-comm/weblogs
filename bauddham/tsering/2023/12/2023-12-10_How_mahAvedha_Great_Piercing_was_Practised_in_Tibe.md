Virupa's Amrtasiddhi text is the first known text to teach some of the main practices of Hatha Yoga such as *Mahāmudrā*, *Mahābandha* and *Mahāvedha*. These are the three main yogic practices of Amrtasiddhi. They were later grouped into four primary trulkhors (Am-grol, Vedha, Bandha and Mahamudra) in Shangpa Kagyu's Amrtasiddhi trulkhor.

Virupa's Amrtasiddhi teachings reached Tibet (c.11th century) through Indian yogis and Panditas like E Deva (E Tepa), Amoghavajra and Latoe Gonpo. All of the Amrtasiddhi texts that are attributed to Virupa were either taught or written by Indian yogis. Later on, Tibetan Shangpa masters such as Tsundue Singhe (also known as Virya Simha - 13th century) wrote the Amrtasiddhi Trulkhor (physical yoga practice) texts of Virupa. From these texts you can see how *Mahāvedha* was adopted and practised in Tibet.

In the Amrtasiddhi root texts in the Tibetan Tengyur collection and in the Shangpa cycle of teachings, the Sanskrit term *Mahāvedha* has been translated into Tibetan. However, in the Amrtasiddhi trulkhor texts from the Shangpa teaching cycle, the Sanskrit term *Mahā-vedha* has been retained as *veta* or *bhedha* (and *Mahābandha* as *bandha*). I have referred to this practice in this article as *vedha* (piercing).

Many of the practices in Vajrayana Buddhism have three states of practice: the outer, the inner and the secret. In the Amrtasiddhi Trulkhor of the Shangpa tradition, *vedha* is also classified into three types: Outer Vedha, Inner Vedha and Secret Vedha. In this article I have presented these three types of vedha from three different Amrtasiddhi Trulkhor texts from the Shangpa teaching cycle, which differ slightly from each other.

In the Outer Vedha in general, the buttocks are hit against the heels of the feet by lifting the buttocks with the head bent and the hands on the ground in the *linga* *mudra*. In the Inner Vedha, the buttocks and heels are hit against the ground by holding the big toes. The Secret Vedha is similar to the Outer Vedha, but here the buttocks are hit more gently against the heels.

*Bep* (controlled fall on your buttocks and legs) and *Vedha*. The Tibetan term and yogic practice called *Bep* (*‘bebs*) is not mentioned in the Tibetan Amrtasiddhi texts. However, for the first time, in the commentary to the Amrtasiddhi Trulkhor texts, there is mention of performing *Bep* at the end of Vedha. *Bep* and Vedha appear to be two different practices. However, one of the different variations of *Bep* called *sGur 'Bebs* (*Bep* with the head down) has some similarities to one of the movements of Inner Vedha.
