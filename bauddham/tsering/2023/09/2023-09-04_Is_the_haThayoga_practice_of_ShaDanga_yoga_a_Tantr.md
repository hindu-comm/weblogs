#### English translation by Francesco Sferra (words in the brackets are mine)

> Now the *haṭhayoga* is explained. Here, when the unchanging moment
> (the moment of unchanging bliss[1](#footnote-1)) does not take place
> because the vital breath (*prāṇa)* is unrestrained (inside the
> central), in spite of the image (image of
> emptiness/Śūnyatā-bimba[2](#footnote-2)) having been seen "by means of
> withdrawal (*pratyāhāra* and *prāṇāyāma)*, and so on", then the
> yogin - after having made the vital breath flow in the central channel
> "violently (forcefully/*haṭhena*) through the continuous exercise of
> sound (*nāda**)***" - "can realize" the unchanging moment (of bliss)
> through non-vibration "by arresting the *bindu*" of the bodhicitta "in
> the vajra-gem (penis) placed in the lotus" of the wisdom. This is the
> *haṭhayoga*.
>
> ------------------------------------------------------------------------

In Vajrayāna Tantric Buddhism there are practices such as Karma-mudra (*las rgya*), where the practitioner uses their actual physical partner to hold and draw the *bindu* upwards during sexual union. Some of the texts that I read are very erotic and also quite striking. However, sexual intercourse with a consort is not the only method in *bindu* practice, nor does it seem to be the case here in the *haṭhayoga* practice of *Ṣaḍaṅga* yoga. Even in Karma-mudra there are two types, one practising with an actual consort and the other using the method of breathing and mind[3](#footnote-3) to meditate on joys, which the text here calls the practice of *nāda* (which I will explain later).

If we interpret the term 'lotus of wisdom' (*abja*/*jalaja*) as vagina (see Wiki), then the practice appears to be a sexual ritual. However, if we know that the term lotus (Tib. *chu skyes*) is also used for the *nāḍī*-chakra (*rtsa 'khor*). For example, in the Tibetan texts you will find the phrases such as *gsang ba'i chu skyes* (lotus of the *yoni*), *lte ba'i chu skyes* (lotus of the navel), *mgrin pa'i chu skyes* (lotus of the throat), *snying ga'i chu skyes* (lotus of the heart), *norbu’i chu skyes* (lotus of penis region). Then it doesn't have a sexual connotation to it.

When I read the Tibetan *Ṣaḍaṅga* *yoga* text, I get the meaning from the phrase / *chu skyes su gnas pa’i rdo rje nor bu* / as ‘vajra-gem (penis) based or located at the lotus (*nāḍī*-chakra)’ instead of ‘vajra (penis) when it is in the lotus’ or ‘vajra-gem placed in the lotus’ translated from Sanskrit. And the reason why the lotus (*nāḍī*-chakra) at the penis area is called wisdom is because of its feminine nature of the wisdom aspects. For example, the crown *nāḍī*-chakra is a method aspect, the throat is wisdom, the heart is method, the navel is wisdom, the *yoni* is method and the penis is wisdom. So, as I understand it, the meaning seems to be more like this: 'the bodhicitta bindu flows down through the central channel and reaches the *nāḍī*-chakra of the penis, and the yogi applies the practice of *nāda* by holding the *bindu* at the tip of the penis making it immobile without releasing it out (without intercourse)'.

When to practice *haṭhayoga*?

“When the yogi’s desired purpose (iṣṭasiddhi) is not achieved through the *pratyāhāra*, dhyāna, *prāṇāyāma* and dhāraṇā, one should then apply *haṭhayoga*”.

The practice of *haṭhayoga* is mainly mentioned in the *prāṇāyāma* section of *Ṣaḍaṅga* Yoga. *prāṇāyāma* yoga anga consists of four main breathing practices.

1.   **Vajra breathing**:

    This breathing practice involves learning to inhale, hold and exhale
    slowly and gently with the sound of Om, Hum and Ah respectively.
    This practice helps to purify the passage of the nadis.

2.  **kumbhaka with gentle breathing method:**

    In this practice, inhale through one nostril (closing the other
    nostril with a finger), press the air a little and bind the air
    below the navel without drawing the lower air to the navel, and
    exhale through the other nostril. This breathing practice helps to
    usher the air into the central channel.

3.  **Kumbhaka with rough breathing**:

    This practice involves a rough and forceful breathing technique and
    must draw the apana vayu forcefully. This practice helps to bind the
    vital air within the central channel.

4.  ***haṭhayoga***:

    This practice is used when the kumbhaka with the gentle breathing
    method and the kumbhaka with the rough breathing method do not
    succeed in directing and binding the vital air in the central
    channel.

    ------------------------------------------------------------------------

Wikipedia says, ‘the actual means of practice are not specified’ but if we read the next two passages, it will tell us little bit more about the *haṭhayoga* practice in *Ṣaḍaṅga* yoga. Taranatha distinguishes the *haṭhayoga* practice into three, based on forcing (*haṭhena*) the *nāḍī*, *vāyu* and *bindu*. He calls the ‘forceful method’ mentioned in above text as ‘forcing or forcefully stopping the *bindu*’. The next two passages talks about:

2. **Forcing** ***vāyu***: From the navel, Shakti (power of *prāṇa*) moves from one chakra to another. When it touches the outer membrane like a needle, the *apāna* *vāyu* forcefully drives it towards the heavenly path (*khechara*).

3. **Forcing** ***nāḍī***: When the vital air that moves in the right and the left channel ceases or are stopped, Shakti (power of *prāṇa*) shoots to the sublime abode by piercing the crown of the head.

#### English Translation by Francesco Sferra (words in the brackets are mind)

> The power (shakti/power of *prāṇa* [4](#footnote-4)), which from the
> middle of the navel (chakra) reaches the supreme plane at the end of
> the twelve fingers (inches) and at the end of the kalas, is restrained
> in the navel, is similar to lightning, and is erect in the form of a
> pole (stick). It is lead from one wheel (chakra) to another along the

> pierces the hole of the *uṣṇīṣa* (crown), just as a thorn \[pierces\]

#### English Translation (in the brackets are mine)

> At this moment, the yogin must push the wind of the *apāna* (*vāyu*)
> with extreme violence (force) in the way that is directed upwards
> (*urdhvamarge*). After having perforated the *uṣṇīṣa* (crown) and
> restrained the two winds (of the left and right channels), this power
> (shakti) reaches the "supreme citadel". Thus, through the awakening of
> the vajra, this power along with the objects, attains the state of
> khecara in the mind and then becomes the yogins' universal mother
> endowed with the five kinds of super-knowledge.

#### Practice of *nāda* as explained by Je Tsongkhapa in his *Ṣaḍaṅga* yoga text

Tsongkhapa writes,

“The *nāda* is translated as mere sound. The sound is the sound of *haṃ*. The way it is practised is:

- Sit in a vajra cross-legged posture.

- Cross the arms over the chest.

- Bend the head slightly towards the chest.

- Without letting the air escape through the mouth, suck the air
  forcefully through the nose into the central channel. Think of the
  sound of the inhalation as the sound of *haṃ*.

-  At the same time, draw the *apāna* *vāyu* through the central channel
  from either of the two lower doors (penis and anus).

- In the beginning, draw the *apāna* *vāyu* just above the navel. After
  more practice, slowly draw the *apāna* *vāyu* a little higher.

- This should be done forcefully and for a long time. But don't do it
  for too long, otherwise the practice won't be forceful because of
  exhaustion.

- In the beginning, it should be practised about twenty-one times at a
  time. As you get used to it, you can increase it.”
