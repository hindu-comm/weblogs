My last two posts were about *Ṣaḍaṅga* *yoga’s* *Prāṇāyāma* and *dhāraṇā* practices. I thought the readers who are interested in Vajrayāna’s *Ṣaḍaṅga* *yoga* practice, might like to know how *Pratyāhāra* (sense withdrawal), the first limb of *Ṣaḍaṅga* *yoga* was taught and practised in Vajrayāna Buddhism according to Tibetan Buddhist texts. This post on *Pratyāhāra* yoga *aṅga* (སོར་སྡུད་ཀྱི་ཡན་ལག་is based on Tsongkhapa’s text: *Kālacakra* *Ṣaḍaṅga* *yoga* instruction.

Generally, our five senses like eyes and ears move towards their objects such as visible forms and sounds. In *Pratyāhāra*, the yogi cuts the tie between the ordinary unruly senses and their objects by withdrawing the senses by not letting them move towards their objects. In *Pratyāhāra*, though the ordinary five senses (ཐ་མལ་གྱི་དབང་པོ་ལྔ་) are cut off from their objects and the unruly five sense-consciousness are withdrawn, the sixth mind still functions and feasts on its objects. As Taranatha says, the word *āhāra* in *Prati-āhāra* (*Pratyāhāra*) has two main meanings. The first is withdrawal and cut-off, and the second meaning is eating or food. Thus, in *Pratyāhāra* *yoga* *aṅga*, the ordinary five senses are cut off from their engagement with the sensory objects, but the sixth mind and the pristine consciousness perceive the image of the emptiness of the five sensory objects such as the form of the empty (སྟོང་པའི་གཟུགས་), the sound of empty (སྟོང་པའི་སྒྲ་) etc.

How the yogi cuts the tie between the ordinary sense-consciousness and their objects? In general, there occurs a time when the senses sometimes stop engaging with their objects but that alone is not enough here. In *Pratyāhāra* yoga, the yogi has to achieve that by controlling the vehicle of sense-consciousness that is by stopping the five *vāyu* (vital air) on which the sense-consciousness rides. The *vāyu* are controlled by the specific body postures, the position of the eyes and the mind. Furthermore, in *Pratyāhāra*, cutting ties between the senses and their objects alone is not sufficient, the yogi also has to perceive the objects of the five senses such as form and sound in the form of emptiness. The function of *Pratyāhāra* *yoga* *aṅga* is to cleanse the central channel and to perceive the *Śūnyā-bimba* (image of void), the image of emptiness inside the central channel.

**The actual practice:**

*Pratyāhāra* yoga consists of two methods, night yoga and day yoga. When the yogi practices these two yogas, the yogi experiences ten visions as a sign of accomplishment. Four visions/signs of the night yoga and the six visions/signs of the day yoga. It is said that the yogi should begin the yoga when the movement of breath shifts to the right nostril.

*Translation:*

**I. The Night yoga (མཚན་མོའི་རྣལ་འབྱོར་/** ***rātriyoga*****):**

1.  **Place where to practice:**

    Yogi should practice night yoga in a pitch-black room where there is
    no light at all. The reason is, in a completely dark room, it is
    easier to perceive the image of void/emptiness
    (*Śūnyā-bimba*/སྟོང་གཟུགས་).

2.  **Body postures:**

    Vajra cross-legged posture, the tip of the tongue touching the
    palate, spine straight, hands forming fists (thumbs tucked in)
    placed on the thighs.

3.  **Eyes-position (Gaze):**

    (Eyes-position is important in this practice as the text says, it
    helps in seeing the image of void/emptiness easier). Eyes should
    neither be completely open nor completely shut (the lower eyelid
    covers half of the eyeball and the upper part is left open). Roll
    the eyeballs upward, looking at the pitch-black (sky) space with a
    fixed gaze (without movement of the eyeballs) around sixteen inches
    away from the level of the spot between the eyebrows.

4.  **Binding/locking the five senses (the doer):**

    What is fastened or locked are the five doers (five senses). What
    fastened them is the two vital air (*prāṇa* and *apāna* *vāyu*). The
    method is, restraining the *vāyu*, the upper *prāṇa* *vāyu* and the
    lower *apāna* *vāyu* which are the vehicle of sense-consciousness.
    This will stop the movement of the five senses to their objects. How
    the vital airs are fastened or restrained is by stopping the
    movement of air in the right and left channels and ushering them
    into the central channel. For that, the yogi should know the method
    to direct the *vāyu* into the central channel.

    The methods are two, hitting the vital points of the body and the
    application of *vāyu*.

    Hitting the vital points of the body: There are six channel wheels
    (chakras). The channels have upper and lower tips. Each of these two
    ends has one entrance each, to usher the *vāyu* into the *Avadhūti*
    channel. These are the vital points to be hit. It hits the vital
    point when the yogi focuses their mind on the spot without letting
    it wander. Since the air and mind move together, as the yogi places
    their mind on that spot, the air will also gather there. The
    *Avadhūti*’s upper tip is in the centre of the spot between the
    eyebrows and the yogi should focus their mind there. The
    *Avadhūti*’s lower tip is below the navel and is called *Tummo*
    (*Cāṇḍālī*). Since the locus of *amṛta* is the forehead, the yogi
    has to hit the vital point here by focusing the eyes, *vāyu* and
    mind at this *Nāḍī*-chakra of the forehead.

5.  **Four signs/visions of the night yoga (མཚན་རྟགས་བཞི):**

    When the yogi hits the vital point of the body and practices as
    such, there will arise four signs/visions of night yoga. The four
    signs are smoke, mirage, the flame of a lamp and fire-fly. The mere
    visions of smoke, mirage etc, are not sufficient. These visions
    should arise due to the entry of *vāyu* in the central channel.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

II. Day yoga (ཉིན་མོའི་རྣལ་འབྱོར་/*divāyogaḥ*):

1.  Place, where to practice:

    The location should be an open unsheltered (roofless) place, where
    there is no river, no snow and a cold breeze that hurts the eyes.

2.  Time, when to practice:

    One should practice it in the morning and in the afternoon by
    showing one’s back to the sun. One should not practice at the noon,
    the sun will damage the eyes.

3.  Body posture and eyes-position are as same as night yoga.

4.  Six signs/visions of the accomplishment of the day yoga practice
    are, blazing fire, moon, sun, *Rāhu*, lightning and *Bindu*. Thus,
    in *Pratyāhāra* *yoga* *aṅga*, there arise a total of ten signs of
    experience to the yogi. These signs arise due to the entry of the
    ten *vāyu* inside the central channel. The image of emptiness may
    arise without confining the *vāyu* in the central channel. However,
    in *Pratyāhāra* yoga *aṅga*, the image of emptiness must arise as a
    result of ushering the *vāyu* into the central channel. Otherwise,
    this won’t be qualified as the instruction of
    *Śūnyābimba*-*Mahāmudrā*. The mind that perceives the image of
    emptiness is a valid direct perception and it is free from
    conceptuality.
