### Translator’s Note

According to Tārānāthā, the first two lines of the Tibetan verse reveal *dhyāna* yoga anga. And the next two lines reveal *pratyāhāra*. In the *dhyāna* practice of *Ṣaḍaṅga yoga*, the yogi focuses his mind and eyes entirely on the image of emptiness \[*śūnyabimbam*\]. The yogi then juxtaposes the image of emptiness with the karmic objects \[such as physical objects, sounds, etc.\], and then both the karmic vision and the image of emptiness disappear, remaining with the vision of luminosity. Tārānāthā explains the phrase ཀུན་ནས་བསྐོར་ \[completely encircled / captured / *bandha*\] as the body of the enemy which is the karmic objects \[impure perception\] being encircled \[captured\] by the light of the image of emptiness, *Mahāmudrā*. He then explains unchanging bliss \[describing *pramuditā*, extreme joy, as *akṣarasukham*, unchanging bliss\] destroys the body of the enemy \[impure perception\]…རབ་དགའ་བས། དགྲ་ཡི་ལུས་ནི་རབ་ཏུ་འགེམས་.

In the *pratyāhāra* \[sense withdrawal\] practice of *Ṣaḍaṅga yoga*, five ordinary senses cuts off its connection with its objects (such as visible form, sound, smell, touch, taste). So the sense consciousness disappears or withdraws from the sphere of objects by restraining the vital air. *Pratyāhāra* consists of night and day yoga practices. Through this practice, the yogi stops the air of the five elements from the left side and the air of the five elements from the right side in the central channel. Through this, the yogi attains the ten signs or visions. (Six signs of day yoga associated with the left side and four signs of night yoga associated with the right side). ([Check here to read for more details)](https://tsering.substack.com/i/142093541/ways-of-practice).

For more details on the *pratyāhāra* and *dhyāna* of Ṣaḍaṅga yoga, see my previous posts, the *Ṣaḍaṅga yoga* text written by Tārānāthā.

### English translation of the Tibetan verse

Homage to you \[Tārā\], who, immersed in rapture, shatters the bodies of all your foes. You manifest from the wisdom-syllable *hūṃ*, And display each of your mantra’s ten syllables.

### Hidden meaning behind this verse

namaḥ pramuditābandha ripugātra prabhedini\|  
  
daśākṣara padanyāse vidyā huṃkāra dīpite\|\|

The body of the enemy \[*ripu-gātra*/དགྲ་ཡི་ལུས་\] is encircled \[*bandha*/བསྐོར་\] and destroyed \[*prabheda*/རབ་ཏུ་འགེམས་\] by unchanging bliss \[*pramuditā*/རབ་དགའ་\]. The body of the enemy refers to the body of impure perception \[karmic object\]; it is surrounded by the light of the image of emptiness, *Mahāmudrā*. Thus the enemy is destroyed by extreme joy \[*pramuditā*/རབ་དགའ་\], which is unchanging bliss \[*akṣarasukham*/གཡོ་མེད་དམ་མི་འགྱུར་བའི་བདེ་བ་\]. This line reveals the *anga* of *dhyāna* yoga.

*Daśākṣara* \[ten letters\] means the ten signs \[of *pratyāhāra*\] attained through the gradual cessation of \[the vital air of the five elements\] five mandalas from the right side and \[the vital air of the five elements\] five mandalas from the left side. *Akṣara* \[letter\] represents the vital air. The Tibetan term for *Akṣara* is ཡི་གེ་, which literally means unchanging/immutable. By seeing the ten visions of immutable *Dharmadhātu*, there arises the ten aspects of immutable bliss.

The heart/knowledge mantra syllable *hūṃ* \[*vidyā huṃkāra*/རིག་པ་ཧཱུྃ་\] \]has five parts, representing five pristine wisdoms. \[So this syllable *hūṃ*\], the *bindu*, which has the nature of five pristine wisdoms, contains the image of the \[entire\] three worlds. The ten signs of *pratyāhāra* are attained by stopping the \[karmic air, the air of the right and left channels\] in the central channel. Remaining in meditative equilibrium in this is the great *vidya* mantra, that is, the lamp \[*dipa*, Tārānāthā has chosen the Tibetan word སྒྲོན་མ rather than སྒྲོལ་མ/Tara\], which is the clear vision of the image of emptiness \[*shunyata-bimba*\]. This line reveals the *anga* of *pratyāhāra*.
