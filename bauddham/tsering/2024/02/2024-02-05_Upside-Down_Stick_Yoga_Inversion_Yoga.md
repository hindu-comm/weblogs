#### Translator’s Note

This text was written by Amoghavajra, the same author who wrote the various Amrtasiddhi yoga manual texts that have been attributed to Virupa. The title and writings of this text are almost verbatim with his Amrtasiddhi texts. However, this text is attributed to the Siddha Krishnacarya and the text pays homage to Cakrasamvara (whereas Amrtasiddhi pays homage to Vajrayogini Chinnamasta).

This is an inversion (upside down) physical yoga practice. The text calls it 'upside-down or head-down stick yoga'. It is a practice in which the perineum and the crown chakra become upside down because of the posture of hanging upside down. There is one particular line in the 'mental observation' section of this text that might suggest that this could be one of the forms of Viparīta (mentioned in Brahmanical Hatha Yoga texts). The text says, དེ་ཉིད་ལོག་པའི་སྦྱོར་བ་ཡིས། 'By means of the yoga/practice of reversing (viparīta) these two (crown and perineum chakra).

This inversion physical yoga practice is the main practice of this text. The entire practice of the text has three phases: preliminary, main, and postliminary. The preliminary has seven yoga sequences, the main has only one (inversion yoga) with breathing practice and mental observation, and the postliminary also has seven yoga sequences. In all, there are fifteen of them. So the full title of the text is "Upside-Down Stick Yoga with Fifteen Trulkhors".

Amrtasiddhi texts (written by Amoghavajra) is based on six chakras (crown, forehead, throat, heart, navel and perineum) and five channel system. In this texts as well, five channels are mentioned instead of three channels. Five channels are, two upper left and right channels, two lower left and right channels, and one central channel.

#### Some notes on Translation

- As far as the Tibetan text is concerned, wherever I thought that there
  might be some major spelling mistakes in the text, I have put my
  suggestions in parentheses.

- Bending the throat is usually translated as bending the neck in
  English. Bending the throat or bending the neck here means pressing
  the chin against the sternum (ཨོག་མས་བྲང་རུས་ནོན་པ་) or pressing the chin
  against the larynx (ཨོག་མས་ཨོལ་མདུད་་ནོན་པ་). Taranatha says that when you
  do this posture, don't bend or bow your neck completely, but the back
  of your neck should be straighten up.

- Making a fist and crossing the arms over the chest is a distinctive
  feature of Vajrayana Buddhist yoga.

- The full lotus posture of the legs in the Brahmanical tradition is
  different from the Buddhist lotus crossed-legged posture mentioned
  here. The Hindu full-lotus posture and the Buddhist vajra
  crossed-legged posture are almost the same.

> དང་པོ་སྔོན་འགྲོའི་རིམ་པ་ནི།

Place your feet in the lotus position. Gather strength in both arms and press down your thighs with the palms of your hands. Through the application of straightening the spine, draw up the (air in) perineum (apana vayu). By applying (bending) the throat, (which is ) the air hole (gavākṣaḥ/window or doorway of the breath), raise (the apana vayu) upwards seven times.

Having interlocked your ten fingers, press your hands strongly just above your left knee. Straighten your spine, bend your neck and stand firm. Do the same with the left side.

Having firmly settled on the lower end of the spine, extend the legs fully forward by bringing the legs close together, and interlink the big toes. Straighten the spine and bend the throat. After making a fist with both hands, gather your strength and extend your arms forward.

Place your feet in the lotus position. Gather strength in your arms and plant the palms of your hands on the ground. Straighten your spine and bend your throat. Then rotate your abdomen thoroughly.

Sit in a squatting position with your feet close together. Straighten your spine and bend your throat. With your arms crossed (over your chest), gather your strength and bind your armpit and shoulder blade tightly with the palms of your hands.

Place your feet in the lotus position. Straighten your spine and bend your throat. Bending your right arm, hold your right shoulder (with your right hand) and rotate (your shoulder joint) seven times.

The second is the main phase: (the yogi) should master and practise the three crucial techniques. They are posture, mental observation and breathing technique.

#### 1. Body posture

> ``` text

After tying a dōrī (a rope) tightly around your ankles with your feet together, tie the rope tightly in the sky. Having remained suspended at a distance of one cubit (from the ground), clasp your (opposite) arm muscles with the palms of your folded arms. After inserting your first limb (head) inside (the folded arms), rise gracefully.

> དེ་གཉིས་ལོག་པའི་སྦྱོར་བ་ཡིས།
> ལུས་སྤྱི་འོད་ཀྱིས་བཀང་བྱས་ཏེ།
> དམིགས་པ་དེ་ལ་བརྟན་པར་བྱ།
> ```

Visualise your body in the form of your personal deity, Akshobhya (Bodhicitta bindu) on the crown of your head. Then meditate on the rainbow and the illusory body. Visualise the upper ends of the five channels (two upper left and right channels, two lower left and right channels and one central channel) pierced to the bindu Hri (Ham?) and the lower ends of the five channels remaining pierced in the perineum (nadi chakra) to the bindus of the seeds of the five classes (of goddesses) on the four petals and the centre of the nadi chakra. **Through the method of inverting these two (crown and perineum chakras)**, (visualise) your entire body filling with light, focus your mind on it firmly.

First, cough three times (to clear your windpipe). (Then) blow out the air of the right and left channels three times. The yogi should learn sixteen ('breathing in and out') with sound from the nose. (Then the yogi should) forcefully press down the upper air and draw up the lower air. Through the method of drawing in the life-bearing air (prana), (the yogi should) remain fully rooted in samadhi. This is the breathing technique.

Bending your arms behind your back, strike the first upper joint of your spine fourteen times one at a time (with your right and left fists). This will release (the stomach gas) in the form of a belch.
