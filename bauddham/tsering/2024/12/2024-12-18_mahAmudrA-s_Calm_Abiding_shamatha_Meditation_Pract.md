## Posture

So the first thing you should learn is how to sit in the seven-point posture of Vairocana. You should practise for several days. Your legs should be in either a vajra posture or a sattva posture, and the back should be straight. The hands should be in Dhyana Mudra four inches below the navel \[the right hand resting on the left palm with the thumbs joined together\]. The neck should be slightly bent, the tongue fixed to the palate, the lips closed but leaving a slight gap between the teeth. Eyes should rest on the tip of the nose in a peaceful gaze.

## Main practices

Calm-abiding \[Shamatha\] has two practices, they are 1. conceptual and 2. non-conceptual.

## 1. Conceptual

The conceptual also has two types of practices, (i) Practising with physical \[sense\] objects, and (ii) without physical object.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### (i) Practising with physical object

During the day, sitting in the seven-point posture of Vairocana, place a piece of wood or stone where your gaze falls. The object should not be bright and white in colour. With your gaze, place your mind on this object in a relaxed way, not holding on to the object with too much concentration, and not making any mental examination and investigation. Your mind should simply rest on the object without any distraction. At night, focus your mind on the white drop \[*bindu*\] at the point between your eyebrows. Practice these in short, frequent sessions and take short breaks. Between sessions, avoid watching entertainment \[*prekṣaṇa*\], talking too much, and doing strenuous activities. Instead, allow your body and mind to relax.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### (ii) Practising without physical object

This also has two types, (a) Practising with breath, and (b) Non-breath

#### (a) Practising with breath

**First, practise using the breath count** by simply being aware of the breath coming in and going out, without letting the mind wander to other objects. By counting the breath coming in and going out as one, keep your concentration on the first three rounds of breathing, then on seven, and so on. If the mind becomes restless, take a short break, then continue the practice and observe the experiential signs of the development of the practice.

**Then practise using the 'filling the breath in' method**. First, blow out the stale air either three times or nine times. Then inhale through both nostrils and swallow some saliva \[without making a sound\]. At the same time, press the air down without using too much force. While holding this breath, remain in the suspended space where there is no movement of thoughts. When you can no longer hold your breath, slowly let the air out through your nostrils. Then, as you breathe in and out, just be aware of the breath coming in and out without letting the mind wander to other objects.

When you feel tired, let the breath flow in and out in whatever way is most comfortable, and let your mind be completely relaxed, but without distraction, just being aware of the breath coming in and out. Take a short rest and then continue the practice, observing the experiential signs of the development of the practice. If during the practice the mind becomes agitated, it is because of too much concentration, so you should relax the concentration. If the mind becomes dense and unclear, it is due to fatigue; you should take a day's rest to refresh yourself.

The above practices are methods of holding the mind on its object, which the mind was previously unable to hold, by which you acquire the ability to settle your mind on an object for a certain amount of time without distraction \[it is not advisable to do analytical meditation before mastering this skill\]. However, if, while practising it, your mental concentration remains only for a short time \[or your mind does not rest in one place for a long time\] because of the increasing movement of the mind, you should not be discouraged and see it as a mistake. Because, compared to when you used to have chain after chain of thoughts before, you have now acquired the ability to focus your mind on one object for even a short time without being distracted.

When you do not see the shapes of the \[distracted mind\], the mind that wanders, and the mind that rests on the object, with no arising of doubtful thoughts, say the word *phat* out loud, and you should recognise that the mind between the end of the previous thought and before the arising of the next thought is the resting place of the non-conceptual mind. Immediately afterwards, when the thought arises, such as I should do this and I should have done that, you should know that this is the movement of thought.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### (b) Non-breath

So, having found the ability to settle the mind on an object for a certain amount of time, sit in the seven-point Vairocana posture and do not use any external objects such as visible objects, sounds, sensation and so forth, and the incoming and outgoing breath, as the object of your mind. Do not think about the past, such as 'I did this and that'. And don't let your mind go into the future, thinking I'm going to do this and I'm going to do that later. Don’t even register in your mind what thoughts are arising now. Keep your mind relaxed, clear and at ease.

In all the practices where you're trying to get your mind to settle on an object with stability, if your mind is too tense with concentration or if there's not enough vigour, you won't be able to bring about a clear and attentive mind. When you try to settle your mind, but your mind becomes agitated or scattered, try sitting in a warm place, eating nutritious food, massaging your body, lowering your gaze \[while meditating\], focussing your mind below the navel, etcetera. If your mind becomes dull and sluggish, try sitting in a cool place, splashing some cold water on your face, blowing out the stale air, raising your gaze, arousing some vigour and energy. Once you are able to rest your mind \[that is, clear, without distraction and thoughts\] on an object, if you don't practise this state of mind without an object or support of the mind, the next non-conceptual practice will not be effective.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 2. Non-conceptual

Non-conceptual also has two types of practice, (i) tightening and (ii) loosening the mind.

### (i) Tightening

Sit in the seven-point vairocana posture. The gaze should be straight ahead \[rather than down\]. Do not allow the mind to be distracted. Gather the mental energy and tighten \[focus\] the mind. Without having a reference point \[object of the mind\] of meditation, let the mind remain in a pure one-pointedness. After a short time, dissolve the one-pointedness, then tighten the mind again, and practise keeping the mind in precision without distraction for even a second.

Before, your undistracted, settled mind was kind of dim and dull, but now it is brightened up and remains clear with the presence of the mind of recollection \[mindfulness\]; this is the fruit of the effectiveness of the practice. For some of the quick-minded \[mentally agile\] people, although their mental sharpness may arise, it won't stay focused without distraction for a long time. Don't take this as a fault, but practise it for a short time and more often.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### (ii) Loosening

Adopt the gaze and posture as above. Do not have the intention that I am going to meditate this and I am not going to meditate that. Let your mind be free from the restraint of the hope and fear that if my mind settles \[without distraction and thoughts\] I'll be happy, and if my mind doesn't settle I won't be happy. Without rejecting or accepting and adjusting your mind, let the mind be relaxed and free in its natural state. In this state of mind, continue your meditation without any restraint, with the presence of the mind of recollection. While you are meditating in this way, if a thought suddenly arises, do not block or suppress it or pursue it, but maintain the steadiness of your mind \[without distraction\]. In the same way, do not suppress or pursue the subtle fleeting movement of thoughts. With the mere absence of distraction, you will be able to maintain your meditative mind. Even though various subtle and gross thoughts arise, with the guard of the mind of recollection, these thoughts become inoperative \[ineffective\], they subside spontaneously, and finally the mind settles down peacefully.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Note**: Calm abiding is considered the foundation of meditation. The meaning of the term calm-abiding \[according to this tradition\] is 'to remain in a state of undistracted mind where the thoughts have subsided'. This Śamatha \[ཞི་གནས\] instruction is based on Dagpo Panchen Tashi Namgyal’s (16th century) text.
