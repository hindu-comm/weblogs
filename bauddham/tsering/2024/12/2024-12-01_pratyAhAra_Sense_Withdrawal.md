## Introduction

- In this post you will find the Tibetan text and English translation of
  the entire part of the *Pratyāhāra* practice of *Ṣaḍaṅga yoga*
  composed by *Tārānāthā* with explanations based on *Tārānāthā’s* his
  own writings. This text is written in verse form as a teaching
  instruction divided into each specific practice, contemplation-round
  or round of practice (དམིགས་སྐོར).

- **Meaning of** ***Pratyāhāra*** **(*****Prati*****-*****āhāra*****):**
  *Pratyāhāra* is commonly known as withdrawal of the senses. According
  to *Tārānāthā*, the suffix *āhāra* has two main meanings, the first is
  withdrawal (སྡུད་པ), relinquishing (སྤོང་བ), cutting off (གཅོད་པ), etc.,
  and the second meaning is food, to eat and to seize. Accordingly he
  says that *Pratyāhāra* has two meanings. The first is that each of the
  five ordinary senses cuts off its connection with its objects (such as
  visible form, sound, smell, touch, taste). So the sense consciousness
  disappears or withdraws from the sphere of objects. The second is that
  the other five senses, which have the nature of gnosis, enjoy or feed
  on the objects, which have the nature of luminosity.

- **Meaning of the Tibetan yogic term བྱེད་བཅིངས
  (*****Karaṇa*****-*****bandhana*****):** One of the main aims of the
  *Pratyāhāra* practice of *Ṣaḍaṅga* *yoga,*is to see the image of
  emptiness (*Śūnyābimba*/སྟོང་པ་ཉིད་ཀྱི་གཟུགས་བརྙན་). This is done mainly by
  binding or restraining the vital air of the five senses (through the
  key postures and eye position), by binding the upper and lower air
  (*prana* and *apana vayu)*; this helps to subdue the mind and
  ultimately helps to see the image of emptiness. The Tibetan yogic
  phrase used for this practice is བྱེད་བཅིངས (*Karaṇa*-*bandhana*)་, བྱེད་
  (*Karaṇa*) refers to བྱེད་པ་དབང་པོ་ལྔ་ (the doer or instrument of the five
  senses), and བཅིངས (*bandhana*) refers to སྟེང་འོག་གི་རླུང་གཉིས་བཅིངས་པ་
  (binding or uniting the two upper (*prāṇa vāyu*) and lower air (*apāna
  vāyu)*. So the Tibetan term བྱེད་བཅིངས means binding the vital air of the
  five senses and the two upper and lower airs.

- **Explanation of 'striking the vital point of the body’**: In the
  practice of Vajrayana *Ṣaḍaṅga* *Yoga*, in order to strike the vital
  point of the body, it is essential to establish the non-conceptual
  mind through which the practitioner is able to see the image of
  emptiness. To 'strike the vital point of the body' means to touch the
  subtle central channel with the air and the mind. As Taranatha says,
  just as the hand cannot touch the sky, the conceptual mind cannot
  touch the central channel; it is only through the non-conceptual mind.
  Through Pratyahara, the practitioner is able to see the image of
  emptiness and Prana Vayu enters the central channel.

- **Yoga of No-Thought**: Yoga of No-Thought is an important practice in
  *Ṣaḍaṅga* Yoga (taught by Pandita Vibhutichandra) that helps to
  establish the non-conceptual mind. An analogy is given here of
  remaining like a statue in a temple. For the statues do not have the
  faculty of thought, although they may have a smiling or wrathful face.
  Similarly, the practitioner remains in a thoughtless state like a
  statue, where there is no thinker and no thoughts. Saraha (in his
  Doha) has given an analogy of keeping one's mind like the mind of a
  newborn baby (who hasn't developed its faculty of thinking compared to
  older people).

- **Self-liberating yoga**: The analogy given for this practice is that
  of three brothers always maintaining kinship with the middle one. This
  means that if there are three brothers, you will always go and play
  with the brother who is about your age, not with the other brothers
  who are older and younger than you. Similarly, in this practice you
  rest your mind in the present, not in the past and future.

- **Effortless Yoga**: The analogy given for this practice is that if a
  camel is tied to a tree, it becomes restless, but if it is untied, it
  stays in one place. This analogy is based on the story of a person who
  had a camel; when he tied the camel to a tree, the camel became
  restless and moved from place to place. However, when he untied the
  camel, it felt less constrained and stayed in one place. Similarly, if
  one concentrates too much in meditation, the mind becomes scattered
  and restless.

*(The Sanskrit word) āhāra means withdrawal (āhāra also means eating or taking) and prati means into each (of the five ordinary senses). (When) these two words are joined together (sandhi), (it becomes) pratyāhāra. (So pratyāhāra has two meanings.) (The first is) cutting the link between the ordinary five senses and the five sense objects, (whereby) the (sense) consciousness that wanders (to their objects) withdraws. (The second is) the five senses, which have the nature of gnosis, enjoy and eat or take the five objects, which have the nature of luminosity. In short, (in pratyāhāra) one's mind withdraws from ordinary perception and follows the light of primordial wisdom (that is image of emptiness).*

Both day yoga and night yoga must be integrated into this powerful method of realising the natural state. It is said that this is the intention of both Sutra and Tantra.*

Incorporate all of the aforementioned last three mental practices into the first basic mental practice. Sometimes concentrate (tighten your mind), sometimes loosen (your mind), sometimes watch clear awareness (the nature of your mind). In general, meditation should be effortless and unfabricated.

If you are a yogi or an ascetic, (practise) on a cliff where (objects) such as trees do not cast shadows on your body and others cannot see you. (Direct) sunlight and the glare of the snow are harmful to the eyes; these (sun, snow, lake, waterfall) should not be in your field of vision.*

Do not practise in the middle of the day (because of the upward gaze), it will damage your eyes. However, one can practise at noon by facing north, for example, where there is plenty of shade from the cliff (of the mountain) and the wall (of the veranda). Do not practise (day yoga) when a (cold) wind is blowing or when there are clouds in the sky. Instead (on such a day) practice night yoga in a dark room.*

These (postures) restrain the vital air of the five sense faculties.*

*Squeeze the belly in towards the spine and expand the belly out, do it one after the other, but not too fast, and increase it to an hour. This will help to bind both Prana and Apana Vayu. Practice (this breathing technique) for a third or a quarter of the session. It is said that this will bring about and enhance the experience and realisation of Pratyāhāra (which has not been able to come about even after performing Pratyāhāra for long time) and it also revitalise those that have diminished. This is the seventh round of practice.*

Through the power of integrating the energy of the daytime practice of Pratyāhāra at the time of sleep, you will be able to attain luminosity in about seven days.*
