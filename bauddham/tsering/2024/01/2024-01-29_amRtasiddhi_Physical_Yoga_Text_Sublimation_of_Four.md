#### Translator’s Note

Amoghavajra, an Indian yogi and Pandita, wrote numerous texts on the *Amṛtasiddhi* teachings of Virupa, almost all of which are attributed to Virupa/Birwapa (none of which have yet been studied by scholars and translated into other languages). In the Tibetan Tengyur canon there is a collection of his writings on *Amṛtasiddhi*. Texts such as *Amṛtasiddhi*, its commentary and various other *Amṛtasiddhi* yoga manuals. The content of Amoghavajra's *Amṛtasiddhi* text is very different from the Sanskrit *Amṛtasiddhi* text of Madhavacandra and the Tibetan *Amṛtasiddhi* texts of E Deva found in the Tibetan Tengyur canon and Shangpa teaching cycle.

This is one of several texts on physical yoga practices written by Amoghavajra. This text is in verse form. The Tibetan title of this text is འཆི་མེད་གྲུབ་པའི་སྡོམ་པ་བཞིའི་འཁྲུལ་འཁོར་. The Tibetan term སྡོམ་པ་ generally has meanings such as to vow, to bind, to tie, to restrain, etc. Without understanding the context of its use, it is difficult to translate and convey the closest meaning. I have taken the liberty of translating the Tibetan term སྡོམ་པ་ as sublimation after consulting Amoghavajra's commentary on *Amṛtasiddhi*. The *Amṛtasiddhi* commentary explains that སྡོམ་པ་ means the separation of the pure essence of the four elements from the impure gross four elements; resulting in the manifestation of the ultra-pure four elements of pristine consciousness (ཤིན་ཏུ་དངས་མ་ཡེ་ཤེས་ཀྱི་འབྱུང་བ་བཞི་).

According to the anonymous annotations in the text, the impurities/waste here refer to the three poisons (desire, anger and delusion), the purity/pure essence refers to bliss, clarity and no-thought, and the ultra-pure essence refers to the four kayas and five primordial wisdoms. It also says that the four elements here refer to the four inner elements, not the outer elements. So I have translated the title as '*Amṛtasiddhi's* Sublimation of the Four Internal Gross Elements Trulkhor'.

In the physical yoga practices of *Amrtasiddhi*, the breathing method is mentioned only in the main phase of the practice. Here, too, the practice of breathing is mentioned only in the main phase of yoga. In this translation, you will see only one sequence of yoga in each of the initial, main and final phases. In the actual text, there is only one sequence of yoga in the initial and the main phase, but there are two sequences of yoga in the final phase. In this translation, however, I have excluded one sequence of yoga from the final phase.

One important posture that comes again and again in Amoghavahra’s *Amṛtasiddhi* yoga instruction is ‘to bend the neck (throat/*kantha*/མགྲིན་པ་)’. This posture is also common in other Tibetan yoga practices. As Tāranātha explains, “When it says, bend the neck/throat, it does not mean to say keep the neck crooked or hanging down. The actual meaning is ‘lengthen the neck straight so that submental (ལྐོག་མ) is pressing on the larynx (*Kaṇṭhamaṇi*/ཨོལ་མདུད).

Another recurring yogic instruction is the breathing technique of 'drawing up the lower air and pressing down the upper air' at the navel. There are eleven different variations of this breathing technique in *Amrtasiddhi* and it is one of the most important aspects of *Amrtasiddhi* yoga practice. As Amoghavajra says in his commentary on *Amrtasiddhi*, 'This is a secret *mudrā* unique to *Amrtasiddhi*. It says,

"The outsiders (non-Buddhists) accept that bathing in Gaya, Ganga and the ghat of Ganga will bring them liberation. The non-Tantric *Mahāyāna* Buddhist accepts that liberation can be attained through five paths and ten grounds. Buddhist tantra in general holds that liberation can be attained through the generation and completion stages. Now here (in *Amṛtasiddhi*) its goal is attained by uniting the upper and lower air instantly at the navel emanation chakra (*nirmāṇacakra*).”

Of the four modes of sublimation of the four gross elements, the first is the sublimation of the earth element, which consists of three phases, the initial, the main and the final.

### I. Sublimation of the earth element

#### 1. Initial phase

Lying on your back with your thumbs interlocked, extend your legs with your legs close together. Bring your arms together and stretch them up (over your head). Make a tight fist and sit up seven times.

> ```

Second, the main phase is: Adopt a lotus cross-legged posture. Having raised the lotus cross-legged upward (knees upright position) and firmly settled on the bottom of the spine, straighten your spine and bend your throat. After interlocking the ten fingers, using your arms as a yoga strap, bind the crossed legs tightly. Having forcefully drawn up the lower air and forcefully pressed down the upper air, gather strength and practise.

> འགྲེང་བའི་ཚུལ་གྱིས་སྒོམ་པར་བྱ།
> ```

Third, the final phase is: Adopt the lotus cross-legged posture. Straighten the spine and bend the throat. Make a fist in both hands, plant them on the ground with strength, and practise in the manner of rising up.

Sit in a squatting position with your legs close together. Make a fist in your left hand and press it firmly against the third upper vertebra. After making a fist in your right hand, stretch your right arm out in front of you. Do the same with the left side.

Adopt a lotus cross-legged posture. Having settled at the bottom of the spine, straighten the spine and bend the throat. Grasp the armpits with the palms (of opposite hands), with the arms crossed (over the chest). After slightly drawing up the lower air, forcefully press down the upper air, with the shoulders and chest expanded, practise in the manner of shaking (dhunvat).

Third, the Final phase is: Adopt a lotus cross-legged posture. Straighten the spine and bend the throat. By means of samaya-mudrā (hands crossed over the chest), Press them firmly against the chest (heart centre). Then rotate the abdomen with strength.

> ``` text
> གསུམ་པ་རྗེས་ཀྱི་རིམ་པ་ནི།
> ཞབས་ནི་པདྨའི་སྐྱིལ་ཀྲུང་བཅའ།
> སྒལ་ཚིགས་བསྲང་ལ་མགྲིན་པ་དགུག།
> ལག་གཉིས་མཐེབ་མོ་བསྣོལ་བྱས་ཏེ།
> ཕྱིར་བཟློག་མདུན་དུ་བརྐྱང་བྱས་ཤིང།
> སྟོབས་རྣམས་བསྐྱེད་ཅིང་བསྒོམ་པར་བྱ།
> ```

Adopt a lotus cross-legged posture. Straighten the spine and bend the throat. Interlock your thumbs and stretch your arms out in front of you. Practice with all your strength.

### IV. Sublimation of the air element

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> རླུང་གི་སྡོམ་པའི་རྣམ་གཞག་ལ།
> སྔོན་འགྲོ་དངོས་གཞི་རྗེས་གསུམ་མོ།
> ```

Fourth, the sublimation of the air element consists of three phases, initial, main and final.

#### 1. Initial phase

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> དང་པོ་སྔོན་འགྲོའི་རིམ་པ་ནི།
> གན་རྐྱལ་དག་ཏུ་ཉལ་བྱས་ཏེ།
> རྐང་གཉིས་གཤིབས་པའི་སྦྱོར་བ་ཡིས།
> ནམ་མཁའ་དག་ལ་འགྲེང་བྱས་ཤིང།
> ལག་གཉིས་སྟོབས་རྣམས་བསྐྱེད་བྱ་སྟེ།
> རྐང་གཉིས་མཐེབ་མོ་མཐེན་བྱ་སྟེ།
> ཡར་གྱི་ཚིགས་པ་བདུན་པ་ལ།
> ལུས་ཀྱི་ལྗིད་རྣམས་བསྐུར་བྱ་སྟེ།
> སྟོབས་བསྐྱེད་རབ་ཏུ་བསྒོམ་པར་བྱ།
> ```

Lie on your back. Keep your legs next to each other. Lift them vertically up to the sky. Gather strength in both hands and pull the legs from the big toes. Put your body weight on the upper seventh vertebra. Gather strength and practise.

#### 2. Main phase

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> གཉིས་པ་དངོས་གཞིའི་རིམ་པ་ནི།
> ཞབས་གཉིས་གཤིབས་པའི་ལངས་བྱས་ནས།
> ལག་གཉིས་བསྣོལ་བའི་སྦྱོར་བ་བྱིས།
> རྐང་པའི་མཐེབ་མོ་གཉིས་བཟུང་ནས།
> འོག་རླུང་དྲག་པོར་ཐེན་བྱས་ཏེ།
> སྟེང་རླུང་དྲག་པོར་མནན་བྱས་ཤིང།
> ལག་གཉིས་སྟོབས་བསྐྱེད་ཐེན་བྱས་ནས།
> སྒལ་ཚིགས་མགྲིན་པ་དགུག་བྱས་ཤིང།
> སྟོབས་བསྐྱེད་རབ་ཏུ་བསྒོམ་པར་བྱ།
> ```

After standing with your feet close together, cross your arms and grasp your big toes (with opposite hands). Having forcefully drawn up the lower air and forcefully pressed down the upper air, gather strength and pull (the big toes) with your hands. Practice with strength by bending the spine and throat.

#### 3. Final phase

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> གསུམ་པ་རྗེས་ཀྱི་རིམ་པ་ནི།
> ཞབས་ནི་པདྨའི་སྐྱིལ་ཀྲུང་བཅའ།
> སྒལ་ཚིགས་བསྲང་ལ་མགྲིན་པ་དགུག།
> པུས་མགོ་གཉིས་ཀྱི་སྟེང་དག་ཏུ།
> ལག་གཉིས་མཐེབ་མོ་གཟུགས་བྱ་སྟེ།
> མཐོ་གཉིས་སྤྲད་པའི་ནང་དག་ཏུ།
> ཡན་ལག་དང་པོ་གཞུག་པར་བྱ།
> ```

Adopt a lotus cross-legged posture. Straighten the spine and bend the throat. Fix the thumbs on your (adjacent) knees. Guide the head (first limb) to the vicinity of the height of two spans.
