### Notes

#### *Vajrolī* \[*Haṭhapradīpikā* 3:82\]

> *yatnataḥ śaranālena phū*
>
> *tkāraṃ vajrakandare /*
>
> *śanaiḥ śanaiḥ prakurvīta vāyusaṃcārakāraṇāt* //

Carefully using a hollow stalk of bamboo grass, \[the yogi\] should very gently blow into the opening of the penis in order to make air move \[into the urethra\].

The term *vajrolī* is certainly derived from the word *vajra*, which has the meaning of penis, especially in Vajrayana Buddhism. In the above verse from *Haṭhapradīpikā*, the term *vajra*-*kandara* is used for penis hole/cavity. In this Tibetan text, the Tibetan term for penis hole is རྡོ་རྗེའི་སྦུབས་ \[*rdo rje'i sbubs*\], which has a similar etymology to the Sanskrit term *vajra*-*kandara*. Another similar terminology we find in this verse with our text is *śara*-*nāla*. The Tibetan term for *nāla* is སྦུ་གུ \[*sbu gu*\], here in this text yogis use a hollow wheat stalk \[གྲོ་སོག་སྦུ་གུ་\]. In this practice, according to the Tibetan text, 'the yogi inserts a hollow wheat stalk into the cavity of the vajra \[penis\]. Blow air while holding the wheat stalk in the mouth. And draw the air through the central channel \[urethra\]'. Yogis also use a bamboo or iron spoon \[a kind of tubular vessel\] to draw water through the penis. In this article I have given the translation of the practice of drinking \[sucking\] air and water through the vajra \[penis\]. The text is written by Drikung Chungtsang \[17th century\]. This practice is called ‘using one’s own body as a method’. I will post the translation of the practice of drawing semen and blood at another time.

**Note**: The above Sanskrit text and English translation of *Haṭhapradīpikā* are taken from Hathapradipika.online.

The term often used in this text is 'single pillar' of the body, when I have referred to other texts 'single pillar' refers to the central channel. I wonder if in this context it could also mean the urethra. The image of the central channel running from the crown of the head to the penis is from the Blue Beryl medical charts. The Blue Beryl medical charts were designed and commissioned by Desi Sangya Gyatso in the late 17th century \[Jeff Watt Himalayan Art resources\].
