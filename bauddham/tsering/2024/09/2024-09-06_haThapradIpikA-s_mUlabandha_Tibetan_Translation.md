The Hathayogic technique of *mūlabandhaḥ* \[root lock\] is taught in the Vajrayana text *Amṛtasiddhi* under the practice of *mahābandha*, which precedes the Hindu texts of Hatha Yoga. According to *Amṛtasiddhi*, there are two types of *bandha*, *yonibandha* \[perineum lock\], also called *mūlabandha*, and *Kaṇṭhabandha* \[throat lock\]. The Tibetan term for *mūlabandha* is རྩ་བ་བསྡམ་པ་ \[*rtsa ba bsdam pa*\]. For the *Amṛtasiddhi*'s *yonibandha* or *mūlabandha* instruction, see [my previous Substack](https://open.substack.com/pub/tsering/p/amrtasiddhi-mahabandha-section-sanskrit?r=19pwkm&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web) article.

I have translated the *mūlabandha* \[root lock\] section of the *Haṭhapradīpikā* into Tibetan. I have seen some similarities in these yogic steps and methods, between the Vajrayana *Caṇḍālī* \[tummo\] and the Brahmanical Hatha Yoga’s kundalini practice of igniting the fire and sending it into the central channel. In Tibetan Vajrayana texts, *[Caṇḍālī](https://open.substack.com/pub/tsering/p/verse-14-the-hidden-yogic-meaning?r=19pwkm&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web)* [\[tummo\] is also sometimes described as a circling serpent-like form or a coiled snake](https://open.substack.com/pub/tsering/p/verse-14-the-hidden-yogic-meaning?r=19pwkm&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web). This is how a 17th century Tibetan master describes *Caṇḍālī* \[Tummo\] as:

> By drawing the lower air upwards,
> The red air of wisdom,
> whirls in a circle (Kuṇḍali) upward.
> Both upper and lower air,
> possessing the heat of fire,
> coils (Kuṇḍali) like a whitish red snake,
> At the navel nirmana chakra.
> Between the navel and the secret (chakra),
> there is the true Cāṇḍālī,
> which (looks) like the eye of an antelope (aiṇeya),
> red in colour, very fine and crooked.
> It is hot and has a sharp point.
> It blazes upward and enters Avadhūtī [central channel].
> It is four inches long.
> It has a thick middle part,
> the size of a grain.
> It has very fine tips,
> as fine as a horse's tail.
> This is the self-born Cāṇḍālī (Tummo).
> ```

### Tibetan Translation of *Haṭhapradīpikā*’s *Mūlabandha* section

I.

pārṣṇibhāgena saṃpīḍya yonim ākuñcayed gudam /

apānam ūrdhvam ākṛṣya mūlabandho’yam ucyate //

> ``` text
> རྟིང་པས་འོག་སྒོའི་བར་དག་ཏུ།
> མནན་ཅིང་བཤང་སྒོ་བསྐུམ་བྱས་པས།
> ཐུར་སེལ་གྱེན་དུ་འདྲེན་པར་བྱ།
> རྩ་བ་བསྡམ་པ་ཞེས་བརྗོད་དོ༎
> ```

The root lock (*mūlabandha*)—When \[the yogi\] presses the perineum with the side of the heel, clenches the anus and draws up *apāna*, it is called the root lock.

II\.

adhogatim apānaṃ vai ūrdhvagaṃ kurute balāt /

ākuñcanena taṃ prāhur mūlabandhaṃ tu yoginaḥ //

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> ཐུར་དུ་འགྲོ་བའི་ཐུར་སེལ་རླུང།
> བསྐུམ་པས་གྱེན་དུ་བཙན་ཐབས་སུ།
> བཀོད་པ་དེ་་ལ་རྣལ་འབྱོར་པས།
> རྩ་བ་བསྡམ་པ་ཡང་བརྗོད་དོ༎
> ```

\[The yogi\] forces the downward-moving *apāna* breath to move upwards by contracting \[the anus\]. Yogis call that *mūlabandha*.

III\.

gudaṃ pārṣṇyā tu saṃpīḍya vāyum ākuñcayed balāt /

vāraṃ vāraṃ yathā cordhvaṃ samāyāti samīraṇaḥ //

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> རྟིང་པས་བཤང་ལམ་མནན་བྱས་ནས།
> འོག་རླུང་དྲག་པོར་ཡང་ནས་ཡང།
> བསྐུམ་པར་བྱས་ཅིང་རླུང་དག་ནི།
> གྱེན་དུ་འོང་བར་བྱས་པའོ༎
> ```

\[The yogi\] should press his anus with his heel and forcefully contract the \[*apāna*\] wind over and over again so that the breath goes upwards.

IV\.

prāṇāpānau nādabindū mūlabandhena caikatām /

gatvā yogasya saṃsiddhiṃ yacchato nātra saṃśayaḥ //

> published
>
> ``` text
> རྩ་བ་བསྡམ་པས་སྲོག་ཐུར་དང།
> ནཱ་ད་ཐིག་ལེ་གཅིག་ཉིད་དུ།
> འགྱུར་བས་རྣལ་འབྱོར་ཡང་དག་པར།
> གྲུབ་པར་སྦྱིན་པའི་ཐེ་ཚོམ་མེད༎
> ```

When *prāṇa* and *apāna* \[and\] *nāda* and *bindu* become united by means of the root lock they are sure to bestow complete success in yoga.

V.

apāne cordhvage jāte saṃprāpte vahnimaṇḍalaṃ /

tadānalaśikhā dīrghā vardhate vāyunāhatā //

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> ཐུར་སེལ་གྱེན་དུ་ཕྱོགས་གྱུར་ནས། 
> མེའི་དཀྱིལ་འཁོར་སླེབ་པའི་ཚེ།
> དེ་ནས་རླུང་གིས་བདས་ནས་ནི།
> མེ་ལྕེ་རིང་དུ་འགྱུར་བའོ༎
> ```

When *apāna* has turned upwards and reached the orb of fire, then the flame of the fire, fanned by the wind, grows tall.

VI\.

tato yātau vahnyapānau prāṇam uṣṇasvarūpakam /

tenātyantapradīptas tu jvalano dehajas tathā //

> published
>
> ``` text
> དེ་ནས་མེ་དང་ཐུར་སེལ་གཉིས།
> སྲོག་འཛིན་དག་དང་འཕྲད་བྱས་ཞིང།
> དེ་ནི་ཚ་བའི་རང་བཞིན་ཅན།
> ལུས་ནང་མེ་ནི་རབ་ཏུ་འབར༎
> ```

As a result, fire and *apāna* reach *prāṇa*, which is hot by nature, and the *prāṇa* makes the fire in the body extremely hot.

VII\.

tena kuṇḍalinī suptā saṃtaptā saṃprabudhyate /

daṇḍāhatā bhujaṅgīva niśvasya ṛjutāṃ vrajet //

> published
>
> ``` text
> གཉིད་ལོག་ཀུཎ་ཌ་ལི་ནཱི་ཉིད།
> མེ་ཡིས་གདུངས་ཤིང་གཉིད་སད་ཅིང།
> སྦྲུལ་ལ་དབྱུག་པ་བརྡུངས་བ་ལྟར།
> དབུགས་ནི་ཕྱུང་ཞིང་དྲང་པོར་འགྱུར༎
> ```

Heated by that \[blaze\], the sleeping *kuṇḍalinī* wakes up. Like a snake struck by a stick, she hisses and becomes straight.

VIII\.

bilaṃ praviṣṭeva tato brahmanāḍyantaraṃ vrajet /

tasmān nityaṃ mūlabandhaḥ kartavyo yogibhiḥ sadā //

> published
>
> ``` text
> དེ་ནས་དེ་ཉིད་ཚངས་རྩའི་ནང།
> བུག་པར་ཞུགས་པ་བཞིན་དུ་འཇུག།
> རྣལ་འབྱོར་པ་ཡིས་དུས་རྒྱུན་དུ།
> རྩ་བ་སྡམ་པ་རྟག་ཏུ་བྱ།
> ```

Then, like \[a snake\] that has entered a hole, she goes into the channel of Brahman. Therefore, yogis should regularly practise the root lock.
