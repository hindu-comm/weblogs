### English translation of Tibetan verse

Homage to you, whose eyes shine with lustre, bright with the fullness of sun and moon. With twice-uttered *hara* and *tuttāre, you pacify the most intractable diseases.*

### Hidden meaning behind the verse as explained by Tārānāthā

namaḥ candrārka-sampūrṇa-nayana-dyuti-bhāsvare\|  
  
tārā-dvir-ukta-tuttāre-viṣamajvara-nāśani\|\|

*Candrārka* \[ཉི་མ་ཟླ་བ་\], the sun and the moon are the inner blood and semen. The two eyes that are completely filled \[*sampūrṇa*-*nayana/*ཡོངས་སུ་གངས་བ་འམ་རྒྱས་པའི་སྤྱན་གཉིས\] with these two elements are the two upper and lower openings of the central channel. Because the primordial wisdom emanates from the central channel, it is called the bright/shining light \[*dyuti*-*bhāsvare*/འོད་གསལ་བ་\].

Tāra means deliverance, deliverance from both \[*tārā*-*dvir*/ཏཱ་ར་གཉིས་\] the extremes of samsara and nirvana through \[the union of\] bliss and emptiness. *Tuttāra* is pain and austerity, that is, pressing the sun downwards and drawing the moon to the crown of the head. This austerity practice overcomes the chronic fever \[*viṣamajvara*-*nāśani*/རིམས་ནད་སེལ་བ་\] caused by the loss of the bindus.
