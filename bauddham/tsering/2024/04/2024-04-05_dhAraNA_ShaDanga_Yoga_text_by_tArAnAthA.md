### Translator’s Note:

- In this post you will find the Tibetan text and English translation of
  the latter half of the *Dhāraṇā* practice of *Ṣaḍaṅga yoga* text
  composed by *Tārānāthā*, with explanations in brackets based on
  *Tārānāthā*'s own writings.

- See my previous articles if you want to read the first, second and the
  third ancillaries of *Ṣaḍaṅga yoga* (*Pratyāhāra*, *Dhyāna and
  Prāṇāyāma*).

- *Pratyāhāra* (the first ancillary of *Ṣaḍaṅga yoga*) contains the
  [first eight rounds of
  practice](https://open.substack.com/pub/tsering/p/pratyahara-sense-withdrawal-practice?r=19pwkm&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web)
  (of *Ṣaḍaṅga yoga*). *Dhyāna* contains [five rounds of
  practice](https://open.substack.com/pub/tsering/p/dhyana-sadanga-yoga-text-composed?r=19pwkm&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web),
  starting from the ninth to the thirteenth round of practice.
  *Prāṇāyāma* contains nine rounds of practice, from the fourteenth to
  the twenty-second. *Dhāraṇā* contains nine rounds of practices
  starting from twenty-sixth to thirty-fourth round of practices.

- The Tibetan text of the 'enhancing practice' is not written in detail
  here in this particular text, so there is no Tibetan text of this
  part. However, Taranatha has explained this 'enhancing practice' in
  detail in another text, which I have presented here in English
  translation.

- *Dhāraṇā* means ‘holding’ the vayus inside the bindu of the central
  channel\*. It doesn’t means contemplation.

- Where the translation is asterisked, this means that I have
  interpreted it myself.

> ``` text
> ༈ གཉིས་པ་འཛིན་པ་སྟོན་པར་བྱེད་པ་ལ། 
> །ཉམས་ལེན་དམིགས་པའི་གནད་ལ་མན་ངག་དངོས།
> ```

*In the instruction of Dhāraṇā (the fourth ancillary of Ṣaḍaṅga yoga), there are (two things to keep in mind): 1. Essential key points of the practice (and, 2. Meaning and purpose of practice of Dhāraṇā). 1. (The first) essential key points of the practice (also has two parts: (i) the actual practice of Dhāraṇā, and (ii) achieving a result through alternative method of enhancing practice), but here (only) the actual practice of Dhāraṇā is taught.*

#### *Kumbhaka* of *Dhāraṇā* Vs *Kumbhaka* of *Prāṇāyāma*

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> །ཇི་སྐད་སྔར་ལྟར་བུམ་ཅན་སྦྱོར་བ་ཡིས། 
> །སྲོག་ནི་དབུ་མའི་ཐིག་ལེར་ཐིམ་པ་སྟེ། 
> །སྲོགའཛིན་བུམ་ཅན་བུམ་ཅན་ཙམ་འདྲ་ཡང་། 
> །སྲོག་རྩོལ་བུམ་ཅན་རོ་རྐྱང་ལམ་དགག་དང༌། 
> །སྲོག་ཐུར་འདྲེ་ཕྱིར་སྒོམ་པ་སྐབས་འདིར་ནི། 
> །སྲོག་ཐུར་འདྲེས་ཟིན་ཐིག་ལེར་ཐིམ་པའི་ཆེད།
> ```

(In Dhāraṇā) prāṇa vāyu is dissolved in the bindu of the central channel through kumbhaka breathing, the practice shown earlier (in Prāṇāyāma). Although the kumbhaka of Prāṇāyāma and the kumbhaka of Dhāraṇā are similar, only both are called kumbhaka (but they have differences). Kumbhaka in Prāṇāyāma stops the path of the right and left channels. It is practised to mix the prāṇa and apāna vāyu together. But here (in Dhāraṇā) it is practised specifically to dissolve the union of prāṇa and apāna vāyu that has been mixed together (during Prāṇāyāma).

#### Navel *kumbhaka*

**(XXVI) Twenty Sixth Round of Practice**

> published
>
> ``` text
> ལྟེ་བའི་བུམ་ཅན་སྔར་དང་འདྲ་བ་སྟེ། 
> །འོན་ཀྱང་འཛིན་པ་རྗེས་མཐུན་སྒོམ་པའི་སྐབས། 
> །རླུང་རྣམས་ལྟེ་བའི་ཐིག་ལེར་ཐིམ་པ་ཡི། 
> །མོས་པ་བྱ་སྟེ་ལྟེ་བའི་བུམ་ཅན་ནི། 
> །ཞག་གྲངས་བཅོ་ལྔ་དམིགས་བསྐོར་ཉེར་དྲུག་པའོ།
> ```

(Here) navel kumbhaka is similar to the earlier (navel kumbhaka in Prāṇāyāma). However, while practising (navel kumbhaka) in accordance with Dhāraṇā, imagine that the vayus are dissolving in the bindu of the navel (in the central channel). You should practise this navel kumbhaka for fifteen days. This is the twenty-sixth round of practice.

#### Heart *kumbhaka*

**(XXVII) Twenty seventh Round of Practice**

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> །སྙིང་ཁའི་བུམ་ཅན་དེ་དང་འདྲ་བ་ལས། 
> །ལྟེ་བའི་བུམ་ཅན་བཅས་ཟིན་སྙིང་གར་འདྲེན། 
> །ལག་གཉིས་རྒྱབ་བསྣོལ་རྡོ་རྗེ་སྐྱིལ་ཀྲུང་གི། 
> །མཐེ་བོང་གཡས་གཡོན་འཐེན་པའི་ལུས་གནད་སྦྲགས། 
> །དམིགས་བསྐོར་ཉེར་བདུན་(མགྲིན་པའི་བུམ་ཅན་ནི།)
> ```

The heart kumbhaka is similar to the navel kumbhaka. At the end of the navel kumbhaka, the union of prāṇa and apāna vāyu is drawn towards the heart. This is done through this posture: folding the arms behind the back, pulling the big toes (with the opposite hands) in a vajra cross-legged posture. This is the twenty-seventh round of the practice.

#### Throat *kumbhaka*

**(XXVIII) Twenty eighth Round of Practice**

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> (།དམིགས་བསྐོར་ཉེར་བདུན་)མགྲིན་པའི་བུམ་ཅན་ནི། 
> །རྐང་པ་སྐྱིལ་ཀྲུང་པུས་མོའི་ནང་ཁུགས་སུ། 
> །གྲུ་མོ་བཙུགས་ཏེ་སོར་མོ་བསྣོལ་བ་ཡིས། 
> །ལྟག་པ་མནན་ཏེ་མགོ་བསྒྱུར་ཁ་ནས་རླུང་། 
> །རྔུབ་སྟེ་ལྟག་པའི་ཐད་ཀར་ཤུགས་བསྒྲིལ་ལ། 
> །ལྐོག་མས་ཨོལ་མདུད་ཐུར་སེལ་མགྲིན་པར་ཕུལ། 
> །དམིགས་བསྐོར་ཉེར་བརྒྱད་(དཔྲལ་པའི་བུམ་ཅན་ནི)།
> ```

Sitting cross-legged, place your elbows on the inside of your knees. Interlocking (the ten) fingers, press your hand down from the back of your neck. Turning (head up), inhale the air through the mouth and hold the pressure/force directly at the back of the neck. Press the larynx with the chin and draw/push the apāna vāyu straight up to the throat. This is the twenty-eighth round of the practice.

#### Forehead *kumbhaka*

**(XXIX) Twenty-ninth Round of Practice**

> published

> (དམིགས་བསྐོར་ཉེར་བརྒྱད་)དཔྲལ་པའི་བུམ་ཅན་ནི།

> ```

The forehead *kumbhaka* is almost as same as above. The difference is, hands in the earlier position, pull (the hands) upward from the crown of the head. Draw in the air through the mouth and hold it in the the palate cavity.

Crown Kumbhaka is almost the same as (Forehead Kumbhaka) except that the object of observation here is the crown. This is the thirtieth round of practice.

#### Perineum *kumbhaka*

**(XXXI) Thirty-one Round of Practice**

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> །མན་ངག་ལས་འབྱུང་གསང་བའི་བུམ་ཅན་ནི། 
> །རྟིང་པ་གཅིག་གིས་བཤང་སྒོ་བརྟེན་བྱས་ནས། 
> །གཅིག་གིས་གསང་བ་མནན་ལ་ལག་པ་གཉིས། 
> །གཞུག་ཆུང་ནས་ནི་ཕར་འཁྱིད་གང་ཙམ་བཙུགས། 
> །གདོང་གྱེན་ཕྱོགས་པས་རླུང་དྲག་རིང་བར་མནན། 
> །བཤང་སྒོ་གསང་སྒོ་གཉིས་ནས་ཐུར་སེལ་རླུང་། 
> །ལན་མང་དྲངས་པ་དེ་དང་སྟེང་རླུང་གཉིས། 
> །རྒྱུ་ཞབས་མནན་བཞག་དམིགས་བསྐོར་སོ་གཅིག་པ།
> ```

Support one heel against the anus and press the other heel down on the genitalia. Place both hands one span away from the tailbone. Turning your face upwards, take a long, powerful breath and press it down. Draw the apāna vāyu upwards several times from both the anus and the genitals. Then press both the upper air (prāṇa vāyu) and the lower air (apāna vāyu) under the intestines and leave them there. This is the thirty-one round of the practice.

> །མི་འདྲེན་རླུང་ནི་གཏོང་ཁར་ལྟེ་བར་འབེབས། 
> །མཐར་ནི་གསང་བའི་བུམ་ཅན་ཚར་རེ་བྱ། 
> །རླུང་རོ་འཁྱམས་དོགས་ལག་ལེན་ཤེས་པར་བྱ།

(Taranatha’s Note: Although some advise beginners not to practise *Kumbhaka* further than the navel *Kumbhaka*, there is no problem with moderate practice. If beginners wish to practise, they should do so as follows.

After practising navel kumbaka about twenty times, practise heart kumbaka once. Do it by bringing navel kumbhaka (up) to the heart. Then bring (the heart kumbhaka) back down to the navel, and then the air should be released.

Though the throat, forehead and crown kumbhakas are not drawn up (directly) from the navel kumbhaka (\*in throat kumbhaka, the kumbhaka is drawn up from the heart, not from the navel), at the time of releasing the air, the kumbhaka (of the throat, forehead and crown) should be brought back to the navel. At the end of these kumbhaka, the practitioner should practise perineum kumbhaka once. If you have concerns about (harmful) residual circles (in the body), you should know the methods (for their removal).

### Enhancing Practice

**(Alternative method when** ***Kumbhaka*** **practice alone does not produce the desired results)**

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> །རླུང་དང་རྩ་ཐིག་འཁྲུལ་འཁོར་སྤོག་ཆོག་དང༌། 
> །བདུད་རྩིའི་བཅུད་མྱང་རྣམས་ནི་བར་སྐབས་སྒོམ།
> ```

The enhancement practice through vayu, nadi, bindu and trulkhor, and the practice of tasting the juice of Amrita, practise them in the interim.

#### Vayu enhancing practice

**(XXXII) Thirty-second Round of Practice**

Even after following the practice of the above Dhāraṇā instruction, if it does not bring about the desired result, it is because kumbhaka alone is not enough to unite the upper and lower airs.

In this case, sit either in the revelling posture or the vajra cross-legged posture, and cover your knees with your hands. First pull the anus upwards, then release the upper air with a long "hum" (ཧཱུཾ) sound. Then draw in the air again with a short 'hum' (ཧུཾ) sound and press it down. After a while, with a rattling ‘hum’ sound, bring the perineum air upwards, fix the tongue on the palate, and remembering the sound of ‘phat’ (ཕཊ) in your mind, hold the union (of the upper and lower air) forcefully at the navel. While holding the air, expand your belly out several times. Then release the air. Practise this again and again. This is Pandita Shakya Shri method and this is the thirty-second round of practice.

#### Trulkhor enhancing practice

**(XXXIII) Thirty-third Round of Practice**

The breathing technique is similar to the above. (The trulkhor is) When exhaling, hold your feet from the toes, then stand up by bending your body forward. When holding the air after inhaling, place your buttocks on the seat and remain in a squatting position. When you draw up the air in the perineum, bend your (upper) body. When expanding the abdomen, bend the neck backwards. This is the thirty-third round of practice.

#### Tasting the juice of Amrita

**(Milking nectar from the small tongue)**

Place the hands in a ‘earth-touching’ mudra, otherwise rest of the body postures are same. Visualise a white ‘ham’ (ཧཾ) bindu at the forehead circled by sixteen vowels. If you can not visualise the syllables clearly, it is ok to visualise just white bindu.

As you draw the apāna vāyu upwards, the air, which is naturally warm, goes up through the abdomen and the chest. And when the warm air touches the white bindu, the Amrita begins to fall down like a stream of milk. Then with the 'shu' sound, draw in the air through the mouth, bringing the air over the tongue. And then swallow the air along with the saliva. Then roll your tongue into the cavity, touching the little tongue. Having filled the air equally in both the upper and lower body, a stream of nectar will flow down and imagine it filling the nāḍīs throughout the body. If you practise this again and again. One day the nectar will flow naturally from the little tongue.
