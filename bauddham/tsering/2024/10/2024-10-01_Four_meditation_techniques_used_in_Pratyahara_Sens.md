# Four meditation techniques used in Pratyahara \[Sense withdrawal\] to conquer thoughts

This technique has come down through Pandita Vibhūticandra \[12th century\]. It is used in the night yoga and day yoga of the Shadanga yoga practice and is particularly associated with the Sakya and Jonang traditions. The four techniques are 1. Yoga of No-thought \[བསམ་མེད་རྣལ་འབྱོར་\], 2. One-pointed Yoga \[རྩེ་གཅིག་རྣལ་འབྱོར་\], 3. Yoga of Self-Release of the Mind \[རང་གྲོལ་རྣལ་འབྱོར་\] and is also called Remaining in the Now \[ད་ལྟ་བར་འཛིན་པ་\], and 4. Effortless Yoga \[རྩོལ་བྲལ་རྣལ་འབྱོར་\], also called Loosening the Mind \[སེམས་ལྷོད་ཀྱི་གློད་པ\]. Each of these four techniques has the following analogies: 1. Like a statue in a temple \[ལྷ་ཁང་ལྡེར་བཟོ་ལྟ་བུ་\], 2. A monkey with one door (with other doors closed) \[སྤྲེའུ་སྒོ་གཅིག་ལྟ་བུ\], 3. Out of three brothers, always maintaining kinship with the middle one (just as your mind always remains in the now, not distracted by the past and future) \[སྤུན་གསུམ་བར་མ་འགྲོགས་ལྟ་བུ\], and 4. A camel tied to a tree becomes restless, but when it is untied it remains in one place \[རྔ་མོ་བཏགས་གྲོལ་ལྟ་བུ་\].

### I. Yoga of No-thought like the statue in a temple

This technique is part of the unwavering body, unwavering breath and unwavering mind. \[མི་གཡོ་གསུམ་ལྡན\].

#### Unwavering body

Sit motionless with legs crossed, spine straight, hands on knees or in dhyana mudra, neck slightly bent. Focus your eyes on the sky twelve or sixteen inches above the level of your nostrils with your eyes rolled up without blinking or moving your eyes.

#### Unwavering breath

Do not deliberately hold air and do not deliberately exhale air. Let breathing be natural, without correction.

#### Unwavering mind

Without correcting your mind, the mind in its innate present state, single-pointed but free and relaxed, abides in samadhi without distraction or thinking anything. The following three techniques help to maintain this unwavering mind.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### II. Single-pointed yoga, like a monkey with one door

This helps to maintain one-pointed mental alertness and concentration. If a monkey is left in a house with six doors open, the monkey will move from one door to another, but if all the doors are closed except one, then the monkey will remain or look through only one door. Similarly, if the five senses are closed, then the one door, the sixth sense, will remain focused single-pointedly on its object. It is done by concentrating the mind, eyes and vital air in the sky/space near the spot between the eyebrows by which your mind and the space merge into one.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### III. Staying in the present, like maintaining kinship with the middle brother

Of three brothers, you always stay with the middle brother, ignoring the elder and the younger. Similarly, don't let your mind wander to the past and the future, and merge the mind that distracts with the sky/space \[where your gaze is\] by bringing your focus to the mind’s \[present\] aspects of awareness and experience. Place your mind on your fresh consciousness of the present moment with the mindfulness of the recognition of one's true nature. This is called Self-liberating or mind freeing itself yoga.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### IV. Effortless yoga like freeing a tied camel

When a camel is tied to a tree, it moves in all directions. But if you untie the camel and it sees that it is free, it will stay in one place without moving. Similarly, if you concentrate \[tighten\] your mind too much, it becomes restless, but if you loosen and relax your mind, you are able to prolong your meditation without effort.
