## Introduction

The title of the text is 'བིཪྺ་པའི་སྐུའི་རྣམ་འགྱུར་དྲུག་’ (Virupa’s Six Physical Expressions), available in the collected works of Ngorchen Kunga Zangpo (15th century). According to the text's colophon, it was compiled by Naljorpa Rinchen Dorje. This text shows six different physical appearances and postures of Virupa, based on the six major events of his life, or six siddhis, which are used for the visualisation of Virupa and in his iconography.

Jamyang Khyentse Wangpo has written a short text explaining how these six physical appearances or siddhis of Virupa \[in Virupa’s supplication prayer\] can be applied in accordance with one's inner practice. Jamyang Khyentse Wangpo supplicates Virupa by associating each of his six siddhi with the six paramitas. It seems that these siddhis are not just physical yogic powers. They are also to be understood as inner yogic accomplishments. For example, Virupa is particularly known for the two siddhis of 'reversing the river Ganga upwards' and 'pawning/stopping the sun for wine'.

> །བླ་མ་དེ་ལ་ཕྱག་འཚལ་ལོ།
> ```

I bow to Guru Virupa, who steered the great river upwards and pawned the sun for wine.

#### A text from the Drukpa’s Great Dharma Treasury explains the meaning of these two siddhis as follows

> When \[internally\] one is able to reverse the \[direction of the\]
> bindu upwards...externally one is able to direct the river upwards.
> When \[internally\] one is able to press the *rajas*, the red element,
> down...externally one is able to stop the sun. In samsara, as in the
> outer world, water flows down and fire burns up. In the body too, the
> red element burns upwards and the white element flows downwards.
> \[When the red element\] goes up, it escapes from the crown of the
> head through the door of the senses, which produces dualistic vision,
> various conceptual thoughts and death. \[When the white element\] goes
> down and falls out, it produces old age, sickness and suffering. The
> yogi blocks the lower door of the white element and the upper door of
> the red element, and he is able to stop the karma and klesha, disease,
> suffering, conceptual thoughts and death.

I would also like to mention here the Mahabandha yoga technique of Virupa's Amrtasiddhi teaching, because this technique also mentions reversing \[*viparītā*\] the direction of the flow of the bindu upwards through Mahabandha, which also fits with Virupa's siddhi of reversing the river Ganga.

> ```

**English translation \[from Tibetan text\]**

Stream \[of bindu\] flows down the channels and always travels downwards. By means of Mahābandha, completely reverses (the flow of direction)

#### Jamyang Khyentse Wanpo’s explanation of Virupa’s siddhi ‘Reversing the river ganga’

> In the natural order, the bindu \[flows down\] as a stream of ganga.
> In the reverse order, the bindu is restrained in jalandhara \[the
> crown of the head\] and remains motionless. I pray to the youthful
> figure of supreme discipline, the vajra body free from defilements.

**Notes**:

- The right and left channels are the passage of the sun and the moon,
  or the red element and the white element. Virupa's right arm
  represents the right channel and the sun represents the red element.
  Virupa's left arm represents the left channel and the river Ganga
  represents the white element.

- There is an English translation of this text available in Himalayan
  Art Resources by Jeff Watts ‘[Indian Adept: Virupa (Six
  Forms)](https://www.himalayanart.org/search/set.cfm?setID=1988&page=1)’
  with images.

  dated 15th century. The second images are photographs of mural
  paintings in Nepal dated to the 17th century. All images are available
  on the Himalayan Art Resources website.
