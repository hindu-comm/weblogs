### Some notes on translation:

- The *Ṣaḍaṅga yoga* (Six limbs of yoga) of Vajrayana has been less
  studied and is not well known in either the academic or lay world. The
  *Ṣaḍaṅga yoga* practice of Kalachakra Tantra is generally mentioned in
  almost all schools of Tibetan Buddhism. However, Jonang, Sakya and
  Gelukpa ( which came down from Buton Rinchen Drup and Tsongkhpa) are
  the main proponents of the *Ṣaḍaṅga yoga* practice. This article will
  tell you what *prānāyāma* is and how it is practised according to
  Jonang Tārānāthā.

- This article contains the Tibetan text and English translation of the
  whole *prānāyāma* section (*prānāyāma* method of Paṇḍita Śākya Śri and
  Paṇḍita Somanātha's ) of the *Ṣaḍaṅga Yoga* text written by Tārānāthā,
  with explanations in brackets based on *Tārānāthā*'s own writings.

- *Pratyāhāra* (the first ancillary of *Ṣaḍaṅga yoga*) contains the
  [first eight rounds of
  practice](https://open.substack.com/pub/tsering/p/pratyahara-sense-withdrawal-practice?r=19pwkm&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web)
  (of *Ṣaḍaṅga yoga*). *Dhyāna* contains [five rounds of
  practice](https://open.substack.com/pub/tsering/p/dhyana-sadanga-yoga-text-composed?r=19pwkm&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web),
  starting from the ninth to the thirteenth round of practice.
  *Prānāyāma* contains nine rounds of practice, from the fourteenth to
  the twenty-second.

- བར་དུ་དགེ་བ་ (propitious in the middle/*madhye-kalyāṇam*): Six
  ancillaries of yoga are divided into three groups: *pratyāhāra* and
  *dhyāna* as the first (propitious in the beginning/*ādau kalyāṇam*),
  *prānāyāma* and *dharaṇa* as the middle (propitious in the
  middle/*madhye-kalyāṇam*), and *anusmṛti* and *Samādhi* as the last
  (propitious in the end/*paryavasāne-kalyāṇam*). These three groups are
  also categorized as body, speech and mind respectively.

- See my previous articles if you want to read the first and second
  ancillaries of *Ṣaḍaṅga yoga* (*Pratyāhāra* and *Dhyāna*).

- I have translated the Tibetan term རྡོ་རྗེ་བཟླས་པ་ or རྡོར་བཟླས as vajra
  mantra breathing practice. Its Sanskrit term, according to the Ṣaḍaṅga
  Yoga text edited by Francesco Sferra, is *mantra-jāpa*.

- In Pandita Shakya Shri's method it mentions turning the face to the
  right and left while inhaling and exhaling. In *Paṇḍita* *Somanātha*'s
  instructions it says to turn your gaze to the left and right.

- Where the translation is asterisked, this means that I have
  interpreted it myself.

- When you see ‘Tārānāthā's note’ in the translation, it is his own
  commentary on the text.

#### Mahapandita Shakya Shri *prānāyāma* instruction: 'Six Vajra Instructions of Attainment of Immortality, the deathless state'

1.  First cleanse the *nāḍī* passages with breathing technique using the
    vajra mantra *Ōṃ (*ༀ), *Huṃ (*ཧཱུྃ) and *Aḥ (*ཨཱཿ) simultaneously with
    inhalation, retention and exhalation respectively.

2.  Next, after cleansing the *nāḍī*, the *prāṇa* (*vāyu*) is directed
    into the central channel using a gentle breathing technique of
    filling the air (breath in/down below your navel) and releasing the
    air (breath out).

3.  Then, once the *prāṇa* (*vāyu*) enters the central channel, the
    *prāṇa* is bound within the central channel using the *kumbhaka*
    rough breathing technique.

4.  If the *prāṇa* couldn't enter and bind in the central channel
    (through gentle breathing and *kumbhaka* rough breathing
    techniques), then apply Haṭhayoga (yoga of the forceful method)
    བཙན་ཐབས་སྦྱོར་བ་ by forcefully blocking the passage of the sun and moon
    (right and left channels). Once they are blocked, the lower orifices
    are forcibly stopped and lead to liberation through the upward path.

5.  The relishing of the inner nectar (amrita) through the method of
    stirring the small tongue (*lambikā*).

6.  The yoga/method of blazing the fire of tummo (*Cāṇḍālī*) mixes the
    *prāṇa* *vāyu* and *apāna* *vāyu* (into one taste).

First (the actual practice of *prānāyāma*), you should learn lu jong (body training) and (physical yoga practice) trulkhor (of *prānāyāma*) from others (text or teacher). (Here it is first explained) how to cleanse the nadi by means of the breathing technique using the vajra mantra (Ōṃ (ༀ), Huṃ (ཧཱུྃ) and Aḥ (ཨཱཿ) simultaneously with inhaling, retaining and exhaling). Only in exceptional circumstances, otherwise in *prānāyāma* and dharaṇa practices, always keep your eyes in the position called 'amrta whorl gaze' (Amṛta kuṇḍali dṛṣṭi), looking slightly above the level of the eyes, gazing sharply at the spot between the eyebrows.

The way to visualise three channels is (as follows). Visualise a blue central (avadhūtī) channel (the thickness of) a medium-sized cane-arrow, the upper tip of which is pierced to the crown of the head and the lower tip of which remains at the navel. To its right is the red Roma (lalanā) channel and to its left is the white Kyangma (rasanā) channel. Both (these left and right) channels are much thinner than the central (avadhūtī) channel.

Visualise the air flowing through (the Roma and Uma channels from the navel to) the throat, then to the crown, and (finally) out through the nostril. As you inhale, visualise the air entering through the nostrils, flowing through the left kyangma channel and the central uma channel between the crown, down to the throat and (finally) to the navel. As the air remains at the navel, visualise that the air (only) remains full in the central channel. (In the beginning) practise exhaling, inhaling and retaining the breath for the same length of time. Gradually increase the duration of the breath retention.

  practise *Kumbhaka*.

  highly praised for prolonging life).

  the end expel the air completely.

Vajra mantra breathing technique (རྡོར་བཟླས་/mantra-jāpa). 2. Drawing/attracting the air (དགུག་པ/Ākṛṣṭi). 3. Filling up (དགང་བ/Pūraka). 4. Restraining the breath (རེངས་པ/stambhana/stambhaka). 5. Exhaling slowly and gently (རྡུལ་མཐའ་ཅན/Recaka).*

*The final fifth recaka is a (supplementary) method for maximum benefit. So there are four main (breathing practices in this tradition).*

#### 1. Vajra mantra breathing practice (*mantra*-*jāpa*)

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> རྡོར་བཟླས་རོ་རྐྱང་ནས་འགྲོ་དམིགས་མི་མཛད། 
> །གཡས་གཡོན་མཉམ་རྒྱུ་གང་ཡིན་ཁྱད་མེད་པར། 
> །ཕྱིར་འགྲོ་དམར་པོ་ནང་འཇུག་དཀར་པོ་གནས། 
> །ནག་པོར་སྒོམ་པ་སྔོན་འགྲོ་གཉིས་མི་དགོས།
> ```

*(Paṇḍita Somanātha's vajra mantra breathing practice is similar to Pandita Shakyashir's instruction, but here in this method of) vajra mantra breathing practice you don't have to visualise whether the breath is flowing through the right, left or evenly through both (channels/nostrils). (So, through whichever channels the air is flowing) visualise the air going out as red, coming in as white, and staying in as black. (Unlike Pandita Shakyashri’s method) here you don’t need the two preliminary practices of vajra mantra breathing practice.*

*(Since this practice is similar to the Shakya shri method, it doesn't require a separate round of practice.)*

### (XVII) Seventeenth Round of Practice

#### 2. Drawing/attracting the air (དགུག་པ/Ākṛṣṭi)

> published
>
> ``` text
> །གཉིས་པ་དགུག་པ་སྣ་གཡས་རླུང་རྒྱུ་སྐབས། 
> །ལྟ་སྟངས་ཅུང་ཟད་གཡས་ཕྱོགས་སུ་བསྒྱུར་ཞིང་། 
> །རླུང་ནང་དལ་བུས་གསང་གནས་མནན་ལ་བསྡམས།

*When the air flows through the right nostril, turn your gaze slightly to the right, draw in the air slowly, press it down at the perineum and bind (the air there as much as you can). (When you can't hold it any longer) let the air out a little, pause and let the air out a little at a time, and finally let the air out completely. This is the seventeenth round of practice.*

### (XVIII) Eighteenth Round of Practice

#### 3. Filling up (དགང་བ/*Pūraka*)

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> །གསུམ་པ་དགང་བ་སྣ་གཡོན་རླུང་རྒྱུ་ཚེ། 
> །ལྟ་སྟངས་ཅུང་ཟད་གཡོན་བསྒྱུར་ནང་དུ་ནི། 
> །ཅུང་ཟད་མགྱོགས་རྔུབས་གསུས་པ་ལྡིར་གྱིས་དགང་། 
> །གཏོང་བ་སྔར་འདྲ་དམིགས་བསྐོར་བཅོ་བརྒྱད་པའོ།
> ```

*When the air flows through the left nostril, turn your gaze slightly to the left. Draw in (inhale) the air a little quickly, filling the belly and inflating it. Let the air out in the same way as above. This is the eighteenth round of the practice.*

### (XIX) Nineteenth Round of Practice

#### 4. Restraining the breath (རེངས་པ/*stambhana*/*stambhaka*)

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> བཞི་པ་རེངས་པ་རླུང་བསལ་སྣ་གཡོན་ནས། 
> །རྒྱུ་ན་ལྟ་སྟངས་གཡས་བསྒྱུར་འོག་སྒོ་བསྡམས། 
> །གཡས་ནས་དལ་ལ་རིང་རྔུབས་གསང་གནས་སུ། 
> །བཟུང་ལ་གཡས་ནས་བཏང་ལ་གཡས་རྒྱུ་ན། 
> །ལྟ་སྟངས་གཡོན་བསྒྱུར་གཡོན་ནས་དལ་བུས་རྡུབས། 
> །འོག་སྒོ་བསྡམས་ལ་ལྟོ་བ་ལྡིར་གྱིས་བྱ། 
> །གཡོན་ནས་བཏང་ལ་རེ་མོས་སྤེལ་གྱིན་སྒོམ། 
> །མཉམ་རྒྱུར་འདུག་ན་ལྟ་སྟངས་ཐད་ཀར་བྱ། 
> །མྱུར་རྔུབས་ལྟེ་བར་ཁ་སྦྱོར་དྲག་པོའི་ངང་། 
> །ལྟོ་བ་ཅུང་ཟད་ཚིགས་པ་ལ་གཅེར་ལ། 
> །ཕྱིར་རུ་དལ་བུས་གཏོང་བ་དེ་ལྟར་ན། 
> །བུམ་པ་ཅན་ཏེ་དམིགས་བསྐོར་བཅུ་དགུ་པའོ།
> ```

- (Note by *Tārānāthā*: According to this tradition, *kumbhaka* is
  achieved through this method. It is basically *kumbhaka* of gentle
  breathing practice)

- (First) clear out the (harmful) residual air.

- When the air passes through the left nostril, turn your gaze to the
  right. Close your anus, take a long, slow breath in and hold the air
  at your perineum. At the end, let the air out through the right
  (nostril).

- When the air flows through the right nostril, turn your gaze to the
  left. As you slowly inhale through the left (nostril) and close the
  anus, make the belly inflate. At the end, let the air out through the
  left (nostril).

- In this way, practise alternately (with each nostril).

- When the air flows evenly through both nostrils, look straight ahead
  and inhale quickly. Forcefully unite the (upper and lower) air,
  squeeze the abdomen against the spine and let the air out slowly.

- This is (this tradition’s) *kumbhaka* (breathing method).

- And this is the nineteenth round of practice.

***Tārānāthā*****’s note**: *This is also the tradition of Drupchen Manlung pa (Manlung Guru: see the article on the Ṣaḍaṅga Yoga tradition in Tibet). Je Chogley Namgyal and Buston had also repeated this practice and it is also similar to their practice.*

This is holding the Kumbhaka breath, the twentieth round of practice.*

These (two) comprise the complete pranayama instruction of this tradition.*

Exhale slowly, so that the movement of the air is unnoticeable, with the mental sound ཨཱ༔ (ah). *The instruction common to all (these traditions) is: When the air goes out (during the exhalation), concentrate your mind intensely on the vision of the sign (the image of emptiness). As the air comes in (during the inhalation), merge your mind (with the image of emptiness). When the air remains in, the experience (as the experiencer\*) and the grasping (as the doer\*) arising from the fusion of the mind and the image of emptiness are destroyed. This is such an extremely profound practice, passed on from ear to ear and mouth to mouth, which is the essential message of all genuine Tantra.*

*This is the twenty-second round of practice. Here the stages of prāṇāyāma instruction are completed.*
