## Notes

*Cittadhāraṇa* \[སེམས་འཛིན་\] is a series of meditation exercises. This article contains the translation of two separate sections from the same text composed by Taranatha. The first part of this post is from the miscellenous section and the second is from Mahamudra section. In the first part, there are sixteen series of mental concentration \[*Cittadhāraṇa*\] techniques to help stabilise the attention and concentration of the mind. In this part I have omitted two instructions, number four and five, as I thought they might not be relevant to readers.

The series of mental concentration instructions in the second part \[Mahamudra\] is the method for resting the mind without mental support \[that is, the objectless mind\]. The Tibetan term for 'mind without mental support' or 'objectless mind' is དམིགས་མེད་ \[*dmigs* *med*\], its Sanskrit term is *Anārambaṇa*/*anālambaṇa*, which is a very important concept in Mahayana Buddhism. In Buddhism, the object of mind or consciousness is called ‘support’ because the mind or consciousness arises and is sustained with the support of their corresponding objects with which the mind engages. And the mind without its object is called ‘objectless’. As the Aṣṭasāhasrikā Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra says,

> Mind arises (*cittam* *utpadyate*) with a support/object
> (*sārambaṇam*) not without support/object (*na* *anārambaṇam*) because
> when one sees, hears and cognizes, one’s mind seizes or follows the
> dharma of consciousness (*dṛṣṭa*-*śruta*= *mata*-*vijñāte* dharmeṣu
> *buddhiḥ pravartate*)

The first part of the translation of mental concentration is related to the generation stage, and there is a lot of visualisation involved in both of these meditation practices. Here I would like to point out why visualisation and visualising oneself in the form of a deity is important in Vajrayāna.

In Tibetan Buddhism, it is primarily the aspect of method \[*upāya*\], rather than the aspect of wisdom \[*prajña*\], that determines the hierarchy of the three *Yānas* - Hīnayāna, Sūtrayāna and Vajrayāna - in ascending order. Mahāyānists seek Buddhahood through the union \[yoga\] of wisdom and method, that is, through the accumulation of wisdom \[*jñānasambhāraḥ*\] and merit \[*puṇyasambhāraḥ*\] respectively. According to Mahāyāna, Hīnayāna lacks the method aspect, the aspiration and the will to attain Buddhahood to help sentient beings because they are unable to attain the *Rūpakāya* to help sentient beings as a Buddha. There are differences in the final attainment of Buddhahood between Mahāyāna and Hīnayāna. As far as non-Tantric \[Sūtrayāna\] and Tantric \[Vajrayāna\] Mahāyāna are concerned, there is no difference between their final attainment. However, there are differences in their method aspect. Non-Tantric Mahāyānists, in order to achieve *Rūpakāya* to help other sentient beings, have to accumulate merit for countless lifetimes. But Vajrayana teaches a method of attaining *Rūpakāya* in this lifetime instead of accumulating merit for thousands of lifetimes. So by visualising oneself in the form of a deity and developing the pride or confidence of being the deity now, the practitioner is already sowing the seed of *Rūpakāya*. This is one of the reasons why it is important to practice deity yoga and guru yoga by visualising oneself and taking pride in being the deity.

# Translation-I

### 1. First round of meditation

First, for a few days, abandon all activity, sit in a comfortable posture without meditating on anything. Then, early in the morning, when your mind is clear, visualise your body turning into a mountain. Then gradually focus your mind on that mountain's valley, its trees, its river flowing down, and so on. If your mind wanders, stop the thought and meditate again.

### 2. Second round of meditation

Next, visualise a very beautiful meadow in your navel area with a leafy tree on one side of the meadow. Focus your mind on the tree and its delicate branches. When your mind wanders, stop it and meditate again.

### 3. Third round of meditation

Next, visualise a very clear lake in the centre of the field in your heart \[chest\] area. Focus your mind on the ripples in the clear water of the lake. When your mind wanders, stop it and meditate again.

### 6. Sixth round of meditation

This practice is called sending away your mental replica. With the knowledge that you are here, imagine your replica of yourself walking away from you. If it has gone quite a distance, imagine it looking back at this person and then this person looking at that person. When it has crossed the mountain and they are very far away from each other, imagine that they are looking from here to there and from there to here.

### 7. Seventh round of meditation

Next, visualise a small lake below you. Visualise the lake gradually getting bigger and bigger and higher and higher and finally reaching above you and out in all directions. Visualise yourself sitting in the middle of the water surrounding you.

### 8. Eighth round of meditation

Place a butter lamp in front of your or any other side as an object of meditation. From time to time look at that butter lamp, other time visualise an unwavering butter lamp in your mind and focus your mind on it.

### 9. Ninth round of meditation

The ninth is to focus your mind on the nine unswerving butter lamps in your perineum.

### 10. Tenth round of meditation

Focus your mind on the object to which your mind has wandered. If your mind wanders from that object again, bring your focus back to the previous object.

### 11. Eleventh round of meditation

Focus your mind on a pleasant object. If a thought arises, bring your focus back to the pleasant object.

### 12. Twelfth round of meditation

Next, \[in the sitting posture\] place your legs side by side and hold your feet with your hands from the arch of the soles and focus your mind on the light emanating from the soles. OR Place a wooden stick of a suitable size under the arch of your feet and hold your toes with your hands. Focus your mind on the sensation of touch between the soles of your feet and the stick.

### 13. Thirteenth round of meditation

On a piece of paper or cloth with a green background, representing a green meadow, draw a tree or mountain in the middle. At the bottom of the tree or mountain, draw a picture of a human being. Place this drawing in front of you. Then visualise yourself walking around the tree or mountain in this green meadow.

### 14. Fourteenth round of meditation

Put a piece of wood on the crown of your head that you can feel. Focus your mind on the radiating Mani.

### 15. Fifteenth round of meditation

If it is uncomfortable to place a piece of wood on the crown of your head, place the palms of your hands together on the crown of your head and meditate.

### 16. Sixteenth round of meditation

Next, place your hands on your thighs. Lean your chest, shoulders and neck back, face up and look into the void space. Then meditate a spherical light, whatever colour suits you, in the centre of your body. Then meditate this light going upwards, leaving the crown of your head and finally disappearing far into the sky.

# Translation-II

I.

Place a black piece of wood in front of you as the object of your mind. Focus your gaze and mind on this object and remain in this state for a while. When subliminal mental stirrings and thoughts arise, stop the flow of thoughts and bring your mind back to this object. Remain in this state for a while. Then, without the mental support \[of the black wooden object\], let your mind rest without any thoughts or thinking.

II\.

Draw a swastika with red ochre on a small flat stone and place it in front of you. Focus your gaze and concentrate your mind on it. Keep your eyes and mind fixed on the drawing of the swastika, which is stationary. Next, having relaxed your concentration and also your gaze, imagine the swastika moving in a circle.

III\.

Visualise yourself floating in the centre of the empty sky/space. Visualise your body falling from the edge of the sky and eventually disappearing from your view. Let your mind remain in this objectless state.

IV\.

Visualise a black human head to your right and left. Visualise them running towards each other from your right and left and fighting in front of you. Meditate this several times with concentration and finally let your mind remain in an objectless state.

V.

Focus your mind on the eastern sky. Focus your mind to the east of that eastern sky. Again, focus your mind on the east of that eastern sky. In this way, focus your mind on the numberless eastern sky. In the same way, focus your mind on the limitless skies of the ten directions. Then let your mind remain in an objectless state.

VI\.

Send your mind to a place where you have been before. Visualise yourself walking \[from where you are sitting\] along the familiar path to that place. And as you gradually reach that place, visualise yourself walking back along the same path and finally coming back to where you are sitting. Meditate in this way again and again, eventually letting your mind to remain in a relaxed, objectless state.
