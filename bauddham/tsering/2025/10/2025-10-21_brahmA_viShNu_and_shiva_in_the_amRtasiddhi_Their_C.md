### **Notes on Translation**

- In Tāranātha’s instructions on the three mudrās — *Mahāmudrā*
  (Aṃ-grol), *Mahābandha*, and *Mahāvedha* — the sequence of practice is
  presented correctly as *Mahāmudrā* first, followed by *Mahābandha*,
  and then *Mahāvedha*. However, it appears highly probable that a
  scribal or editorial error occurred in the text, wherein the names
  *vedha* and *bandha* were inadvertently interchanged. Accordingly, in
  this translation I have taken the liberty to interchange the order as
  *Mahāmudrā* (Aṃ-grol), *Bandha*, and *Vedha*, rather than *Mahāmudrā*
  (Aṃ-grol), *Vedha*, and *Bandha*.

- Another liberty I have taken concerns the terminology. The
  *Haṭhayogic* *Mahāmudrā* technique was given new names by the Shangpa
  masters when they adopted the *Amṛtasiddhi* teachings from the Indian
  yogis came down through Virupa—here referred to as *Aṃ-grol* in
  Shangpa sources. To make the reading easier for readers, I have used
  the term *Mahāmudrā* in place of *Aṃ-grol*, while retaining the latter
  in brackets.

- Another innovation introduced by the Shangpa masters is the
  association of the three principal *mudrās* of the *Amṛtasiddhi*:
  Mahamudra, Mahabandha and Mahavedha with the four well-known *mudrās*
  of Vajrayāna: *Karma-mudrā*, *Dharma-mudrā*, *Samaya-mudrā*, and
  *Mahāmudrā*. Thus, within the Shangpa tradition, four principal
  *trulkhor* (or *mudrā*) practices emerged instead of the original
  three. The *Haṭhayogic Mahāmudrā* technique was given a new
  designation, *Aṃ-grol*, while the added *Mahāmudrā* corresponds more
  to a meditative posture. Accordingly, *Mahāmudrā (Aṃ-grol)* represents
  the *Karma-mudrā*, *Bandha* corresponds to the *Dharma-mudrā*, and
  *Vedha* to the *Samaya-mudrā*. Though we have to keep in mind that
  karma, dharma and samaya mudras are mentioned in the Tibetan
  Amrtasiddhi root text.

## Introduction

Both the Sanskrit and Tibetan *Amṛtasiddhi* root texts mention three *granthis* (knots), each of which must be pierced or destroyed, though their precise locations are not explicitly identified. These three knots are named Śiva, Viṣṇu, and Brahmā. In Tāranātha’s text, the loci of these deities are referred to as “cities” (*pura* in Sanskrit). In the Tibetan *Amṛtasiddhi* root text, the corresponding names of these three deities appear in their common Tibetan equivalents: *dbang phyug* (Śiva), *khyab ’jug* (Viṣṇu), and *tshangs pa* (Brahmā). However, Tāranātha seems to employ slightly different terms—*Raudra* (“Wrathful,” Tib. *drag po*) for Śiva, *Hari* (“Ravisher,” Tib. *phrog byed*) for Viṣṇu, and ཆིང་པའི་ (*ching pa’i)* for Brahmā, though the latter may be a scribal error for ཚངས་པའི་ (*tshangs pa’i)*.

Tāranātha (16th–17th century) composed few *Amṛtasiddhi* instruction manuals—transmitted through the Shangpa lineage, which traces its origin to Virūpa of Oḍḍiyāna—that provide further clarification on these points. Among these, the two texts referred to here include one written in verse and another presented in prose.

In these works, Tāranātha explains that *Mahāmudrā* (Aṃ-grol) destroys the city of Raudra-Śiva, *Mahābandha* destroys the city of Hari-Viṣṇu, and *Mahāvedha* destroys the city of Brahmā. When examining his instructions on these three *mudrās*, we find that each is associated with a specific seed syllable and a corresponding locus within the body.

- **Mahāmudrā** is associated with the syllable **Aṃ** at the
  *nābhi-cakra* (navel center)—which may explain why the Tibetan
  tradition renamed *Mahāmudrā* as *Aṃ-grol*. In this practice, the
  *vāyu* and fire are thrust downward toward the anus while focusing the
  mind on the navel and perineum. It is said to destroy the city of
  Śiva, indicating that the abode of Śiva corresponds to the navel and
  perineum region.

- **Mahābandha** is associated with the syllable **Hūṃ** at the *hṛdaya*
  (heart center). In this practice, after drawing the united *vāyu*
  upward and then thrusting it downward, the *vāyu* and fire contend and
  dissolve at the heart. It is said to destroy the city of Viṣṇu,
  indicating that the abode of Viṣṇu corresponds to the heart.

- **Mahāvedha**, though not assigned a particular syllable, is connected
  with the *kaṇṭha* (throat) and *uṣṇīṣa* (crown) centers. In this
  practice, rays of light from the soles of the feet and the two lower
  gates spread throughout the body, circling around the throat and
  crown, and finally emerging through the pores of the body and the
  crown of the head. It is said to destroy the city of Brahmā,
  indicating that the abode of Brahmā corresponds to the throat and
  crown centers.

In order to understand the following verse from Tāranātha’s text—how Mahāmudrā destroys the city of Raudra-Śiva, Mahābandha destroys the city of Hari-Viṣṇu, and Mahāvedha destroys the city of Brahmā—I have presented here the Tibetan text and my English translation of each of these three *mudrās*, drawn from two different *Amṛtasiddhi* manuals by Tāranātha. The **“Method”** sections belong to one text, while the **“Inner Principle”** sections are taken from another.

As noted earlier, I have taken the liberty of employing the Haṭhayogic term *Mahāmudrā* for the Tibetan *Aṃ-grol*, as well as interchanging the names *bandha* and *vedha*, since the original Tibetan sequence appears to have been reversed.

#### Excerpt from Tāranātha’s *Amṛtasiddhi* Manual

> In the yoga of the three *mudrās*,  
> Rāhu is the indispensable ally.  
> Mahāmudrā destroys the city of Raudra (Śiva),  
> Bandha destroys the city of Hari (Viṣṇu),  
> Vedha destroys the city of Brahmā.  
> The three *mudrās* lay siege to the city,  
> Yet it is Rāhu who utterly destroys them.
>
> If one practises these *mudrās* for three years,  
> One will attain victory over the *vāyus*.  
> All the *nāḍīs* will become pliant,  
> The *bindus* purified and mastered,  
> And the boundless higher cognition of the *Vidyādhara* will be
> revealed.

# Text and Translation

### I. Mahāmudrā — Karma-mudrā (Am-grol)

#### Method

> རྟིང་པ་ཕྱོགས་གཅིག་གིས་འོག་སྒོ་གཉིས་ཀྱི་བར་བསྡམ། གཅིག་གི་སྟེང་ནས་མནན་ཏེ་ཅུང་ཟད་སྒུར་བ་ཡིན།
> ལག་གཉིས་གཅིག་གིས་རྐང་རྩེ་དང་གཅིག་གིས་བོལ་གོང་ནས་བཟུང་བ་ཡིན།
> རླུང་བཤང་སྒོ་བཙུམ་སྟེ་འོག་རླུང་ལྟེ་བར་དྲངས།
> དེ་ལ་སྟེང་རླུང་ཁ་སྦྱར་ཏེ་གཡས་གཡོན་དབུས་གསུམ་གྱིས་གཏུན་རྡུང་དྲག་ཏུ་བྱེད་པ་ཡིན།

With one heel, bind the space between the anus and the genitals, pressing the heel down from above \[with the weight of the body\] while remaining slightly bent forward. \[With the other leg extended\], grasp the tip of the foot with one hand and the upper part of the foot with the other. Contract the anus to draw the lower *vāyu* upward to the navel, and there unite the lower and upper *vāyus*. Then perform the inner yogic technique of pounding like a pestle, \[thrusting the air downward\] forcefully from the left, right, and middle.

### II. Mahābandha — Dharma-mudrā

#### Method

> ཨཾ་གྲོལ་བདུན་ཙམ་སོང་བའི་རྗེས་རླུང་ཁ་སྦྱོར་བུམ་ཅན་གྱི་ངང་ནས།
> ཡར་སྟོད་ལ་འཐེན་འཐེན་པའི་རྗེས་སུ་ཤུགས་དྲགས་པ་རྒྱུ་ཞབས་སུ་དྲག་ཏུ་བརྡབས་ཏེ་གང་རུང་དུ་མནན།
> འདི་རླུང་འཆིང་བའི་ཐབས་ཡིན།

After performing the Mahāmudrā (Am-grol) about seven times, while in *kumbhaka*, wherein the upper and lower *vāyus* are united, draw this unified *vāyu* upward and, with great force, hurl it downward into the lower abdomen, pressing it firmly down. This is the method of locking or capturing the *vāyu*.

This is the secret instruction of Dharma-mudrā.

### III. Mahāvedha — Samaya-mudrā

#### Method

> རླུང་བུམ་ཅན་གྱི་ངང་ནས། རླུང་ཐེངས་རེའི་ཕྱེད་རྗེས་མའི་ཆ་ལ་སླེབ་པ་ན།
> རྐེད་པ་ཉག་བྱ་སྟེ་དབུས་སུ་སྒྲིལ་ནས། དེ་མཚམས་སྟེང་དུ་དྲག་ཏུ་མནན།
> འཕོངས་སྟེགས་ནས་འབེབས་ཆུང་གཅིག་བྱ་རྗེས་སྣ་ནས་སང་སྟེ་བཏང༌། འདིས་རླུང་བསད་པ་ཡིན།

While in kumbhaka, as the breath reaches its latter phase, compress the waist and roll it inward toward the center. At that moment, press the air forcefully upward. Then perform a small yogic drop by lifting the buttocks \[dropping it down\], and release the air gently through the nose. This kills the vāyu.
