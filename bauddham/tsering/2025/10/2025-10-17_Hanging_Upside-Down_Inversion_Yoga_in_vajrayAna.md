### Note to the readers

This *“Upside-down Stick Yoga”* practice is a form of Inversion Yoga, in which the practitioner remains inverted, thereby reversing the perineum and crown chakras. Refer to the *Body Posture* and *Mental Observation* sections of the main phase of this text. This may represent one of the forms of *Viparīta* mentioned in Brahmanical Haṭha Yoga texts. The text itself states: **དེ་ཉིད་ལོག་པའི་སྦྱོར་བ་ཡིས།** — “By means of the yoga of reversing (*viparīta*) these two (the crown and the perineum chakras).”

The *Upside-down Stick Yoga* consists of fifteen sequences of physical yoga — seven in the preliminary phase, one (the body posture) in the main phase, and seven in the postliminary phase. The title of the text derives from the body posture taught in the main phase of this practice.

There exists a set of physical yoga manuals catalogued in the Tibetan *Tengyur* as separate texts, such as the *Yoga of the Cart-horse Wheel*, *Kumbhaka*, *Pūraka*, *Caṇḍālī*, and the *Head-downward Stick*. However, in examining the final text — the *Trulkhor* of *Caṇḍālī* — the colophon shared among these works appears there. Although all of these texts were composed by the same author (Amoghavajra), according to the colophon they are attributed to *Kṛṣṇācāryavarta* rather than to *Virūpa*. This raises a question: who is this figure called “Black” (*nag po* / *Kṛṣṇa*)? Could he perhaps be Virūpa’s disciple known as *Kāṇha* (or *Kṛṣṇa*)? Tibetan scholars, moreover, often attribute the *Amṛtasiddhi* teachings to the “Black Virūpa” (*Kṛṣṇa Virūpa*).

#### Colophon:

> སྤྱོད་པ་ཆེན་པོ་རིག་པའི་གནས་ལྔ་ལ་མཁས་པ།་གཉིས་སུ་མེད་པའི་དོན་རྟོགས་པ།་ནག་པོ་སྤྱོད་པ་བས་མཛད་པ་རྫོགས་སོ།
> བོད་ཡུལ་དུ་རྣལ་འབྱོར་གྱི་དབང་ཕྱུག་ཨ་མོ་ཀ་བཛྲས་འགྱུར་དུ་གསུང་ཞིང་ཡི་གེར་བཀོད་པའོ༎

Thus concludes the composition by Kṛṣṇācāryavarta, who has mastered the five fields of knowledge and realized the meaning of non duality, which in the land of Tibet was taught and committed to writing in translation by the Yogeśvara Amoghavajra.

As I have been studying Amoghavajra’s works for a long time, it appears that the term *trulkhor* is a later addition. It occurs only in the titles and colophons of the texts, suggesting that it may have been inserted by later editors or compilers. Throughout his writings, Amoghavajra consistently uses the term *lus gnad* — the Tibetan equivalent of *āsana*, meaning “body posture.” For a more detailed discussion of the usage of *’trulkhor* and *lus-gnad* in Amoghavajra’s *Amṛtasiddhi* texts, see my article, “**[Yoga Posture and Forceful Yoga: Amoghavajra’s Amṛtasiddhi Text](https://journalofyogastudies.org/index.php/JoYS/article/view/106)**,” published in the *Journal of Yoga Studies*.

I first posted a translation of this text some time ago. On revisiting it, I have revised the translation to make it easier to read and have incorporated few corrections. Upon reviewing this passage, I realized that my earlier translation of the seventh sequence of the postliminary physical yoga practice might be inaccurate. Previously, I rendered it as “fixing the wooden stick in the ground.” However, the term *gdang* *shing* (Skt. *dantaka*; sometimes glossed *nāgadantaka*) clearly refers to a wooden stick or peg projecting from a wall, not one planted in the earth. The correct translation should read:

> **“Fix a stick of the proper size firmly in the air, like a wooden peg
> (*****dantaka / nāgadantaka*** **projecting from a wall\]. Rest the
> abdomen upon the stick, and let both arms and legs hover in the air at
> a distance of one handspan.”**

The *Amṛtasiddhi* texts (written by Amoghavajra) are based on a system of six chakras — crown, forehead, throat, heart, navel, and perineum — together with a five-channel configuration. In this text as well, five channels are mentioned instead of the more common three-channel system. Regarding the five-channel system see the **Notes on translation**. The following illustration of the chakra system, along with the respective names and seed syllables, is based on Amoghavajra’s *Amṛtasiddhi* commentary. In the *Mental Observation* section, the seed syllable of the crown chakra is written as *Hrīṃ*, though it most likely could be *Haṃ*.

### Some notes on translation:

- All words in brackets are my additions.

- The phrase *“bend the throat”* in my translation refers to drawing the
  chin inward toward the chest, a mudra commonly known as
  *Jālandhara-bandha* in Haṭha Yoga terminology.

- The “lotus cross-legged posture” mentioned in this text does not
  correspond to the full lotus posture of Haṭha Yoga, whereas the
  *vajra* cross-legged posture corresponds to the full lotus position of
  classical Haṭha Yoga.

- **In the First sequence:** The line in the text, *“gsang gnas gyen la
  drang”* (“draw the secret area upward”), is difficult to interpret. I
  see two possible understandings. The first follows Amoghavajra’s
  commentary on the *Amṛtasiddhi*, which mentions drawing the perineum
  chakra—together with its vital air and bindu—upward toward the navel.
  The second is found in the Shangpa Kagyu *Amṛtasiddhi*, where a
  similar practice is described as *gshang lam ’khor lo gyen la zlog*,
  “reversing the anal chakra upward” which is done by bending your upper
  body down and pointing your buttocks to the sky in standing position.
  In the present text, therefore, I have understood “drawing the secret
  area upward” as referring to the upward drawing of the chakra of the
  secret place or perineum, since this practice particularly concerns
  inversion yoga.

- **In the main sequence of body posture**: I presume that the
  Sanskrit-derived term *རྡའུ་རི (rda’u ri)* used in this main sequence
  refers to *dōrī*, meaning “rope.” The context supports this
  interpretation, as the passage describes a suspension posture
  involving something tying around the ankles and fastening above.
  Hence, I have understood *rda’u ri* here as indicating a rope used for
  the inversion practice.

- **In the Mental Observation section of the main phase**: I am not
  entirely certain about the precise identification of these five
  channels. The *Amṛtasiddhi Yoga* of Amoghavajra is based on a
  five-channel system (not the widely known three channels). From his
  descriptions, it appears that these five channels consist of two upper
  right and left channels, two lower right and left channels, and one
  central channel.

- In the Vajrayāna tradition, *Akṣobhya* is also referred to as the
  *bodhicitta bindu*.

- **In the breathing technique section of the main phase**: The term
  *srog rtsol* is generally understood as *prāṇāyāma*. However, in some
  Tibetan Vajrayāna traditions, *srog* and *rtsol* carry distinct
  connotations, referring respectively to inhalation and exhalation, or,
  in certain contexts, to the movement of air through the right and left
  channels. In the present translation, I have adopted this latter
  meaning.

Visualize your body in the form of your personal deity. Visualize Akṣobhya (the bodhicitta bindu), at the crown of the head. Then meditate on the rainbow and illusory body. Visualize the upper ends of the five channels as remaining pierced to the bindu *Hrīḥ* (*Haṃ*) at the crown. The lower ends of these five channels remain pierced at the perineum (nāḍī-cakra) to the seed bindus of the five families of goddesses, located on the four petals and at the navel center of that chakra. Through the method of inverting these two—the crown and perineum chakras—visualize the whole body filling with light, and focus the mind firmly upon it.

First, cough three times \[to clear the windpipe\]. Then blow out the \[residual harmful\] air from the right and left channels three times. Having practised the sixteen breathings through the nose with sound, forcefully press down the upper air and gently draw up the lower air. By the method of drawing in the life-bearing air (prāṇa), remain firmly rooted in samādhi. Thus is the breathing technique.

### **III. Postliminary phase (Seven sequences)**

#### First sequence

> ``` text
> ཞབས་ནི་པདྨའི་སྐྱིལ་ཀྲུང་བཅའ།
> སྒལ་ཚིགས་བསྲང་ལ་མགྲིན་པ་དགུག།

> རྒྱབ་ཏུ་བཀུག་པའི་སྦྱོར་བ་ཡིས།
> ཡར་གྱི་ཚིགས་པ་དང་པོ་ལ།

> ```

Place both feet in the lotus cross-legged posture. Straighten the spine and bend the throat. Gather strength in both arms and clench both hands into fists. Bending the arms behind the back, strike the first upper joint of the back fourteen times, alternating right and left fists, and completely expel the belch.
