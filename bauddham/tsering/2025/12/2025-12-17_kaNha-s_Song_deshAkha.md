### Deśākha

*Ḍombī, outcaste lady,*  
*Your house stands*  
*Outside the town.*  
*I, the Brahmin boy,*  
*Walk on, touching you.*

*Hey Ḍombī, outcaste lady!*  
*With you I will dwell.*  
*I, Kanhā, the Kāpāla yogi,*  
*Have overcome revulsion.*

*In oneness, at the navel,*  
*With sixty-four nāḍī-petals,*  
*She abides,*  
*And dances.*

*To you, Ḍombī,*  
*I ask the truth:*  
*On what boat*  
*Does it come and go?*

*Ḍombī, Garland-seller,*  
*For you I will place*  
*A bed of flowers,*  
*And a seat of reed.*

*Hey, you, the Ḍombī,*  
*I, the Kāpālī,*  
*For you have donned*  
*The garland of skulls.*

*Having destroyed the pond,*  
*The Ḍombī ate the lotus root.*  
*Having taken the Ḍombī,*  
*I have slain the enemy.*

------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### Explanation of the doha based on Tāranātha’s commentary

Outcaste Ḍombīs are not permitted to live within the town and therefore dwell beyond its boundaries. They earn their living by selling fruit, flowers, meat, wine and the like, and also by singing and dancing. Brahmins and other higher castes would not only avoid physical contact with a Ḍombī, but would not even allow their shadow to touch them. Kanhā himself comes from a Brahmin background, while his consort, the yoginī from Sri Lanka, is an outcaste Ḍombī. To the world, this appears an unsuitable match, yet true cleanliness is not of caste or body, but the cleanliness of the heart/mind.

The doha has an inner, deeper meaning. The town refers to one’s own body, and “outside the town” refers to the five sense objects. The Ḍombī, the outcaste lady, refers to the avadhūtī, the central channel, and the Brahmin boy Kanhā is the mind. When mind-air dissolves into the Ḍombī, the central channel, the five sense objects are transformed into bliss-emptiness and become its sphere of activity. Thus it is said, “Ḍombī, these outer spheres are your home.”

When the fire of *tummo* blazes, the mind—“I, Kanhā, the Brahmin boy,” primordially pure—becomes of one taste (*eka-rasa*) with the bodhicitta *bindu*. It flows in touch with the Ḍombī, the central channel, and the six nāḍī-cakras, bringing forth the four joys (*caturānanda*), and so forth. Thus he calls out to the Ḍombī, the outcaste lady referring to the central channel, saying that Kanhā, the Kāpāla yogi of great blis that is, one’s own mind, always remains together with her, having conquered the filth or revulsion of conceptual mind.

The method of cohabiting with one’s own inner Ḍombī is as follows. There are sixty-four nāḍī-petals at the navel nāḍī-cakra. At the centre of each of these sixty-four nāḍī-petals there are sixty-four *tummo* (*caṇḍālī*). All these sixty-four *caṇḍālīs* become one with the principal *sahaja caṇḍālī* at the centre of the navel nāḍī-cakra. This single principal *caṇḍālī* then radiates light, filling the entire nāḍī-cakra, and appears again in the form of sixty-four *caṇḍālīs*. This display of emanation and re-gathering of *caṇḍālī* is here called “dancing”. At the very moment when the mind-wind dissolves within the central channel, when *tummo* blazes or *bindu*/*amṛta* drips down, there arises the wisdom of direct cognition of selflessness (*nairātmya*). Thus the Ḍombī, the central channel, is called the goddess of selflessness (*nairātmikā*).

Rigpa, the king of mind—here identified with Kanhā—asks the Ḍombī: *“In order to cross the samsaric river of the conceptual mind, what acts as the boat?”* (The answer not stated is the restraint of the bliss of the melted bodhicitta *bindu*). The garland of flowers of the bodhicitta *bindu* is spread (the *doha* uses the word “sold”) throughout the channels. In order to kindle the Ḍombī, the navel *tummo*, it is said that he has laid down the seat of reed, which refers to the karmic vayu below the navel.

He then calls out, “Hey, Ḍombī!”, actually referring to the central channel. “I, Kanhā,” referring to the mind, “am the Kāpālī,” meaning the one who sustains and guards bliss, the *bindu*. Then it continues: for the sake of the Ḍombī—that is, the central channel—“I, Rigpa, the mind, have donned the garland of the six charnel-ground ornaments,” referring to the restraint of the indestructible *bindu* at the centres of the six nāḍī-cakras. This signifies the dissolution of mind and *vāyu* into the *bindu*.

The last verse, “destroying the pond,” refers to the pond of body, speech, and mind. The Ḍombī, the central channel, “consuming the lotus root” means that the central channel assimilates the seventy-two thousand channels of the body. Adopting the Ḍombī, *tummo*, primal wisdom as a helpful companion, one slays the foe, namely the karmic *vāyu*.
