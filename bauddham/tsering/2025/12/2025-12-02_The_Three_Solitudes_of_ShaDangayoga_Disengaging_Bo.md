### Introduction

My previous post discussed the *general preliminary practice of Ṣaḍaṅgayoga* that is shared with other practices. Today’s translation concerns the preliminary practice that is unique to Ṣaḍaṅga Yoga. As this practice appears immediately before *pratyāhāra*, the first limb of Ṣaḍaṅgayoga, one may also describe it as the *preliminary practice to pratyāhāra*.

The Tibetan name of the practice is *dben pa gsum*, literally translated as “three solitudes” or “three seclusions”, corresponding in Sanskrit to *viveka* (seclusion) or perhaps *vyapakarṣaḥ* (“drawing back”). However, in this context the Tibetan term *dben pa* carries the sense of disengagement or freedom from bodily, verbal, and mental activity. This *threefold disengagement practice* is not exclusive to Ṣaḍaṅgayoga; it is also found in other traditions, including Dzogchen.

This practice begins with the brief, general preliminaries such as taking refuge, generating bodhicitta, guru yoga, and the generation stage. For details, see my previous post. I have not yet written about the generation-stage (utpattikrama) practice of Ṣaḍaṅgayoga. What follows is the short generation-stage practice of Ṣaḍaṅgayoga, taken from the same text composed by Tāranātha:

> Practise the refuge and bodhicitta as before.  
> Recite the śūnyatā mantra: *Oṁ
> śūnyatā-jñāna-vajra-svabhāva-ātmako’ham.*
>
> From within emptiness, in an instant, one’s own being transforms into
> the *sahaja Kālacakra*.  
> One stands upon a seat formed of the stacked discs of four elements,
> Mount Meru, a lotus, the moon, the sun, and Rāhu.
>
> There, one arises in the very form of *Kālacakra* himself:  
> of blue complexion, one face, two arms, and three eyes;  
> holding a vajra and bell, and embracing the consort.
>
> The left leg, white and bent at the knee, treads upon the chest of
> white *Maheśvara*,  
> and the right leg, red and extended, treads upon the chest of red
> *Kāma*-*deva*.
>
> The head is crowned with matted hair (*jaṭāmukuṭa*), adorned with
> precious gems and a crescent moon.  
> The body is arrayed in vajra ornaments, with a lower garment of tiger
> skin.
>
> The finger joints are three-coloured, and the fingers themselves are
> in five colours.  
> The crown is marked by Vajrasattva (*vajrasattva*-*mukuṭa*).
>
> One stands at the centre of the five-coloured mountains of blazing
> fire,  
> bearing an expression in which wrath and desire are intermingled.
>
> Embracing him is the Mother Viśvamātā, her body yellow in colour, with
> one face, two arms, and three eyes. Her right hand holds a curved
> knife, and her left hand holds a skull-cup (*kapāla*). She embraces
> the Father. Her left leg is extended and her right leg bent, abiding
> in union with the Father. She is naked, adorned with the five bone
> symbolic *mudrās* (ornaments), and her hair partially cascades freely.
>
> Visualise *Oṁ* at the forehead; *Āḥ* at the throat; *Hūṁ* at the
> heart; *Svā* at the perineum; and *Hā* at the crown. From your heart
> (region), rays of light radiate outward. The outer inanimate world
> transforms into a celestial palace (*vimāna*), and all the beings
> inhabiting it transform into Kālacakra’s host of deities. All of this
> then melts into light and dissolves into you. Remain in meditative
> equipoise on the image of the deities — luminous yet empty — for as
> long as possible. It is beneficial to meditate in this way again and
> again.

**Note**: This instruction is drawn from a long and detailed text on Ṣaḍaṅgayoga composed by the Jonang master Tārānātha

## Three Solitudes or Three Disengagement Practice

#### Location and Place

The place should be one where people do not pass by in the daytime, and where there are no disturbing noises in the night-time. It should be a location that poses no threat to life or safety, and the drinking water is not harmful to the physical health.

If one has substantial means, the dwelling should contain a dark chamber on either the top floor or the middle floor, with three layers of entrance covering. The light opening (a round window or skylight) should be narrow on the outside and wider on the inside. When left open, it should admit enough light to brighten the sight. When covered or blocked with a lumps earth, not even the slightest glimmer, not so much as a strand the width of a horse’s tail hair should enter.

For those who have renounced everything, or who live without resources, the dwelling may be whatever is manageable: an underground hollow (*bhūmiguhā*), a rock cave, or any form of shelter or house that can be obtained. However, it must be dry, without damp or ground vapour, and extremely dark within. If these conditions are present, the place may be anywhere; it will suffice.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Three Seclusions or Three Disengagement (*dben pa gsum*)

#### 1. Body Seclusion/Disengagement (*lus dben pa*)

In that dark room, lay down the meditation cushion and assume the following key bodily postures. Place the legs in the vajra cross-legged posture (full lotus). Straighten the spine and the back of the neck (making sure not bending forwards). Bend the throat so that the chin rests lightly against the larynx. Leave the lips and teeth in their natural positions, and place the tip of the tongue against the palate.

For the time being, while cultivating the ‘threefold seclusions’, you may let the gaze and the arms rest in whatever position feels comfortable. However, during the actual first limb of Ṣaḍaṅgayoga, namely pratyāhāra, a specific posture must be adopted. Therefore, it is beneficial to begin familiarising to that posture here.

**Gaze:** Roll the eyeballs upward, as though looking towards the sky in the upper direction.

**Arms:** Form a vajra-fist by pressing the base of the ring finger with the thumb and enclosing it from the outside with the remaining fingers. Press the fists down at the roots of the thighs, straightening the arms.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### 2. Speech-Breath Seclusion/Disengagement

After assuming those key bodily postures, immediately clear away any earlier agitated or wandering air/breath. To prevent later disturbances or defects arising from the air/breath, expel the residual (harmful/impure) air three times through the nostrils. After that, until the end of the session, let the breath flow in its natural manner, allowing it to settle into whichever rhythm feels most comfortable.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### 3. Mental Seclusion/Disengagement

The key mental technique is as follows: think nothing at all (*acintyaḥ*); pay attention to nothing (*amanaskāraḥ*); do not deliberately tighten the mind (as in concentrating), nor deliberately loosen it (as in letting it wander). Do not look intently outward at the sky or space, nor look intently inward towards yourself. Let the mind remain without any support or object.

In that open state, in which the arising of appearances is merely potential, simply leave it unaltered and at ease. If you can settle into meditation in that state, it is excellent.

When the mind is settled and begins to wander, you must apply its antidotes. There are two ways in which the mind becomes distracted.

First, thoughts arise abruptly and vivid images of objects, such as visual forms, appear clearly. At the very moment these thoughts arise, if you recognise that the mind has become distracted, then—without judging or reflecting on it (*vicāraḥ*), and without engaging in liking or disliking—cut off the distraction completely, and continue the meditation by returning the mind to its previous settled state. That will suffice.

Second, the mind may drift into a succession of various thoughts, one following after another in an unbroken stream. If you drift along in this flow for a long time, it is highly problematic. However, when you catch it with the faculty of recollection or mindfulness (*smṛti*), generate a strong resolve: “From now on, I will not drift into this undercurrent of thoughts; whenever such distraction arises, I will catch it with mindfulness.”

In the state of absence of thought (*acintā*), apply the full energy of your mind and repeatedly stand guard with the faculty of mindfulness or recollection (*smṛti*), whether the mind is still and settled or not, whether it becomes distracted or not. As a result, drifting into the undercurrent of thoughts will become less and less, and before long it will subside completely.

At the end of the session, dedicate the merits of the practice. During the intervals between sessions as well, do not lose the gleam of that unfabricated state of mind, and do not engage in chatter or idle talk. Refresh the body with gentle massage and appropriate food.
