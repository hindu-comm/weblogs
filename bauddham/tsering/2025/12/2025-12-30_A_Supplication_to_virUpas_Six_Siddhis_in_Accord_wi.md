### Translator’s Notes

The title of this text may be rendered as *“Amṛta Waves: A Supplication to Virūpa’s Six Physical Manifestations, in Accord with Inner Purification.”* The work is preserved in the collected writings of Khyentsé Wangpo, and the colophon attributes its authorship to a figure named Kelsang Khyentsé.

This supplication text presents the six well-known siddhis of Virūpa in the form of inner yogic practices, each associated with the *pāramitās*. In a previous post, I translated a related text that depicts six distinct physical appearances and postures of Virūpa, corresponding to six major events in his life. Those six manifestations—or siddhis—serve as the basis for visualising Virūpa and play a central role in his iconography.

It is quite clear that these siddhis are not merely physical yogic powers. They are also to be understood as inner yogic practices and accomplishments. For example, Virūpa is particularly renowned for the two siddhis of “reversing the River Gaṅgā upwards” and “pawning or stopping the sun in exchange for wine.” A passage from a text in Drukpa’s *Dharma Treasury* explains the inner meaning of these two siddhis as follows:

> When, internally, one is able to reverse the direction of the bindu
> upwards, externally one is able to direct the river upwards. When,
> internally, one is able to press the rajas, the red element, downward,
> externally one is able to stop the sun. In saṃsāra, as in the outer
> world, water flows downward and fire blazes upward. Likewise, within
> the body, the red element burns upward and the white element flows
> downward. When the red element rises upward, it escapes from the crown
> of the head through the gate of the senses, producing dualistic
> perception, proliferating conceptual thoughts, and death. When the
> white element descends and is lost, it gives rise to ageing, sickness,
> and suffering. The yogin blocks the lower gate of the white element
> and the upper gate of the red element, thereby halting karma and
> kleśas, disease, suffering, conceptual thought, and death.

The descending stream of the Gaṅgā of the four bindus flows forth;  
By reversing the Gaṅgā, the bindu is bound at Jālandhara, the crown of the head.  
Unmoving bindu, the youthful vitality of supreme **śīla**;  
I supplicate the vajra body, free from defilement.

Caṇḍikā, whose nature is the extreme views of eternalism and nihilism,  
having overcome them through the accomplishment of yogic practice;

I supplicate the mind supreme in all aspects.

In short, the four nāḍī-cakras of one’s vajra body  
abide in essence as the undefiled four kāyas of the Buddha.  
All-conquering Yogīśvara Virūpa,  
inseparable from one’s glorious guru—to you I supplicate.
