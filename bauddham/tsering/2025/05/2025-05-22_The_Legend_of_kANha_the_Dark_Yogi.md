## Note

The term *Nagpo* (meaning "black" or "dark", Skt. Kanha, Krishna) is associated with the first three lineage masters of the Amṛtasiddhi tradition: Black Virūpa (*Krishna Virūpa* or *Nagpo Virūpa*), the Black Brahmin (*Krishna Vipra* or *Drangze Nagpo*), and the Black Charnel Ground Dweller (*Dhurkrod Nagpo*). If, as some Tibetan scholars suggest, Virūpa—the source of the Amṛtasiddhi—is not the well-known Mahāsiddha Virūpa associated with the Sakya Lamdré, then the question naturally arises: Who is this *Black Virūpa* or *Junior Virūpa*?

Reading the legend of Mahāsiddha Kanha (Nagpopa), particularly the episode in which he imparts the teachings of Chinnamastā Devī shortly before his death, prompts further reflection on whether this dark yogi could be identified with the *Black Virūpa*. In that story, he is described as a disciple of Mahāsiddha Jalandhara. Yet in a hagiography attributed to Tāranātha, he later encounters and studies under Virūpa—suggesting a complex lineage transmission and perhaps overlapping identities.

This post tells the final section of the story, not the full narrative.

## The Legend of Kāṇha

Kāṇha (Nagpopa), the dark yogi, arrived in a village and spotted a woman standing beneath a lychee tree. He asked her for a lychee fruit, but she refused. Enraged, Kanhapa used his mystical power to pluck a lychee from the branch with his gaze. The woman then fixed her eyes on the fruit, causing it to reattach itself to the tree. Furious, Kanhapa chanted a deadly mantra at her; she immediately collapsed, bleeding profusely.

Bystanders gasped and cried out, ‘A yogi has killed a woman!’ Realizing the gravity of what he had done, Kanhapa’s anger melted away and compassion filled him. He chanted a healing mantra and placed a protective yantra on the woman, reviving her. The woman, now barely able to stand, hurled an enchanted mantra at Kanhapa.

The spell struck Kanhapa, and he began to vomit blood from both mouth and anus, wracked with pain. Desperate, he summoned his attendant, Dakini Bandha, and ordered her to fetch a sacred medicine from a distant place. Bandha hurried to the designated spot, but instead of medicine she found the same woman from before — now in the form of an old woman, weeping.

Concerned, Dakini Bandha asked, ‘Why are you crying, dear one?’ The old woman sobbed, ‘Why shouldn’t I be? Yogi Kanhapa is dead.’ Hearing this, Bandha believed Kanhapa had died. In despair, she returned with no medicine. When she arrived back, she found Kanhapa still alive. He explained the misunderstanding, and they now faced his illness with no cure.

For the next seven days, Kanhapa gathered his disciples and imparted the secret teachings of Chinnamasta devi to them. After completing these esoteric lessons, he prepared for the end of his earthly life. Finally, he cast off his mortal body and ascended to Khecara, the celestial realm.
