# TRANSLATION

### Four Yogas of Vital Air (*Mārutayoga*)

1.  **Gentle Breathing Practice**

2.  **Middle Air Breathing Practice**

3.  **Kumbhaka — Pot Breathing Practice**

4.  **Forceful Breathing Practice**

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### I. Vajra Japa — Gentle Breathing Practice

**Preparation** — First, expel the residual harmful air either nine times or three times.

**Inhalation** — Inhale the breath with the sound of Oṃ, drawing the air down through the right and left channels as if blowing through the entrails, until it touches the fire of the A-stroke at the point where the three channels meet \[about four inches below the navel\].

**Retention** — When the air remains inside, press it downward with the sound of Āḥ, thereby intensifying the heat of the fire tenfold. As the heat from this fire rises and is about to melt the bodhicitta bindu at the crown of your head, imagine the arising of bliss.

**Exhalation** — As you exhale and release the air, focus your mind on the A-stroke at the navel without distraction.

**Practice rhythm** — Begin this gentle breathing practice gradually: start with seven breathing cycles (inhalation, retention, and exhalation), then increase to thirteen, twenty-five, and so on, until you can sustain one hundred breathing cycles. Do not perform the breathing practice when the breath is flowing only through the right nostril, as this is harmful. When the breath flows only through the left nostril, it is beneficial and suitable for practice. When the breath flows evenly through both nostrils, this is the most auspicious and effective time to practise.

**Benefits of the Practice** —  
When the vital air and the mind (which rides upon it) are focused at the meeting point of the three channels, they are able to enter the central channel. This meeting point (*Śṛṅgāṭaka*), located below the navel, is the sacred abode of the Great Mother Prajñāpāramitā. By mastering the vital air at this spot, the entire conceptual mind of subject–object duality is transformed into the undefiled great bliss.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### II. Middle-Air Breathing Practice

Begin the Middle-Air Breathing Practice once you have become somewhat accustomed to the Gentle Breathing Practice.

**Posture** — Assume the six-corner fire-stove posture: sit with the legs crossed and the knees upright, holding the knees either with your arms or secured with a yoga strap. Then focus the mind on the fire of the A-stroke at the navel, now visualised as one hundred times hotter than before.

**Breathing** **method** —

- Lower air: Restrain the lower air slightly.

- Middle air: Expand the middle forward in the direction of the navel,
  arching the waist inward.

- Upper air: Press the upper air down slightly.

**Continuity of practice** — Throughout the entire day — at dawn, morning, afternoon, and evening — and in all activities, do not lose hold of the middle air.

**Benefits** —  
This practice is conducive to all stages of both generation and completion.  
It aids in mastering the holding, drawing up, and diffusing of the bindu throughout the body.  
It dispels mental dullness and restlessness, removes old diseases, and prevents new ones from arising.  
It also fosters the arising of bliss, clarity, and non-conceptual awareness.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### III. Kumbhaka — Pot Breathing Practice

**Visualisation** —  
Visualise the flame of fire rising to the navel, now a thousand times hotter than before. The intense heat of this fire spontaneously melts the bodhicitta bindu at the crown of the head. When it drops upon the fire with a sharp sizzling hiss, it gives rise to a irresistible bliss.

**Breathing Method** —  
Four breathing techniques are essential in Kumbhaka practice:

1.  Drawing in the air (inhalation)

2.  Filling up the air

3.  Pressing down the air

4.  Shooting out the air (exhalation)

------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### **1. Drawing in**

**Method** —  
Straighten the upper body and slightly bend the throat. Draw in a continuous stream of air without making any sound. At the end of the inhalation, swallow a drop of saliva and simultaneously press the air downward.

If this technique is not done correctly, the practice will not be effective. For example, if the mouth of a bellows is not in close contact with the fire, it will not be able to make the fire blaze. In the same way, although your body—like a leather bellows—may be full of air, if that air is not directed precisely to the A-stroke at the navel, it will not generate heat.

**Benefits** —  
By directing the vital air and mind below the navel, the bliss-heat is quickly kindled.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### **2. Filling up**

**Method** —  
At the end of the complete inhalation, press the upper air down to just below the navel—as if pressing a bellows—and draw the lower air upward. Because the upper air and lower air differ in temperature, if you are unaccustomed to this practice it may cause problem. Therefore, at the beginning, press the upper air downward while allowing the lower air to remain in its natural resting place.

**Benefits** —  
All the primary and secondary vital airs enter their vital points, causing the undefiled bliss-emptiness to blaze forth.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### **3. Pressing Down**

**Method** —  
When you can no longer hold the union of the upper and lower air, take a short inhalation and press the air down through the right, middle, and left.

If this technique is not performed correctly, it can have adverse effects on the body—like medicine turning into poison. The key point is not to practise this method at the wrong time. When done at the right moment, however, it is highly effective.

**Benefits** —  
This practice helps to open the channels and release channel knots.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### **4. Shooting Out**

**Method** —  
Exhale all the air you can, leaving none inside, while focusing your mind below the navel. When you can no longer hold the air out and your head begins to quiver, draw the air in slowly, with a soft hiss through the nose.

**Benefits** —  
By placing the pristine air of wisdom in an unmoving state, this practice extends the yogi’s life.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

In brief, during inhalation, draw the air in as a powerful wrestler bends a bow.  
When the air remains inside, hold it below the navel as a pool-maker encircles the water. During exhalation, release the air like a skilled archer shooting an arrow.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Note:** This text is taken from the *Richen Terzö Chenmo*. The fourth breathing practice is omitted here, as it is unlikely to be of interest or practical benefit to most readers.
