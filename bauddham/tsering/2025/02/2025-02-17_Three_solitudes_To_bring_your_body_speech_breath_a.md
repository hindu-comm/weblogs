#### To bring your body, speech \[breath\], and mind into a proper condition prior to the practice of Śamatha and Vipaśyanā (Mahamudra system)

## I. Body (posture)

### Sit in the seven-point Vairocana sitting posture

1.  Sit cross-legged in the vajra posture.

2.  Place both hands in the dhyana mudra position, just below the navel.

3.  Straighten your spine.

4.  Expand your chest and shoulders.

5.  Bend your throat and press a little on the larynx.

6.  Press your tongue against your palate.

7.  Keep your eyes open and still, with a gaze that is about the length
    of a yoke.

### Five-point meditation postures based on key functions corresponding to five primary *vāyus*.

1.  Vajra cross-legged posture: It helps to direct *apāna-vāyu*
    \[downward moving air\] into the central channel.

2.  Hands in dhyana mudra: It helps to direct *Samāna-vāyu* \[fire
    accompanying air\] into the central channel.

3.  Keep the back straight and the shoulders expanded: This helps to
    direct the *vyāna-vāyu* \[pervasive air\] into the central channel.

4.  The throat is bent: It helps to direct *Udāna-vāyu* \[upward moving
    air\] into the central channel.

5.  The tongue against the palate and the position of the eyes help to
    direct *prāṇa-vāyu* \[life-bearing air\] into the central channel.

Non-conceptual primordial wisdom arises with the help of these postures when the five primary *vāyus* and all the *vāyus* in the body enter the central channel.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## II. Speech (Breath)

Stop all verbal activities. Blow out the \[harmful\] residual air. Let your breathing be natural.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## III. Mind

**Doha**

> Having discarded all thinking and thoughts, 
> like a baby sitting alert and relaxed, 
> If you diligently practise the Guru's teachings, 
> Sahaja will manifest without a doubt.

Don't dwell on the past. Do not think about the future. Do not examine and analyse in the present. Whatever sensory objects \[such as visual objects, sounds and sensations\] appear to the five senses, instead of conceptually analysing this is this and this is that, turn your mind inwards and let your mind settle into its natural state, like a baby sitting alert and relaxed. Do not allow your mind to be distracted even for a second. It is through the presence of དྲན་པ་ \[*Smṛti*-*upasthāna*/དྲན་པ་ཉེ་བར་བཞག་པ་\] that the undistracted mind is maintained. {The Sanskrit term *Smṛti* and the Tibetan term དྲན་པ་ (*dran pa*) are widely known as mindfulness. However, its literal meaning is ‘not forgetting’ the object on which the mind is focused, or the object of the mind doesn't disappear from the focus of the mind}. As it says,

> དྲན་པ་ཉེ་བར་བཞག་པ་བཞི་ནི་དྲན་པའི་གཟུངས་དང་ལྡན་པས། བརྗེད་ཅིང་ཡལ་བ་མེད་དེ།
> གཟུགས་བརྗེད་ཅིང་ཡལ་བ་མེད་དོ།

Because the four Smṛtyupasthāna/Satipatthana possess the rope of Smṛti/དྲན་པ་ \[not forgetting\], the mind does not forget the object of its meditation, which is body, sensation, mind and Dharma. Or the object of meditation does not disappear from the mind's focus.

> །དྲན་པ་ནི་མ་ཡེངས་པ་སྟེ། །ཆོས་མངོན་པ་ལས། དྲན་པ་ནི་འདྲིས་པའི་དངོས་པོ་མ་བརྗེད་པའོ།

Smṛti/དྲན་པ་ is the mind not being distracted or wandering away. In the Abhidharma text it is said that Smṛti/དྲན་པ་ is not forgetting the things with which you are familiar.
