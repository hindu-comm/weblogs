### **Translator’s Note**

This post presents a translation of the hagiography of Sukhasiddhi, a renowned female siddhā of India, who attained realization under the guidance of the great tantric master Virūpa, who later became the root teacher of Khyungpo Naljor. This story is translated as told in Khyungpo Naljor’s biography. She was a 61-year-old destitute woman when she met Virūpa and attained realization, becoming the wisdom ḍākinī known as Sukhasiddhi.

## The Life of Sukhasiddhi

### **Exile in a Time of Famine**

Once, in the northwestern region of Kashmir, there lived a family comprising seven households. At that time, the land was gripped by famine, and all they had for sustenance was a single pot of rice. The husband and sons said to the old woman, “Keep this pot of rice safe—we’ll need it when the venerable Jowo Namda Ringpo comes for alms tomorrow. We’re heading out now to search for food.”

The husband, sons, and daughters departed in all directions in search of provisions. But wherever they went, they encountered only desolation—famine had touched every corner of the land, and not a single grain could be found.

While they were away, a blue bhikṣu appeared at the house. He said to the old woman, “I am Jowo Namda Rinpo. Serve me the pot of rice.” Without hesitation, the old woman offered him the rice. She also partook of a small portion herself.

Later that day, the husband and sons returned empty-handed. “Mother,” they said, “open the pot of rice—we are starving. There is famine everywhere, and we found nothing.”

The old woman replied, “The venerable Jowo Namda Ringpo came today, dressed in robes and carrying an alms bowl. I offered him the rice. There is none left.”

When they heard this, the husband and sons grew furious. “Old woman,” they shouted, “you would not go in search of food, and you could not even guard what little we had. There can be no peace in this house while you remain. Leave at once!”

### **The Journey to Urgyen (Oddiyana)**

Silently, the old woman departed. As she wandered, she asked those she met, “Where should I go now?” Some replied, “To the west lies a land of blessings called Urgyen. It is said to be a pure and sacred realm where all the men are ḍākas and all the women are ḍākinīs. The people there have naturally clear minds and live effortlessly, free of hardship.”

So she set off for Urgyen. There, she saw rice growing in abundance. Gathering a bushel, she journeyed onward to a place called Bheta. Settling there, she came into possession of even more rice and eventually became a wine seller.

### **Meeting the Yoginī**

At that time, in a remote cave in Urgyen, the great master Virupa was engaged in a secret tantric practice. For this purpose, a yoginī would often descend into the valley to procure meat and wine, which were needed as sacramental offerings.

One day, the old woman asked her, “You come here every day to buy wine—who are you taking it for?”

The yoginī replied, “There is a great spiritual master who resides at the edge of the forest. I bring this wine to him.”

The old woman said, “Due to the lack of merit in my past lives, I fell into poverty and was cast out by my own family. There is no lasting essence in wealth. From now on, I will offer the best portion of wine every time, free of charge.”

### **Meeting Virupa and her Transformation**

One day, Virupa asked the yoginī, “Where do you get such fine wine—and that too, given freely?”

She replied, “There is a deeply devoted old woman. When I told her the wine was for a great master, she vowed to send it always, asking for nothing in return.”

Virupa then said, “This woman must be liberated from saṃsāra. Bring her to me.”

When the yoginī conveyed this to the old woman, she was overjoyed. In her devotion, she brought two pots of her finest wine, along with an offering of pork, and presented them to Virupa.

That very evening, Virupa bestowed upon her the complete four empowerments of the secret Vajrayoginī sādhana. In that very moment, she was utterly transformed—her ordinary body dissolved into the form of a wisdom ḍākinī, appearing as a sixteen-year-old maiden.

She had been 59 years old when her husband and sons cast her out. She spent one year selling wine, and was 60 when she first encountered the yoginī. When she met Virupa, she was 61. Upon receiving the empowerments, she took on the radiant form of a 16-year-old maiden and became known as Sukhasiddhi—indistinguishable from Nairātmyā Devī herself.

### **Lineage Masters of the Six Yogas of Sukhasiddhi (སུ་ཁ་ཆོས་དྲུག་)**

1.  **Virupa**  
    (Indian Mahāsiddha, 8th–9th century)

2.  **Sukhasiddhi**  
    (Indian Ḍākinī, 10th–11th century)

3.  **མཁས་གྲུབ་ཁྱུང་པོ་རྣལ་འབྱོར།**  
    *Khedrup Khyungpo Naljor* (11th–12th century)

4.  **རྨོག་ལྕོག་པ་རིན་ཆེན་བརྩོན་འགྲུས།**  
    *Mokchokpa Rinchen Tsöndrü* (12th century)

5.  **སྐྱེར་སྒང་པ་ཆོས་ཀྱི་སེངྒེ།**  
    *Kyergangpa Chökyi Sengé* (12th–13th century)

6.  **སངས་རྒྱས་གཉན་སྟོན།**  
    *Sanggyé Nyentön* (12th–13th century)

7.  **བརྩོན་འགྲུས་སེངྒེ།**  
    *Tsöndrü Sengé* (13th century)

8.  **མཁས་གྲུབ་ཤངས་སྟོན།**  
    *Khedrup Shangtön* (13th–14th century)

9.  **ཆོས་དཔལ་བཟང་པོ།**  
    *Chöpal Zangpo* (13th–14th century)

10. **ཚུལ་ཁྲིམས་རྒྱལ་མཚན།**  
    *Tsultrim Gyeltsen* (15th century)

11. **གཞོན་ནུ་ཆོས་གྲུབ།**  
    *Shyönnu Chödrup* (15th–16th century)

    *Jetsün Drölwé Gön* (Tāranātha, 1575–1634)

13. **རྗེ་བཙུན་མ་ཕྲིན་ལས་དབང་མོ།**  
    *Jetsünma Trinlé Wangmo* (16th–17th century)

14. **ཀུན་བཟང་དབང་པོ།**  
    *Künzang Wangpo* (17th century)

15. **ཚེ་དབང་ནོར་བུ།**  
    *Tsewang Norbu* (1698–1755)
