# Text and Translation

### Meaning of Prāṇāyāma

> རླུང་རོ་རྐྱང་ལ་རྒྱུ་བ་བཀག་ནས་དབུ་མར་འཇུག་པའི་རླུང་སྦྱོར་ལ་སྲོག་རྩོལ་གྱི་ཡན་ལག་ཏུ་བྱེད་

*Prāṇa* (སྲོག་) refers to the vital air — the life-sustaining air — and *āyāma* (བརྩོལ་བ་), while sometimes interpreted as "lengthening," in this context means "restraint" or "stopping." Thus, *prāṇāyāma* (སྲོག་བརྩོལ་) refers to the yogic practice of stopping the movement of vital air in the left and right channels and directing it into the central channel.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### At What Stage to Practise Prāṇāyāma

> དང་པོར་སོར་བསམ་གཉིས་སྒོམ་པའི་དུས་སུ། དངོས་སུ་རླུང་ལ་མ་དམིགས་ཀྱང་།
> ལུས་ཀྱི་གནད་ཆེ་སར་བསྣུན་པས་རླུང་དབུ་མར་ཚུད་དེ་  
> དབུ་མ་སྦྱངས་པའི་མཚམས་ནས་ཡིན་ཏེ།
> དེ་ནས་རླུང་ལ་དངོས་སུ་དམིགས་པའི་སྲོག་རྩོལ་བསྒོམས་ན་གནད་དུ་འགྲོ་ཚབས་ཆེ་བའི་དགོས་པ་ཡོད་པའི་  
> ཕྱིར་རོ།

One should first practise *Pratyāhāra* and *Dhyāna* to purify the passage of the central channel. In *Pratyāhāra* and *Dhyāna*, although the practice does not directly focus on the breath, it stimulates the vital points of the body. This causes the vital air (*prāṇa*) to enter the central channel and purify it. This helps to enhance the effectiveness of *Prāṇāyāma*, which is centered on the breath.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### **Two Types of Breathing Practices in Prāṇāyāma**

1.  **Vajra Breathing**  
    A breathing practice in which the movement of air in the left and
    right channels is not truly stopped.

2.  **Kumbhaka:**  
    A breathing practice in which the movement of air in the left and
    right channels is brought to a stop.

    ------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### 1. Vajra Breathing

**Posture:**

> ལུས་གནད་སོགས་ནི་སོར་སྡུད་དང་འདྲ་ལ། ཁྱད་པར་ནི།
> རྐང་པ་གཡོན་པའི་རྟིང་པས་དྲི་ཆེན་གྱི་ལམ་བཀག་པའམ། རྡོ་རྗེའི་རྩ་བ་མནན་པའོ།

The body posture is the same as in *pratyāhāra* and *dhyāna* practice, with the difference that the right heel is pressed against either the anus or the perineum.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Eyes Position:**

> ལྟ་སྟངས་ནི་བདུད་རྩི་འཁྱིལ་བའི་ལྟ་བ་

*Amṛtakuṇḍalī* (Bliss-whorl) gaze — looking straight ahead, in line with the spot between the eyebrows.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Focus of Mind:**

> རྡོར་བཟླས་ཀྱིས་རླུང་འཇུག་གནས་ལྡང་གསུམ་ལ་དམིགས་

Focus on the inhalation, retention, and exhalation of the breath.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Breathing Method:**

> ཨོཾ་ཡིག་གིས་འཇུག་པ་དང་། ཧཱུཾ་ཡིག་གིས་འགོག་པ་དང་། ཨཱཿཡིག་གིས་འབྱུང་བ།
> ད་ལྟ་དྷཱུ་ཏཱི་ནས་འབྱུང་འཇུག་མི་བྱེད་པ་སྟེ། དབུ་མ་ནས་འཇུག་གནས་ལྡང་གསུམ་གྱི་སྦྱོར་བ་བྱེད་པ།
> རྣལ་འབྱོར་པས་བྱེད་པ་སྟེ་མོས་པས་བྱེད་པའོ།
> །རླུང་སྔགས་སྦྱོར་ཚུལ་ཡང་རླུང་གི་རང་གདངས་ཡི་གེ་གསུམ་དུ་སྒྲོག་གིན་ཡོད་པ་ཉན་པའི་རྣམ་པ་ལྟ་བུར་བྱའི།
> ཡི་གེ་གསུམ་གྱི་གཟུགས་དང་སྒྲའི་རྣམ་པ་ལྟ་བུར་བྱས་ན་ཕྱིན་ཅི་ལོག་ཏུ་བཟུང་བས།
> གནད་བླ་མའི་ཞལ་ལས་ཤེས་དགོས་སོ།

The *Vajra breathing* method consists of a sequence of inhalation, retention, and exhalation. Inhalation is accompanied by the sound **Om** (ཨོཾ་), retention by **Hūṃ** (ཧཱུཾ་), and exhalation by **Āḥ** (ཨཱཿ). At this stage, although the breath does not actually move through the central channel, one must contemplate as though it flows through it. The three phases of breath and the three mantras are joined in such a way that the natural tone of the breath aligns with the sound of the mantra. Simply concentrating on the *shape* or *audible sound* of the mantra, however, is incorrect practice. You should learn this from your guru.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### 2. Kumbhaka Breathing

> སྤྱིར་རླུང་འབྱུང་འཇུག་མི་བྱེད་པར་ནང་དུ་གནས་པ་ལའང་བུམ་པ་ཅན་དུ་བཤད་པ་ཡོད་ལ།
> དེ་རྒྱུད་སྡེ་འོག་མ་ལའང་ཡོད་  
> ཅིང་། དེར་མ་ཟད་གྲངས་ཅན་ལའང་ཡོད་པར་བཤད་པས།
> པཎྜི་ཏ་སའི་སྙིང་པོས་མུ་སྟེགས་དང་མ་རྒྱུད་ཀྱི་རླུང་སྦྱོར་ཁྱད་པར་འབྱེད་དཀའ་  
> གསུངས་། གྲངས་ཅན་སོགས་ལ་རླུང་ཁ་སྦྱར་ནས་རླུང་བུམ་པ་ཅན་བྱེད་པ་ཙམ་ཡོད་ཀྱང་ཁྱད་པར་ཆེ་སྟེ།
> རྩ་འཁོར་གྱི་དྷཱུ་ཏཱིའི་དབུས་སུ་ཁ་སྦྱར་བྱས་ནས་  
> བཟུང་ནས་ལྷན་སྐྱེས་འདྲེན་པ་མེད་པའི་ཕྱིར་རོ།

*Kumbhaka* — the mere retention of breath, without inhalation or exhalation — and the method involving the union of the upper and lower airs are practices found in both Buddhist Vajrayāna and Brahmanical Sāṃkhya traditions. As Pandita Kṣitigarbha noted, it is difficult to draw a clear distinction between the breathing practices of the Buddhists and those of the Brahmanical schools.

While Brahmanical systems such as Sāṃkhya include the basic practice of *kumbhaka* through the uniting of upper and lower airs, important differences remain. In Vajrayāna, the *kumbhaka* involving the union of upper and lower airs is performed at the center of a *nāḍī-cakra* within the central channel, and it brings forth the *sahajānanda* (co-emergent joy), whereas in Sāṃkhya no such practices are found.

The distinct *kumbhaka* practice of Vajrayāna *Ṣaḍaṅga-Yoga* can be understood through these five key points: the sequence in which the upper and lower airs are drawn, the specific channels through which they are moved, and the location where they are united after being drawn.

**(i) Sequence of Drawing the Upper and Lower Airs:**

> སྟེངའོག་གི་རླུང་འཐེན་པའི་གོ་རིམ།

If one does not know the method for restraining the *bodhicitta bindu* from falling, the *prāṇa-vāyu* (upper air) should first be drawn down to the navel. Only then should the *apāna-vāyu* (lower air) be drawn upward. This order helps prevent the *bindu* from descending or leaking out. However, if one is proficient in the method of restraining the *bindu*, the *apāna-vāyu* may be drawn upward first.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**(ii) Channels Through Which the Upper and Lower Airs Are Drawn:**

> སྟེང་འོག་གི་རླུང་གང་ནས་འཐེན་པ།

It appears that some earlier masters would draw the upper air downward and the lower air upward to the navel merely from within the general interior of the body, without fully understanding the correct or effective method. For the practice to be effective, the *prāṇa-vāyu* (upper air) should be drawn downward through the central channel, and the *apāna-vāyu* (lower air) should likewise be drawn upward through the central channel.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**(iii) Where to Unite the Upper and Lower Airs:**

> འཐེན་ནས་གང་དུ་ཁ་སྦྱར་བྱ་བ།

After drawing the upper and lower airs to the navel through the central channel, they should be united at the center of the navel’s nāḍī-cakra, the point where the left and right channels form a knot around the central channel.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**(iv) Focus of Mind**

> ཁ་སྦྱར་དེ་ཉིད་ལ་སེམས་གཏོད་པ།

Direct the mind to the sphere of air where the upper and lower airs have merged into one — at the center of the navel’s *nāḍī-cakra*.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**(v) How Long to Practise Kumbhaka**

> སྒོམ་པའི་ཡུན་ཚད་

Practise *kumbhaka* repeatedly while maintaining focus on the breath, but do so with caution. If signs such as head pain, visual halos around the body, or heart palpitations arise, continuing the practice may be dangerous and could pose a risk to life. Therefore, the practice should be stopped immediately upon noticing such symptoms. Practise only as long as there is no discomfort in the body.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### **Haṭhayoga (Yoga of the Forceful Method): Enhancement Practice** 

> Now the *haṭhayoga* is explained. Here, when the unchanging moment
> (the moment of unchanging bliss) does not take place because the vital
> breath (*prāṇa)* is unrestrained (inside the central), in spite of the
> image (image of emptiness/Śūnyatā-bimba) having been seen "by means of
> withdrawal (*pratyāhāra* and *prāṇāyāma)*, and so on", then the
> yogin - after having made the vital breath flow in the central channel
> "violently (forcefully/*haṭhena*) through the continuous exercise of
> sound (*nāda**)***" - "can realize" the unchanging moment (of bliss)
> through non-vibration "by arresting the *bindu*" of the bodhicitta "in
> the vajra-gem (penis) placed in the lotus" of the wisdom. This is the
> *haṭhayoga*. (Anupamarakshita-Shadanga yoga text)[1](#footnote-1)

If the *kumbhaka* described above does not succeed in gently guiding and confining the vital air (prana) inside the central channel, the practitioner should apply *Haṭhayoga* — the forceful method. This involves forcefully stopping the movement of air in the right and left channels and directing it into the central channel through the practice of Nāda.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Practice of Nāda**  
*Nāda* here refers to a subtle sound — specifically, the sound of the syllable **huṃ**.

**How to Practise**  
Sit in *Vajra posture* (full lotus), with the legs crossed. Cross the arms over the chest, forming fists with the hands. Lower the head slightly and forcefully inhale through the nose with the sound **huṃ**, visualizing the air entering the central channel. At the same time, draw up the *apāna-vāyu* (lower air) from either of the two lower openings (genitals or anus), beginning from just above the navel, and gradually pull it upward. This should be done with force and sustained duration — but not too long. If continued beyond one’s strength, the method will lose its forcefulness because of exhaustion.
