### **Note to the Readers**

This physical yoga practice takes its name from the kumbhaka breathing technique, which is mentioned in the main phase of the breathing practice. The principle underlying Amoghavajra’s yogic method is the unification of the upper-air (above the navel) and the lower-air (below the navel) at the navel centre. Through the dynamic force of these two vital airs, the union of method — the male aspect (white element or seed), and wisdom — the female aspect (red element or seed), takes place at the navel. In this way, the union of wisdom and method is realised, which is the key to enlightenment in Buddhism. As the two side channels cease to function, the three channels merge into one (see the section on mental observation, as well as my [journal article on Amoghavajra’s](https://journalofyogastudies.org/index.php/JoYS/article/view/106) *[Amṛtasiddhi](https://journalofyogastudies.org/index.php/JoYS/article/view/106)* text). There appears to be an analogical relationship between the three channels and the three goddesses associated with Chinnamastā Devī, to whom the *Amṛtasiddhi* pays homage.

The practice consists of fifteen sequences of physical yoga: seven in the preliminary phase, one in the main phase, and seven in the postliminary phase. In the Virupa’s *Amṛtasiddhi* yoga manual composed by Amoghavajra, there are also sets of physical yoga practices named after breathing techniques such as *kumbhaka*, *pūraka*, and *recaka*. These are quite similar to the present set of practices, which Amoghavajra ascribes to the siddha Kṛṣṇācāryavarta.

There exists a set of physical yoga manuals catalogued in the Tibetan *Tengyur* as separate texts, such as the *Yoga of the Cart-horse Wheel*, *Kumbhaka*, *Pūraka*, *Caṇḍālī*, and the *Head-downward Stick*. However, in examining the final text — the *Trulkhor* of *Caṇḍālī* — the colophon shared among these works appears there. Although all of these texts were composed by the same author (Amoghavajra), according to the colophon they are attributed to *Kṛṣṇācāryavarta* rather than to *Virūpa*.

#### **Colophon:**

> སྤྱོད་པ་ཆེན་པོ་རིག་པའི་གནས་ལྔ་ལ་མཁས་པ།་གཉིས་སུ་མེད་པའི་དོན་རྟོགས་པ།་ནག་པོ་སྤྱོད་པ་བས་མཛད་པ་རྫོགས་སོ།
> བོད་ཡུལ་དུ་རྣལ་འབྱོར་གྱི་དབང་ཕྱུག་ཨ་མོ་ཀ་བཛྲས་འགྱུར་དུ་གསུང་ཞིང་ཡི་གེར་བཀོད་པའོ༎

***Thus concludes the composition by Kṛṣṇācāryavarta, who has mastered the five fields of knowledge and realized the meaning of non duality, which in the land of Tibet was taught and committed to writing in translation by the Yogeśvara Amoghavajra.***

### **Some notes on translation:**

- The source of the Tibetan Tengyur text is the Narthang edition. Where
  I think I found major spelling errors, I have provided corrections in
  brackets.

- In his *Amṛtasiddhi* texts, Amoghavajra sometimes uses the Sanskrit
  terms *kumbhaka*, *pūraka*, and *recaka*, transliterated directly into
  Tibetan.

- The phrase *“bend the throat”* in my translation refers to drawing the
  chin inward toward the chest, a mudra commonly known as
  *Jālandhara-bandha* in Haṭha Yoga terminology.

- The “lotus cross-legged posture” mentioned in this text does not
  correspond to the full lotus posture of Haṭha Yoga, whereas the
  *vajra* cross-legged posture corresponds to the full lotus position of
  classical Haṭha Yoga.

- In the Vajrayāna tradition, *Akṣobhya* is also referred to as the
  *bodhicitta bindu*.

- I am not entirely certain about the precise identification of the five
  channels system of Amoghavajra. The *Amṛtasiddhi Yoga* of Amoghavajra
  is also based on a five-channel system (not the widely known three
  channels). From his descriptions, it appears that these five channels
  consist of two upper right and left channels, two lower right and left
  channels, and one central channel.

- The term *srog rtsol* is generally understood as *prāṇāyāma*. However,
  in some Tibetan Vajrayāna traditions, *srog* and *rtsol* carry
  distinct connotations, referring respectively to inhalation and
  exhalation, or, in certain contexts, to the movement of air through
  the right and left channels. In the present translation, I have
  adopted this latter meaning.

- A question mark is inserted in places where the translation remains
  uncertain or ambiguous.

> ལྟེ་བ་སྤྲུལ་པའི་འཁོར་ལོ་རུ།
> དེ་གཉིས་འཛོམས་པའི་སྟོབས་རྣམས་ཀྱིས།
> སྲོག་རྩོལ་གཉིས་དང་འཁོར་འདས་དང།
> ཐབས་དང་ཤེས་རབ་དབྱེར་མེད་ཅིང།
> དེ་ཉིད་ངང་ལ་གནས་པར་བྱ༎
> ```

***Visualise your body in the form of your personal deity. Visualise Akṣobhya \[the bodhicitta bindu\], at the crown of the head. Then meditate on the rainbow and illusory body. Visualise the upper ends of the five channels pierced to the five syllables of the great syllable A within the crown great-bliss chakra, and the lower ends of the five channels pierced to the five syllables of the great syllable RAM within the perineal bliss-sustaining chakra. From the syllable RAM, rays of light shine forth; from the syllable A, rays of light also emanate. These two streams of light meet at the navel emanation chakra. Through the power of their union, the dual airs of the left and right channels \[sun and moon\], samsāra and nirvāṇa, and method and wisdom become indivisibly one. Abide in that very state.***

***First, cough three times \[to clear the windpipe\]. Then expel the residual air from the right and left channels three times each. Inhale through the mouth and practise the sixteenfold cooling breath. Thereafter, by the method of kumbhaka, forcefully press down the upper-air, draw up the lower-air, and hold the two airs at the navel. Thus is the breathing technique.***
