**Note:** This is a brief Mahāmudrā instruction by Ju Mipham Gyatso, presenting the practice of the three essential states of mind and meditation for recognising the nature of mind and the truth of reality.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Three Essential States of Mind and Meditation in Mahāmudrā: Abiding (Stillness), Moving (Thinking), and Rigpa (Awareness/Noticing)**

When you turn your mind inward and look at your own mind, if it remains steady without straying elsewhere, this is called *abiding* (stillness). When a thought arises, this is called *moving* (thinking). Whether the mind is abiding or moving, when one sees one’s own mind, that is called *rigpa* (awareness/noticing). If one sustains this repeatedly, then whatever pleasant or unpleasant appearances (thoughts and feelings) arise, one recognises their basic nature: they all arise from the mind and ultimately dissolve back into the mind. When this is understood, one recognises that everything appearing within the mind is the mind’s own self-display.

And when you sharply examine the fundamental nature of both states of mind—abiding (stillness) and moving (thought)—though they may present various appearances, you will understand that they are empty, lacking any established identity whatsoever. Here, *void* does not mean utterly empty like physical space; it refers to the emptiness of inherent existence, possessing the clarity of cognisant awareness.

When you realise this secret pith of the mind, although the observer and the observed are not separate entities, one experiences the self-luminous mind. This is what is meant by recognising *rigpa*. This is the pointing-out instruction of Mahāmudrā and Dzogchen.
