### **Notes to the Reader**

Vajrayāna follows the *Ṣaḍaṅga Yoga* system rather than the more widely known *Aṣṭāṅga Yoga* system. The six limbs of *Ṣaḍaṅga Yoga* are *Pratyāhāra*, *Dhyāna*, *Prāṇāyāma*, *Dhāraṇā*, *Anusmṛti*, and *Samādhi*.

The *Ṣaḍaṅga Yoga* (Six Limbs of Yoga) of Vajrayāna has received comparatively little attention and remains largely unfamiliar in both academic and lay circles. Within Vajrayāna Buddhism, its practice originates primarily from the *Kālacakra Tantra’s* *dzogrim* (completion-stage) teachings. The *Ṣaḍaṅga Yoga* of *Kālacakra* is, however, widely practised across the various Tibetan Buddhist traditions. Click on the link to read *[The Spread of the Ṣaḍaṅga Yoga Tradition in Tibet: Lineage and Transmission](https://open.substack.com/pub/tsering/p/the-spread-of-sadanga-yoga-tradition?r=19pwkm&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&showWelcomeOnShare=false)* by Tāranātha.

The *Prāṇāyāma* section presented here is based on the writings of Drikung Chungtsang Chökyi Drakpa (17th century) of the Drikung Kagyu lineage. Though brief, his exposition closely parallels the *Ṣaḍaṅga Yoga* texts of Jonang Tārānātha.

For readers interested in Tārānātha’s presentation of *Prāṇāyāma* according to the Paṇḍita Śākya Śrī lineage, see: **“***[Vajrayāna Ṣaḍaṅga Yoga’s Prāṇāyāma: Paṇḍita Śākya Śrī’s Method of Prāṇāyāma](https://open.substack.com/pub/tsering/p/vajrayana-sadanga-yogas-pranayama?r=19pwkm&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&showWelcomeOnShare=false).***”**

There are two principal lineages of the *Kālacakra* tradition: the Ra (Ra Chödrak Lotsāwa, རྭ་ལུགས་) and the Dro (Drontön Lotsāwa, འབྲོ་ལུགས་) translator traditions. Two slightly different modes of *Prāṇāyāma* practice have descended from these lineages. The Dro tradition’s *Prāṇāyāma* is based on the teachings of Paṇḍita Śākya Śrī and Paṇḍita Somanātha, while the teachings of Venerable Samanta Śrī are identified with the Ra tradition. In this article, I present the translation of Paṇḍita Somanātha’s method of *Prāṇāyāma* as transmitted by Drikung Chungtsang Chökyi Drakpa.

If you wish to read the *Prāṇāyāma* instructions of all the major *Ṣaḍaṅga Yoga* lineages in Tibet as presented by Tārānātha, click here *[Prānāyāma: Ṣaḍaṅga Yoga \[Six Limbs of Yoga\] text by Tārānāthā](https://open.substack.com/pub/tsering/p/pranayama-sadanga-yoga-six-limbs?r=19pwkm&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&showWelcomeOnShare=false)*

# TRANSLATION

### **Ṣaḍaṅga Yoga’s Prāṇāyāma — The Kashmiri** Somanātha’s **Tradition**

**Meaning of Prāṇāyāma (*****srog rtsol*****):**  
In other tantric traditions, *prāṇa* (*srog*) is described as the breath flowing through the right nostril, and *āyāma* (*rtsol*) as the breath flowing through the left. Here, however, in *Ṣaḍaṅgayoga*, *prāṇa* (*srog*) refers to the breath flowing through both nostrils, while *āyāma* (*rtsol*) signifies the effort to gain mastery over the breath.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Body Posture:**  
Bend the right leg and press the heel firmly against the perineum. Place the left leg in front of it. Place the hands on the thighs or fold the arms across the chest.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Eyes Position:**  
Direct the gaze upward into the space about eighteen inches from the point between the eyebrows.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### **Prāṇāyāma consists of following practices**

**1. Cleansing the Channels through Vajra Breathing:**  
Without distraction, focus the mind on the inhalation of the *prāṇa-vāyu* with the syllable *Oṃ*, which signifies the Vajra Body; on the retention of the breath with the syllable *Huṃ*, which signifies the Vajra Mind; and on the exhalation with the syllable *Āḥ*, which signifies the Vajra Speech.

Gradually shorten the duration of inhalation and exhalation, while lengthening the period of retention. As a sign of accomplishment, the movement of the breath will eventually cease. Throughout *vajra*-breathing, keep the mind steadily focused upon the breath.

**2. Guiding the Vāyu into the Central Channel through the Gentle In-and-Out Breathing Method**  
After cleansing the channels through *vajra*-breathing, practise the *gentle in-and-out* breathing method in order to guide the *vāyu* into the central channel.

Before beginning, ensure that the breath flows evenly through both nostrils. If it does not, first perform the yogic exercise of *straightening the crooked channels* (inhaling through one nostril while closing the other, then exhaling through the alternate nostril while turning the head).

Once the breath flows evenly through both nostrils, expel the residual harmful air. Then begin the gentle in-and-out breathing, which consists of four breathing techniques (inhaling, filling, pressing down, and exhaling).

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**3. Binding the Prāṇa within the Central Channel through Kumbhaka (Rough Breathing Practice):**  
First, expel the residual impure air three times from each of the three channels. Then inhale and draw in the air through both nostrils, simultaneously restraining the rear *vāyu* with firm control. Thereafter, hold \[the union of the upper and lower airs\] through *kumbhaka* for an extended period. This directs the *prāṇa* into the central channel.

The sign that the *vāyu* has entered the central channel is that the movement of the *prāṇa-vāyu* (breath) becomes imperceptible, the duration of *kumbhaka* increases, and the *prāṇa* ceases, thereby cutting the stream of conceptual thought.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**4. Applying the Forceful Method (*****Haṭha*****) to Block the Passages of the Sun and Moon:**  
If the gentle in-and-out breathing and the *kumbhaka* rough breathing methods do not succeed in directing and binding the *prāṇa* within the central channel, then apply the forceful method (*haṭha*). Sit in the posture described above, and with both hands, press the waves (the pulsating veins at the throat).

As a result, at first various illusory appearances may arise. Then, through the application of the five factors of *dhyāna*, there dawns a luminous bliss and emptiness free from conceptual elaboration, appearing like an illusion. This practice should be learned in depth from the mouth of one’s teacher.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**5. Ejection of the Mind (*****Phowa*****) through Force (*****Haṭha*****):**  
Visualise your body clearly in the form of a deity, with the central channel running within it, green in colour above the navel and blue below. Its upper end pierces the cranial aperture (*brahmarandhra*), and its lower end extends to the perineum.

At the navel, visualise the red element in the form of the vowel *A*, shaped like a triangle the size of a grain. At the crown, visualise the white element, the syllable *Haṃ*, facing downward. At the heart centre, visualise the black syllable *Huṃ*, the essence of mind itself.

First, expel the impure air. Then draw in the upper *vāyu*, and as you press it downward, visualise the syllable *Haṃ* at the crown melting and merging into the syllable *Huṃ* at the heart. Next, as you draw the rear *vāyu* (lower air) upward with force, the syllable *A* below the navel rises and merges into the syllable *Huṃ* at the heart.

Then, binding the waves (the pulsating veins at the throat), visualise *Huṃ* blazing forth. Imagine the *vāyu* and mind united, ascending and leaving through the cranial aperture. While practising, do not allow the *Huṃ* to pass beyond the aperture; instead, seal the passage with two crossed *vajras*.

At the moment of death, release the two crossed *vajras* and project the *Huṃ* into the sky, binding the waves firmly and sustaining the practice for a long time. Ultimately, as space and awareness merge into one, the supreme *siddhi* is attained.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Result:**  
All the karmic *vāyus* will subside, and only the *vāyu* of wisdom will continue to flow. As signs of this, the skin and flesh at the point between the eyebrows will throb and feel an itching sensation, and an intense heat and burning will arise at the crown of the head. The *śūnyatā-bimba* (image of emptiness) will become ever clearer and more distinct.
