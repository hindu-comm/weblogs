### **Translator’s Note**

These two breathing methods are part of the prāṇāyāma practice of Ṣaḍaṅga Yoga (the Six Limbs of Yoga), taken from the *Ṣaḍaṅga* instruction text of the Kālacakratantra, composed by Drigung Chungtsang Chökyi Drakpa (16th–17th century).

A general overview of the prāṇāyāma sequence according to the text:

- Cleansing the channels — **Vajra breathing** (exhalation, inhalation,
  and retention).

- When the channels are cleansed — practise **gentle “in-and-out”
  breathing** to guide the prāṇa-vāyu into the central channel.

- Once the prāṇa has entered — practise **rough kumbhaka breathing** to
  bind the prāṇa within the *bindu* of the central channel.

- If unable to guide or bind the prāṇa within the central channel
  through above methods— then apply the **forceful (*****haṭha*****)
  method**.

### **I. In-and-Out Gentle Breathing Method**

**Overview**  
After cleansing the channels through the practice of vajra breathing, the practitioner proceeds to gentle breathing — inhaling through one nostril and exhaling through the other — in order to guide the prāṇa-vāyu into the central channel. This method is known as “in-and-out gentle breathing.” The term *in-and-out* refers specifically to inhaling through one nostril and exhaling through the other. Since there are three types of breath — gentle, rough, and forceful — and this belongs to the gentle category, it is called gentle breathing. Its function is to help direct the vāyu into the central channel.

#### **Straightening the Crooked Vāyu**

At the beginning, the practitioner should first practise the Trülkhor of straightening the crooked vāyu in the body—that is, when the vāyu is not flowing evenly through both nostrils or channels. Sit in either the vajra or sattva cross-legged posture and bend the throat, pressing the chin against the chest.

If the breath is flowing through the left nostril, it must be redirected through the right. To do this, place the right hand beneath the left armpit and press it down with the left arm. With the left hand in a vajra-fist, close the right nostril with the left forefinger. Then, turning the throat to the left, inhale slowly and fully, filling the breath to your capacity. When you can no longer hold it, turn the face to the right, close the left nostril, and exhale slowly through the right nostril. This should redirect the flow through the right channel. If it does not, repeat the process a few more times.

If instead the breath is flowing through the right nostril and must be redirected to the left, repeat the same process with the positions of the nostrils and arms reversed. Once you have succeeded in bringing the breath to flow evenly through both nostrils, take a rest for a while. Let the upper air and lower air remain in their own resting places, and engage the intermediate air by expanding the belly outward.

Next, assuming the same posture as before, inhale slowly through both nostrils. Swallow the saliva and simultaneously press the vāyu down below the navel, filling it to your full capacity. When you can no longer hold it, release the air slowly and gently, without producing any sound. Repeat this practice several times — beginning with about seven repetitions in one set, and gradually increasing up to twenty-one.

### **II. Kumbhaka Rough Breathing Method**

**Overview**  
After directing the prāṇa-vāyu into the central channel, the practitioner should practise rough kumbhaka breathing in order to unite the prāṇa with the *bindu* in the central channel. It is called *rough breathing* because it is stronger than gentle breathing yet gentler than forceful breathing. It is called *kumbhaka* (“pot”) because, like a pot holding water, it contains the union of the upper and lower airs, binding the prāṇa-vāyu within the *bindu* of the central channel. In the earlier gentle breathing, the prāṇa was made able to enter the central channel; here, however, it not only enters but is bound there, rendered immobile.

**Method**  
Begin by expelling the residual harmful air. Then inhale and fill the breath three times through the right nostril, three times through the left nostril, and three times through both nostrils.

When inhaling through the right nostril, keep the body upright, turn the face slightly to the right, and bend the throat gently. As you fill the breath, bring the face forward; when exhaling, turn the face again to the right and release the air. Repeat the same process when inhaling through the left nostril and through both nostrils.

Next, draw the lower air upward \[toward the navel\] with force, uniting it with the upper air, and hold them tightly together. Bind the lower air, and when you can no longer hold it, release the air slowly. Then again close the lower opening, inhale through both nostrils, and press the air down gently—without excessive force. Bind the rear air firmly, and when you can no longer sustain it, let it out slowly. Continue and repeat.

**Unit of Duration of Kumbhaka**  
The duration of kumbhaka is measured in counts. Snap the fingers once after touching the knee with the hand three times. Completing this sequence thirty-six times is counted as one small kumbhaka. Doubling that number constitutes a middling kumbhaka, and tripling it makes a great kumbhaka.
