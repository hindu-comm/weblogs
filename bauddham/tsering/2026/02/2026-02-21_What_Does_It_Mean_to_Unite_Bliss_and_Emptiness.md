# What Does It Mean 'to Unite Bliss and Emptiness'?

In Anuttarayoga Tantra, it is said that the practitioner can attain Buddhahood within a single lifetime. This is entirely due to the power of the wisdom of indivisible union of bliss and emptiness. One may wonder what this great bliss and ultimate emptiness are truly like. How could a dull person such as myself possibly fathom them? Nevertheless, I will explain as I have been taught by my gurus.

This great bliss is more secret than the secret, more subtle than the subtle. Let alone directly realising it within oneself, it is exceedingly difficult even to understand it properly.

When one cultivates samādhi in which the mind rests one-pointedly and attains firm mental stillness, there arises mental pliancy. As this develops, it activates the vital air throughout the body, from head to toe. From this arises physical pliancy, characterised by a smooth and deeply pleasant sensation. As a result, pleasant feeling arises in the body and great joy arises in the mind.

However, such pleasant feeling and joy cannot compare with the great bliss (*mahāsukha*) of Anuttarayoga Tantra, known as *sahaja ānanda* (co-emergent bliss). The bliss that arises from mental and physical pliancy is common to both Buddhist and non-Buddhist traditions. By contrast, the *sahaja ānanda* described in Anuttarayoga Tantra is generated through activating the vajra body (its nāḍīs, vāyus, and bindus) during the completion stage. The difference between these two kinds of bliss is like that between sky and earth.

You may wonder what the secret lies in here. The secret is this: when the crucial points of the vajra body are activated, the movement of the vital air in the right and left channels ceases. The vital-air enter the central channel and begin to move there. This causes the inner heat (*tummo*) between the navel and the perineum to blaze forth, melting the crown bindu (the HAM). As a result, the vital air that sustains the movement of conceptual mind dissolves. Emptiness becomes manifest, and simultaneously the co-emergent bliss arises.

So what is meant by “to unite bliss and emptiness inseparably as one”?

It means the great bliss, the mind itself transforms into the very nature of the mind that realises emptiness. Just as we speak of “cultivating devotion,” where the mind itself becomes the nature of devotion.

It does not mean uniting bliss and its emptiness as though they were two separate entities brought together. If great bliss and it’s emptiness were separate from the outset, the yogin could never unite them into an indivisible unity. Nor does it mean merely applying an analysis of emptiness to the experience of great bliss—such as analysing that great bliss lacks inherent existence. If that were the case, the indivisibility of bliss and emptiness could not arise.

**Note:** Excerpt from Tsechokling Yongzin Yeshe Gyaltsen (18th century). Translation mine.
