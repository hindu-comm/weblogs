### Note

This section belongs to the *prāṇāyāma* practice of Ṣaḍaṅga Yoga and is composed in verse form. The text is attributed to Kālachakrapāda and was translated into Tibetan by the paṇḍita Somnatha together with the lotsāwa ’Dro Shes rab grags. The English translation and accompanying commentary are my own, based on a comparative reading of multiple Ṣaḍaṅga Yoga sources.

> །མི་འགྱུར་བདེ་བ་སྐྱེ་བའི་ཕྱིར། 
> །སྲོག་རྩོལ་ཡན་ལག་བསྒོམ་པར་བྱ།

> །རེངས་དང་སྔགས་སྦྱོར་བརྡའ་ཡིས་ནི། 
> །བུམ་པ་ཅན་དང་རྡུལ་མཐའ་ཡིས།

> །ཟླ་བ་དྲུག་ཏུ་བརྩོན་བྱས་པས། 
> །ཞག་རེ་རླུང་བཅུ་འགག་འགྱུར་ཏེ། 
> །ཉི་ཁྲི་ཆིག་སྟོང་དྲུག་བརྒྱ་འགག།
> །དེ་ཡིས་ལྟེ་བར་རླུང་གཉིས་འདོམ། 
> །ཡེ་ཤེས་གཏུམ་མོ་འབར་བ་ཡིས། 
> །སངས་རྒྱས་ལྷ་མོ་བཅུ་སད་ནས། 
> །ཁུ་བ་བཅས་པའི་བདེ་བ་འབྱུང་། 
> །སྟེང་དུ་འོག་ཏུ་གནས་གཉིས་འདོད།
> མ་ཟོས་བཀྲེས་མེད་ཟོས་ཀྱང་འཇུ། 
> །ཐུར་སེལ་མི་འཛག་བདེ་བ་འཛག། 
> སྒྱུ་འཕྲུལ་རྡོ་རྗེ་གསུས་པོ་ཆེ། 
> །ལུས་ཡང་ལ་སོགས་མི་རྟོག་བདེ། 
> །ཡིད་ཀྱི་མངོན་སུམ་ཞེས་བྱའོ།

#### I. Purpose of prāṇāyāma

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> དེ་ནས་སྟོང་གཟུགས་མཐོང་ནས་ནི། 
> །མི་འགྱུར་བདེ་བ་སྐྱེ་བའི་ཕྱིར། 
> །སྲོག་རྩོལ་ཡན་ལག་བསྒོམ་པར་བྱ།
>
> Then, having seen the image of emptiness,
> In order for imperishable bliss to arise,
> Practise the auxiliary limb of prāṇāyāma.
> ```

Then, after perceiving the image of emptiness (*śūnya-bimba*) through *pratyāhāra* and *dhyāna*, the yogin should practise *prāṇāyāma* in order to generate imperishable bliss (*akṣara-sukha*).

#### II. Posture

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> །རྐང་པ་གཅིག་གིས་གཅིག་མནན་ཅིང༌། 
> །མཐེ་བོང་ནང་བཅུག་ཁུ་ཚུར་བསྡམ།
>
> With one leg pressing upon the other,
> Form a fist, the thumb enclosed within.
> ```

As for the leg posture, one may either assume the vajra cross-legged posture (*vajra-paryaṅka*), or press one heel against the perineum and place the other foot on top or in front. The hands are formed into a vajra-fist, with the thumbs enclosed.

#### III. Object of Focus

> published
>
> ``` text
> །ཡུལ་ནི་རྩ་གསུམ་རླུང་ཡིན་ནོ།
>
> The object is the three channels and the vital air.
> ```

The object of focus is the three channels and the vital air.

#### IV. When to practise

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> །འདུ་བྱེད་ཕུང་པོའི་རླུང་རྒྱུའི་དུས།
>
> When the vital air of the aggregate of formations moves.
> ```

Commence the practice when the breath flows through the right nostril.

#### V. Gaze

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> ལྟ་སྟངས་བདུད་རྩི་འཁྱིལ་པའོ།
>
> The manner of gaze is the whirling of amṛta.
> ```

The position of the eyes should be the amṛta whorl gaze (*amṛta-kuṇḍalī-dṛṣṭi*)— looking straight ahead through the point between the eyebrows.

#### VI. Breathing methods

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> །ཕྱི་རུ་དང་པོ་རླུང་གཏོར་ནས། 
> །ནང་དུ་མྱུར་དང་རྣམ་སྡུད་དང༌། 
> །རེངས་དང་སྔགས་སྦྱོར་བརྡའ་ཡིས་ནི། 
> །བུམ་པ་ཅན་དང་རྡུལ་མཐའ་ཡིས། 
> །རླུང་གཉིས་དབུས་སུ་རབ་བཅུག་ནས། 
> །རླུང་བཅུ་རབ་ཏུ་ཞི་བར་བྱ། 
> རྡོ་རྗེ་མེ་ལ་སྲོག་ཞུགས་པས།
> བདེ་བ་ངེས་པར་སྐྱེ་བར་འགྱུར།
>
> First, expel the breath outward;
> Then swiftly draw it in and fill it.
> By directing the two vital airs into the central channel,
> Through stambhaka and vajra-japa,
> Kumbhaka and recaka,
> The ten vāyus are pacified.
> As the prāṇa enters the vajra-fire,
> Bliss will certainly arise.
> ```

First, the residual and harmful air is expelled. One then inhales and fills the breath. Thereafter, by employing breathing techniques such as vajra breathing—exhalation, inhalation, and retention accompanied respectively by the mantras *Aḥ*, *Oṃ*, and *Hūṃ*. Together with the four essential breathing methods (*pūraka*, *recaka*, *kumbhaka*, and *stambhaka*), the airs of the two side channels are directed into the central channel. This pacifies the ten *vāyus*: the five primary and the five secondary *vāyus*.

As the prāṇa (vital air) enters the fire of primordial wisdom, bliss will certainly arise.

#### VII. Cessation of karmic air

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> །ཟླ་བ་དྲུག་ཏུ་བརྩོན་བྱས་པས། 
> །ཞག་རེ་རླུང་བཅུ་འགག་འགྱུར་ཏེ། 
> །ཉི་ཁྲི་ཆིག་སྟོང་དྲུག་བརྒྱ་འགག།
>
> By practising diligently for six months,
> Each day the ten vital airs will cease;
> In all, twenty-one thousand six hundred [breaths] will cease.
> ```

By practising *prāṇāyāma* diligently for six months, ten karmic *vāyus* cease each day. In total, the gross karmic air corresponding to twenty-one thousand six hundred breaths (the number of breaths that flow in a twenty-four-hour period) are brought to cessation.

#### VIII. Blazing of Tummo and dripping of bodhicitta

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> །དེ་ཡིས་ལྟེ་བར་རླུང་གཉིས་འདོམ། 
> །ཡེ་ཤེས་གཏུམ་མོ་འབར་བ་ཡིས། 
> །སངས་རྒྱས་ལྷ་མོ་བཅུ་སད་ནས། 
> །ཁུ་བ་བཅས་པའི་བདེ་བ་འབྱུང་། 
> །སྟེང་དུ་འོག་ཏུ་གནས་གཉིས་འདོད།
>
> Through this, the two vāyus gather at the navel.
> As the Tummo of primordial wisdom blazes forth,
> The ten female Buddha goddesses are awakened,
> And bliss arises together with the white elements.
> There are two regions, above and below.
> ```

Through the practice of *prāṇāyāma*, specifically *kumbhaka*, the two *vāyus* (*prāṇa* and *apāna*) are gathered and united at the navel. As a result, when the fire of primordial wisdom blazes forth, the ten karmic *vāyus* are transformed into pristine *vāyus*, symbolising the awakening of the ten female Buddha goddesses. This fire melts the *bodhicitta* bindu at the crown, which then flows downward, generating bliss at each nāḍī–cakra. The white bindu is located above, at the crown, while the red bindu (*tummo*) abides below, at the navel.

#### IX. Benefits

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> མ་ཟོས་བཀྲེས་མེད་ཟོས་ཀྱང་འཇུ། 
> །ཐུར་སེལ་མི་འཛག་བདེ་བ་འཛག། 
> སྒྱུ་འཕྲུལ་རྡོ་རྗེ་གསུས་པོ་ཆེ། 
> །ལུས་ཡང་ལ་སོགས་མི་རྟོག་བདེ། 
> །ཡིད་ཀྱི་མངོན་སུམ་ཞེས་བྱའོ།
>
> Without food, there is no hunger;
> Even when eating, the food is digested.
> Apāna does not drip; instead, bliss flows.
> The illusory vajra body acquires a great belly;
> A light body and the like arise, 
> non-conceptual and at ease.
> This is called direct mental perception.
> ```

Without food, one does not experience hunger. Even when food is eaten, it is properly digested. *Apāna*—which flows in the region of the lower abdomen and, if left uncontrolled, causes the white element to fall outward—here restrains downward leakage. Instead, the white bindu at the crown begins to drip downward. As a result, the body acquires a great belly, becomes light, and remains at ease in a state of non-conceptuality.
