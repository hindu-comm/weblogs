### Translator’s Note

**Name of the Technique:** This technique is originally and widely known as Mahāmudrā (*phyag rgya chen po*) in both the Sanskrit and Tibetan versions of the *Amṛtasiddhi*, as composed by Indian yogins. However, from the time of the Shangpa master Tsundü Singhe (the second lineage holder of the *Amṛtasiddhi* within the Shangpa tradition) and particularly with his composition of the *Amṛtasiddhi* *Thirty-Two Trülkhor* texts, this technique is no longer referred to as Mahāmudrā, but instead as Am-grol (*ཨཾ་གྲོལ*). In this post, I have retained the term Mahāmudrā in the title for the convenience of readers.

**The Tibetan phrase** ***ཁམས་འདུས་*** **(*****khams ’dus*****)** is difficult to interpret and translate with certainty. According to the *Amṛtasiddhi*’s *Instruction on the Five Elements* (attributed to Virūpa), *khams* (*dhātu*) in this context refers to the six inner *dhātus*—namely, the five elemental *dhātus* (earth, water, fire, air and space) together with the sixth, the consciousness *dhātu*. On this basis, *khams ’dus* may be understood as the coming together or aggregation of the six inner *dhātus*, which functions as a causal condition for birth in saṃsāra. This interpretation resonates with the Mahāmudrā technique of “closing the nine doors of the body”, whereby the gateways leading to rebirth in the six realms of saṃsāra are sealed, and liberation is effected through the upper door, the crown aperture.

The *Amṛtasiddhi*’s *Instruction on the Five Elements* further explains two modes of *khams ’dus*—the aggregation of the six *dhātus*. In the first mode, aggregation occurs above the seventh spinal joint from the top. Here, the five elemental *dhātus* give rise to coarse *kleśas*—ignorance, aversion, and desire—which in turn open the womb of rebirth in the three lower realms: animals, hungry ghosts, and hell beings.

In the second mode, the airs of the six *dhātus* gather below the seventh spinal joint from the bottom. This gives rise to subtle *kleśas*—bliss, clarity, and non-conception—which open the womb of rebirth in the three higher realms: gods, demigods, and humans.

**Closing the Nine Doors of the Body**  
(Skt. *nava dvārāṇi saṃyamya* / Tib. *sgo dgu dgag pa’i sbyor ba*)

This method is mentioned in the Mahāmudrā section of both the Sanskrit and Tibetan versions of the *Amṛtasiddhi*. According to these sources, the practice involves sealing the nine doors of the body. The two lower doors—the anus and the genital opening—are closed by means of the heel, while the seven upper doors—the two ears, two nostrils, two eyes, and the mouth—are sealed through the application of the throat lock.

The *Amṛtasiddhi*’s *Secret Instruction on the Five Elements* provides further detail on how the movement of the winds (associated with the six elements) through these doors gives rise to rebirth in saṃsāra. It explains that the direction through which the breath or vital air moves, determines the realm of rebirth:

> When the breath flows through the mouth, one is born in the realm of
> hungry ghosts.  
> Through the genitals, rebirth occurs in the hell realms.  
> Through the anus, one is born among animals.  
> Through the eyes, rebirth occurs as a human being.  
> Through the ears, rebirth occurs among demigods.  
> Through the nostrils, rebirth occurs among the gods.
>
> When the five sense faculties and the nine doors of the body are
> completely sealed, consciousness moves instead through the cranial
> aperture.

**Note:** This text is taken from the *Amṛtasiddhi* *Thirty Trülkhor* text composed by the Shangpa master Tsundü Singhe (Vīrya Siṃha, 13th century). The present translation is based on an annotated manuscript, which includes later-added marginal and interlinear notes clarifying the movements and postures.

#### Translation

Seated upon a comfortable seat, straighten the spine. With the heel of the left foot block the anus, firmly pressing the root \[of the penis\], \[thus\] sealing both doors of the channels.

Having slightly extended the right leg, grasp the big toe with the \[thumb, forefinger, and middle finger of the\] right hand, and turn it slightly upward. With the left hand, hold \[the right leg\] from the upper foot \[near the ankle\]. \[Then\] forcefully expel \[exhale\] the residual harmful air, in order to prevent the gathering or adhering of \[the air of the six\] dhātus.

\[Then,\] inhale the air from the right and left \[channels/nostrils\], and insert it into the opening of the central channel \[four inches below the navel\]. This eliminates \[delusional/*kleśa*\] craving and pain \[*tapas*\]. The purpose is to conquer the three channels. When the three \[channels\] become one \[the central channel\], Mahāmudrā is accomplished. With all the *Trulkhor* practices, the wise should understand them through the mastery \[of the breathing technique: forcefully blowing out the air, then inhaling through the side channels and inserting it into the central channel\].

Then, having bent the throat \[placing the chin on the chest\], unite \[*sampuṭitam*\] the upper air \[*prāṇa vāyu*\] and the lower air \[*apāna vāyu*\] by drawing \[the lower air\] upward and forcefully pressing \[the upper air\] downward, and hold \[the union of the airs\] for as long as one is able. This is known as breaking the \[movement of the air of the\] sun and moon \[the right and left channels\]. It is also known as the yoga of blocking the nine doors \[of the body\].
