### Translator’s Note

- I have planned to publish the *Anusmṛti* section of Tārānāthā's
  *Ṣaḍaṅga* *Yoga* (Six limbs of yoga) text in series. In this post you
  will find the Tibetan text and English translation of the first part
  of the *Anusmṛti* practice of *Ṣaḍaṅga* *yoga* text composed by
  Tārānāthā, with explanations in brackets based on Tārānāthā's own
  writings.

- See my previous articles if you want to read the first, second, third
  and the fourth ancillaries of *Ṣaḍaṅga yoga* (*Pratyāhāra*, *Dhyāna,
  Prāṇāyāma* and *Dhāraṇā*).

- I translated the Tibetan word སྒྱེད་ as དགྱེད.

-  ཐ་མར་དགེ་བ་ (propitious in the end/paryavasāne-kalyāṇam): Six
  ancillaries of yoga are divided into three groups: *pratyāhāra* and
  *dhyāna* as the first (propitious in the beginning/*ādau kalyāṇam*),
  *prānāyāma* and *dhāraṇā* as the middle (propitious in the
  middle/*madhye-kalyāṇam*), and *anusmṛti* and *Samādhi* as the last
  (propitious in the end/*paryavasāne-kalyāṇam*). These three groups are
  also categorized as body, speech and mind.

Third, *Anusmṛti* and *Samādhi* are the two yoga angas that are propitious in the end (paryavasāne-kalyāṇam).

> ``` text
> རྗེས་དྲན་དངོས་སྒོམ་ཚུལ། 
> །ལུས་སྦྱོར་རླུང་གི་རྩོལ་སོགས་མ་ལྟོས་ཏེ། 
> །འཁོར་ལོ་དྲུག་གི་རྩ་མདུད་གྲོལ་བ་དང༌། 
> །བདེ་བའི་རླུང་ལ་དབང་ཐོབ་གཏུམ་མོ་ལ། 
> །ཅི་དགར་སྤྱོད་པའི་སྐབས་ཡིན་རྒྱུད་གསུངས་བཞིན། 
> །ཉམས་སུ་བླངས་པས་ཆོག་མོད་(སྐབས་འདིར་ནི)། 
> ```

The actual practice of *Anusmṛti* is: without relying on physical yoga and breathing practice, one releases the channel knots (granthi) of the six chakras, gains control over the blissful vital air, and it is time to enjoy the tummo according to one's ease. Certainly one can adopt this practice as taught in Tantra. But for now….

(But for now) the approximate (practice of *Anusmṛti* is explained) and its instruction consists of the actual practice and the auxiliary practice.

First (the actual practice of *Anusmṛti*) is practised taking into account that the practitioner has developed appropriate experience in pratyahara and *Dhyāna*, has trained perfectly in all the ordinary and advanced kumbhaka of *prānāyāma* and Dhāraṇā, and has developed experience in the mixing (uniting) of prāṇa and apāna vāyu.

> །ཇི་ལྟར་སྒོམ་ཚུལ་གཉིས་ལས་(དང་པོར་གསུམ)།
> ```

(The actual practice of *Anusmṛti* consists of) preliminary breathing practice and the main mode of practice.

First, (the preliminary breathing practice) has three: (1. Postural practice: stabilising the practice of uniting prāṇa and apāna vāyu). 2. Auxiliary breathing practices; and. 3. Trulkhor practice.

### 1. Postural practice

#### (XXXV) Thirty-fifth Round of Practice

#### i. Wrathful Lion posture

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> །དང་པོ་ཁྲོ་བོ་སེང་གེའི་འདུག་སྟངས་ནི། 
> །རྐང་པ་བཤང་སྒོར་སྣོལ་ལ་པུས་མོ་བསྲངས། 
> །ཙོག་པུ་སྒལ་ཚིགས་བསྲངས་ཏེ་ལག་པ་ནི། 
> །ཐབ་འགལ་བྱ་ཞིང་ཡང་ན་པུས་བཙུགས་ནས། 
> །སྐེད་པ་བསྲངས་ཏེ་ལག་གཉིས་བརླ་སྤོམ་ཕྲའི། 
> །མཚམས་སུ་གཏད་ཅེས་གཞན་བཤད་གང་ལྟར་ཡང་། 
> །མཇིང་པ་བསྟུང་ཞིང་གཙུག་ཏོར་འཁོར་བསྒྱུར་གྱི། 
> །ལྟ་སྟངས་བྱ་ལ་མདུན་རྩ་གནད་བསྣུན་ཡིན།
> ```

Sit in a squatting position by bringing your legs together at the buttocks and straightening (lifting) your knees. Straighten your spine and place your hands under your opposite armpits. Alternatively, as others have taught, place your knees on the floor, straighten your waist and place your hands on the middle of your thighs. Either way, the neck should be contracted (shortened) and the gaze should be directed upwards at the level of the crown of the head. This will hit the vital point of the nadi facing forward.

> ```

Alternatively, place your knees and elbows on the ground or sit with your legs crossed. Straighten your waist and place your hand on each side (right and left) of your hips, bending your throat. This will hit the vital point of the Nāḍīs facing backwards.

#### (ii). Semi-wrathful Elephant posture

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> །ཞི་(མ)ཁྲོ་གླང་པོ་ཆེ་ཡི་འདུག་སྟངས་ནི། 
> །པུས་བཙུགས་ལག་གཉིས་ཁུ་ཚུར་འགྲམ་པ་སྐྱོར། 
> །གྲུ་མོ་སྟེགས་བཙུགས་ཡང་ན་རྐང་པ་བརྐྱང་། 
> །མཉམ་པའི་རླུང་སྦྱོར་སྟེང་ནས་ཐུར་སེལ་ནི། 
> །སྟེང་དུ་འགྲོ་བར་བྱེད་དོ་(ཡང་སྐྱིལ་ཀྲུང་)། 
> ```

Place your knees on the ground or alternatively extend your legs, fix your elbows to the seat and support your jaw with your hands. This will help the upward movement of apāna vāyu through the equalising breathing practice.

> published
>
> ``` text
> ཡང་སྐྱིལ་ཀྲུང་། 
> །ལུས་ལྟག་སྒྱེད་(དགྱེད)་དུ་བྱེད་ལ་ལག་གཉིས་ཀྱི། 
> །རྡོ་རྗེ་ཁུ་ཚུར་སྐེད་པའི་རྒྱབ་ནས་བརྟེན།
> ```

Alternatively, in a cross-legged position, bend your (upper) body and neck backwards and support your body from the back of your waist.

> published

> ``` text
> །ཡང་ན་གན་རྐྱལ་རྐང་གཉིས་ཅུང་ཟད་བརྐྱང་། 
> །པདྨའི་ཁ་སྦྱོར་ཁུ་ཚུར་སྙིང་གར་བསྣོལ། 
> །སྲོག་ཐུར་འདྲེན་ལ་ལྟ་སྟངས་བདུད་རྩི་འཁྱིལ།
> ```

Alternatively, while lying on your back, extend your legs slightly into a lotus (position\*) and cross your vajra fist over your chest. Draw the prāṇa and apāna vāyu and keep the eyes in an amrita whorl gaze (looking straight ahead at the level of the spot between your eyebrows).

#### (iii). Peaceful Rishi posture

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> །ཞི་བ་དྲང་སྲོང་འདུག་སྟངས་སེང་གེ་ཡི། 
> །འདུག་སྟངས་འདྲ་བ་ཁུ་ཚུར་སྙིང་ཁར་བསྣོལ། 
> །ཡང་ན་ཙོག་པུ་རྟིང་བཏེག་ཁུ་ཚུར་བསྣོལ། 
> །རྣ་བཟུང་(གཞན་ཡང་བུས་མོ་སར་འཛུགས་ནས)།
> ```

(Rishi posture) is similar to the lion posture. However, the fists are crossed over the chest. Alternatively sit in a squatting posture, heels up, fist crossed (against the chest) and ears held (with opposite hands\*).

> གཞན་ཡང་བུས་མོ་སར་འཛུགས་ནས། 
> །ལག་གཉིས་ཁུ་ཚུར་སྙིང་ཁར་བསྙོལ། 
> །ཡང་ན་ཟླུམ་པོའི་རྐང་སྟབས་ཁུ་ཚུར་ནི། 
> །བརླ་རྩ་བཙུགས་ལ་ལྟ་སྟངས་ཐུར་དུ་བལྟ།
> ```

Alternatively, put your knees on the ground and cross your fists over your chest. Alternatively, in the Maṇḍalapadam posture, fix your fists at the root of your thighs and gaze downward.

**Taranatha’s Note**: The first of the series of different postures is derived from Choegyal Choglay Namgyal and the next **alternative** postures are based on Drupchen Yumo and Kunpang Chenpo's students such as Latoe-pa Wangyal.
