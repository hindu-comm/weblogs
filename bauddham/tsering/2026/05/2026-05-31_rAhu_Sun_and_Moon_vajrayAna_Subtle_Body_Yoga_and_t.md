### **Translator’s Note**

This Hindu tale of the churning of the ocean is retold in many Tibetan texts. Within this narrative, it explains how hostility arose between the demigod Rāhu, the maker of eclipses, and the sun and moon.

These hostile dynamics are also applied in Vajrayāna subtle body yoga, where Rāhu becomes an indispensable ally of the yogi, devouring the inner sun and moon of the subtle body. In this context, the central channel itself is referred to as the “channel of Rāhu.”

Jamyang Loter Wangpo (19th–20th century) explains what “Rāhu devouring the sun and moon” means within the subtle body system:

> “This is called ‘the sun and moon, the four pairs, being devoured by
> Rāhu.’
>
> The two (right and left) channels are the sun and moon of the
> channels.  
> The two breaths, incoming and outgoing, are the sun and moon of the
> airs.  
> The two syllables, *a* (at the navel) and *haṃ* (at the crown), are
> the sun and moon of the syllables.  
> The two elements, white and red, are the sun and moon of the bindu.
>
> When these four pairs dissolve into the central channel, this is
> called ‘being devoured by Rāhu.’”

The same text also describes the outer Rāhu and the inner, subtle-body Rāhu, the latter being identified with the central channel, which extends from the crown to below the navel:

> According to the Kālacakra Tantra: externally, the head of Rāhu is
> water, the tail is fire, and the body in between is clear like
> crystal. In the same way, internally, the head of the central channel
> is the white element, the bodhicitta bindu, the syllable *haṃ* \[at
> the crown\]; the lower end is the red element, the short *a*,
> associated with tummo \[at the navel\]; and in between, the prāṇa-vāyu
> is clear like space. Therefore, this is called the inner Rāhu.

The three channels that run upward are distinguished at the guhya-cakra. The three channels above this point are identified as the channels of the sun, moon, and Rāhu, rather than the lower channels below the guhya-cakra. The same text presents these three as three tips, like a trident, through which water, fire, and space flow respectively through the channels of the moon, sun, and Rāhu.

> “There are three upward tips: the tip of Rāhu abides in the centre;
> the tip of the sun is on the right; the tip of the moon is on the
> left. Water, fire, and space circulate.”

## Churning of the Ocean

In the tale of the churning of the ocean, there once fell a rain of poison from the sky, causing harm to sentient beings. At that time, under the instruction of Brahmā, the gods gathered on one side and the anti-gods (asuras) on the other.

The lord Viṣṇu took hold of Mount Mandara from above. The serpent Vāsuki served as the churning rope, and the mountain was supported from below by the back of a great tortoise. In this way, they churned the vast ocean of milk.

Then there arose a being of deadly poison called *kālakūṭa*. When Viṣṇu swallowed it, he lost consciousness, fell to the ground, and lay unable to speak. Śiva (Maheśvara) drew it out and placed it in his own throat. As a result, his throat turned blue (Nīlakaṇṭha).

Then, when the crescent moon arose, Śiva (Maheśvara) took it and placed it upon his head. Then the glorious Śrī arose, and Viṣṇu took her. Then horses, the heavenly tree (*pārijāta*), and other things arose, and Indra, together with other gods, took them as they saw fit.

Then four exceedingly beautiful women arose. When they were asked, “Who are you?”, they replied: “I am called wine. If I am drunk, intoxication, fainting, pride, and various such transformations will arise.” When this was said, the demigods (asuras), being afraid, did not drink it, and thus came to be known as non-gods. But the gods drank it, and thus came to be known as gods.

Then, when the nectar (*amṛta*) arose, the demigods (asuras) took away eight great vessels filled with it. The gods became greatly dejected. At that time, Viṣṇu transformed himself into an exceedingly beautiful woman and went there, while Indra, in an invisible form, remained in the sky. Then the demigods thought, “This is our woman,” and set her to guard the nectar, while they themselves went off to bathe. At that moment, Viṣṇu and Indra stole the nectar and carried it to the residence of Indra.

As they returned from bathing and did not see the nectar, they searched for it intently. When they heard that it had been taken by the gods, they thought it must be true, since the gods go upward into the sky.

Then the gods assembled in order to partake of the nectar. At that time, Rāhu assumed the form of a god, entered among them, and obtained a share of the nectar. The Sun \[god\] saw this and said to Viṣṇu, “This is Rāhu.” Thereupon, he cut off his head with his wheel. Nevertheless, since he had partaken of the nectar and it had reached \[upto his throat\], his head remains even now, while the rest of his body died. From that time, enmity arose between Rāhu and the Sun, and thus he seizes and obscures the disc of the Sun.
