### **Notes and Context**

This trülkhor set comes from the Cakrasaṃvara Hearing Lineage cycle, attributed to Marpa Chökyi Lodrö of the Kagyu tradition. It comprises six primary trülkhors and thirty-nine secondary ones. This post presents only the six primary trülkhors; translations of the secondary practices will be shared in due course.

In the Vajrayāna subtle body system, the bindu is understood to descend through the body from the crown and to dissipate, leading to illness and bodily weakness. These trülkhors function as a forceful method for reversing the downward flow of the bindu: first holding it to prevent its escape, then drawing it upward, and finally distributing it throughout the body. Accordingly, two of the trülkhors serve to hold the bindu, two to draw it upward, and two to spread it throughout the body.

I have consulted a few commentarial works to better understand the steps of these trülkhors, though they are not entirely consistent for example, the text by the 9th Tai Situ. In what follows, I will outline the underlying principles of holding, reversing, and spreading the bindu, along with the associated breathing techniques, as explained by Drukchen Pema Karpo (16th century). As I have noted elsewhere, Vajrayāna trülkhor consists of three components: body posture, breathing technique, and mental support. In Pema Karpo’s text, the mental support \[visualisation\] and breathing technique are explained, but for the bodily posture he gives only the names of the trülkhors, without detailing their steps.

> #### **1. Holding the bindu**
>
> First, allow the \[bodhicitta\] bindu to descend. Once it has
> descended, apply the method of holding the bindu. The bindu is held
> within the avadhūti (central channel).
>
> **Visualisation:** Focus the mind steadily, without movement, at the
> lower end of the central channel, visualising a white, shiny bindu the
> size of a pea.
>
> **Breathing method:** As soon as the bindu has descended, contract the
> anus and forcefully draw the lower air upward \[apāna-vāyu\]. Then
> press the upper air downward \[prāṇa-vāyu\] and spread apart the anus.
>
> **Trulkhors:** Lion’s play and Tigress Vomiting
>
> #### **2. Reversing the Bindu Upward**
>
> Once the bindu has been held, immediately reverse it upward.
>
> **Visualisation:** Visualise the bindu moving upward through the
> central channel like an arrow shot, and imagine it dissolving into the
> *haṃ* at the crown of the head.
>
> **Breathing method:** Forcefully draw the lower air upward and unite
> it with the upper air, then stabilise it in an upward direction. As
> the bindu is drawn upward from the penis to the navel, apply the sound
> *hi hi*; from the navel to the heart \[chest\], *hum hum*; from the
> chest to the throat, *ha hag*; and from the throat to the crown, *hi
> hig*.
>
> **Trulkhors:** Elephant’s poise and Tortoise contracting.
>
> #### **3. Spreading the Bindu**
>
> **Visualisation:** Visualise the bindu from the crown’s *haṃ* filling
> the entire nadi-chakras. As it spreads, the channel knots formed by
> the compressed channels are disentangled. Imagine the bindu, vāyu, and
> nāḍīs mixing together in a one-taste \[eka-rasa\].
>
> **Breathing method:** Hold the kumbhaka breath of union \[of upper and
> lower airs\], filling the entire body.
>
> **Trülkhors:** The Peacock’s Flight and the Garuḍa’s Flight.

**Analogies for Holding, Drawing, and Spreading the Bindu (Pema Karpo)**

1.  Being skilled in holding is like blocking the outlet of a reservoir.

2.  Being skilled in drawing upward is like drawing water from a well
    with a rope.

3.  Being skilled in spreading is like a woman releasing water across a
    field.

#### Similarities in Amrtasiddhi and Chakrasamvara hearing lineage’s primary trulkhors

The Cakrasaṃvara Hearing Lineage trülkhor contains six primary trülkhor and thirty-nine secondary trülkhor, whereas the *Amṛtasiddhi* contains ninteen secondary trülkhors, with twelve primary trülkhor. The three primary trulkhors from Cakrasaṃvara Hearing Lineage text namely Elephant Poise, Peacock’s Flight and Tortoise contracting show some resemblance with the *Amṛtasiddhi*’s \[mahā\]vedha and \[mahā\]bandha practices.

In both Elephant Poise and the *Amṛtasiddhi*’s outer \[mahā\]vedha trülkhor, the practitioner places the hands on the ground \[though in a different manner\], raises the buttocks, and strikes the buttocks upon the heels. Likewise, in both Peacock’s Flight and the *Amṛtasiddhi*’s inner \[mahāvedha\] trülkhor, the practitioner grasps the corresponding big toes with the hands, alternately lifts the buttocks and heels, and strikes them upon the ground.

Similarly, in both the Tortoise contracting and the *Amṛtasiddhi*’s outer \[mahābandha\] trülkhor, the practitioner wraps the arms around the corresponding legs from inside to outside and grasps the big toes, an action which the text states helps to draw the bindu upward.

# Six Primary Trulkhors

#### I. Loin’s Play

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> སེང་གེ་རྩེ་བ་སྐྱིལ་ཀྲུང་བཅའ། །
> ལག་གཉིས་བསྣོལ་ལ་རྐང་ལ་འཇུས།
> གྱེན་ལ་འཐེན་ལ་ཀློད་པར་བྱ། །
> དེ་ཡིས་ཐིག་ལེ་འཛིན་པར་བྱེད།
> ```

Assume a \[sattva\] cross-legged posture. Cross the arms and hold \[right\] leg \[from the ankle with left hand\] and hold \[left\] leg \[from the mid foot with the right hand. Then bending your left leg place it near the anus\]. Then pull \[the legs\] upward and release them \[and pull them again, do this three times\]. This helps to hold the bindu.

#### II. Elephant Poise

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> གླང་ཆེན་འགྱིང་བའི་འཕྲུལ་འཁོར་ནི།
> རྐང་གཉིས་བར་དུ་མཁྱིད་གང་བཞག།
> ལག་གཡོན་སྟེང་དུ་ལག་གཡས་བཞག།
> འཕོངས་དང་བྱིན་མཇུག་ལན་དགུ་བརྡབ། 
> ཐིག་ལེ་གྱེན་ལ་འདྲེན་པར་བྱེད། 
> ```

\[In a standing position\] keep a span’s distance between the legs. \[Then place the hands on the ground by\] setting the right hand over the left. \[In that position, with the head bent,\] strike the buttocks and the lower calves nine times. This helps to draw the bindu upward.

#### III. Peacock’s Flight

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> རྨ་བྱ་འཕུར་བའི་འཕྲུལ་འཁོར་ནི།
> ལག་གཉིས་རྐང་བའི་མཐེ་བོང་བཟུང༌།
> འཕོངས་དང་རྟིང་པ་རེས་མོས་བརྡུང་།
> ཐིག་ལེ་ལུས་ལ་ཁྲེམས་པར་བྱེད།
> ```

With both hands, grasp the \[corresponding\] big toes of the legs \[in a rounded shape\]. Then alternately strike the buttocks and heels \[on the ground\]. This helps the bindu to spread throughout the body.

#### IV. Tigress Vomiting

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> སྟག་མོ་སྐྱུག་པའི་འཕྲུལ་འཁོར་ནི།
> སྐྱིལ་ཀྲུང་བར་དུ་ལག་གཉིས་བཅུག།
> རོ་སྟོད་བསྙེགས་ཤིང་སྐྱུག་ཚུལ་དགུ།.
> དེ་ཡིས་ཐིག་ལེ་འཛིན་པར་བྱེད།
> ```

Insert the hands between the crossed legs \[on the ground\]. Extend the upper body and perform nine retching motions. This helps to hold the bindu.

#### V. Tortoise Contracting

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> རུས་སྦལ་བསྐུམ་པའི་འཕྲུལ་འཁོར་ནི།
> རྐང་གཉིས་བར་དུ་ཁྲུ་གང་བཞག།
> ལག་གཉིས་བརླ་ཡི་ནང་ནས་ནི། 
> ཕྱིར་ལ་བཏོན་ལ་རྐང་ཐེབ་བཟུང༌། 
> ཐིག་ལེ་གྱེན་ལ་འདྲེན་པར་བྱེད།
> ```

\[In a standing position\] keep a span’s distance between the legs. \[Bending down\] wrap the arms around the legs, passing the hands from inside the thighs to the outside, holding the \[respective\] big toes. This helps to draw the bindu upward.

#### VI. Garuda’s Flight

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> བྱ་རྒོད་འཕུར་བའི་འཕྲུལ་འཁོར་ནི། 
> རྐང་པ་འཐེང་འགྲོས་རེས་མོས་བྱ། 
> ལག་པ་ཕྱོགས་བཅུར་སྤྲུག་ཅིང་འཁྲབ། 
> དེ་ཡིས་ཐིག་ལེ་ཁྲེམས་པར་བྱེད།
> ```

\[In a standing position\] move the legs in a lopsided, alternating gait. Shake and swing the arms in the ten directions. This helps to spread the bindu throughout the body.
