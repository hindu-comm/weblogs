### Translator’s Notes

- This article contains the Tibetan text and English translation of the
  first half of the *Dhyāna* Practice of *Ṣaḍaṅga Yoga* text written by
  *Tārānāthā*, with explanations in brackets based on *Tārānāthā*'s own
  writings.

- *Pratyāhāra* (the first ancillary of *Ṣaḍaṅga yoga*) contains the
  first eight rounds of practice (to read the *pratyāhāra* section,
  click here for [Part
  1](https://open.substack.com/pub/tsering/p/pratyahara-sense-withdrawal-practice?r=19pwkm&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web)
  and [Part
  2](https://open.substack.com/pub/tsering/p/2nd-part-sense-withdrawal-pratyahara?r=19pwkm&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web)).
  The next *dhyāna* contains five rounds of practice, starting from the
  ninth to the thirteenth round of practice.

- The eleventh round of practice is on the ordinary and extraordinary
  sense faculties of the eyes and their visible objects (karmic object
  and luminosity/image of emptiness). The twelfth round of practice is
  on the ordinary and extraordinary senses of the ears, etc, and their
  karmic object and emptiness object.

- The relationship between *pratyāhāra* and *dhyāna* practices:
  *Pratyāhāra* is practised to attain the image of emptiness
  (*Śūnyābimba/*སྟོང་པ་ཉིད་ཀྱི་གཟུགས་བརྙན). *Dhyāna* stabilises the image of
  emptiness that was attained in *pratyāhāra*.

- Alternative method (སྤོགས་ཆོག་/སྤོགས་པའི་ཆོ་ག): I couldn't find a Sanskrit
  equivalent for the Tibetan term སྤོགས་ཆོག་ (*spogs chog*). *spogs chog*
  is basically an alternative method to be used when the main method
  doesn't bring the result or benefit. Many *Ṣaḍaṅga yoga* texts advise
  to do the main actual practice first, and if that does not work, then
  use the *spogs chog*. That is why I have used the words 'alternative
  method' to clarify.

- Forceful method: The Tibetan term used for 'forceful method' in
  *Tārānāthā*'s other writing on the same subject is '*btsan thabs sbyor
  ba*', the Sanskrit equivalent of which is 'hathayoga'. So I have used
  the 'forceful method' in the parenthetical commentary.

In the instruction of *dhyāna*, the second ancillary of *Ṣaḍaṅga yoga*, there are two (things to keep in mind): 1. Essential key points of the practice and, 2. Meaning and purpose of practice of *dhyāna*. *1. The first (essential key points of the practice) also has two parts: (i) the actual practice of dhyāna, and (ii) achieving a result through alternative method of enhancing practice.*

## I. First, the actual practice of *dhyāna*

#### (ix) Ninth Round of Practice

> །གཙོ་བོར་མཚན་མོའི་རྣལ་འབྱོར་བསྒོམ་བྱ་ཞིང་།

Although *dhyāna* is generally practised in the place of both night (dark room) and day yoga (roofless blue sky). It is mainly practised in the place of (*pratyāhāra*'s) night yoga.

The posture and breathing techniques of *dhyāna* are the same as those of *pratyāhāra*. In terms of technique of the mind, the way the mind is kept fixed without movement in *dhyāna* and *pratyāhāra* are very similar. (Although there is a slight difference, the difference is that) in *pratyāhāra* the mind is (always) focused on space (even though the practitioner sees the image of emptiness), but here (in *dhyāna*) the mind is focused on the image of emptiness (instead of space).

The moment you see the image of emptiness, focus your mind directly on the image of emptiness without any conceptual thoughts. If there is no image of emptiness, practise like *pratyāhāra*.

(If the image of emptiness moves up and down or to the right and left) do not let your eyes and mind follow the image of emptiness. Let your gaze and mind remain fixed on the place where they were originally focused.

> །ཕྱག་སྲོལ་ཡིན་ལ་དམིགས་བསྐོར་དགུ་པའོ།

This is a month-long practice and is a tradition. This is the ninth round of practice.

### II. Second, the alternate methods

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> །གཉིས་པ་སྤོགས་ཆོག་བོགས་འདོན་རྣམ་པ་གཉིས།
> ```

Second, (the alternative method) includes two, (i) the forceful method of achieving the result of *dhyāna*, and (ii) the practice of enhancing (to bring about maximum benefit).

#### 1. The alternative forceful method to achieve the result (of *dhyāna*)

#### (x) Tenth Round of Practice

Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published

``` text །དང་པོ་ལ་ནི་དུས་ཞབས་སྙན་རྒྱུད་ལས། 
།བསམ་གཏན་སྟོང་གཟུགས་དྲག་ཏུ་སྒོམ་ཞེས་གསུངས། 
།སོར་སྡུད་སྤོགས་ཆོག་ལྟར་བྱེད་དམིགས་པ་ཡང་། 
།སྟོང་གཟུགས་ཕྲ་མོ་ཞིག་ལ་སེམས་དྲག་ཏུ།

*Ṣaḍaṅga yoga’s oral transmission by* Kālacakrapādaḥ (in Tibetan Tengyur text) says, 'Practice the image of emptiness forcefully in *dhyāna*' (using a forceful method of posture and breathing), like the alternative method as taught in *pratyāhāra*. In terms of mental support, focus your mind intensely (in the centre) on a very small part of the image of emptiness (as if your mind were entering into it). This is the tenth round of the practice.

#### 2. The practice of enhancing (to bring about maximum benefit)

> published

> ``` text
> །གཉིས་པ་ཉིན་དང་གཉིད་ཀྱི་འོད་གསལ་འབྲེལ།

Second enhancing practice to bring about maximum benefits (has two), associated with (i) daytime and (ii) night-time practice: the recognition of luminosity in sleep.

### (I) Day-time enhancement practice

### (xii) Eleventh Round of practice

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> །དང་པོ་སྣང་བ་གསལ་བའི་ཕྱོགས་སུ་ཡང་། 
> །སྟོང་གཟུགས་འཆར་ན་ལྟ་སྟངས་སྔར་ལས་ཀྱང་། 
> །ཅུང་ཟད་སྨད་ནས་ཞག་གསུམ་བཞི་ཙམ་བསྒོམས།
> །སྔར་ལས་རྟགས་སྣང་མི་གསལ་གྱུར་བ་ན། 
> །བོགས་འདོན་མ་རན་སྔར་བཞིན་ཞག་ཤས་སྒོམ།

When a (firm/stable) image of emptiness appears in the direction of clear vision, lower your gaze a little more than before (the original position of your eyes: gazing upward in the space at the level of your crown). Practice in this way for about three or four days. If (while doing this) the vision of the sign (the image of emptiness) becomes blurred, then it is not the right time to do the 'enhancing practice'. So (bring your gaze back up) and practise as before.

> །སྟོང་གཟུགས་བརྟན་པོ་ལེགས་པར་ཆར་ནུས་ན།

(Then) after practising with your gaze (slightly) lower (than the original position of your eyes) as before, if you are able to see a clear and stable image of emptiness, then gradually move your gaze directly forward and downward (achieving) a stable (vision of) image of emptiness.

(When the vision of the image of emptiness becomes clear and stable in the front and downward direction of your gaze, place a suitable stone or other object (not harmful to the eyes, preferably black stone, not too large or too small) in front of you. Then, from the direction of your gaze, bring the image of emptiness down on this object. Meditate with your mind single-pointedly on the image of emptiness, while at the same time seeing the object. If, after practising in this way, you see both the image of emptiness and the object (of karmic vision) equally, then this is the final mode of vision (of the image of emptiness). However, it will be more effective if you practise (become familiar with) it for a longer time.

(There are three probable outcomes of seeing the image of emptiness on the karmic object equally.) For some, (the karmic object disappears from their vision and) they see only the sign of luminosity. This is a ( wholesome but ) middling mode of vision. For some, both the karmic object and the vision of the sign disappear, and they immediately see the vision of luminosity. This is more beneficial and is a wholesome vision. For some, if the vision of the sign disappears and only the karmic object is seen, then it is not the right time to do the 'enhancing practice'; instead, they should practice on the main object of the mind (the image of emptiness).

(After becoming proficient in this practice) practise this bullet (pellet) of *dhyāna* (on larger and larger objects), seeing the image of emptiness (even) on an object as large as a mountain. Practice this for about a month. This is the eleventh round of practice.

#### (xii) Twelfth Round of Practice

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> །སྟོང་སྒྲ་ཐོས་པ་བརྟན་པོ་ཡོད་ན་ནི།

> །སེམས་གཏད་ལས་སྣང་འགགས་ཏེ་སྟོང་སྒྲ་སྣང་། 
> །སྒྲ་རིགས་ཀུན་ལ་སྦྱངས་པས་སྟོང་སྒྲར་འགྱུར། 
> །དྲི་རོ་རེག་བྱ་གསུམ་ལའང་དེ་བཞིན་དུ། 
> །ཤེས་པར་བྱ་དགོས་དམིགས་བསྐོར་བཅུ་གཉིས་པའོ།
> ```

(One who is adept at) hearing the sound of the empty, merge the karmic sound (such as the sound of the waterfall) with the empty sound, and focus your mind on these two sounds. The karmic sound (such as the sound of the waterfall) will cease and only the empty sound will be heard. Practice in this way with all kinds of sounds (such as the voices of people in a crowd) and they will all turn into empty sound. In the same way one should practise with smell, taste and touch. This is the twelfth round of practice.

Second, (the night-time practice of *dhyāna*) is very similar to (*pratyāhāra*). However, (in *dhyāna*) when you sleep, you focus your mind just on the image of emptiness and fall asleep without any conceptual thoughts. By practising in this way, you will be able to grasp the luminosity (of sleep) more quickly. If you are able to see the image of emptiness in your body, bring it to your heart (chest), and if you are able to fall asleep by focusing your mind on it, you will certainly achieve the luminosity of sleep. This is the thirteenth round of practice.
