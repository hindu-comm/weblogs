## The “Lotus Posture” in the *Amṛtasiddhi*

One foundational seated posture that repeatedly appears throughout the *Amṛtasiddhi* yoga manual raises a question: what exactly is meant by the “lotus posture”?

In Amoghavajra’s *Amṛtasiddhi* yoga instructions, most of the physical practices begin with what is simply called the “lotus posture” (*pad ma’i skyil krung*), rather than the “vajra posture” (*vajrāsana*), the signature crossed-legged posture of Vajrayana Buddhist traditions. Yet the text never clearly explains how this “lotus posture” is actually to be performed. As a result, it remains difficult to determine exactly what kind of seated posture the text has in mind.

To understand this problem more clearly, we first need to look at how Tibetan Buddhist sources themselves describe the “lotus posture” (*pad ma’i skyil krung*).

Male deity in vajra posture and the female deity in lotus posture

One description of the “lotus posture” can be seen in images of the male and female deities in union. In these depictions, the male deity sits in the *vajra-posture* (*vajrāsana*), that is, in a tightly crossed-legged seated position. The female deity, seated upon his lap, wraps her legs around his waist, forming what is described as the “lotus posture.” In this form, the legs curve into a rounded shape, with the soles of the feet lightly touching one another. This is one of the best-known Buddhist descriptions of the “lotus posture.”

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SQW2!,w_720,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6a79ca2d-1ff7-467d-959f-77e67ff0127c_426x640.webp)

In Lotus cross-legged posture

Another common description of the ‘lotus posture’ found in Tibetan Buddhist sources is the posture in which the right calf \[lower leg\] is placed over the left, allowing both knees to rest upon the ground. For further discussion of other important Buddhist seated postures, readers may consult my previous post, *“[Seven Important Buddhist Āsanas (Seated Postures)](https://open.substack.com/pub/tsering/p/seven-important-buddhist-asana-seated?r=19pwkm&utm_campaign=post-expanded-share&utm_medium=web).”*

It is also important to compare the “lotus posture” described in Buddhist and Brahmanical yoga traditions. The term does not always refer to the same seated form in both traditions. In Brahmanical tradition, *padmāsana*, now widely known as the “full lotus” posture, corresponds more closely to the Buddhist *vajrāsana*, since both describe a tightly locked crossed-legged seated posture, as shown in the image below.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!57jX!,w_720,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbb484d06-d0a5-424a-93a7-b014c5e01b7a_372x592.webp)

Yogi sitting in vajra posture Buddhist tradition, and the Hindu Yogi sitting in padmasana from Jogapradipika

This becomes especially important when we look at Tibetan Vajrayāna trülkhor \[physical yoga\] practices. In these practices, *vajrāsana* \[fully crossed legs\] is the main seated posture most commonly used, although other forms of sitting are used as well such as sattva posture. This can be seen in the Sakya Lamdré Thirty-two Trülkhor practice, in the textual instruction and illustration, where nearly all the physical practice are performed in vajra posture \[*vajrāsana*\]. Readers interested in this system may also consult my translation, *[The Thirty-Two Yogic Trülkhor](https://open.substack.com/pub/tsering/p/the-thirty-two-yogic-trulkhor?r=19pwkm&utm_campaign=post-expanded-share&utm_medium=web)*

The *Amṛtasiddhi*, however, presents a rather different picture. Throughout the text, many of the physical practices are performed in what is simply called the “lotus posture.” Yet in Buddhist traditions, the expression “lotus posture” does not necessarily mean a tightly locked, fully crossed-legged posture.

At first glance, one might therefore assume that the “lotus posture” mentioned in the *Amṛtasiddhi* refers to one of the more common Buddhist seated forms rather than to the full lotus posture. However, after carefully reading the instructions for the physical yoga practices, another possibility begins to emerge.

Although the text gives only limited clues, several instructions strongly suggest that the “lotus posture” described in the *Amṛtasiddhi* may actually refer to a fully crossed-legged posture — namely *vajrāsana*.

One particularly important example appears in the following instruction from the *Amṛtasiddhi*:

> “Place the feet in the lotus cross-legged posture. Then cross the arms
> behind the back and grasp the big toes.”

Although the *Amṛtasiddhi* describes this posture simply as the ‘lotus posture,’ the instruction itself strongly suggests that the legs are arranged in a fully crossed-legged position. In Brahmanical traditions, the same posture is formed with the legs in *padmāsana* \[full lotus\] and is known as *Baddhapadmāsana*, as shown in the *Jogapradīpikā*. In Vajrayāna Tibetan Buddhist traditions, however, the posture is performed with the legs in *vajrāsana* \[fully crossed legs\]. A similar posture also appears in the Sakya Lamdré Thirty-two Trülkhor tradition, where the seated position is explicitly described as the vajra posture rather than the lotus posture.

The same fully crossed-legged posture as depicted in two different traditions: in Vajrayāna it is referred to as vajrāsana, while in Brahmanical traditions it is known as padmāsana.

If this interpretation is correct, an important question still remains: what exactly does the posture termed *vajrāsana* refer to in the text?

#### Vajrasana \[Vajra posture\]

The following yogic posture is commonly known in Brahmanical yoga traditions as “Rooster Pose” (*Kukkuṭāsana*). Although the *Amṛtasiddhi* does not explicitly provide a name for this posture, the instructions clearly describe the same bodily form. Significantly, the posture is performed with the legs arranged in *vajrāsana*, a term which here appears to denote a fully crossed-legged posture.

A very similar posture is also found in the Sakya Lamdré Thirty-two Trülkhor tradition, where it is referred to as “Swan’s Wings” (*Haṃsa Pakṣa*). In the root text itself, the precise type of crossed-legged posture is not specified. However, one commentary explicitly states that the legs should be placed in *vajrāsana* (*rdo rje’i skyil krung*).

**Amṛtasiddhi**

> “Arrange the legs in vajra posture (*vajrāsana*). Having made fists
> with both hands and inserted them inside the crossed legs, generate
> all the strength and plant the hands upon the ground. Placing the
> weight upon the hands, lift the crossed legs into the air.”

**Sakya Lamdré Thirty-two Trülkhor Root Text**

> “In *Haṃsa Pakṣa*, after inserting the hands into the legs, the legs
> are bent and the weight of the body is placed upon the hands. This is
> the Swan-Wing Trülkhor.”

**Sakya Lamdré Thirty-two Trülkhor Commentary**

> “In the vajra cross-legged posture, cross the arms with the fists over
> the chest. Then bring the fists together inside the thighs and place
> them upon the ground. Straighten the elbows and place the weight of
> the body upon them. Raise the crossed legs almost to the elbows and
> remain seated in this way.”

This raises an important question: do both the “lotus posture” and the “vajra posture” mentioned in the *Amṛtasiddhi* refer to fully crossed-legged postures, or does the term “lotus posture” denote a different seated form that is not necessarily fully crossed-legged?

The question of the “lotus posture” in the *Amṛtasiddhi* therefore remains more complex than it appears. If the “lotus posture” and the “vajra posture” in the text ultimately refer to the same seated posture, why are two different terms used? On the other hand, if they do not refer to the same posture, then what exactly is the “lotus posture” described throughout the *Amṛtasiddhi*? The text itself does not provide a definitive answer, leaving the question open for further research.
