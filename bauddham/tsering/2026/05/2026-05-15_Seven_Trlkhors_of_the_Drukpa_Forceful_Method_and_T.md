### Notes and Context

These seven trülkhors are called *btsan thabs sbyor ba’i ’khrul ’khor* (“trülkhors of forceful methods”). For readers’ reference, *btsan thabs sbyor ba* is one of the commonly used Tibetan terms for Sanskrit *haṭhayoga*.

One of the most interesting aspects of these trülkhors is their similarity to several primary trülkhors found in the Shangpa Kagyü Amṛtasiddhi tradition especially \[Mahā\]bandha, \[Mahā\]vedha, and Amgrol (the Haṭhayogic Mahāmudrā). They also resemble the six primary trülkhors of the Cakrasaṃvara Hearing Lineage transmitted through Marpa Lotsāwa.

The present text belongs to the Drukpa Kagyu tradition and is preserved in the collected works of Pema Karpo as well as in the Drukpa Dharma Treasury.

[In an earlier post, I discussed the similarities between the six primary trülkhors of the Cakrasaṃvara Hearing Lineage and the primary trülkhors found in the Shangpa Amṛtasiddhi cycle of thirty-two trülkhors.](https://open.substack.com/pub/tsering/p/six-principal-trulkhors-of-the-cakrasamvara?r=19pwkm&utm_campaign=post-expanded-share&utm_medium=web) Similar parallels can also be seen here, especially in the first, fourth, fifth, and sixth trülkhors.

- The first trülkhor is not exactly the same as the Haṭhayogic Mahāmudrā
  technique, which the Shangpa Amṛtasiddhi tradition calls *Amgrol*.
  However, several important features are very similar: pressing the
  left heel against the perineum, extending the right leg forward,
  grasping the right big toe with the right hand, and keeping the spine
  straight. One notable difference is that the yogi here strikes the
  right heel against the left foot or ankle region, perhaps to apply
  deeper pressure to the perineum.

- The posture in the fourth trülkhor is almost identical to the
  \[outer\] Bandha technique found in the Amṛtasiddhi thirty-two
  trülkhors.

- The posture and movements of the fifth trülkhor are very similar to
  the \[outer\] Vedha technique, especially the hand position placed on
  the ground. In the Amṛtasiddhi, this hand gesture is called *liṅga
  mudrā* — holding the right thumb with the left hand — together with
  lifting the buttocks and striking them against the calves.

- The sixth trülkhor does not exactly match any posture or movement
  found in the Amṛtasiddhi thirty-two trülkhors, though some aspects
  resemble Vedha practice, perhaps the \[inner\] Vedha.

Readers interested in the Haṭhayogic Mahāmudrā (*Amgrol*), Mahābandha (*Bandha*), and Mahāvedha (*Vedha*) as described in the Shangpa Kagyü thirty-two trülkhor cycle may consult my full translation: *[The Sun that Clarifies the Practice of the Thirty-two Trülkhors of Amṛtasiddhi](https://open.substack.com/pub/tsering/p/the-sun-that-clarifies-the-practice?r=19pwkm&utm_campaign=post-expanded-share&utm_medium=web)*[.](https://open.substack.com/pub/tsering/p/the-sun-that-clarifies-the-practice?r=19pwkm&utm_campaign=post-expanded-share&utm_medium=web)

**Note**: I have not included the names of each trülkhor here. However, the names themselves show some similarities to those found in the six primary trülkhors of the Cakrasaṃvara Hearing Lineage text.

## Trülkhors of the Forceful Method

#### 1.

\[Bending the left leg\], press the vajra cord \[perineum\] firmly with the heel of the left foot, while holding the upper part of the foot with the left hand. Then place the right foot straight in front, about a span away. Grasp the big toe of the right foot with the thumb, index finger, and middle finger of the right hand. Straighten the spine and forcefully hold the kumbhaka breath. Then, with the right heel, press strongly against upper part of the left foot near the ankle. Do this nine times. At the end, massage the whole body and release the breath.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### 2.

Here there is no other difference \[from the earlier method\], except that a distance of about five finger-widths is kept between the two feet.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### 3.

In a squatting posture, cross the two legs \[with the knees upright\] and grasp the \[opposite\] big toes with the middle fingers of both hands crossed over one another. Then pull the lower body upward three times to the right, left, and centre. At the end, as before, massage the whole body and release the breath.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### 4.

In a standing position, place the two feet side by side, keeping about a span’s distance between them. Thread both hands inside the thighs and grasp the big toe of each foot with the thumb, index finger, and middle finger. Then, using the same breathing method as before, draw the lower body upward as strongly as possible and to the full extent of one’s ability. Afterwards, sit down and, as before, massage the body and release the breath.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### 5.

Stand with the feet evenly side by side, keeping about a span’s distance between them. With the right hand clenched into a fist, \[bending the body downward,\] place it on the ground about three spans away from the feet. With the left fist, grasp the right thumb in a pressing manner. Then, using the same breathing method as before, control the lower body, lift \[the buttocks\], and strike them firmly against the inner calves three times. Afterwards, sit down and, as before, massage the body and release the breath.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### 6.

Sit in a vajra cross-legged posture. Place the hands upon the knees and rotate the waist three times to the right and left. Then plant the hands behind the back, stretch the legs out forcefully, and \[by bending the legs\] strike the \[heels against the\] buttocks three times. At the end, proceed as before.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### 7.

Stand with the sole of the left foot and the right knee planted on the ground. Bind the palms together \[with the fingers interlocked\] and turn the palms outward. Then, strongly extend the body \[and the arms upward\] toward the left sky, tensing the body. Reverse the same to the right side. At the end, shake \[the body\] with the “horse-voice” technique, massage the body, and expel the breath outward.
