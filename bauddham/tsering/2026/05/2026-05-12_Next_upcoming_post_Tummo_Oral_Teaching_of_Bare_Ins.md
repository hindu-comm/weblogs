The next post will be on an important Kagyu Tummo text attributed to Phagmodrupa Dorje Gyalpo (12th century), one of Gampopa's main disciples. I hope to publish the translation in early May. The title of the text is 'Tummo Oral Teaching of Bare Instruction', written in verse form.

Gampopa had three main disciples known as 'khampa mi gsum' (three men from Kham). They are Phagmodrupa Dorje Gyalpo, Karmapa Dusum Khyenpa and Seltong Shogum. I would like to share here a short extract from Gampopa's question and answer text with three men from Kham in relation to the practice of tummo.

> When asked which spiritual teaching is the most profound to practise?
> A follower of the Atisha lineage tradition said, 'It is to practise
> the teachings of death and impermanence, the law of cause and effect,
> and love and compassion. One group said, 'It has no effect without
> understanding the view of emptiness. Some said, 'It is more effective
> to do sadhana on the personal deity. Milarepa said, 'Tummo and
> breathing practice is most effective. Gampopa explained, 'So if you
> gain full faith and conviction in the teachings of your own tradition,
> that is the deepest path. But if you practise tummo and mahamudra
> together with training your mind in compassion and bodhicitta with
> reverence for your guru, that is the deepest path. It is beneficial
> for oneself and for others. Someone asked, 'If that's the case, is it
> better to learn Mahamudra first or Tummo first? Gampopa replied, 'It
> depends on the individual. For young people in their prime, because
> their channels and body elements are good, if they start their
> practice from tummo, the signs of tummo progress will appear more
> quickly. Then, if you add Mahamudra practice, yogic experience and
> realisation will come quickly……….
