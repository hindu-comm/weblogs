## Notes and Context

The *bhru* breathing practice is a distinctive yogic method attributed to an enigmatic figure whose name appears in Tibetan sources in several variant spellings, including Jahar-pīr (ཛ་ཧར་པཱིར་), Jaha-vīra (ཛ་ཧ་བཱི་ར), and Jabhira (ཛ་བྷི་ར). He is described as a *jogi* and a *mahātapasvin* (great ascetic). On the basis of the names of his lineage successors—such as Brahmanātha, Māṇikanātha, and Vajranātha—it is highly likely that he was connected to the Nātha yogi tradition. At the same time, the presence of the title *pīr* (a Muslim saint) within his name suggests a possible Islamic element. This is further supported by a form of salutation associated with him in the guru yoga section, in which the practitioner kneels with the hands placed on the knees, raises the right hand to the forehead three times, and recites ‘nama Jaharpīr,’ which may reflect elements of Islamicate devotional practice.

However, there was another Indian saint with a similar name *Jaharpīr (Jahar-peer/*Poison*-*saint*)* in north-west India (11th or 13-14th) famously known as Gogga pir or Gugga pir (who was a prince and has some connection with Gorakhnatha). He is revered by both Hindu and Muslim communities and is well known in Rajasthan, Punjab, Haryana and Himachal Pradesh. Jahar-peer is also mentioned in the Sikh Gurbani: [Jahar peer Jagat Guru Baba](https://youtu.be/7lrgRiT4ksE?si=CGdT69_BI6GlSPPo) (Translation: Jahar Peer, Guru of the entire world). However, according to the Sikh, Jahar peer is an attribute of Guru Nanak in the Sikh Gurbani.). He is worshipped for ailments like snake bites in India. There is a secret instruction attributed to the Buddhist yogi on how to neutralise the potency of poison. If you would like to read more about Indian account of Jahar-pir or Goga pir, here is the link [Pilgrimage to the Abode of a Folk Deity](https://arrow.tudublin.ie/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1159&context=ijrtp).

Jahapīr is said to have had eight chief disciples (བུ་ཆེན་བརྒྱད་): Vajranātha, Brahmanātha, Devanātha, Śrīnātha, Jinabhira, Māṇikanātha, Paṇḍita Sokhya, and Amarasukha. His influence appears to have been considerable in Tibet, particularly within the Sakya, Nyingma, and Kagyu traditions. Jamyang Khyentse Wangchuk (16th century) is regarded as the first Tibetan lineage holder of these teachings, having received them from Māṇikanātha. Drikung Namchag Mebar (16th century) also received the complete transmission from Paṇḍita Vajranātha when he and his eleven students arrived in Tibet.

The present short text is drawn from Lelung Zhepé Dorjé’s *Secret Wisdom Cycle of the Ḍākinīs* and is accompanied by a commentary. The translation presented here includes both the root text and its commentary. What distinguishes this *bhru* breathing method is that, in addition to kumbhaka (breath retention), it incorporates a practice of sustaining oneself on the subtle essence of the sky—drawn in through the breath—while abstaining from ordinary food and drink. Through this integrated method, it is said that one may attain the deathless state. The text also includes auxiliary yogic techniques, such as blocking the four sensory gates and performing ninefold trülkhor movements, to clear illnesses, particularly those affecting the head and the sense faculties.

Several lineage transmissions of this teaching are preserved in Tibetan sources. For example:

**I. Sakya lineage (Jamgön Ame, 27th Sakya Trizin):**  
Gorakṣa → Jaha-bira → Māṇikanātha → Jamyang Khyentse Wangchuk → Öser Tsencan → Drupchok Wangchuk Rabten → Künga Döndrup → Sakya Trizin

**II. Mindroling lineage (Terdak Lingpa):**  
Jaha Bir → Brahmanātha → Māṇikanātha → Khyentse Wangchuk → Zhönu Tobden → Wangrab → Sönam Chokdrup → Kunkhyen Lama → Terdak Lingpa

**III. Lineage recorded by Jamgön Kongtrul:**  
Padmasambhava → Jaha-vīra → Brahmanātha → Māṇikanātha → Khyentse Wangchuk → Jampa Kelsang → Wangchuk Rabten → Zhalupa Sönam Chokdrup → Fifth Dalai Lama → Pema Trinley → Morchen Jungné → Chögley Namgyal → Jampa Ngawang Tenzin → Jamyang Khyentse Wangpo → Jamgön Kongtrul

### Expelling the residual harmful air through the nose

> Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when
> published
>
> ``` text
> །རང་ཉིད་ཀུན་ཏུ་བཟང་པོར་བསྒོམ། 
> །ཉི་མར་དབྱུང་ནས་ཟླ་བར་རྔུབ།
> ```

#### **Root text:**

Visualise oneself as Samantabhadra. Exhale through the sun \[the right nostril\] and inhale through the moon \[the left nostril\].

#### **Commentary:**

In a nonconceptual state, visualise oneself in the form of Samantabhadra: naked, blue in complexion, seated with the hands in the meditative posture \[the right hand resting upon the left palms with the thumbs touching\].

\[Yogi’s\] right hand clenched in a vajra fist \[with the index and middle fingers extended\], place the tip of the forefinger on the third joint of the middle finger \[from the base\], and use the middle finger to press the left nostril. While visualising that all illness and karmic obscuration are thoroughly expelled outward, forcefully expel the residual air through the right nostril.

At the end, when the breath has been completely expelled, clench the left hand in a vajra fist \[with the index and middle fingers extended\], place the tip of the left forefinger on the third joint of the middle finger, press the right nostril, and then inhale through the left nostril so gently that even the hairs within the nostril do not move.

In this manner, expel the residual air and draw in the air three times.

### Expelling residual harmful air through the mouth

> །ཀ་ལིང་ཀ་ནས་དྲག་པོར་གསུམ། 
> །ཁོང་འདར་ཤིན་ཏུ་ཐོན་པར་དབྱུང་།
> ```

#### Root text

*In the skeleton \[three-pronged vajra\] posture, forcefully expel \[the residual harmful\] air three times through the mouth \[kalingka\], so that the interior of the chest trembles.*

#### **Commentary**

Although there are various forms of the three-pronged vajra posture, follow this one here: bring the palms together at the crown of the head, with the elbows joined. Place the soles of the feet together, \[bending the knees\] spread the knees outward, and press the heels firmly against the perineum.

Then, through the mouth, with a strong “ha” sound, forcefully expel the breath so that the interior of the chest trembles. Do this three times.

By completely expelling the residual air in this way, it facilitates successful kumbhaka and helps prevent the arising of illness.

### Main practice: *Bhru* breathing method

> །ཅི་བདེར་འདུག་པའི་རྣལ་འབྱོར་པས། 
> བྱ་ལམ་ཟ་ཞིང་དྲག་པོར་རྡུབ།

> །དེ་སྲིད་ཚོད་དང་སྦྱར་ཞིང་བྱ།
> །སོ་མ་སིནྡྷེ་ཅི་བདེར་བཞག།
> །འགྲོ་བར་རྩོམ་ན་ཅུང་ཟད་མནན།
> ```

#### Root text

Seated comfortably in a cross-legged posture, the yogin eats the sky’s essence, drawing the air in forcefully through the mouth. Continue in this way until the abdomen is filled, regulating the intake in proper measure. Let the abdomen (*soma-sindhe*) remain at ease. When the breath begins to move outward, \[inhale a little and\] press it down slightly.

#### Commentary

**Posture**: Bend the neck so that the adam’s apple is slightly pressed. Turn the tip of the tongue slight upward. Let the teeth and lips remain in their natural position. Sit with whatever crossed legs posture is comfortable. Place the hands either in the meditative posture \[right hand resting on the left, with the thumbs touching\] or form vajra fists with both hands and rest them on the knees.

**Breathing method**: Draw the lower air slightly upward. While inhaling, imagine all the outer essence of the sky arising as a blue stream with the breath and filling within the body. \[Draw in the air in this way:\] Move the lips slightly from left to right and suck in the air \[through the mouth\] with a ‘hub’ sound. Hold the breath four finger’s width below the navel. Press the upper air down and let it settle naturally.

**Duration:** Although it is said that the breath should be held to a count of sixty-four—the duration of the intermediate kumbhaka—one count is measured by circling the five fingers of the right hand from the left knee to the right and then snapping the fingers at the chest. For beginners, however, one should begin with a shorter duration and gradually increase it over time.

**Continuation of the breathing method:** When you can no longer hold the breath, take a short inhalation and press the air down again. Finally, let the breath out through the nostrils so gently that even the hairs within the nostrils do not move.

#### Root text

*Practise this bhru breathing practice for three weeks, abstaining from other foods. Then, if you continue gradually for six months, it is said that you will attain the deathless state.*

#### Commentary

First, for one week completely cut off all intake of food and drink. In place of all food and drink, partake of the pure essence of the sky \[by sucking the air in\] and holding the breath. Then, gradually increase the period of practice from two weeks to three weeks, and so on. Once accustomed, extend it up to six months without interruption from other activities.

> །འགོ་བའི་ནད་རྣམས་ཀུན་བྱང་ནས། 
> །སྐྲ་ལི་ནག་ཅིང་འཁྱིལ་བར་འགྱུར།
> ```

#### Root text

*Through the method of blocking the four gates \[closing the eyes, nose, ears, and mouth with the fingers\] and the ninefold trülkhor \[three sets of three head turnings\], all diseases of the head are cleared away, and the hair becomes black and curly.*

#### Commentary

**Clearing Head Ailments:** If you wish to clear head-related illnesses, then during the main practice, as you draw in the breath, close the four gates: block the ears with the thumbs, the eyes with the index and middle fingers, the nostrils with the ring fingers, and the lips with the little fingers. Then, retaining the breath in kumbhaka, perform the ninefold trülkhor: turn the head clockwise three times, counterclockwise three times, and then bend it forward and backward three times. When exhaling, release the fingers from the ears, eyes, nose, and mouth, and visualise all head-related illnesses and discomforts leaving through these four gates. Repeating this several times helps to clear head-related illnesses.

**Clearing ailments of the eyes and other sense faculties:** With the fingers blocking the four gates of the senses, during exhalation release the fingers from the eyes while keeping the other sense gates closed, and imagine all ailments of the eyes leaving through the eyes; then finally release the breath. In the same way, apply this to the other sense faculties.

**Clearing ailments of other parts of the body:** As before, while exhaling with the four sense gates closed by the fingers, imagine an opening appearing at the site of the ailment and all the illness leaving through it; then finally release the breath.

**Clearing ailments of the abdomen:** At the end of kumbhaka during the main practice, sit in the skeleton posture and, applying the breathing technique together with the mental support, visualise all ailments of the abdomen being cleared away.

### Concluding remarks

If one now practises this with great effort, one will attain the vidyādhara of immortality; all illness and harmful influences will be completely purified, and countless qualities will arise. This completes the chapter of instructions known as *The Excellent Vase of the Life-Essence*, the instruction on the *bhru* breathing practice.
