## Notes and Context

These instructions on physical yoga practice are drawn from the *Amṛtasiddhi Seventeen Principal Practices*, attributed to Virūpa (Birwapa) and written by Amoghavajra. As is typical in Vajrayāna *trülkhor* systems, these physically oriented yogas consist of three components: *lus gnad* (body postures), *rlung gi gnad* (breathing techniques), and *dmigs pa’i gnad* (mental supports). The breathing method is incorporated only in the main phase of each sequence.

The author refers to the physical instructions presented in this post—excluding the breathing techniques—as *lus gnad*, which is generally translated as “key body postures.” Many of these postures are complex and bear resemblance to āsanas found in Haṭha Yoga and other Brahmanical sources. However, unlike in those traditions, these postures are not individually named within the sequence itself.

That said, in the *Amṛtasiddhi Seventeen Principal Practices*, each of the seventeen principal practices is assigned a name in both Tibetan and Sanskrit. Each principal practice consists of approximately six to nine yogic sequences, divided into preliminary, main, and postliminary phases. A close reading of the text suggests that the name assigned to each principal practice corresponds specifically to its main phase. Accordingly, in this post, I present the names of the physical yoga practices in relation to the corresponding main phase within each sequence. The numbering in the **Notes on Translation** corresponds to the numbering of each physical yoga practice presented with the Tibetan text and translation.

Some of these names correspond to breathing methods, such as *kumbhaka* (*bum pa can*), *pūraka* (*gang ba can*), and *recaka* (*re dza ka*). Many others, however, are unusual and difficult to interpret. I have therefore attempted to identify and reconstruct them as carefully as possible.

In the *Amṛtasiddhi* Seventeen Principal Practices, the fundamental breathing technique consists of uniting the upper air (*prāṇa-vāyu*) and the lower air (*apāna-vāyu*) by drawing the lower air upward and pressing the upper air downward at the navel region, together with the application of the throat lock (drawing the chin towards the chest). It should be noted, however, that this core breathing technique has eleven different variations.

### Notes on Translation

1. **Chariot’s Wheel**

The Tibetan name for this *lus gnad* (body posture) is ***shing rta’i ’khor lo*** (“chariot’s wheel”). The Sanskrit term is given in transliteration as ***dza ra na***. Tibetan sources are often inconsistent in their rendering of Sanskrit, and such spellings can be difficult to reconstruct with certainty. By comparing the transliterated form with the Tibetan designation, I suggest that ***dza ra na*** corresponds to Sanskrit ***cakraṇa*** or ***cakra*** (“wheel”), likely related to expressions such as *śakaṭa-cakra* (“chariot wheel”).

2. **Hanging Upside Down \[Holding\] a Stick**

The Tibetan name for this *lus gnad* (body posture) is ***mgo thur dbyug pa’i sbyor ba*** (“upside-down stick method”). The Sanskrit term is given in transliteration as ***u dza ni***. I have not been able to identify a corresponding Sanskrit form that clearly matches either the Tibetan designation or the transliterated spelling. For the sake of convenience, I render the term as ***Ujani***. This posture resembles the **Tapakarasana** illustrated in Jogapradipika.

3. **Channel-Cleansing**

The Tibetan name for this *lus gnad* (body posture) is ***rtsa stong ’khyil ba’i sbyor ba*****.** According to the author’s commentary, the term ***stong*** here means “empty,” that is, empty of impurities through the cleansing of the channels. The term ***’khyil ba*** is also explained in some contexts as purification or cleansing (***’khyil ba ni rnam par dag pa’o***).

The Sanskrit term is given in Tibetan transliteration as *sa ra na* (***sarana***). In light of the explanation “cleansing the channels and making them empty” (*rtsa bshal ba btang zhing rtsa stong du byed pa’o*), this may represent a misspelling of Sanskrit ***kṣālana*** (“washing, cleansing”). The text says,

> ས་ར་ན་ཞེས་པ་རྩ་བཤལ་བཏང་ཞིང་རྩ་སྟོང་དུ་བྱེད་པའོ།

4. **Binding the Nine Channels**

The name of this *lus gnad* (body posture) is ***rtsa dgu bcings pa’i sbyor ba*** (“binding the nine channels”). The Sanskrit term is given in Tibetan transliteration as ***rna ba na li pra bha dha*** (རྣ་བ་ན་ལི་པྲ་བྷ་དྷ་). Based on the Tibetan designation, this likely corresponds to Sanskrit ***nava-nāḍī-prabandha*** (“binding of the nine channels”). This posture is also widely known as **baddha-padmasana** (“bound lotus posture”). I have translated *srog shing dgu yi* as *srog shing dgug pa’i sbyor ba*.

6. **Caṇḍālī / Tummo**

This *lus gnad* (body posture) is called ***caṇḍālī*** (*gtum mo*, tummo). The posture appears to involve the sealing of the nine doors of the body: the two lower doors \[anus and genitalia\] are bound with the heels; the nose, mouth, and eyes are controlled through the throat lock; and the ears are closed with the hands (crossed over). The instruction to “press the Uḍḍiyāna” is less straightforward. **Uḍḍiyāna** the sacre site usually refers to the ears, in this context also I have understood it as referring to the ears, based on the overall logic of closing the sensory openings.

7. **Jālandharī** / **Jaladharī**

This lus gnad is known as Jaladhari or Jalandhari. Mahasiddha Jalandhari is famously seen in this posture as shown in the image. Tibetan term dra mig \[jala\] here refers to the channels.

9. **Kumbhaka**

This *lus gnad* is called ***bum pa can*** (“kumbhaka”). However, the Sanskrit term given in the text is ***pūraka***. On the basis of the Tibetan designation and the breathing technique, the intended term is more likely **kumbhaka** rather than ***pūraka*** (inhalation).

I have understood the Tibetan word ***rbe sha*** as referring to the muscles of the arms. The term ***sprul pa*** in this context refers to the navel, as in *lte ba sprul pa’i ’khor lo* (navel nirmana-cakra).

Generating all the strength and firmly bind the \[union of\] upper and lower \[airs\]. Then, placing the weight on the lower body, rise up as if standing.

> སྟེང་རླུང་དྲག་པོར་མནན་བྱ་ཞིང།
> འོག་རླུང་ཅུང་ཟད་ཐེན་བྱ་སྟེ།
> སྤྲུལ་པ་དག་ཏུ་བསྐྱིལ་བྱས་ནས།
> དྭངས་སྙིགས་འབྱེད་པའི་སྦྱོར་བའོ།
> ```

Bind the secret doors \[anus and genitalia\] with the heels of the feet. Forming the *sahaja-mudrā* \[arms crossed over the chest\], press the Uḍḍiyāna region \[ears\]. Straighten the spine and bend the throat, applying the lock. Then forcefully press down the upper air and slightly draw up the lower air, gathering the airs at the navel. This is the method for separating the pure from the impure.

Interlace the fingers of both hands to form the mudrā known as rdo rje phyed mgo’i phyag rgya, and place it at the crown of the head. Forcefully draw the lower-air upward and forcefully press the upper-air downward. By the method of filling the jāla \[channels with air\] bend the chest forward. From this, intense bliss arises.

Then forcefully draw up the lower air and press down the upper air, and gather the air at the navel region.
