### **Notes**

The “fourfold breathing technique” is a distinctive Vajrayāna Buddhist method taught in several Tibetan Buddhist traditions. In this method each stage of the breath must be practised carefully and in the correct sequence in order to cultivate proper kumbhaka breathing, which is an essential element of Dzogrim (*rdzogs rim*, completion stage) practice. It is also said that if the method is practised incorrectly, it may cause more harm than benefit.

It is taken from the collected works of Pema Karpo (16th century). I have translated the same breathing technique from another text in an earlier post—click here to read it: [Kumbhaka pot breathing practice](http://Kumbhaka%20—%20Pot%20Breathing%20Practice).

The four breathing techniques are inhalation, filling, pressing down, and release of the breath.

**How the practice should be performed is taught through these similes:**

> ལྕགས་ཀྱུ་བཞིན་དུ་དགུག་པར་བྱ། 
> །བུམ་པ་བཞིན་དུ་དགང་བ་དང༌། 
> རྒྱ་མདུད་བཞིན་དུ་བསྡམས་ནས་སུ། 
> མདའ་ལྟར་གྱེན་དུ་འཕང་བར་བྱ།
> ```

Draw in the breath like a hook \[hook-shaped air passage\].  
Fill it like a pot \[two third with upper air and one third with lower air\].  
Bind it tight like a knot \[air held immovably\].  
Then shoot it like an arrow \[projecting upward\].

**How it is done is as follows:**

**1. Drawing in the breath:**  
Without making any sound, the air is drawn inward through both nostrils. At the same time it is pushed downward to the area directly behind the navel.

**2. Filling (kumbhaka / pot):**  
Two-thirds of the upper air (*prāṇa-vāyu*) are pressed downward, while one-third of the lower air (*apāna-vāyu*) is drawn upward from behind, and the two airs are united. This is likened to the saying that two-thirds of a pot should be filled with water and one-third left empty.

**3. Binding / pressing down/retention:**  
When one reaches the point where it can no longer be maintained, the air below the navel is gently pressed downward. A short inhalation is then taken again and pressed down. This is called **binding** or **pacifying**.

**4. Releasing the breath:**  
When the air can no longer be held and must be released, it is expelled through both nostrils—gently at the beginning and the end, but more forcefully in the middle.
