## Notes and Context

This text is attributed to a siddha named Kṛṣṇacaryāvrata (Tib. *brtul zhugs kyi spyod pa nag po pa*, “the Black One”), associated with the Sakya tradition. It was written down by Jetsun Drakpa Gyaltsen (1147–1216). The title of the text is *“Instruction on Straightening the Crooked Nāḍīs, Vāyus, and Bindus, composed by Nagpo Utsita.”*

------------------------------------------------------------------------

#### Who is Nagpo Utsita?

As the text states that it is composed by “Nagpo Utsita,” one might initially assume that this refers to a single individual. However, a closer reading of the text suggests that “Nagpo” (Kṛṣṇa, “the Black One”) refers to Kṛṣṇacaryāvrata himself, who received this instruction from a siddha named Utsita.

#### Who is Nagpopa (Black One)?

Regarding the siddha known as Nagpopa (“the Black One”), there appear to be two figures bearing this name in the Sakya tradition:

- one identified as a disciple of Virupa

- another identified as a disciple of Jalandhara

The siddha associated with the present text is the latter, the disciple of Jalandhara. However, when comparing related sources, there remains the possibility that these two figures may in fact be the same individual. In at least one account, a siddha named Nagpopa is said first to have studied under Jalandhara and later to have travelled to Uḍḍiyāna, where he received teachings from Virūpa.

#### Who is the Siddha called Utsita?

A reader in the comments has suggested that the Sanskrit name *Utsita* may represent *Acyuta* (“the imperishable” or “the deathless”), and this is a very strong possibility, especially given that the siddha in the present text is described as free from ageing and death. However, I will retain my original interpretation below while keeping this possibility in view.

When reading Tibetan sources, two prominent siddha lineages emerge: that of Gorakṣa and that of Kṛṣṇa (Kanha, “the Black One”). These two figures appear to belong to roughly the same period, and there are narratives describing their meeting and exchange.

Gorakṣa’s teacher is Minapa, who in several sources is also referred to as Acinta (Acintya). Tibetan Buddhist materials make it clear that both Gorakṣa and Caurangi are disciples of Minapa. This is also reflected in a *Gorakṣa doha* preserved in the *Tengyur*, where Minapa is referred to as Acinta:

> Having served and venerated Caurangi with devotion,  
> The seed of the elements was thereby sown.  
> Acinta revealed the nectar of immortality;  
> Through this, Gorakṣa realised the nondual.  
> Wondrous indeed is Gorakṣa, the cowherd, king of the three worlds.

In the hagiographical accounts of Gorakṣa, the story of Caurangi is also recounted in detail how his limbs were severed, and how the guru Acinta instructed Gorakṣa to care for him.

There is therefore a strong possibility that Minapa is also known as Macchindra (Matsyendra). In this context, the Tibetan form *a tsin dra* (ཨ་ཙིནྡྲ་) may represent a variant or corrupted rendering of Macchindra.

In the present text, however, the siddha Utsita is said to have as his teacher Maheśvara (Śiva). This is significant, since in Vajrayāna and Nāth traditions it is widely known that Minapa (Matsyendra) received teachings while inside the belly of a fish, overhearing the dialogue between Śiva and Umā.

On this basis, it is plausible that the name “Utsita” may represent a scribal or phonetic corruption of “Acinta,” which itself may in some cases stand for Macchindra (Matsyendra/Minapa). If so, the siddha named Utsita in this text could potentially be identified with Minapa/Macchindra.

However, this identification is not without difficulty. There is also another siddha known as Acinta (sometimes referred to as the “Avaricious Hermit”).

# TRANSLATION

#### **Homage to all the excellent gurus.**

The siddha Kṛṣṇacaryāvrata, together with his disciples, set out from Jālandhara. All the yogins had gone on ahead, and while he was following behind along the road, he reached the outskirts of a great town.

Having arrived there, he overheard some people talking. They were saying:

“A few days ago there was a man here called Ucitta the Immortal. He travels through the sky; he does not grow old and does not die.”

On hearing this, he asked, “Where is he now?”

They replied that they did not know his exact dwelling place. Some said, “He stays on that rocky mountain,” while others said, “He stays in that small forest,” and they pointed out a nearby grove.

When he went to that forest, he saw a man seated in meditation naked, blue in colour, and with a body that appeared like ice.

Without examining who he was, Kṛṣṇacaryāvrata immediately bowed down and requested that he accept him as a disciple.

The man said, “I cannot tame you. Do not bow down before requesting the teachings.”

Then they had the following conversation:

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Kṛṣṇacaryāvrata:** Who is your teacher?

**Immortal Ucitta:** My teacher is Maheśvara, the disciple of Vajradhara.

**Kṛṣṇacaryāvrata:** Where is he now?

**Immortal Ucitta:** He is beneath the earth, in the ninth subterranean realm, within the hollow interior of Mount Meru, abiding in union with the goddess Umā.

**Kṛṣṇacaryāvrata:** When will he arise from that meditative absorption?

**Immortal Ucitta:** He will arise at the end of around two thousands Buddhas.

**Kṛṣṇacaryāvrata:** If that is so, do you accept that this teaching of yours is a path to Buddha\[hood\] or not?

**Immortal Ucitta:** Yes, I do.

**Kṛṣṇacaryāvrata:** In that case, please accept me as your disciple.

**Immortal Ucitta:** If that is so, then this is my teaching.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

And Immortal Ucitta spoke the following verse:

> Holding the vayus by means of a wrathful posture,  
> One abandons grey hair and wrinkles;  
> Freed from old age and death,  
> One becomes deathless like space.

**This instruction is called “straightening the crooked nāḍīs, vāyus, and bindus.”**

The crooked nāḍīs are the two side channels, the right and the left (*roma* and *kyangma*).  
The crooked vāyus are the two movements of inhalation and exhalation.  
The crooked bindus are the two, semen and blood.

To straighten these crooked nāḍīs, vāyus, and bindus means that \[the lower ends of the left and right channels, together with the vāyus and bindus that move within these solar and lunar channels, enter into the central channel (the channel of Rāhu). By binding or restraining the nāḍīs, vāyus, and the bodhicitta-bindu, this becomes the path to Buddha\[hood\].

**The stages for undertaking this practice are as follows:**

For a few days, massage the body and engage only in unhurried and relaxed activities. At the beginning of the practice, sit on a comfortable seat, and then:

**Preliminary**

- Take refuge in the Three Jewels.

- Cultivate bodhicitta.

- Visualise the guru above the crown of the head and generate devotion.

- Bring to mind one’s personal deity (*yidam*).

**Then, the main practice is as follows:**

*“Holding the vāyu by means of a wrathful posture”* (i.e. the meaning of the first line of the verse):

- Assume a proper cross-legged posture.

- Form vajra fists and place them on the knees.

- Keep both eyes open and direct the gaze upward.

- Press the tongue against the palate.

- Set the teeth together and spread the lips.

Then:

- Unite the upper vāyu (*prāṇa*) and the lower vāyu (*apāna*).

- Forcefully press the abdomen against the spine.

- With the hands in vajra fists, gradually extend them away from the
  knees until the elbows reach the knees.

- Tighten the entire body so that the muscles of the arms and calves
  become taut, and a slight tremor arises.

At that point, the vāyu is restrained (bound).

------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Signs and regulation of the practice:**

When performing such forceful *trulkhor*, if a burning sensation arises in the chest, or pain occurs in the head, one should release the exertion. If this does not subside, one should call out loudly and take some food.

One should perform this *trulkhor* approximately twenty-one times. Then, releasing the exertion, while body in relax recognise the mind as luminous and empty.

Thereafter, as before, one again continue the trulkhor applying exertion. Practising in this way, without relying on a fixed number or duration of sessions, there will come a point when, immediately upon performing the *trulkhor*, one enters into a samādhi of luminous emptiness. When this occurs, the first *trulkhor* has been perfected.

**In order to stabilise the above** ***trulkhor*****, one should also practise the following** ***trulkhors*****:**

- Form vajra fists with both hands. Place the left hand at the chest and
  raise the right hand upward into the sky.

- Then unite the upper vāyu (*prāṇa*) and lower vāyu (*apāna*), tighten
  the body, and practise as before.

- Then reverse the hand positions: raise the left hand and place the
  right at the chest, and practise as before.

- Then move the right and left arms in the manner of drawing a bow, and
  practise as before.

- Then raise both hands upward into the sky and, as before, tighten the
  body and practise.

- Then, forming vajra fists with both hands, cross the hands over the
  chest, and again practise as before.

In this way, whichever *trulkhor* is practised, if a samādhi of the inexpressible \[suchness, nonduality, free from conceptual elaboration\] arises within one’s continuum, this is the sign of having perfected the practice. If grey hair and wrinkles appear, one should apply saliva to them and massage. By doing so, they will disappear.
