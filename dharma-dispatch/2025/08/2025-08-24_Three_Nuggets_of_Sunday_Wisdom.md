## 1. The Traits of a True Mahatma

<div class="pencraft pc-display-flex pc-alignItems-center pc-position-absolute pc-reset header-anchor-parent">

<div class="pencraft pc-display-contents pc-reset pubTheme-yiXxQA">

<div id="§the-traits-of-a-true-mahatma" class="pencraft pc-reset header-anchor offset-top">

</div>

</div>

</div>

This famous verse is drawn from the *Niti Shataka* of the incomparable Bhartrhari. Using short and pithy adjectives, he fleshes out the essential traits that one should possess in order to be truly called a Mahatma. A notable facet of this verse is the brilliant use of situation-response.

विपदि धैर्यमथाभ्युदये क्षमा  
सदसि वाक्पटुता युधि विक्रम:।  
यशसि चाभिरतिर्व्यसनं श्रुतौ  
प्रकृतिसिद्धमिदं हि महात्मनाम् ।।५२।।  
vipadi dhairyamathābhyudaye kṣamā  
sadasi vākpaṭutā yudhi vikrama:।  
yaśasi cābhiratirvyasanaṃ śrutau  
prakṛtisiddhamidaṃ hi mahātmanām \|\| 52 \|\|

Courage in adversity, forbearance in success, mastery over the art of speech in an assembly, valorous in war, a desire to acquire (noble) stature, and assiduously devoted to the Sastras – these are the traits of a Mahatma.

## **2. The Consequence of Human Ambition**

<div class="pencraft pc-display-flex pc-alignItems-center pc-position-absolute pc-reset header-anchor-parent">

<div class="pencraft pc-display-contents pc-reset pubTheme-yiXxQA">

<div id="§the-consequence-of-human-ambition" class="pencraft pc-reset header-anchor offset-top">

</div>

</div>

</div>

This gem is from D.V. Gundappa’s renowned *Mankutimmana Kagga,* a collection of philosophical and contemplative verses written in Kannada. Here, he gives a poetic caution regarding the ultimate folly of human ambition.

ತನ್ನ ಮನೋರಥಂಗಳ ಚಕ್ರವೇಗದಿನೆ ।  
ತನ್ನ ಮಣಿಹಾರಗಳ ಸಿಕ್ಕು ಬಿಗಿತದಿನೇ ॥  
ತನ್ನ ಸಂಕಲ್ಪ ವಿಪರೀತದಿನೆ ಮಾನವನ ।  
ಬೆನ್ನು ಮುರಿದೀತೇನೊ! – ಮಂಕುತಿಮ್ಮ \|\|  
tanna manorathaṃgaḻa cakravegadine ।  
tanna maṇihāragaḻa sikku bigitadine ॥  
tanna saṃkalpa viparītadine mānavana ।  
bennu muridīteno! – maṃkutimma \|\|

The Human is running fast chasing the cycle of ambition, its satisfaction and greater and greater ambition spurred by such temporal satisfaction. In the process, he may wear a necklace of pearls, which, as he runs faster, may get entangled and suffocate him. His back may be broken under the weight of all the grand endeavours to satisfy his unending ambitions – Mankutimma

## 3. Be the Wise Swan

<div class="pencraft pc-display-flex pc-alignItems-center pc-position-absolute pc-reset header-anchor-parent">

<div class="pencraft pc-display-contents pc-reset pubTheme-yiXxQA">

<div id="§be-the-wise-swan" class="pencraft pc-reset header-anchor offset-top">

</div>

</div>

</div>

This ancient Sanskrit verse from an unknown poet is perhaps more relevant today than ever. It’s a terse guidance on how to separate sense from nonsense.

प्राज्ञो हि जल्पतां पुंसां श्रुत्वा वाचः शुभाशुभाः।

गुणवद्वाक्यमादत्ते हंसः क्षीरमिवाम्भसः॥

Just as the swan is capable of separating milk mixed with water, and drinking just the milk, the wise can distinguish what is good and what is not good in a person’s speech and accept only the good.
