The **Tamil epic** ***Silappadhikaram*** stands as one of the most significant literary works of ancient Tamilakam, offering a vivid portrayal of society under the three great dynasties — the Cheras, Cholas, and Pandyas. Through the framework of a *nataka kavya* (dance-drama), it weaves the poignant tale of the merchant couple Kovalan and Kannagi, blending romance, tragedy, and moral justice to reflect the values and social order of the time.

For those unfamiliar with the narrative, a brief outline follows.  
Kovalan, the son of a prosperous merchant from the Chola country, marries **Kannagi**, who hails from another wealthy mercantile family. The couple begins their life in the bustling port city of **Poompuhar**. However, domestic harmony is soon disrupted when Kovalan becomes infatuated with the courtesan **Madhavi**, leading to estrangement from Kannagi. After a brief period of cohabitation, Kovalan separates from Madhavi and returns repentant to Kannagi. Seeking a fresh start, the couple travels to **Madurai**, the capital of the Pandyas. Misfortune strikes when Kovalan is wrongly accused of theft and executed on royal orders.

Overcome with grief and righteous fury, Kannagi confronts **King Nedunchezhiyan**, proving her husband’s innocence. Realizing his grave error, the king and his queen **Kopperundevi** die of remorse. Kannagi’s wrath, however, consumes her — she curses and sets **Madurai ablaze**. Witnessing the destruction and the suffering of innocents, she is consoled by the goddess **Meenakshi**, who reveals that the burning of the city was divinely ordained. Kannagi then journeys northward to the **Chera kingdom**, where she attains salvation. Hearing her story, Chera king Chenguttuvan resolves to immortalize her virtue. He leads an expedition to northern India, brings back a sacred stone, and consecrates a temple for Kannagi, culminating in his performance of the **Rājasūya Yajna**.

The **chronology of** *Silappadhikaram* remains a subject of scholarly debate. It is important to distinguish between the period of the *events narrated* and the *composition* of the epic itself. Most researchers agree that *Silappadhikaram* was composed in the post-Sangam period (3rd–5th century CE), while the kings featured in it belong to the Sangam era. The traditional belief that **Ilango Adigal**, the author, was the brother of Chenguttuvan has been challenged by later scholars. Thus, while the composition likely dates to the 3rd century CE, the incidents it recounts belong to an earlier historical setting. Among the varying hypotheses, three distinct chronological frameworks have been proposed, each linking the epic to different stages in Tamil literary and political history.

#### **3rd Century BCE**

A few historians have attributed *Silappadhikaram* to as early as the 3rd century BCE, but this theory presents several inconsistencies. The epic vividly refers to three celebrated rulers of the Sangam era — Chola Karikalan, Pandya Nedunchezhiyan, and Chera Chenguttuvan.

In the *Arangetru Kathai* (Prologue Canto), Karikalan is praised as the monarch who journeyed to the Himalayas and engraved the Chola emblem, the **tiger**, upon the sacred mountain, defeating northern kings along the way. Similarly, the *Madurai Kandam* (Madurai Canto) extols **Pandya Nedunchezhiyan**, lauded by Sangam poets as *“Ariya Padai Kadantha”* — *the one who vanquished the Aryan armies*. The Chera ruler **Chenguttuvan**, in turn, is portrayed as leading an expedition to the north to procure a sacred stone for Kannagi’s consecration, triumphing over several northern rulers. Collectively, these accounts suggest **three successive northern campaigns** undertaken by Tamil monarchs.

However, assigning these events to the 3rd century BCE is historically untenable. This period corresponds to the early phase of the Third Tamil Sangam, when the Sangam poet **Mamulanar** mentions a Mauryan invasion from the north, but not any Tamil counter-expeditions. Literary evidence indicates that this incursion occurred during the reign of Bindusara, while Ashoka’s edicts later describe the Tamil kings as *“friendly neighbours.”* It is therefore implausible that Tamil rulers undertook successful northern conquests during the height of the Mauryan Empire’s power.

Furthermore, Tamil royal chronology places both Karikalan and Chenguttuvan in a later historical timeframe, making it unlikely that *Silappadhikaram’s* events occurred in the 3rd century BCE. The epic must thus have been composed subsequent to this period.

#### **756 CE**

Conversely, Diwan Bahadur V. S. Samikkanu Pillai proposed that *Silappadhikaram* corresponded to 756 CE, basing his argument on an astronomical reference found in the text. While the planetary configuration cited may align with that year, this claim requires corroboration through historical cross-evidence**.**

By the mid-8th century, Tamil Nadu’s political landscape was well documented: the Pallavas governed the northern region, and the Medieval Pandyas ruled the south. Copper-plate grants and inscriptions from this era form a continuous chronology, yet none mention a Pandya king named Nedunchezhiyan, nor any burning of Madurai, as described in *Silappadhikaram*.

Moreover, the 8th century marked the zenith of the Bhakti movement, led by Nayanmars and Alwars, who revitalized Saivism and Vaishnavism. In contrast, *Silappadhikaram* and its sequel *Manimekalai* portray a cultural milieu dominated by Sramana (Jain) and Buddhist traditions — indicating an earlier religious context. The absence of any reference to the powerful Pallavas, who were flourishing during the 8th century, further weakens this hypothesis.

Hence, despite the astronomical conjecture, the 756 CE dating lacks historical support and cannot be reconciled with the religious, political, and literary evidence of the period.

### **2nd Century CE**

This brings us to the third — and most plausible — chronological framework proposed by several scholars: the 2nd century CE. This era marks the closing phase of the Sangam period, a time when the Tamil country was ruled by powerful and well-documented monarchs. By then, the Mauryan Empire had declined, and northern India had fragmented into several regional kingdoms. It is within such a political context that the northern expeditions described in *Silappadhikaram* become historically credible. The internal Tamil chronology of the Chola, Chera, and Pandya rulers also aligns with this timeframe.

Significantly, *Silappadhikaram* records that the Sri Lankan king Gajabahu (Gayavahu) was present at the consecration ceremony of the Kannagi temple:

> *“Kudagak Kongarum Maluva Ventharum  
> Kadal Suzh Ilangai Gayavahu Venthanum”*

This verse clearly states that Gajabahu of Lanka attended the ceremony. The king referred to here is generally identified as Gajabahu I, who reigned over Sri Lanka between 114 CE and 135 CE, as per the *Mahavamsa*. Sri Lankan chronicles further note that Gajabahu returned to his kingdom bearing the anklet of Paththini Deivam, the chaste goddess — an unmistakable reference to Kannagi.

Additionally, *Silappadhikaram* mentions that a ruler named Nutruvan Kannan (Sata Karni) assisted Chera Chenguttuvan during his northern expedition:

> *“Vetrumai indri ninnodu kalantha Nutruvar Kannarum”*

The name *Satakarni* is associated with the Satavahana dynasty, whose kings ruled the Deccan from the late 2nd century BCE to the 3rd century CE. Several rulers bearing this name reigned during the 2nd century CE, strengthening the hypothesis that *Silappadhikaram’s* events occurred in this period. This evidence supports the view that Ilango Adigal, possibly living a few decades later, composed his literary masterpiece based on historical incidents still fresh in public memory.

### **Astronomical Correlation**

To further verify this timeline, I examined the astronomical reference cited in the epic — the same one used earlier by Diwan Bahadur Samikkanu Pillai — using the software *Jagannatha Hora* to identify a matching celestial configuration in the 2nd century CE.

The relevant verse from the *Katturai Kathai* section reads:

> *“Adi Thingal Per iruḷ pakkattu,  
> Azhal ser kuṭṭattu, aṭṭami nandru,  
> Veḷḷi vaarathu, oḷ eri uṇṇa,  
> Uraisal Maduraiyodu arasu kedurum  
> eṉum uraiyum unde, nirai thodiyoye.”*

In this passage, Goddess Meenakshi informs Kannagi that the destruction of Madurai was preordained — destined to occur on a specific day. The verse identifies that day as:

- Adi Thingal (Kataka month),

- Per Irul Pakkam (Krishna Paksha),

- Azhal Ser Kuttathu (first quarter of Krittika Nakshatra),

- Velli Vaaram (Friday).

In essence, the tragedy was foretold to happen during the solar month of Kataka, on a Krishna Paksha Ashtami, when the first leg of Krittika Nakshatra coincided with a Friday.

Upon analysis of the 2nd-century astronomical calendar, I found that when the Sun was in Kataka Rasi(corresponding to the Tamil month of Adi), Krishna Ashtami generally aligned with Revathi, Ashwini, or occasionally Bharani Nakshatras. The coincidence of Ashtami with Krittika was exceedingly rare. Notably, on June 19, 116 CE, an Ashtami did occur on a Friday — though under Revathi Nakshatra rather than Krittika.

This suggests that while the astronomical alignment in the text may not match perfectly with 116 CE, the broad temporal correspondence remains consistent with the 2nd-century setting — reinforcing the view that *Silappadhikaram’s* narrative and composition both belong to this era.

After several iterations and cross-checks, I was able to identify a rare celestial alignment on July 14, 130 CE that precisely matches the astronomical description in *Silappadhikaram*. On that day, a Krishna Ashtami occurred on a Friday, during the Tamil month of Adi (when the Sun was in Kataka Rasi), with the Krittika Nakshatra in its first quarter during the night. According to the epic, this was the very day when Kannagi set fire to the city of Madurai — an event preordained by divine will.

This alignment fits perfectly within the reign of King Gajabahu I of Sri Lanka (114–135 CE), as documented in the *Mahavamsa* and corroborated by the *Silappadhikaram* verse referring to his presence at the consecration of the Kannagi temple. It also harmonizes with the Satavahana chronology, in which kings bearing the name Satakarni ruled central India during this same period — one of whom is mentioned as aiding Chera Chenguttuvan in his northern expedition.

While further research may be necessary to examine other astronomical references in the epic for additional validation, the cumulative evidence strongly supports the conclusion that the events of *Silappadhikaram* occurred during the 2nd century CE, with the burning of Madurai likely taking place around July 14, 130 CE. This synthesis of astronomical, literary, and historical data provides one of the most coherent chronological frameworks yet proposed for the epic.
