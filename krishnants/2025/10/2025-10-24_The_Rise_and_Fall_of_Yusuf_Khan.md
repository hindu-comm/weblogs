Tamil Nadu’s role in India’s freedom struggle was a significant one.  
Even before the flame of freedom began to spread across the nation, this land had already seen great warriors who rose against British domination as soon as they set foot here—heroes like Poolithevar, Kattabomman, Oomathurai, the Marudhu brothers, and Gopala Nayakar.

At the same time, Tamil Nadu also witnessed some traitors who betrayed their own soil, aiding foreign powers and serving as mercenaries in their armies. One such man was Marudhanayagam, also known as Yusuf Khan or Khan Sahib.

**Initial years**

Marudhanayagam was born in Panaiyur, near Ramanathapuram. Though born a Hindu, he left home after quarreling with his parents in his youth. He joined an Islamic teacher, converted to Islam, and took the name Muhammad Yusuf Khan. This teacher was a wrestling instructor, under whom Yusuf learned various martial skills. Later, he joined the French at Pondicherry for employment.

During that period, Tamil Nadu was in great turmoil. European powers like the British, French, Portuguese, and Dutch were vying to gain control over Indian territories. With the decline of the Nayak rule, local chieftains (Poligars) had established semi-independent rule in their regions, while the Nawab of Arcot was trying to subdue them and collect tribute by force. It was a time when anyone who could wield a stick was seen as an enforcer. Skilled fighters were in great demand, and Yusuf naturally found work as a hired soldier with the French.

While serving the French in Pondicherry, Yusuf befriended a French officer named Marchand, and they became close companions. However, due to some serious crime he committed, Yusuf was soon dismissed from service. Humiliated and dismissed from Pondicherry, Yusuf left and joined the army of the Thanjavur ruler, Pratap Simha. There, he not only learned various forms of military training from a British officer but also mastered several languages. However, he did not remain in Thanjavur for long; he soon left and took up employment as a physician under one Muhammad Kamal in Nellore. Kamal was a relative of Dost Ali Khan, the Nawab of Arcot.

Through this connection, Yusuf gradually joined the Arcot army and rose through the ranks until he became a Subedar (commander). It was during this time that his true abilities began to gain recognition, and he came to be known as “Nellore Subedar” Yusuf Khan.

Around this time, the clouds of war began to gather over Tamil Nadu. When Anwaruddin Khan, the Nawab of Arcot, died, a succession dispute arose between his son Muhammad Ali and his relative Chanda Sahib, the son-in-law of the former Nawab Dost Ali Khan.

Sensing an opportunity to seize control of Tamil Nadu through this succession conflict, both the French and the British intervened. The French supported Chanda Sahib, while the British backed Muhammad Ali. Chanda Sahib, a seasoned commander with strong forces, defeated Muhammad Ali with the help of the French Governor Dupleix and drove him out of Arcot.

Witnessing Chanda Sahib’s growing power, Yusuf Khan joined his army. The defeated Muhammad Ali sought refuge in the Trichy fort. Chanda Sahib then marched toward Trichy with a massive army to capture it.

Seizing this opportunity, the British Governor Saunders promoted his clerk Robert Clive and sent him to Arcot with a small force. Since Chanda Sahib had left for Trichy, Arcot was left with minimal defense, and Clive easily captured it. Shocked by this, Chanda Sahib sent an army led by his son Raja Sahib to retake Arcot, with Yusuf Khan joining the campaign.

However, the Maratha forces of Murari Rao allied with the British, and in the ensuing battles—first at Arcot and later at Kaveripakkam (February 1752)—the British defeated Raja Sahib’s army. Despite Yusuf Khan’s valiant efforts and military skill, the defeat could not be averted.

**Joining the British**

Realizing that British power was growing stronger, Yusuf Khan abandoned Chanda Sahib and joined the British forces. Impressed by his courage and abilities at the Battle of Kaveripakkam, Robert Clive recruited him into his own army.

Elated by their victory at Arcot, the British turned their attention to rescuing their ally, Muhammad Ali, who was besieged at Tiruchirappalli. Under the command of Major Stringer Lawrence, who had recently returned from England, the British joined forces with Robert Clive’s troops and marched toward Trichy. Yusuf Khan was part of this army.

Meanwhile, the French Governor Dupleix sent reinforcements under the command of Jacques Law to assist Chanda Sahib. The ensuing Battle of Trichy was fierce, and in the end, Chanda Sahib was killed, giving the British a decisive victory. In this battle, Yusuf Khan displayed extraordinary courage and tactical brilliance. His contribution to this war—which proved to be a turning point in establishing British supremacy in India—was recognized and praised by several British officers, including Captain Dalton.

After the war, however, logistical complications in the British camp forced Major Lawrence to stay in Trichy for some time. Dupleix, determined to trap Lawrence under siege, set up French strongholds at places like Koviladi and Erumbeeswaram to block supplies of food and ammunition to the British troops.

But each time, Yusuf Khan successfully ensured that the provisions reached the British forces without obstruction. Impressed by his efficiency and bravery, Major Lawrence wrote to the British Council in Madras, commending Yusuf Khan’s service and placing him on par with Robert Clive in terms of ability and loyalty. He also urged that Yusuf Khan be suitably honored.

The Council accepted Lawrence’s recommendation, and in March 1755, they conferred upon him the title **“Muhammad Yusuf Khan, Khan Bahadur, Commander of Sepoys”**, along with a gold medal in recognition of his valor. From that time onward, he came to be known as **Khan Sahib** or **Commander Sahib**.

Once Yusuf Khan attained his new rank, new responsibilities soon followed.  
Although Muhammad Ali had secured his throne after the war, he was heavily burdened with debts incurred during the military campaigns. To settle them, he authorized the British to collect taxes from his territories.

**Southern War**

Several southern Tamil Nadu Poligars (local chieftains) refused to pay this tax and raised their flags in rebellion. To suppress them, a British force under Colonel Heron marched from Madras. Because Yusuf Khan was well acquainted with those regions, he was sent along with the expedition.

From the beginning, however, Colonel Heron disliked Yusuf Khan. Perhaps due to jealousy—since Yusuf had been praised by Heron’s rival, Major Lawrence—the two never got along. On the way, Heron even wrote a complaint to headquarters against him, saying:

> “I see no use in having these sepoys led by the Subedar of Nellore
> accompany me. They spend their time looting villages along the way,
> harassing Brahmins, and frightening people to extract money. Yusuf
> Khan may be a brave soldier, but he is not fit to command troops.”

Although this assessment would later prove to be accurate, the Company ignored Heron’s warning.

As the army advanced toward Madurai, they reached a place called Manapparai. There, Yusuf Khan demanded tribute from a local chieftain named Lakshmi Nayakar. When he refused, the British troops ravaged his territories and plundered the villages. Left with no choice, Lakshmi Nayakar eventually paid the demanded sum.

At that time, Madurai was ruled by a man named Alam Khan. Hearing about the British army’s strength and the devastation at Manapparai, Alam Khan fled the city, leaving control to his subordinate, Mayana. When the British approached Madurai, Mayana too panicked and abandoned the city, seeking refuge in a nearby village called Kovilkkudi (present-day Thirumogur).

Kovilkkudi was a village inhabited by the Kallar community and home to an ancient temple. Mayana hid inside that temple for safety. With no resistance left in Madurai, Heron decided that the war would not be complete until Mayana was captured. He therefore sent Yusuf Khan with a detachment to Kovilkkudi.

For reasons unknown, Yusuf delayed attacking the place. Enraged by this, Heron personally led another force to Kovilkkudi. By the time both of them entered the village, Mayana had already escaped. Heron blamed the delay—and thus Mayana’s escape—entirely on Yusuf Khan.

Frustrated at his failure, Heron ordered the temple gates to be set on fire. Furious over being accused, Yusuf Khan too vented his anger on the temple—looting it and desecrating it. The sacred idols inside were stolen, marking one of the darkest episodes in the campaign.

After returning to Madurai, Colonel Heron left Yusuf Khan stationed there while he proceeded toward Tirunelveli with the British forces and Muhammad Ali’s brother, Mahboob Khan. Their objective was to subdue the Poligars who had refused to pay tribute.

The British army laid siege to Poolithevar’s strong fort at Nerkattanseval. However, Poolithevar’s fierce resistance overwhelmed them, and the British were forced to retreat in defeat. Mahboob Khan was captured. Considering this a great humiliation, the British summoned Heron back to Madras for a military inquiry.

**In charge of Madurai**

Following this, the British placed the administration of Madurai and all of southern Tamil Nadu under Yusuf Khan’s charge. But this decision displeased Muhammad Ali, the Nawab of Arcot, who saw Yusuf Khan as an independent and defiant subordinate. Still, because Yusuf enjoyed the confidence and backing of the British authorities, the Nawab could do little but conceal his resentment — a tension that would later ignite into open conflict.

Despite his growing power in the south, Yusuf Khan continued to support the British in various military campaigns. At Nattam, he fought against the forces of Hyder Ali and defeated them. When Murtuza Ali, the commander of Vellore Fort, revolted against the Nawab of Arcot, Yusuf besieged the fort and subdued him.

Later, when the French general Lally attempted to besiege Madras, Yusuf Khan fought valiantly alongside the British troops, forcing the French army to retreat. These consistent victories increased his reputation among the British day by day.

Robert Orme, a senior member of the Madras Council and the chronicler of the early British successes in India, wrote admiringly of Yusuf Khan on several occasions. His growing influence led the British government to appoint him as the **Governor of Madurai**, giving him full authority to collect revenue from the region and remit it to the Company.

Around this time, Yusuf Khan married a Portuguese woman named **Masa (or Marcia)** — a union that symbolized his growing integration into the Europeanized world of the colonial elite, even as he ruled over a land seething with resistance.

**Repression of the Poligars (Local Chieftains)**

The honors and promotions Yusuf Khan received from the British made him increasingly arrogant. As he had been entrusted with the task of collecting taxes from the Poligars of southern Tamil Nadu, he carried out this duty with extreme severity. In the wars waged against those who refused to pay tribute to the British, Yusuf Khan became notorious for his ruthless tactics.

His military strategies often relied on sudden, aggressive assaults, and once victorious, he showed no mercy — resorting to brutal executions of his enemies. One stark example was the fierce battle fought between Yusuf Khan and Veeran Alagumuthukon, the ruler of Kattalankulam. After defeating him, Yusuf Khan ordered Alagumuthukon, along with his six lieutenants and 248 soldiers, to be executed by cannon fire at a place called Nadukaattu Seemai.

Similarly, at Thirupparankundram, he captured the Kallar leader and 500 of his men and had them all hanged. Lands belonging to Poligars who opposed him were often burned and destroyed. Despite such intimidation, several Poligars continued to defy his authority.

When Yusuf Khan attacked Sivagangai, ruled by Muthu Vaduganatha Thevar, the British forces under him were repelled and forced to retreat. In another campaign, Yusuf Khan joined hands with the Travancore ruler to besiege Poolithevar at Vasudevanallur fort — but Poolithevar once again emerged victorious.

Only during the third expedition. Yusuf Khan was finally able to capture the Nerkattanseval fort after the mysterious disappearance of Poolithevar.

**Revenue Dispute with the Nawab of Arcot**

The British government had granted Yusuf Khan full autonomy in tax collection. He initially agreed to pay an annual lease amount of 9, 10, and 10 lakhs for the first three years. However, Nawab Muhammad Ali refused to accept this arrangement and demanded 12 lakhs per year instead.

After negotiations led by British Governor Pigot, the final settlement was fixed at 11 lakhs. Initially, Yusuf Khan sent this revenue directly to the Nawab, but later, the British ordered him to remit the money to them instead.

Sensing the growing rift between the Nawab and the British, Yusuf Khan took advantage of the situation and hoisted the British flag in Madurai — symbolizing his allegiance to them.

Enraged that the payments were no longer reaching him, Nawab Muhammad Ali pressured the British Governor to intervene. Due to the Nawab’s repeated complaints, the Governor instructed Yusuf Khan to send the lease payments to the Nawab and to replace the British flag with the Nawab’s flag.

Yusuf Khan refused both requests and delayed sending the money. Furious, the Nawab lodged more complaints against him with the British government. In response, the Governor informed Yusuf Khan that a British commission would be sent to Madurai to investigate the matter and also summoned him to Madras for a meeting.

However, Yusuf Khan, sensing danger and mistrusting the Nawab’s influence, avoided the trip to Madras on various pretexts — a decision that would soon escalate into open rebellion.

Meanwhile, Yusuf Khan’s old friend—the Frenchman Marchand—arrived in Madurai with a small force and joined him. Together, they began to toy with the idea of expelling the British and establishing an independent kingdom, much like Hyder Ali had done in Mysore.

Already irritated by the news that the British planned to send a commission to monitor him, Yusuf Khan’s loyalty began to waver. In February 1763, he made his move: the British flag was lowered, and the French flag was hoisted over Madurai.

This act stunned the British Council in Madras. They immediately convened and decided to take strong action against him. Yusuf Khan, realizing the gravity of his decision, sent a letter of apology to the Council, stating that he could not come to Madras due to certain reasons but would soon arrive to offer a full explanation.

However, Governor Pigot saw through this ploy. Knowing Yusuf’s military cunning, he dispatched Major Lawrence, who was familiar with Yusuf’s tactics, to attack him. Lawrence reached Tiruchirappalli and in July sent a detachment under Captain Preston to Madurai.

Preston’s forces reached Madurai in August and laid siege to the city. But they had underestimated Yusuf Khan’s strength. Yusuf and Marchand launched fierce counterattacks, and with the onset of the northeast monsoon, the British troops suffered heavy losses and were forced to retreat to Trichy.

Predicting that the British would soon return with greater strength, Yusuf Khan wrote again to the Council, promising to pay the arrears of revenue regularly and requesting to continue as the Governor of Madurai.

In reply, the Council demanded that he appoint only officers nominated by them to key posts and immediately pay all the arrears due. Yusuf Khan refused these terms.

Undeterred by the first failure, Lawrence sent a larger army under Major Campbell to Madurai. Campbell’s forces camped to the east near Teppakulam and to the southwest near Thirupparankundram, cutting off all supply routes to the city. The fort was now completely surrounded, and nothing could enter.

Yusuf Khan’s repeated sorties from inside the fort were successfully repelled by the British. As the siege dragged on, food supplies inside the fort dwindled, leading to severe famine. Eventually, chaos broke out among the inhabitants, and most civilians were expelled from Madurai.

Desperation grew so severe that those remaining in the fort began to slaughter and eat horses, monkeys, donkeys, and even cats.

Realizing his desperate position, Yusuf Khan sent Marchand as an emissary to Major Campbell, proposing that if his family and a few soldiers were allowed to go safely to Dindigul, he would surrender everything else to the British. But Campbell insisted that Yusuf Khan himself must surrender unconditionally.

When Marchand returned and conveyed this to Yusuf Khan, he urged him to surrender, saying there was no other way. A heated quarrel ensued between them, and it is said that Yusuf slapped Marchand in anger.

Enraged, Marchand spread a rumor among the soldiers that Yusuf Khan was secretly planning to escape alone. This rumor quickly eroded Yusuf’s authority and trust among his troops.

On the evening of October 13, 1764, around 5 p.m., Marchand and a few of his associates went to Yusuf Khan’s residence while he was engaged in evening prayers. Taking advantage of the moment, they tied him up with his own turban and locked him in a room.

When one of Yusuf Khan’s loyal commanders, Muthali, rushed to rescue him upon hearing the news, he too was killed— sealing Yusuf’s tragic downfall at the hands of his own friend and soldiers.

After capturing Yusuf Khan, Marchand sent a message to the British forces, stating that he would hand him over alive on the condition that he and his men be granted safe passage. Major Campbell, who had long sought Yusuf Khan’s capture, accepted the offer. He also knew it would be difficult to detain a Frenchman like Marchand, so he agreed to the exchange. Accordingly, Yusuf Khan was handed over to the British troops.

Nawab Muhammad Ali, still burning with rage over the humiliation Yusuf had caused him, demanded that the prisoner be delivered to him immediately. Campbell complied, and Yusuf Khan was surrendered to the Nawab.

On October 15, 1764, near Sammatipuram, close to Madurai, Yusuf Khan was hanged to death. Even after his execution, his head was severed and sent to Tiruchirappalli, while his body was buried at the site, where a small mosque was later built over his grave.

Yusuf Khan was a man of power — a brilliant strategist and a formidable warrior — yet his life ended in betrayal and disgrace, for he remained disloyal to every side he served: his homeland, his allies, and his masters.
