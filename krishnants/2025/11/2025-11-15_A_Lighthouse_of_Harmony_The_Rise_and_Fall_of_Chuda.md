In the history of Tamilakam, few monuments illustrate the spirit of true secularism as powerfully as the Chudamani Vihara of Nagapattinam.Built with the patronage of the great Chola emperor Rajaraja I, the Vihara stood as a reminder of how genuinely our kings practised secularism by respecting all religions. Even though the Cholas were staunch Saivites—following the rigorous Pāśupata tradition—they allowed diverse faiths to flourish under their rule.The construction of a grand Buddhist Vihara, at the request of a foreign king, and the extensive land grants and privileges offered for its upkeep, reflect a deeply rooted cultural ethos: that all paths of worship deserve dignity.

**Thirumangal Azhwar and the Vihara**

Before discussing the history of the Vihara, it is important to address a piece of misinformation that is often repeated—that Tirumangai Azhwar, one of the Vaishnava saints, robbed a Buddhist statue from the Vihara and used it to build the Srirangam temple wall. There is no historical evidence to support this claim. Many records refer to the monastery as ‘Chudamani Vihara’, which was constructed several centuries after the time of Thirumangai Azhwar.

There was also no proof of a Vihara at Nagappattinam even before. During the time of Pallavas, when the Chinese pilgrim Xuanzang visited Tamilakam during the reign of Narasimhavarman Pallava, he does mention Nagapattinam. But he does not record the presence of any Buddhist Vihara there. This is significant, because Xuanzang meticulously noted down every Buddhist centre he encountered throughout India.

Next, Chinese sources say that the Pallava king Rajasimha, owing to his good relations with the Chinese emperors, built a Buddhist Vihara in Tamilakam. They also mention that an inscription engraved with the words “Ko-ho-che” was sent by the Chinese emperor and placed in front of that Vihara. However, Pallava records do not mention that Rajasimha built any such Buddhist temple. Even so, there is no evidence anywhere indicating where this Vihara was constructed. It could well have been built at Mamallapuram, the port city of the Pallavas.

Thirumangai Azhwar, whose original name was Neelan, belonged to the time of Pallava king Nandivarman II. He ruled over Ali Nadu and fought several battles on behalf of the Cholas. Neither the internal references in his hymns nor the Chola–Pallava inscriptions suggest that there was a Buddhist Vihara in Nagapattinam, or that Thirumangai raided such a Vihara. Those who call themselves rationalists keep repeating this baseless allegation even though there is no historical evidence whatsoever.

**Chudamani Varman**

Now, coming to Chudamani Vihara, readers of *Ponniyin Selvan* will recall the Vihara at Nagapattinam vividly. In the novel, when Arulmozhi Varman escapes from Lanka and suffers from high fever, the monks of this Vihara take him in, heal him, and protect him; later, when a devastating cyclone and flood arrive, the story describes how he escapes from there. But this is only fiction. Because, during the youth of Arulmozhi Varman, this Vihara did not even exist. It was only later—after he ascended the throne as Rajaraja—that the Vihara was constructed. The Leiden (Anaimangalam) copper plates, along with several inscriptions, provide detailed information about this.

During Rajaraja’s time, relations were cordial between the Cholas and the Sailendra kings of the Southeast Asian maritime empire of Srivijaya. The inscriptions praise the Srivijaya king Māra Vijayōttunga Varman as one who excelled in knowledge, outshone even the preceptors of the gods, was like the wish-fulfilling tree for supplicants, and like the sun to the lotus forest. However, this friendly situation completely changed during the reign of his son Sangrāma Vijayōttunga Varman, when Rajendra Chola raided several cities of Srivijaya—a separate story altogether.

In the 21st regnal year of Rajaraja (around 1006 CE), Māra Vijayōttunga Varman approached him with a request: that a Buddhist Vihara be constructed in memory of his father, Chudamani Varman. Rajaraja agreed and allocated nearly 450 acres of land at Anaimangalam near Nagapattinam for this purpose. The order for this donation was issued verbally, which is noteworthy. It must have taken more than nine years to complete the construction. By then, both Rajaraja and the Srivijaya king had passed away, and so Rajendra Chola recorded his father’s verbal instructions formally as a copper-plate charter.

It is clear that the Vihara was built on a large scale. Inscriptions state that its towers made even Mount Meru appear small. Even though Rajendra later fought against Kadaram, the prominence of this Vihara seems not to have diminished during his rule. Sir Walter Elliot later noted that it was a three-storeyed brick structure and likely served as a lighthouse because of its height.

But granting land alone was not enough. The charters clearly record the rules the Vihara had to follow. First, it was exempted from 18 types of taxes, including 8,943 *kalam* of paddy that had to be paid annually to the king. However, certain conditions were also laid down: canals could be dug through the donated villages; no one could obstruct canals passing through other villages en route to these lands; the owners of these lands could not use the water for drinking, bathing, or gardening; after use, any surplus water had to be released by the Vihara authorities themselves. Further, since this was a Buddhist religious establishment, the local Eelavas were prohibited from tapping toddy from the palm and coconut trees in the donated area. The road leading to the Vihara could have decorative arches, and large drums could be used inside the Vihara.

Thus, Rajendra ensured that his responsibility did not end with constructing the Vihara. He took care to inscribe what must be done to sustain it perpetually. He also earnestly appealed—in his father’s name—that future rulers respect and uphold this endowment. But did that happen?

**Demolition of Vihara**

After the Cholas, when Buddhism declined in influence, this Vihara too lost its prominence. Yet the building itself survived. In the 19th century, the Jesuits—who had been expelled by the French government and had taken refuge in Nagapattinam—set their eyes on this structure. They began to pressure the British government to demolish it. But the British feared public backlash if they demolished a structure revered by the people. Locals informed the Madras Governor that since the Vihara served as a lighthouse, its demolition would anger the fishermen. Sir W. Elliot, whom we mentioned earlier, recommended to the government that the structure should not be demolished as it had historical significance.

But the pressure from the Jesuits continued. Eventually, the Madras Governor, Sir Charles Trevelyan, said he would personally inspect the site. Claiming that the structure was in a state of collapse, he ordered that a protective wall be built around it and that the nearby residents move away for safety. But the building did not survive. Under relentless pressure from the Jesuits, the government finally issued orders in August 1867 to demolish the structure. The St. Joseph’s College at Nagapattinam was authorised to dismantle it and reuse the materials to construct a school.

Thus, a monument built as a symbol of secular harmony with the help of Rajaraja ended up destroyed—ironically—in the name of religion itself.
