The Guru Pooja of the revered Shaiva saint Sekkizhar was observed this week. He was the great poet-saint who composed the Thiruthondar Puranam, more popularly known as the Periya Puranam, the monumental Tamil work that narrates the lives of the 63 Shaiva saints, the Nayanmars.

**Sekkizhar - The Chief Minister**

For those who may not know much about him, here is a brief account of his life.

Sekkizhar, whose original name was Ramadevan, was born at Kundrathur near Chennai, in the Sekkizhar clan, from which he derived his celebrated name. During his time, the region was under the Cholas. He entered the Chola administration as an officer and rose to become the chief minister of Kulothunga Chola II.

Recognising his deep knowledge of Shaiva scriptures, Tamil literature and temple traditions, Kulothunga Chola II, himself a devout Shaivite, requested Sekkizhar to compose a grand work on the lives of the Shaiva saints, the Nayanmars, or Sivan Adiyars.

There is a popular story that Kulothunga was drawn towards the worldly charm of Jivaka Chintamani, a Jain text, and that Sekkizhar composed the Periya Puranam to turn him back towards Shaiva philosophy. But this story does not stand historical scrutiny.

Kulothunga Chola II came from a strong Shaiva background. From the beginning of his reign, he was deeply involved in the renovation and glorification of the Chidambaram temple. He also had Ottakkoothar, the great Tamil poet and a Shaivite, as his teacher. Therefore, the claim that he drifted away from Shaivism is difficult to sustain. It is far more reasonable to understand the Periya Puranam as a sacred literary project undertaken by Sekkizhar at the request of Kulothunga Chola II.

**His work**

Sekkizhar did not create his work in isolation. He used the Thiruthondar Thogai of Sundaramurthy Nayanar as the foundation. Sundarar, himself one of the great Shaiva saints, had referred to many of the Nayanmars in brief — often in a single line or two. Later, Nambiyandar Nambi, who lived during the time of Rajaraja Chola I, expanded this devotional tradition into an anthathi form, where the last word of one verse becomes the opening word of the next. His work came to be known as the Thiruthondar Thiruvanthathi.

Yet even Nambi’s work was largely a devotional praise of the saints. Sekkizhar’s task was far greater. He had to gather traditions, study temple memories, preserve oral histories, understand the sacred geography of Tamil Shaivism and transform all of it into a grand literary and spiritual epic.

The lives of saints naturally contain hagiographical elements, miracles and devotional symbolism. But Sekkizhar’s brilliance lies in the way he interwove these sacred elements with real history, social memory, temple culture, geography, royal patronage and the lived religious world of Tamilakam.

That is why the Periya Puranam is not merely a religious text. It is a civilisational archive. It preserves the spiritual history of Tamil Shaivism, the cultural geography of the Chola age, the devotion of ordinary people, the role of temples, and the deep continuity of bhakti in Tamil society.

One can find many such historical references in the Periya Puranam. In fact, they reveal the depth of research undertaken by Sekkizhar while composing this monumental work.

**Vatapi victory**

A striking example appears in his narration of the life of Paranjothi, who later came to be known as Siruthonda Nayanar.

Paranjothi served as the commander-in-chief of the Pallava king Narasimhavarman I. From the Vatapi inscription of Narasimhavarman and from Pallava copper plate records, we know that Narasimhavarman defeated the Chalukya king Pulakeshin II, marched all the way to his capital Vatapi, and captured the city. The inscription of Narasimhavarman can still be seen at Badami.

Remarkably, this historical event is recorded by Sekkizhar while narrating the life of Paranjothi.

மன்னவர்க்குத் தண்டு போய் வடபுலத்து வாதாவித்

தொன் நகரம் துகள் ஆகத் துளைநெடுங்கை வரை உகைத்துப்

பல் மணியும் நிதிக் குவையும் பகட்டு இனமும் பரித் தொகையும்

இன்னன எண்ணில கவர்ந்தே இகல் அரசன் முன் கொணர்ந்தார்.

The verse says that Paranjothi marched to Vatapi in the northern country, reduced that ancient city to ruin, and brought back immense wealth, jewels, elephants and horses before presenting them to his king.

This is not merely a devotional detail. It is a powerful historical memory preserved within a sacred Tamil text. The Vatapi campaign, known from inscriptions, is echoed in the Periya Puranam through the life of Siruthonda Nayanar.

This is one of the finest examples of how Sekkizhar interwove bhakti with history.

**Celestial Voice heard by Rajasimha**

Another remarkable historical reference in the Periya Puranam appears in the life of Poosalar Nayanar.

Poosalar was the saint who built a temple for Shiva entirely in his mind. While narrating this episode, Sekkizhar mentions that Shiva appeared in the dream of a Pallava king who was also building a stone temple — kattrali — at Kanchipuram.

Since the consecration dates of both temples fell on the same day, Shiva instructed the Pallava king to postpone the consecration of his temple, because He had to first enter the temple built by Poosalar in his heart.

Sekkizhar describes this beautifully:

காடவர் கோமான் கச்சிக் கற்றளி எடுத்து முற்ற

மாடெலாம் சிவனுக்கு ஆகப் பெருஞ் செல்வம் வகுத்தல் செய்வான்

நாடமால் அறியாதாரைத் தாபிக்கும் அந்நாள் முன்னாள்

ஏடலர் கொன்றை வேய்ந்தார் இரவிடைக் கனவில் எய்தி

The key phrase here is “கச்சிக் கற்றளி” — the stone temple at Kanchi.

Who was this Pallava king?

The answer becomes clear when we connect Sekkizhar’s account with inscriptional evidence. The Kailasanathar temple at Kanchipuram is well known in inscriptions as “Kachippettu Periya Thirukkattrali”. This great stone temple was built by Rajasimha Pallava, also known as Narasimhavarman II.

Therefore, the Pallava king mentioned by Sekkizhar in the life of Poosalar Nayanar can be identified as Rajasimha Pallava.

Even more strikingly, the Kailasanathar temple itself preserves an inscription that refers to the king hearing a divine voice:

दुष्यन्तप्रमुखैः श्रुताम्बरगता वाणी शरीरं विना

क्ष्मानाथैः सुरदृश्वभिर्यत्कृते ।

कण्वादिभिः स्वीकृतैस्तन्नाश्चर्यं

पुनः कलियुगे दूरीभवत्सद्गुणे सः

अश्रोत् तां गिरं महदहो विस्मापनं श्रीपरः ॥

The verse says:

“It is no surprise that kings such as Duṣyanta once heard a celestial voice from the heavens, for that occurred in the presence of divine seers and sages like Kaṇva. But in this Kali Yuga, when noble qualities have receded far from the world, that he should again hear such a divine utterance — ah, that is indeed a great marvel. Glory to Śrīpara.”

This inscriptional reference beautifully corroborates Sekkizhar’s narration. The Periya Puranam says that Shiva appeared in the dream of the Pallava king and instructed him; the Kailasanathar inscription remembers the same king as one who heard a divine voice.

This is not a casual poetic embellishment. It shows how deeply Sekkizhar’s work was anchored in temple traditions, inscriptions, sacred geography and living historical memory.

**Nelveli war**

Not only these, there are many areas of Tamil history where the Periya Puranam becomes extremely useful in clarifying doubts about the chronology and identity of historical events.

One such important example is the reference to the Nelveli war, won by the Pandya king Nindra Seer Nedumaran Nayanar.

In his Thiruthondar Thogai, Sundaramurthy Nayanar briefly refers to him as:

“நெல்வேலி வென்ற நின்றசீர் நெடுமாறன்”

— “Nindra Seer Nedumaran, who won at Nelveli.”

But this naturally raised an important historical question: Who was the king defeated by Nedumaran at Nelveli?

For a long time, many assumed that the defeated ruler was a Chera king. But the Periya Puranam gives us crucial clues which show that this interpretation is not correct.

While narrating the history of Nedumaran Nayanar, Sekkizhar says:

அமர் வேண்டி வந்து ஏற்ற சேய புலத் தெவ்வர் எதிர்

நெல்வேலிச் செருக் களத்துப் பாய

Here, the phrase “சேய புலம்” refers to a distant land. Sekkizhar is clearly speaking of an enemy who came from far away to wage war.

He further says:

முனை அழிந்த வடபுலத்து முதல் மன்னர் படைசரிய

This is even more decisive. The defeated king is described as one who came from the northern region. These descriptions do not fit the Cheras.

When we look at the political history of the period, the identity becomes clear. It was Vikramaditya I, the Chalukya king, who invaded Tamilakam during this time. He attacked the Pallava country, besieged Kanchi, and drove Paramesvaravarman I out of Kanchipuram. After this, he moved further south and faced the Pandya king Nedumaran at Nelveli.

Therefore, the king defeated by Nedumaran at Nelveli was most probably Vikramaditya I of the Chalukyas, not a Chera ruler.

This perfectly matches Sekkizhar’s description. Vikramaditya came from a distant northern land, and his campaign into Tamilakam fits the phrase “வடபுலத்து முதல் மன்னர்” far better than any Chera identification.

This reading is also supported by the Kendur inscription of Kirtivarman II, which records that Vikramaditya fought against the Pandya and Chola armies.

**Timeline of Sundaramurthy Nayanar**

Thus, a brief phrase in Sundarar’s Thiruthondar Thogai becomes historically clearer through Sekkizhar’s expanded narration in the Periya Puranam. What looked like a devotional reference becomes a valuable clue for reconstructing the political history of 7th-century Tamilakam.

Another important historical question concerns the timeline of Sundaramurthy Nayanar.

In his Thiruthondar Thogai, Sundarar says:

“கடல்சூழ்ந்த உலகெலாம் காக்கின்ற பெருமான்

காடவர்கோன் கழற்சிங்கன் அடியார்க்கும் அடியேன்”

From this verse, it is clear that Sundaramurthy Nayanar is referring to a contemporary Pallava king — Kadavarkon Kazharsingan.

But who exactly was this Kadavarkon?

If the identity of this king is established, then the period of Sundaramurthy Nayanar can also be fixed more securely from the king’s timeline. Here again, Sekkizhar comes to our help.

In the life of Cheraman Perumal Nayanar, Sekkizhar says that both Cheraman Perumal and Sundaramurthy Nayanar visited Madurai, where the Pandya king received them with great honour. He also mentions that the Chola king, who was related to the Pandya king, was present in Madurai at that time.

This description fits well with Ko Chadaiyan Ranadhiran, the Pandya king, whose mother was a Chola princess. During this period, the Cholas and Pandyas appear to have maintained friendly relations.

The Pallava king who was contemporary to Ranadhiran was Rajasimha Pallava, who, as seen earlier, was a great devotee of Shiva and the builder of the Kailasanathar temple at Kanchipuram.

Thus, by connecting Sundarar’s reference to Kadavarkon Kazharsingan with Sekkizhar’s narrative, the Pandya context, and the Pallava chronology, it becomes reasonable to identify Sundaramurthy Nayanar as a contemporary of Rajasimha Pallava.

**Kalabhras**

Apart from these examples, Sekkizhar mentions several other historically valuable details throughout the Periya Puranam.

In the Murthy Nayanar Puranam, while referring to the Kalabhra invasion, Sekkizhar says:

கானக் கடி சூழ் வடுகக் கரு நாடர் காவல்

மானப் படை மன்னன் வலிந்து நிலம் கொள்வானாய்

This passage indicates that the Kalabhras came from the Karnataka region. Thus, Sekkizhar preserves an important clue about the origin of the Kalabhra power that disturbed the older Tamil political order.

Similarly, one of the Nayanmars, Kootruva Nayanar, is said to have belonged to the Kalabhra lineage. Sekkizhar refers to him as:

நல் திருத் தொண்டின் முயன்றார் களந்தை முதல்வனார்

Many historians have identified this Kootruva Nayanar with the Kootruvan mentioned in the Poolankurichi inscription.

These examples show the extraordinary importance of the Periya Puranam for Tamil historical studies. What appears on the surface as a sacred text narrating the lives of Shiva’s devotees also preserves political history, dynastic memory, regional conflicts, temple traditions and social geography.

This was possible only because of the depth of Sekkizhar’s research, his command over earlier devotional literature, his awareness of temple traditions, and his ability to weave history into sacred biography.

Thus, the Periya Puranam is not merely a Purana of devotion. It is a treasure house of Tamil history.

The world of Tamil historical studies is deeply indebted to Sekkizhar — not only for preserving the spiritual lives of the Nayanmars, but also for preserving the historical memory of Tamil civilisation.
