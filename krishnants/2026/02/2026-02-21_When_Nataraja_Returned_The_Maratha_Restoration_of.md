The flight of temple deities during the relentless invasions of the Delhi Sultans and later Mughal forces is one of the recurring themes in medieval Indian history. Many assume that South India was immune to such devastation — but that is a misconception. Several great temples of the South faced similar hardships. Each carries a story of resilience, narrating how Dharmic Hindus endured immense suffering to safeguard their sacred icons and later restore their temples despite overwhelming adversity.

In Tamilakam, the journey of Namperumal of Srirangam during the invasion of Ulugh Khan is well known. The utsava murti was taken on a long and arduous journey — through parts of Tamil Nadu and Kerala, then to Melkote in Karnataka, and later even to Tirupati — to protect it from desecration. After nearly forty-eight years in exile, Namperumal returned triumphantly to Srirangam in 1371 CE following the restoration of Hindu rule in the region.

However, a comparatively lesser-known story is that of the trials faced by the great temple of Chidambaram.

Chidambaram is one of the sacred Pancha Bhoota Sthalams, representing the element of Akasha (space), and holds a uniquely exalted place in the Shaivite tradition. In Tamil Saiva parlance, the very word “Kovil” traditionally refers to Chidambaram — such is its spiritual pre-eminence. It is here that Shiva manifests as Nataraja, the cosmic dancer. Nataraja was the tutelary deity of the Cholas, and both the Chola and Pandya monarchs gold-plated the sanctum in reverence and devotion.

By the early fourteenth century, the temple was flourishing and endowed with immense wealth, making it an attractive target during the Sultanate campaigns in the South. Malik Kafur’s invasion was particularly devastating. Historical accounts indicate severe destruction, large-scale looting, and the tragic killing of many unarmed Dikshitars. The sanctity and prosperity of the temple were deeply scarred.

Yet, as with many sacred centres of Bharata, Chidambaram rose again. The Vijayanagara emperors and later the Nayak rulers undertook restoration works, revived worship, and reconsecrated the temple — reaffirming a civilisational resilience that outlived political turbulence.

However, during the seventeenth century, the temple once again faced turbulence. The forces of the Bijapur Sultanate, taking advantage of the weakened political situation in the Tamil region, launched incursions into the area. There were multiple raids during this period, creating an atmosphere of fear and instability.

To safeguard the sacred icons of Nataraja and Sivakami Amman, the devotees removed the vigrahams from Chidambaram in 1647 CE. A contemporary text titled “Arasavanathu Aranilaiyam” records that a certain Sivaprakasar took extraordinary efforts to ensure that the daily poojas and rituals, which had been interrupted during this turbulent phase, were resumed and maintained. He received assistance from Lingama Nayaka in sustaining temple worship under these precarious conditions.

The same source states that the Nataraja vigraha was initially taken to Thuraiyur Palayam for safety. Subsequently, the icons of Nataraja and Sivakami were moved to Kudumiyanmalai, and later to Madurai, reflecting the continuing uncertainty of the times.

This narrative finds corroboration in a set of copper-plate inscriptions issued by the Thanjavur Maratha rulers, which were preserved at Thiruvarur. These copper plates provide valuable chronological clues and help us reconstruct this critical episode in the temple’s history.

One of the copper-plate inscriptions records that Nataraja left His abode in the Sarvadhari year during the Tamil month of Margazhi. It further states that in the Raktakshi year, during the month of Karthigai, and under the reign of King Sambhaji, Nataraja was restored to His original abode at Chidambaram.

It must be noted that Raja Sambhaji, the son of Chhatrapati Shivaji, ruled from Senji between 1680 and 1689 CE. It was during this period that the second major restoration of the Chidambaram temple took place and the sacred icons were ceremonially reinstalled. The copper plates contain beautiful Tamil verses poetically describing this triumphant return and the revival of worship.

விடையில்வந்த செம்பொன்அம் பலத்தான் அந்த

வெள்ளியம் பலத்திலே விரும்பிச் சென்ற

நடையில்வந்த வருடமோ சர்வ தாரி

நாயபமார் கழியிருபத் தைந்து நாளாம்

கடையில்வந்த வருடங்கார்த் திகையீ ரேழு

கதிர்வாரம் திரும்பவந்து கலந்த நாளாம்

இடையில்வந்த வருடமெண்ணிப் பார்க்கில் முப்பத்

தேழுபத்து மாதநாள் இருப தாமே !

(Nataraja left Chidambaram during Sarvadhari year Margazhi month and returned after thirty seven years during the month of Karthigai )

The date of the return of Nataraja to Chidambaram is recorded as 1684 CE. It was in this year that Raja Sambhaji initiated extensive renovation works at the temple. His official, Gopal Dadaji, was entrusted with overseeing these restoration efforts.

The second copper plate, dated 1686 CE, reverentially refers to Nataraja as “Oorudaiya Peruman” — the Lord who belongs to the town, emphasizing His inseparable identity with Chidambaram. The inscription poetically notes that after His return, abundant rains blessed the region, leading to prosperous and bountiful harvests. It further records that Sambhaji undertook the gold plating of the Ponnambalam, restoring the sanctum to its former glory.

The third copper-plate inscription provides a crucial ritual detail. It records that the consecration (Kumbhabhishekam) of the Chidambaram temple was performed under the patronage of King Sambhaji, with the guidance of his guru, Muthaiya Dikshitar. Nataraja was ceremonially reinstalled in the Ponnambalam on November 21, 1684 CE.

The fourth copper plate corroborates these events and further records that another consecration took place in 1686 CE, apart from the one conducted in 1684. This likely indicates that additional renovation works were carried out after the initial reinstatement, necessitating a second ritual consecration two years later — once again under the aegis of Sambhaji.

Taken together, these four copper plates firmly establish that the vigrahas of Nataraja and Sivakami were taken out of Chidambaram for approximately thirty-seven years before being restored through the concerted efforts of Raja Sambhaji and his officials. The temple renovation began in 1684, continued for nearly two years, and culminated in two major consecration ceremonies during the Maratha reign.

This episode stands as yet another testament to the resilience of temple traditions — and to the role played by the Maratha rulers in upholding and restoring Tamil sacred heritage during periods of political instability.
