**The Controversy**

From the completion of the first phase of excavation itself, the narrative around Keeladi drifted away from data and stratigraphy into ideology and politics. Ironically, it is Tamil history that has suffered the most—now bereft of a sober, evidence-driven understanding of one of the most significant archaeological sites of Tamilakam.

The Keeladi excavation began in 2013 under the Archaeological Survey of India (ASI). After completing the initial phases of excavation, the Superintending Archaeologist, Mr. Amarnath Ramakrishna, was transferred in 2017—an entirely routine administrative practice in government departments. However, this transfer became the flashpoint of controversy when he opposed the move, transforming a standard administrative decision into a public dispute.

Subsequently, the excavation was taken over by Mr. P. S. Sriraman on behalf of ASI. From the outset, he faced stiff opposition. After further assessment, he concluded that the site had reached a stage where continued excavation would not yield significant new results, and accordingly wound up ASI’s operations at Keeladi. Thereafter, the Tamil Nadu State Department of Archaeology took over and has since been continuing excavations at the site.

**Core Issue**

The central controversy surrounding Keeladi concerns the dating of the site and its artefacts, particularly pottery sherds bearing Tamil-Brahmi inscriptions. A widely publicised claim places these inscribed sherds as early as the 6th century BCE. In the earlier excavation reports, the age of the site was placed in the 3rd century BCE.

This sharp revision in chronology—from the 3rd century BCE to the 6th century BCE raised some fundamental questions.

**The Report**

As per established procedure, excavation reports must be submitted to the Archaeological Survey of India (ASI), which then undertakes technical vetting before publication. In the case of Keeladi, it appears that Mr. Amarnath Ramakrishna submitted the excavation report covering the seasons 2014–15 and 2015–16 only in 2023, nearly seven to eight years after the fieldwork was completed.

ASI subsequently took time to examine the report and raised several objections—an entirely normal and mandatory process in the evaluation of any archaeological report. A major concern flagged by ASI related to the chronological claims made in the report. While the report proposed an older limit of the 8th century BCE, ASI pointed out that no substantiating evidence—whether stratigraphic, palaeographic, or absolute dating—had been provided to support this claim.

A demand for evidence to substantiate the claims made in the report is being portrayed by some as a ‘delaying tactic’, and even as a denial of ‘Tamil pride’. This is notwithstanding the fact that older sites, such as Adichanallur, dated to the 9th century BCE, were excavated by ASI and the findings were published through formal reports.

Mr. Ramakrishna rejected these objections, maintaining that the report was correct as submitted and declining to revise the chronology.

Following this impasse, the report was reviewed by a five-member expert committee, which examined the contents independently.

According to press reports, the committee raised multiple objections—not merely on presentation or structure, but on substantive archaeological issues. In response, Mr. Ramakrishna reportedly stated as per this news report that the committee’s comments were “more mechanical than factual” and alleged a lack of “application of human mind” in the evaluation. He also refused to modify the report based on the committee’s observations.

Now, let us examine some of the specific objections raised by the expert committee, setting aside stylistic and formatting issues, and focus purely on the core archaeological and methodological concerns, to assess whether the rebuttal offered carries factual merit.

- The claim in the report proposing an 8th century BCE horizon for
  Keeladi is not acceptable, as it lacks absolute dating evidence. The
  report itself acknowledges that the Carbon-14 (C14) samples do not
  corroborate such an early date. In the absence of reliable absolute
  dates, proposing the 8th century BCE as the lower limit remains
  speculative rather than scientific.

- Further, the stratigraphic sequence shows clear inconsistencies and
  disturbances. In certain instances, upper layers have yielded older
  dates than the layers beneath them, a situation that violates basic
  principles of stratigraphy unless convincingly explained through site
  formation processes. Such anomalies require careful correction and
  transparent documentation.

- The stratigraphic evidence also indicates a mixed cultural deposit
  rather than a single, uniform cultural phase. This again weakens any
  attempt to project a linear or monocultural narrative for the site.

- Importantly, the Tamil-Brahmi inscribed pottery sherds are
  consistently associated with layers dated to the 3rd–2nd century BCE,
  and not to any earlier horizon. This aligns with established
  palaeographic and archaeological understanding across Tamilakam.

If one examines the above observations, they ideally should have been countered with factual, verifiable evidence, if such evidence exists. However, the responses cited in press reports do not appear to provide any such data. In the case of absolute dating, the matter could have been conclusively addressed by producing C14 laboratory reports with clear sampling contexts and calibrated dates. That alone would have settled the controversy.

Similarly, the issue of disturbed or inverted stratigraphic layers can only be clarified by the excavator, through detailed explanations of trench history, post-depositional disturbances, or excavation methodology. Leaving such inconsistencies unexplained only deepens doubt.

It must also be noted that coins of Rajaraja Chola (10th–11th century CE) were reportedly recovered from the site. However, the exact stratigraphic context of these coins remains unclear, further underscoring the need for precise documentation and transparency.

With respect to the claim of a mixed cultural assemblage, the evidence is already visible within the pottery inscriptions themselves. The presence of sherds bearing Prakrit and Sanskrit linguistic elements—as acknowledged in Mr. Amarnath Ramakrishna’s own paper—clearly indicates cultural plurality rather than isolation.

All this points to one unavoidable conclusion: what Keeladi requires is not rhetoric, but rigorous, peer-reviewed, and evidence-based scholarship. The Archaeological Survey of India, as a professional statutory body, has every right to demand methodological clarity and evidentiary rigour. Once published, an excavation report carries the authority of the institution, not the individual author.

Finally, it is vital to reiterate a foundational principle of archaeology: excavations must never begin with a predetermined conclusion. Conclusions must emerge only from stratigraphy, dating, material culture, and reproducible scientific analysis.

Tamil history deserves an unbiased, methodologically sound, and factual presentation of Keeladi’s findings—nothing more, and nothing less.
