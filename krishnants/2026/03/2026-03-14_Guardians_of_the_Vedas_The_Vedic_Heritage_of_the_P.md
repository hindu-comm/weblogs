The Pandya dynasty is well known as one of the most ancient dynasties to have ruled Bharath, with a history extending for more than two thousand years. Tamil traditions speak of their early rule at Kapadapuram, near the mouth of the Tamirabarani river, before the later capital at Madurai, while legends also refer to territories extending south of present-day Kanyakumari. They were also the traditional patrons of Tamil language and literature, fostering the famous Tamil Sangams that gathered hundreds of poets and scholars under their patronage.

One of the striking features of Pandya rule is the remarkable continuity of their association with the Vedic tradition. From the earliest Sangam poems to the inscriptions of the later medieval period, the Pandya rulers consistently appear as patrons of Vedic learning, performers of yajñas, and protectors of Sanatana Dharma. Sangam literature itself records Pandya kings conducting great sacrifices and other Vedic rituals, while later copper plates and stone inscriptions speak of land grants, the establishment of agraharas, and endowments for the recitation of the Vedas in temples.

This unbroken thread — stretching from the early historic age of Tamilakam to the fourteenth century — demonstrates that the Pandya polity was deeply embedded within the broader framework of our Dharma, where royal authority, temple culture, and Vedic ritual formed an integral and mutually reinforcing civilisational order.

Let us look at some examples from literature and inscriptions that clearly demonstrate this continuity.

**Sangam Era**

One of the earliest Pandya kings, said to have ruled from Kapadapuram, was Palyagasalai Muthukudumi Peruvazhuthi, whose very name indicates that he had performed numerous yajñas. A Purananuru verse exclaims that no one could count the number of yupas (sacrificial posts) erected in the yajña halls of this king:

**வீயாச் சிறப்பின் வேள்வி முற்றி,**

**யூபம் நட்ட வியன் களம் பலகொல்**

It is not only the literary sources that attest to this tradition. The “Peruvazhuthi” coins issued by the Pandya kings also corroborate the performance of Vedic sacrifices such as the Aśvamedha, much like the contemporary rulers of northern India. One variety of these copper coins depicts a horse tied to a Yūpa (sacrificial post) placed before an altar, clearly symbolising the Aśvamedha sacrifice. On the reverse appears the characteristic fish emblem of the Pandyas. Numismatists generally assign these coins to the 2nd–3rd century BCE, indicating that the association of the Pandya kings with Vedic ritual traditions goes back to a very early period.

Epigraphical sources further reinforce this tradition. The Chinnamanur copper plate grants (small plates) mention that a Pandya king performed a thousand yajñas, referring to Palyagasalai Muthukudumi Peruvazhuthi — the very epithet Palyagasalai meaning “the one who performed many sacrificial rituals.” The grant records this with the phrase “**ஓர் ஆயிரம் கிரது செய்தும்**”, emphasising the scale of these Vedic performances.

The famous Velvikkudi copper plates of the medieval period corroborate this even more clearly. They narrate how land originally donated by Muthukudumi Peruvazhuthi for the performance of Vedic sacrifices was later usurped during the rule of the Kalabhras, and how it was subsequently restored to the Brahmanas by the Pandya king Parantaka Nedunjadaiyan. This inscription thus preserves the memory of a much earlier royal endowment connected with Vedic ritual activity.

Interestingly, references to the Pandya kings and their association with Vedic rites are not confined to Tamil sources alone. Even Kalidasa’s Raghuvaṃśa refers to the Pandya rulers in this context. In the famous episode where Indumati is introduced to the assembled kings, the Pandya king is described as belonging to a lineage whose kulaguru is the sage Agastya, the very sage who is said to have subdued the growth of the Vindhya mountains and drunk the ocean. The passage poetically alludes to the king returning from the Avabhṛtha snāna — the sacred concluding bath of the Aśvamedha sacrifice — after which Agastya enquires whether the ritual ablution following the great Vedic sacrifice had been properly performed.

The poem Madurai Kanchi, composed by Mankudi Maruthanar, describes the capital city of the Pandya king Nedunjeliyan in great detail. Among the many scenes of the bustling city, the poet specifically refers to Brahmins reciting the Vedas and performing Vedic rituals in Madurai. The poem portrays the city as a place where Vedic chanting resounds continuously, indicating the royal patronage extended by the Pandya kings to Vedic scholars and sacrificial rituals. The epic Silappadhikaram provides further corroboration. It describes that when Kovalan entered Madurai, the capital of the Pandyas, he heard the resonant chanting of Brahmins reciting the four Vedas (**“நான்மறை அந்தணர் நவின்ற ஓதையும்”)**

**Medieval Era**

The medieval inscriptions and copper plate grants of the Pandyas contain numerous references to their patronage of the Vedas and Vedic rituals. One of the earliest among these is the Ilayanputhur copper plates of the Pandya king Arikesari Maravarman, also known as Nindra Seer Nedumaran (Circa 7th Century CE). These plates record a land grant made to Narayana Bhatta Somayaji, who is described as a learned Brahmana well versed in the four Vedas, a performer of three yajñas daily, and an expert in the śāstras as well as Vedanta. This inscription is significant as it constitutes one of the earliest references to the practice of Vedanta in Pandya Nadu.

***Ilayanputhur Copper Plates***

The same copper plates also state that Nedumaran Pandya performed the Hiranyagarbha and Tulabhara donations, which are described in the Vedic and Dharmashastra traditions as royal rites of great merit. Interestingly, similar information is also recorded in the Vaigai river-bed inscription of the same king, further confirming the close association of the Pandya rulers with Vedic ritual practices.

The Dalavaypuram copper plates likewise emphasise that the Pandya kings performed several Aśvamedha and Rājasūya yajñas. The inscription also notes that the coronation of the Pandya rulers was conducted according to the rites prescribed in the Atharva Veda (Sloka 12), showing that even the royal consecration ceremonies followed Vedic injunctions.

**Later Pandyas**

This tradition continued during the period of the later Pandyas. The meikeerthi of Maravarman Sundara Pandyan I (1216–1238 CE) proudly proclaims that during his reign the four Vedas flourished and the five great yajñas were regularly performed:

**“நால்வகை வேதமும் நவின்றுடன் வளர,**

**ஐவகை வேள்வியும் செய்வினை இயற்ற”**

Similarly, the meikeerthi of Jatavarman Sundara Pandya I (1251 - 1271 CE) states that under his rule the three sacred fires and the yajñas performed by the Brahmanas, based on the four Vedas and the six Vedangas, prospered greatly:

**“முத்தீயின் நால்வேதத்து அருமறையோர் உரை**

**வேள்வி ஆறங்கமுடன் சிறப்ப”**

Together, these inscriptions demonstrate that the Pandya kings not only patronised temples but also actively supported Vedic scholarship, sacrificial rituals, continuing a tradition that can be traced back to the Sangam age.
