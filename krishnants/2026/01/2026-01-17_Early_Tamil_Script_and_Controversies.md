# Early Tamil Script and Controversies

In ancient times, Tamil is believed to have been written in a script known as “Kaṇ Ezhuthu” (கண் எழுத்து), literally meaning “eye-shaped script”, so called because the characters were said to resemble the form of the eye. References to this script occur in the Tamil epic Silappadhikaram, which states:

***Vamba mākaḷ tam peyar poṟitta***

***kaṇṇeḻuttup padutta eṉṉum palpoti***

***iru patināyiram kaṇṇeḻuttup paduttana***

***kaipuṇai sakaṭamum***

This passage indicates the prevalence of Kaṇ Ezhuthu during the period of Silappadhikaram. Since the epic is generally dated to around the 2nd century CE, it may be inferred that Kaṇ Ezhuthu was in use at least until that time. It has been suggested by some scholars that later scripts such as Vaṭṭeḻuttu may have evolved from Kaṇ Ezhuthu; however, this remains a subject of ongoing research. Importantly, no direct physical evidence—in the form of inscriptions or palm-leaf manuscripts—of Kaṇ Ezhuthu has survived to the present day.

The earliest attested and historically verifiable Tamil script is what is now known as Tamil-Brāhmī, which represents a regional adaptation of Aśokan Brāhmī to suit the phonetic requirements of Tamil. In 1903, the first Tamil-Brāhmī inscription was discovered at Keezhavalavu, near Madurai. Subsequent discoveries followed at sites such as Maṅgulam, Anaimalai, Aḻagarmalai, and several other locations in the Madurai region.

It was Thiru. K. V. Subramanya Iyer who first successfully deciphered the early Tamil inscriptions written in the Brāhmī script and demonstrated that the script had been systematically customised to suit the phonology of the Tamil language. In doing so, he rendered an invaluable service to the study of the Tamil script and language. Unfortunately, he was unable to continue this line of research in greater depth.

This pioneering work was later carried forward and significantly expanded by Thiru. Iravatham Mahadevan, who, through extensive fieldwork and analysis—especially of inscriptions discovered at Pugalur and several other sites—systematically established the corpus and formally coined the term “Tamil Brāhmī.” Subsequent archaeological excavations revealed Tamil Brāhmī inscriptions not only on rock surfaces but also on potsherds, rings, seals, and other artefacts, notably from sites such as Karur, highlighting its widespread social and commercial usage.

It is noteworthy that a large concentration of Tamil Brāhmī inscriptions occurs in and around Madurai, the capital of the Pandya country. Literary traditions speak of the Pandyas as patrons of the Tamil Sangam (Academy) at Madurai, and this geographical clustering may point to royal, mercantile, or institutional patronage behind the early spread of Tamil literacy.

However, the origin of the Tamil Brāhmī script remains a subject of scholarly debate. Certain Tamil exceptionalist positions argue that Tamil Brāhmī originated independently in Tamilakam and that Aśokan Brāhmī was later derived or adapted from a southern prototype. Others propose that both Tamil Brāhmī and Aśokan Brāhmī evolved from a common ancestral script, diverging regionally to accommodate different linguistic needs. Proponents of these views cite early Tamil Brāhmī graffiti on potsherds and cave markings, some of which are tentatively dated to before the 3rd century BCE, the period conventionally associated with Aśoka. A recent example often cited is the potsherds recovered from the excavations at Keeladi, which are claimed to be datable to the 6th century CE. However, no supporting archaeological or scientific evidence has yet been published to substantiate this chronological attribution.

Nevertheless, both these theories face significant challenges. A closer examination of the context, content, and physical placement of early Tamil Brāhmī inscriptions is essential to understanding their nature. The majority of these inscriptions are found in rock-cut caves used by ascetics, primarily Śramaṇas (Jain monks). They frequently occur on stone beds or head-rest cuttings carved into cave floors. Another striking feature is their placement at considerable and often inaccessible heights, suggesting deliberate and formal execution rather than casual graffiti. Epigraphically, these records are usually brief—often just one or two lines—and are predominantly donative or commemorative, recording gifts made to the ascetics, likely for the provision of food and basic sustenance to renunciants who lived austere and reclusive lives in these isolated locations.

What is perhaps even more striking than the script itself is the language employed in these inscriptions. While the majority of them are recognisably Tamil, they are very often intermixed with Prākrit forms. Moreover, the Tamil used in these records appears rudimentary and unrefined, not conforming to the grammatical structures or literary standards prevalent during the same period, particularly when contrasted with the sophistication evident in Sangam literature. Certain verbal forms, such as “Koḍupittān”, are anomalous—neither attested in contemporaneous literary Tamil nor seen in later linguistic traditions. On the face of it, the language of these inscriptions gives the impression of being composed by individuals who were in the process of learning Tamil rather than native, trained users of the language.

Taken together, these linguistic features strongly suggest that the Brāhmī script was introduced into Tamilakam by itinerant Jain and Buddhist ascetics who travelled southward from northern India. As they attempted to adapt the script for recording Tamil, they progressively modified it to accommodate Tamil phonetics, resulting in the introduction of distinctive characters, particularly for sounds such as ṟa, ḷa, and ṉa, which were absent in the northern Brāhmī tradition. Further supporting this view is the presence of Tamilised forms of Sanskrit or Prākrit vocabulary in several inscriptions—for instance, Adhiṣṭhānam appearing as Athittanam—reflecting phonological accommodation by non-native speakers.

In sum, the available epigraphic and linguistic evidence points to a complex and multi-layered process in the emergence of Tamil Brāhmī rather than a simple, linear origin narrative. While Tamil Brāhmī unquestionably represents a successful adaptation of the Brāhmī script to Tamil phonology, the language of the earliest inscriptions themselves raises important questions about the identity and linguistic competence of their authors.

The frequent mixing of Tamil with Prākrit, the presence of non-standard and ungrammatical Tamil forms, and the occurrence of verb constructions and lexical items unattested in both Sangam literature and later Tamil traditions suggest that these inscriptions were not composed within a mature, native Tamil literary milieu. Instead, they appear to reflect a transitional linguistic register, possibly produced by individuals acquiring Tamil as a second language. The Tamilised rendering of Sanskrit and Prākrit terms further strengthens this impression.

The archaeological contexts of these inscriptions—predominantly associated with rock-cut cave dwellings and stone beds used by Jain and Buddhist ascetics—provide an important explanatory framework. These ascetics, who are historically known to have migrated from northern India into Tamilakam, likely carried the Brāhmī script with them and adapted it pragmatically to local linguistic needs. The gradual modification of letter forms to represent Tamil-specific sounds should therefore be understood as a functional process of localisation, not necessarily as evidence of independent scriptual origin.

Given these considerations, arguments for either a purely indigenous Tamil origin of Brāhmī or a simple north-to-south transmission remain inadequate when treated in isolation. A more satisfactory explanation lies in recognising Tamil Brāhmī as the product of cultural interaction, religious mobility, and linguistic accommodation. Future research must continue to integrate epigraphic form, linguistic usage, archaeological context, and historical movement, consciously avoiding ideological or emotional predispositions, in order to arrive at a more nuanced understanding of the early history of the Tamil script.
