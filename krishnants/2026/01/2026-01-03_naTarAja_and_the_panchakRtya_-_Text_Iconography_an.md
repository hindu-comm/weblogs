# Naṭarāja and the Pañcakṛtya - Text, Iconography, and the Five Cosmic Functions

The Thiruvathirai festival is celebrated across Tamil Nadu today. The nakshatra Thiruvathirai (Ārdrā) is traditionally associated with Śiva, particularly in His Naṭarāja form. At Chidambaram, the sacred Ārdrā Darśana is observed in the early hours of the morning, with special pūjās performed for Naṭarāja.

The dance of Śiva, celebrated as Śiva Tāṇḍava, is referred to in several ancient texts and is also extensively represented in temple sculptures across India. Tamil literary traditions, in particular, extol Śiva as “Kūthan”, the supreme dancer. The Sangam work Kalithogai vividly describes multiple forms of Śiva’s dance, performed on different occasions. One such form is Koḍukoṭṭi, which the text poetically portrays as follows:

*“O eight-armed Nīlakaṇṭha! You taught the Vedas to the Brahmins who mastered the six Vedāṅgas; You set Tripura ablaze; Gaṅgā dwells in Your matted locks; Yet You remain beyond the grasp of words—now listen.*

*When You perform the Koḍukoṭṭi dance, creating and dissolving the universes to the rhythm of beating drums, is it the Goddess who gives the tāḷa, the ‘Śīr’, for Your dance?”*

It is believed that Śiva performs His five supreme cosmic functions—Sṛṣṭi (creation), Sthiti (preservation), Saṃhāra(destruction), Tirodhāna (veiling), and Anugraha (grace)—through different forms of Tāṇḍava. These distinct expressions of the cosmic dance may be briefly explored in the following paragraphs.

#### **Kālika Tāṇḍava**

The first cosmic function—**creation (*****Sṛṣṭi*****)**— is performed by Śiva through the Kālika Tāṇḍava. This form should not be confused with Kālī Tāṇḍava; it is distinct and is also known as Muni Tāṇḍava. In this dance, Śiva is depicted with eight hands, holding various attributes such as the pāśa (noose), fire, bell, kapāla (skull), and the ḍamaru (drum). The legs are positioned in a square stance, suggesting stability and order at the moment of creation. The left front hand is usually shown in a free-hanging posture, most often in Gaja-hasta mudrā.

This form of Naṭarāja is represented in several places, most notably at Badami. Here, Śiva is sculpted with eighteen arms. Although a few arms are damaged, the remaining hands clearly hold the drum, akṣamālā (rosary), axe, sword, and triśūla (trident). Three hands are shown in the Abhaya, Varada, and Sūci mudrās. The right leg is firmly planted on the ground, while the left leg is slightly raised. The upper left arm hangs loosely across the right side in Gaja-hasta, lending grace to the otherwise dynamic composition.

#### **Sandhyā Tāṇḍava**

The second cosmic function—**protection (*****Sthiti*****)**— is performed by Śiva through the Sandhyā Tāṇḍava. This dance is also known as Gaurī Tāṇḍava, Rakṣā Tāṇḍava, and Bhujanga Lālita. Since it was performed in the presence of Gaurī (Pārvatī), it is called Gaurī Tāṇḍava. As it takes place during the evening twilight (*sandhyā*), it is referred to as Sandhyā Tāṇḍava. During this form, Śiva specifically adorns the serpent Kārkoṭaka, and hence it is also called Bhujanga Lālita, “the graceful dance with the serpent.” The Kāraṇāgama states that while Śiva performed this dance, Brahmā, Viṣṇu, Indra, and the other Devas stood as witnesses.

This dance is traditionally linked with the legend of Samudra Manthana, the churning of the Milk Ocean. When the terrible Hālāhala poison emerged from the ocean, spewed forth by Vāsuki, the Devas fled, unable to bear its heat. Śiva alone consumed the poison, while Pārvatī restrained it at His throat. Though the poison did not truly affect Him, Śiva feigned distress, closed His eyes, and remained motionless throughout the night. The Devas performed uninterrupted arcana until dawn. At Pradoṣa kāla the following evening, Śiva opened His eyes and performed this Sandhyā Tāṇḍava, assuring the protection of the cosmos.

Iconographically, while some forms of Sandhyā Tāṇḍava Naṭarāja show eight or ten arms, the more common representation has four arms. One of the right hands usually holds the ḍamaru, while the other is shown in Abhaya mudrā, or occasionally holding a peacock feather. One of the left hands commonly bears a serpent, while the other rests in Gaja-hasta. Notably, this form does not include fire in the hands, nor is Apasmāra (Muyalaka) shown beneath the Lord’s feet.

There are numerous temples where Naṭarāja is depicted performing the Sandhyā Tāṇḍava. Among the earliest known representations is the Pāṇḍya-period rock-cut temple at Tirumalapuram, which preserves a very early iconographic form of this dance.

***(Thanjavur Rajarajeswaram Temple)***

A striking representation is found at the Rajarajeswaram Temple. Here, a tall image of Naṭarāja performing Sandhyā Tāṇḍava is sculpted in the sandāra passage on the first storey of the vimāna. Significantly, this form faces west, symbolically marking the evening twilight, the prescribed time for Sandhyā Tāṇḍava. Numerous textual and epigraphic sources attest to the importance of this dance form.

In the Subhāṣita Ratna Kośa, composed by Ratnākara, in the section describing Somanātha Prasthiti, it is stated that during Sandhyā Tāṇḍava, as the Lord’s sacred hands whirl in motion, mountains, winds, and oceans themselves are set into rotation, creating a storm-like cosmic upheaval.

When Kālidāsa sings of Mahākāla Śiva’s Sandhyā Tāṇḍava, he describes a multi-armed dance posture known as Bhujatāruvanam. In this form, the Sandhyā Tāṇḍava Murti does not have Muyalakan (Apasmāra) beneath His feet. The iconography may show eight, ten, or twelve arms, and the Lord may be depicted wearing a serpent around His waist and holding another aloft above His head.

According to the Kāraṇāgama, one who performs ritual worship of Śiva in the proper manner on the full-moon day of the month of Chitrai and beholds this Sandhyā Tāṇḍava is granted prosperity (śrī), victory (kotra), and enduring fame (yaśas).

#### **Saṃhāra Tāṇḍava**

The destructive aspect of Śiva is expressed through the **Saṃhāra** Tāṇḍava, performed at midnight. After living beings exhaust the results of their karma, destruction ensues, both at the individual level and at the cosmic scale during Pralaya. In Tamil tradition, this dance is known as Ūḻikkoothu, meaning the dance performed at the end of the universe. In this state, Śiva alone remains; all else dissolves.

Scriptures describe Śiva performing Saṃhāra Tāṇḍava with eight arms. The right hands typically carry the triśūla, pāśa, and ḍamaru, while the left hands bear the kapāla (skull) and fire, with one hand shown in a free-hanging posture. Śiva’s face is portrayed as fierce and wrathful, emphasizing the overwhelming force of dissolution. Fire is the central symbol of this dance, and iconographic representations of Saṃhāra Tāṇḍava are rare in temple sculpture.

#### **Tripura Saṃhāra Tāṇḍava**

The Tripura Saṃhāra Tāṇḍava is associated with the function of **Tirodhāna** (veiling or concealment). The three cities (*Tripura*) symbolize the three impurities (mummalam) that bind the soul—āṇava (egoic ignorance), karma(bondage of action), and māyā (material illusion). Since these impurities remain hidden within the soul, their destruction through Śiva’s action is understood as the unveiling of truth, and hence this dance signifies the hidden cosmic function.

According to legend, the sons of Tārakāsura—Tārakākṣa, Vidyunmālī, and Kamalākṣa—manifested as three fortified cities: gold in heaven, silver in the mid-air, and iron on earth. These cities moved continuously and aligned only once in a thousand years, rendering the demons nearly invincible. When their oppression disrupted dharma, the gods sought Śiva’s intervention. At the destined moment, Śiva appeared as Tripurāntaka, mounting the cosmic chariot—Earth as the chariot, Mount Meru as the bow, Viṣṇu as the arrow, Brahmā as the charioteer, and the Sun and Moon as wheels—symbolizing the unified action of the cosmos.

With a single arrow, Śiva reduced all three cities to ashes, demonstrating that divine wisdom (jñāna) acts decisively when time matures.

***(Tripurantaka - Chola Mural, Thanjavur)***

After destroying the three cities, Śiva is said to have performed a series of dances described as Pāṇḍaraṅgam and Koḍukoṭṭi, danced upon a white stage. The Suprabhedāgama states that Śiva performed eight distinct dancesfollowing the Tripura Saṃhāra, with Umā Devī providing the rhythmic accompaniment, while Śiva Himself embodied the cosmic movement.

#### **Ūrdhva Tāṇḍava**

Ūrdhva Tāṇḍava symbolizes the **Anugraha (grace)** function of Śiva. This dance is also referred to as Kālī Tāṇḍava, as Śiva is said to have performed it in the presence of Kālī. The Śaiva saint Appar (Tirunāvukkaracar) extols this extraordinary dance as *“Āḍinār perum kūttu Kālī kāṇa”* and describes it as one of the most difficult dance forms, achievable only by Śiva Himself.

The posture of Ūrdhva Tāṇḍava is also alluded to in the Bharata Muni’s Nāṭya Śāstra, where it appears in a form closely related to Lalāṭa Tilakam, albeit with certain variations. According to tradition, this dance arose from a divine competition between Śiva and Kālī. When the contest reached an impasse and it became impossible to declare a winner, Śiva suddenly raised His leg straight above His shoulders and executed the Ūrdhva Tāṇḍava. Kālī, bound by modesty and cosmic decorum, could not replicate the posture, and Śiva thus emerged victorious. This momentous dance is believed to have taken place at Thiruvalangadu, a site deeply revered in Śaiva tradition.

***(Ūrdhva Tāṇḍava - Madurai)***

Numerous sculptures and bronze icons depict the Ūrdhva Tāṇḍava posture. A notable example from Madurai vividly captures the dynamism of this pose, where the veins and sinews of the right arm are shown taut as Śiva lifts His leg in a full vertical extension—an artistic testimony to both divine energy and sculptural mastery.

#### **Ānanda Tāṇḍava**

Ānanda Tāṇḍava is the supreme dance of Śiva, encompassing all five cosmic functions—Sṛṣṭi (creation), Sthiti(preservation), Saṃhāra (dissolution), Tirodhāna (veiling), and Anugraha (grace). Scriptures state that this dance was performed at Chidambaram, within the sacred Golden Hall (Kanaka Sabhā). The Ārdrā Darśana in the Tamil month of Mārgaḻi is considered especially auspicious for beholding Naṭarāja in this form. Tradition holds that Śiva performed this dance at the request of the sages Patañjali and Vyāghrapāda. Since all five functions are simultaneously enacted, this dance is also known as “Pañcakṛtya Paramānanda Nartanam.” In this form, Apasmāra (Muyalaka)—symbolizing ignorance—is shown beneath the right foot of Naṭarāja.

***(Ānanda Tāṇḍava - Gangai Konda Cholpapuram)***

Numerous texts, including the Āgamas, Śilpa Śāstras, and Śaiva canonical works, meticulously describe and glorify this form. The Kāraṇāgama states that Ānanda Tāṇḍava is the dance in which the white-hued Parameśvara appears with four arms, three eyes, and expanded matted locks, adorned with peacock feathers, the crescent moon, datura flowers, and the river Gaṅgā. He wears a makara-kuṇḍala in the left ear and a karṇa-patra in the right. Sacred thread and ornaments are worn across the chest, and He is clad in a tiger-skin garment. The front left hand is extended outward, the front right hand displays the gesture of divine instruction (aruṭ-kuri), the rear right handholds the ḍamaru, and the rear left hand bears the flame of fire. His foot rests upon Muyalakan, while the upraised left leg is gracefully curved, its tip turned towards the right—this posture is designated as Ānanda Tāṇḍava.

According to the Śilparatna, a luminous halo surrounds Śiva, while the Pūrvakāraṇāgama notes that the fire held in the left hand has three tongues. Between the two arms on the left side, the tiger skin is visible, and Muyalakan is depicted with three distinct folds, completing the iconographic canon of this exalted form.

Together, these varied Tāṇḍava forms present Śiva not merely as a cosmic dancer, but as the total embodiment of divine function, philosophy, and aesthetics. From Kālika Tāṇḍava, where creation takes form, through Sandhyā Tāṇḍava, which assures protection and balance, to Saṃhāra and Tripura Saṃhāra, where dissolution and the removal of hidden bonds occur, and finally culminating in Ūrdhva and Ānanda Tāṇḍava, where grace and supreme bliss prevail, each dance expresses a distinct aspect of the Pañcakṛtya of Mahādeva. Rooted in Āgamic prescriptions, celebrated in Sangam and later Tamil literature, elaborated by Sanskrit poets, and immortalised in temple sculpture and Chola bronzes, these dances operate simultaneously on ritual, artistic, mythic, and metaphysical planes. Ultimately, the vision of Naṭarāja affirms a profound Śaiva truth: that the universe is sustained, dissolved, veiled, revealed, and liberated not through chaos, but through rhythmic order, divine consciousness, and compassionate grace, eternally expressed in the cosmic dance of Śiva.
