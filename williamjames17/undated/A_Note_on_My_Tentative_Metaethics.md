Metaethics is a field to which I have given woefully little of my time. Until recently, it wasn’t even something I bothered to articulate a coherent view about. I’ve always found that, whenever the necessity to treat moral issues arose in other intellectual contexts, dealing with them more or less on a case-by-case basis proved sufficient enough for my purposes. A rather unideal consequence of this, though, is that the internal logic of my own moral thought, implicit in the heuristics I was using whenever I had to, remained hopelessly opaque to me. I barely even knew how to accurately characterise them. Due to other philosophical commitments I have, I often lazily concluded that I probably held to some form of moral non-cognitivism or another. Perhaps universal prescriptivism. All without having the diligence to check if those characterisations stuck. Upon prompting by a Twitter mutual, I decided to examine what my metaethics actually were— and what do you know? They don’t stick.

I still am not entirely sure how to characterise my metaethics in any sort of definitive way, so I suppose this is where this essay comes in— here, I want to adumbrate my views on the subject as lucidly as I can, precisely to see how others might characterise them. I’ll demonstrate my thoughts on the matter through these four ‘axioms’ on the nature of proscribability. One could just as easily formulate an analogous set of axioms using *prescribability*, but the logic should be clear nevertheless:

1.  *‘Proscribability’ is the quality of a behaviour wherein an
    individual will seek to prevent its occurrence due to its
    detrimentality to the integrity of their self.*

2.  *Insofar as the spontaneous attribution of intentional states onto
    others is integral to the functioning of selfhood itself, the self
    is intersubjectively constituted.*

3.  *Insofar as the self is intersubjectively constituted, behaviours
    not directed towards a given self, or even behaviours initiated by
    that self, can nevertheless be detrimental to that self’s integrity,
    and thus acquire proscribability.*

4.  *For any behaviour X, if X is ‘wrong,’ then for any individual A who
    accurately represents their intersubjective constitution to
    themselves, A will proscribe it.*

Ethics, on this system, is the behavioural superstructure arising from the existence of a multiplicity of individuals constituting themselves *as selves among selves.* It arises in and through, but is necessarily not reducible to, self-interest. It is as inevitable to the rational agent as self-interest is. This is manifestly not any sort of moral non-cognitivism, since moral statements are clearly truth-apt here. The performative characteristics of proscription and prescription are nevertheless quite important, but in a manner that preserves cognitive content in statements concerning the qualities of proscribability and prescribability more generally. Hence, it is clearly not *universal* prescriptivism. Neither of them even remotely fit the bill.

Still, I’m not really sure how this should be positively characterised. Perhaps as a sort of Kantianism? This is where my lack of reading in the subject really does rear its head. In any case, I’d love to hear people’s thoughts as to what this looks like to them. I do hope to be developing this more extensively in the feature and answering objections or criticisms as comprehensively as I am able to. This could be quite interesting indeed!
