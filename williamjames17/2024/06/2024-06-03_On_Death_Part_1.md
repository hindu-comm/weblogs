One of the most salient, if overlooked, features of the question of post-mortem survival (“What happens to us after we die?”) is that it is, essentially, an extension of the problem of other minds— if we are willing to accept that ‘selfhood’ can be instantiated in other loci (that is, in this case, other individuals), what warrants us doing so? Under what conditions can selfhood be predicated to external phenomena, if at all? Where is the definitional boundary drawn? Answers to these questions are necessarily required before any investigation into what becomes of selfhood following a transformation in its biological substrate can be undertaken.

This, then, brings us to where our inquiry ought to begin: an analysis of the essential characteristics and definitional boundaries of ‘selfhood’ itself. What even *is* selfhood? Once more, I feel that a distinctive feature of the phenomenon under consideration has flown under the radar— its peculiar relationship to the principle of the identity of indiscernibles.

The principle of the identity of indiscernibles, formulated by German philosopher and mathematician Gottfried Leibniz, can be displayed as follows:

∀F (Fx ↔ Fy) ↔ (x = y)

Meaning that the proposition that, for any property F possessed by x, F is necessarily also possessed by y, is simply equivalent to the proposition that x and y are identical. The intuitive appeal of this principle becomes obvious when it is considered that spatiotemporal location is also an individuating property, albeit a relational one. Black (1952)’s counterexample of two equidistant spheres otherwise identical to one another in an otherwise empty possible universe fails due to the fact that the very positing of their twofoldness depends on the presupposition of an observer, a third object relative to which they possess relational spatial properties. Without this presupposition, any description of these two spheres is functionally equivalent to a description of a single sphere. Now, my point here is not to defend Leibniz’s principle or to demonstrate its universality, but to demonstrate the unique manner in which consciousness violates this principle. A simple corollary of the principle is as follows:

1.  ∀F(Fx ↔ Fy) ↔ (x = y)

is equivalent to

2.  (x = y) ↔ ∀F (Fx ↔ Fy)

which, in turn, entails that

3.  (x ≠ y) ↔ ∃F(Fx ⊻ Fy)

Now, let us define a capacity C with an unspecified range of property variables F in which an object x can be considered, such that:

C(F1, F2,… Fn)→∀x ∃F(F1 ⊻ F2 ⊻ … ⊻ Fn)

From 3., we know that the properties of two distinct objects x and y are not necessarily correlated and can (and indeed must in at least one instance) differ from one another. Thus, it does not violate the law of non-contradiction for x and y, when considered in the same capacity C, to simultaneously possess different variable values, despite this theoretical occurrence violating non-contradiction in an individual object. This can be exemplified in the fact that there is obviously nothing contradictory in two distinct objects, when considered in the capacity of colour, to be, respectively, red and blue in the same manner as each other. Wait, wasn't this supposed to be about death again?

Anyways, the purpose of this digression was to demonstrate the behaviour of standard properties with regards to their objects. Consciousness, I submit, violates this behaviour. *Consciousness is that which cannot be simultaneously nor disjunctively predicated of objects without violating the law of non-contradiction.* This is because consciousness is one’s individuality *qua* individuated by neurobiological structure. But why should *that* be considered the case?

It should be, in my opinion, due to the extraordinary explanatory successes of neuroscience in accounting for individuated mental faculties, and on the plea that it is far more parsimonious to posit the mental as consisting of the linguistic hypotheses formed in response to the behaviours neurobiological structures produce than as a seperate substance entirely. If an appeal to qualia is made in response, I reply that they are indeed explicable within this paradigm. Take, for example, a perception of the colour red. It may be claimed that, in one’s rememberence of perceiving the colour red, the quale of red inheres just as it did in the actual perception. However, one’s supposed postperceptual cognition of a red quale may instead be a *reflexive* cognition that takes one's past cognition, *qua* active perception of the red wavelength, as its object. Given that the current cognition stands in a causal relation with the prior cognition it takes as its object, and this cognition in turn was incited by the active perception of a certain wavelength of light, this causal continuum produces the impression of an isomorphism between the perceptual object (in this case, the red light) and one's recollection of it. If it is claimed one can recollect red independently of any perceived instantiation of it, this can be easily explained as an artificially created exemplar instance of red deriving from multiple perceptual incidents.

Thus, consciousness refers to the perceptual system summatively integrated and individuated by one’s neurobiological apparatus. Any one conscious entity cannot licitly predicate consciousness to another, as that would be tantamount to identifying that other with itself. It is not predicated of individuals such that it attempts to refer to an inclusive category or genus they are members of, but instead refers to the very process of differentiation that allows individual members to be defined as such relative to the various genuses they *do* participate in.

But does this account not run the risk of solipsism? What is actually going on when we refer to external individuals, and how does the phenomenon we call “death” affect such references? I intend to confront these questions in the second part of this piece.
