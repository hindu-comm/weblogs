Christian exclusivism, among its adherents, is generally taken to be necessitated by the *lakṣyalakṣaṇasambandha* (that is, the relation of *definiens* and *definiendum* constituting a valid definition) indicated by Jesus’ statement in John 14:6: “I am the way and the truth and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me.” In this verse, the *lakṣaṇa* (or definiens) is the phrase "the way, the truth and the life" and the *lakṣya* (or definiendum) is Jesus himself, *qua* sole means of salvation. ‘Way,’ ‘truth’ and ‘life’ (in Greek, ‘ὁδὸς,’ ‘ἀλήθεια’ and ‘ζωή’) are in a state of grammatical apposition (*sāmānādhikaraṇyam*) and thus denote a common object. Jesus, it is implied, bears a relationship of absolute identity (*aikyam)* with this object, and therefore, *qua* this identification, is the sole means of ‘coming onto the Father.’ As Jesus' nature is defined in such a manner, it is taken that this statement necessarily entails exclusivism. However, the inherent grammatical structure of this statement undermines any attempt to construe it as a lakṣaṇa in such a manner, and I intend to demonstrate, relatively succinctly and intuitively, why.

In this statement, the type of sāmānādhikaraṇyam that inheres within the three relevant predicate nouns is specifically one of *viśeṣaṇe sāmānādhikaraṇyam,* that is, qualificatory apposition, wherein the appositioned nouns maintain their coreferentiality to their common substratum by mutually qualifying one another. It is this, I submit, that vitiates this statement’s potential as a definiens defining Jesus *qua* exclusive means of salvation. The reasoning, I believe, is quite simple. Predicate nouns in a state of mutual qualificatory apposition, in order to intelligibly *qualify* one another such that they are not redundant relative to the others, obviously must differentiate themselves from the other qualifiers by qualifying their common object in a unique capacity. While the *object* remains the same, the *capacity qua itself* might not. ‘Philip the Second, King of Spain’ designates the same object with two noun phrases in apposition to one another, and nevertheless, aikyam does not inhere between the historical personage of King Philip the Second and the position of monarch of Spain *qua* itself, as there were multiple individuals who inhabited that position over time. That, of course, would not compromise King Philip’s ability to veridically say, while alive, “I am Philip the Second, King of Spain.” Analogously, the three predicate nouns in John 14:6, when taken together as a lakṣaṇa, denote three different capacities in which their common object (that except by which no one can come to the Father) can be considered. This disjunction in sense, inherent in qualificatory apposition, between the three predicate nouns means that Jesus’ statement, *even as an ‘am’-statement*, cannot be sufficient to evince aikyam.

As a result, John 14:6 is entirely explicable under an interpretation of *prakāraprakārībhāva* or *dharmadharmībhāva,* in which Jesus himself and the predicate nouns within his statement inhere as modalities within their common object, rather than in a relation of absolute identity. Jesus could easily be saying that he is a *dharma* (constituent modality) of the *dharmī* (substance) that is the way, the truth and the life, and that the latter is the sole means of attaining God, therefore, *qua* the degree of *abheda* between *dharma* and *dharmī*, this extends to him. John 14:6 is, in essence, an analogue to the statement “the wave is water, and it alone hydrates.” If *dharmadharmībhāva* is taken, the statement "I am the way, the truth and the Life" is meant to be understood in the same way that "the wave is water" holds as a relative identity relation without the converse holding in return, and that the wave, *qua* water, is the exclusive means of hydration— naturally, it would be absurd to state that the wave *qua individual wave* is the exclusive means of hydration. Thus, "no one comes to the father except through me” and “it alone hydrates” are to be understood as contextually bound by the immediately antecedent statements (the identification with water in general and the way, the truth and the life in general), which qualify in which capacity the later statement is to be understood. Just as the *tattvamasi mahavākya* need not be interpreted in terms of aikyam, so need not this vākya. The fact that John 14:6, when taken as a lakṣaṇa, is capable of coherently taking this relation as its lakṣya, means that it fatally underspecifies the desired lakṣya (of Jesus inhering in an absolute identity relation with the way, the truth and the life) and thus cannot serve as a lakṣaṇa for it, having incurred the defect of ativyāpti, of being too generic.

Right off the bat, it may be objected that the apposition in John 14:6 is not *qualificatory* at all but is instead *identitary*, that is, an instance of *aikye**sāmānādhikaraṇyam*. In this scenario, ‘way,’ ‘truth’ and ‘life’ function akin to the three predicate nouns in “*satyaṁ jñānam anantaṁ brahma”* (Taittīriyopaniṣad 2.1.1.) as interpreted in the Kevalādvaitika tradition; not as positive conceptual qualifiers but as negators that merely indirectly denote their common object. This interpretation ought to be rejected, as it would render the relation of the predicate nouns to the subject identifying itself with them unintelligible, and therefore meaningless with reference to the historical Jesus as an individual.

Another possible argument against this interpretation is that it undermines the obvious rhetorical intent of Jesus' statement in John 14:6, but this is not accurate either. Jesus is telling his disciples that he has definitively instantiated and revealed the exclusive path to salvation, and anything they might find outside of being his disciple would only be salvific insofar as they also embodied the same way, truth and life embodied in himself, making the initial rejection entirely redundant. Jesus has presented the sole truth to them, and by rejecting him, they reject that truth. That much remains the same under either interpretation.
