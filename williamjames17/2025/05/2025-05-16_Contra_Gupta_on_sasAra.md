<div class="pullquote">

*नमः कृष्णायाच्युताय प्रेमसुधासुधारिणे।*

*ममप्रियोत्तमाय च राधाकान्ताय स्वामिने॥*

On the site that shall not be named, I (relatively) recently wrote a short thread on a claim I often see trotted out, even by Hindus themselves, concerning the *karma-phala-saṁsthāna* underlying the traditional Hindu doctrine of metempsychosis: that there is a direct and discrete causative linkage between the experiences one undergoes and one’s actions in past lifetimes. This, then, would naturally lead to the conclusion, better or worse, that victims of horrendous crimes are receiving their just desert for their past actions. By the lights of the tradition, however, this is complete nonsense. An infinite number of past lives necessitates an endless backlog of unfructified karmas both positive and negative, for every individual. When one individual harms another, they are indirectly facilitating the release of a specific configuration of negative karma from the victim’s backlog *in accordance* *with the act performed, not necessarily in accordance with any particular configuration of negative karma incurred through a prior act of the victim.* Naturally, of course, the perpetrator in turn receives an amount of negative karma proportionate to how *disproportionate* the configuration of negative karma released on the victim was, but even then, that this amount will fructify in exactly this configuration is not a given. Thus, the conclusion (and oftentimes criticism) that victims of heinous crimes are merely receiving their just desert on Hinduism does not hold. Case closed.

Writing on this, however, did make me curious as to how the doctrine has been treated in the context of contemporary analytic philosophy, particularly if similar misconceptions or oversimplification have crept in and whether the full facility of the tradition has been utilised by those that may consider themselves the doctrine’s defenders. As it turns out, most recently, the doctrine of karma has received explicit treatment through the work of Akshay Gupta, who received his PhD in Religious Studies at the University of Cambridge in 2023. Particularly, I would like to focus on certain claims Gupta makes in his *A Close Examination of Beginningless Karman and Vedāntic First Causes* (Sophia, 2024) and *Reincarnation and Universal Salvation* with Alex Gallagher (Faith and Philosophy, forthcoming). In the latter paper, Gupta opines that the doctrine of karma is not only *consistent* with a doctrine of the fall of the self from a divine realm but that the latter is explanatorily preferable to the account of the self shared nigh-universally across Hindu traditions. I submit that not only are the stated reasons for such preferability entirely unconvincing, but the avenue of permissibility he attempts to find for the latter doctrine in the tradition is not viable either.

*The Argument*

In *Reincarnation and Universal Salvation*, Gupta presents an argument from elimination in favour of the fall-doctrine (henceforth referred to as FD) that runs as follows: by the law of truly large numbers, if a state of affairs X has a non-zero probability of obtaining, as the number of trials tends to infinity, probability of X obtaining, in turn, tends to one. Assuming that the probability of attaining liberation for any individual in a given lifetime is always non-zero, and an infinite number of past lives, the implication would be that the probability of every individual having already attained liberation tends to one. Manifestly, however, not every individual has already attained liberation. Therefore, unless we wish to concede that the probability of any individual soul attaining liberation in a given lifetime is zero, we must conclude that we have only had a finite number of past lifetimes and that this, in Hinduism, can apparently be accounted for by FD.

This argument, however, has a fatal omission— it fails to consider that, for many Hindu traditions, there is an additional factor at play in the attainment of liberation, one that effects a qualitative alteration in the system so as to render liberation possible— divine grace (*anugraha*). For both the Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava and Śaiva Saiddhāntika sampradāyas, among others, the initial catalyst of ultimate liberation is an inherent potency (*śakti*) of Īśvara exerting itself on the individual, one that is not supervenient upon on any action of the individual within the karmic causal milieu. In the key Saiddhāntika śāstra, the Pauṣkarāgama, for example, Śiva explicitly refutes the view that grace is conditional on the equalisation of an individual's negative and positive karma (*karma-sāmyam*) and states instead that the action of grace upon the individual is actually on what is called *mala-paripāka. Mala-paripāka*, for our purposes, refers to a qualitative change occurring in the potency (called the *malaśakti*) underlying the individual's participation in the karmic causal milieu, a change marking the start of its irreversible degeneration. The Gauḍīya tradition goes even further, with the renowned ācārya Viśvanātha Cakravartī, in his Mādhuryakādambinī, describing bhakti itself— considered to be derived from the *hlādinīśakti* of Īśvara— as being *yadṛccha*, or manifesting in the heart of the individual solely of its own sovereign accord, based on no external causal condition whatsoever.

For these traditions, the attainment of ultimate liberation is the product of a qualitative alteration in the causal dynamics of an individual’s existence, one originating radically externally from the system in which those dynamics occur. What this, in turn, means is that it is no contradiction for these systems to simultaneously uphold the existence of infinite past lives *and* that the probability of liberation for any given individual soul has not tended to one, as until the descent of anugraha or bhakti, the probability of liberation is indeed zero, since the karmic causal milieu is inherently incapable of internally generating the dynamics conducive to liberation. Consequently, it is not the case, within the karmic causal milieu, that liberation is a state of affairs with a non-zero probability of obtaining. It is only *after* the perturbation of the system effected by anugraha/bhakti that liberation becomes a genuine possibility, and it is only *then* that the law of truly large numbers could theoretically result in the probability of liberation tending to one over a sufficient number of lifetimes. This does not, however, actually occur, as the law of truly large numbers is only relevant for cases in which *statistical independence* between trials obtains. For these traditions, however, after anugraha, it does not obtain— the fructification of received anugraha through an individual's agency actively facilitates, in the altered causal dynamics they now inhabit, their continued entry into circumstances progressively more conduive to further fructification in future lifetimes, thus creating a cycle of positive reinforcement that results in the probability of liberation tending to one far sooner than it otherwise would under the law of truly large numbers— anugraha is a *necessary* condition for liberation, its condition of possibility even, but it is not a *sufficient* condition for its occurance. Thus, FD is not necessary to explicate the absence of universal liberation given infinite past lives.

*The Precedent*

In *A Close Examination of Beginningless Karman and Vedāntic First Causes*, Gupta also invokes FD in order to rebut the suggestion that the doctrine of karma would necessitate an infinite causal regress, though, since he provides alternative rebuttals that do not depend on FD in the paper itself, and thus does not claim that FD is explanatorily necessary here, I do not need to treat it here. What *does* require treatment, however, is Gupta's claim that FD is viable to hold in the Hindu tradition. Gupta claims that the doctrine can be found in the writings of Abhaya Caraṇāravinda Bhaktivedānta Svāmī, the renowned Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava missionary and founder of ISKCON. But is this true? More importantly, if it is true, would it be enough? The crucial scriptural source in this matter is the twentieth śloka of the Kṣetra-kṣetrajña-vibhāga-yoga of the Bhagavad-gītā, which reads:

> *prakṛtiṁ puruṣaṁ caiva viddhyanādī ubhāvapi।*
>
> *vikārāṁśca guṇāṁścaiva viddhi prakṛtisambhavān॥*
>
> *Know that both matter and consciousness are beginningless. Their
> emergent products and attributes are derivatives of matter.*

On FD, Gupta would have us believe that this verse, while specifiying the eternalitiy of matter and consciousness individually, does not necessarily imply that their *conjunction*, in the form of the individual's entanglement in the karmic causal milieu, is also eternal. The Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava sampradāya, though, unsuprisingly has a strong commentarial tradition on the Bhagavad-gītā, and thus has had plenty of opportunities to consolidate normative interpretations of its content. Viśvanātha Cakravartī, mentioned above, authored his own commentary, the Sārārthavarṣiṇī-ṭika, on the Gītā and interpets this verse as the speaker, Bhagavān Śrī Kṛṣṇa, answering two questions through it— how and why did the conjunction of souls with māyā occur? Both, he states, are answered by the use of the adjective *anādi*, ‘beginningless’. In regards to the former, anādi is taken to connote *na vidyate ādi karaṇam yayoḥ* — *“Their* (conjunction) *is without a first cause.”* For the latter question, anādi connotes that, as the conjunction is beginningless, it did not begin at any discrete instance in time. Similarly, the later Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava ācārya, Baladeva Vidyābhūṣaṇa, while commenting on this śloka, uses the adjectival compound *anādikālikatva* to describe the conjunction of the soul with māyā, specifying in no uncertain terms that what specifically is anādi is the period of time the conjunction has lasted. Given that Bhaktivedānta Svāmī readily acknowledged the authoriativeness of these ācāryas— and even dedicated his English translation and commentary on the Gītā to Baladeva— what could he have said that would lead Gupta to claim he diverges from established opinion and would endorse FD? Writing in the aforementioned English translation and commentary, Bhaktivedānta Svāmī comments on the same śloka as follows:

> *Actually the living entity is originally the spiritual part and
> parcel of the Supreme Lord, but due to his rebellious nature, he is
> conditioned within material nature.* (Bhaktivedānta Svāmī 1972)

It is due to statements like these that many of Bhaktivedānta Svāmī’s disciples in ISKCON, mainly westerners, would later come to interpret him as having endorsed some version of FD. Does this interpretation of his words hold up, however? I do not think so. There is indeed a way to perfectly reasonably interpret Bhaktivedānta Svāmī’s statements here and elsewhere as consonant with the stance of the precious Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava ācāryas. Jīva Gosvāmī, the foundational theologian of the Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava sampradāya, states in his Tattva-sandarbha, likewise a foundational theological text for the sampradāya, that the souls’ conjunction with māyā is a punishment by māyā itself for their *vaimukhyam*, or essential aversion towards Īśvara. This is not, however, a choice on the behalf of the souls themselves, but a beginningless disposition against Īśvara. Nor does this imply that their punishment is temporally subsequent to their aversion, since, for every instant that they have maintained this beginningless disposition, they have been punished by māyā by subjection to matter and karma. It is *this* that Bhaktivedānta Svāmī is referring to when he refers to the living entity's “rebellious nature,” not some temporally instantiated choice to reject Īśvara and voluntarily enter the material world, which would have worrying implications for the eternality and security of the liberated state, which we are repeated assured in the scriptures there is no possibility of losing once attained. Similarly, descriptions by Bhaktivedānta Svāmī of the soul as ‘fallen’ have also contributed to the perception that he taught FD, but in fact, such references are almost certainly pedagogical devices deriving from common motifs and concepts in Christianity used to explain Vaiṣṇava theology to a Western audience. The soul is ‘fallen’, for the Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava sampradāya, in the sense that it is ‘fallen’ from its essential nature and immersed in māyā, though, yet again, this does not necessarily connote ‘fall’ in a temporally distributed sense. Gupta’s claim, then, that FD is a licit view within the Gauḍīya Vaiṣṇava sampradāya is without substance. The Sampradāya, like all others, affirmed no such doctrine in its centuries-long history, and it only ever ‘crept-in’ in a specific organisation through certain ambiguituies in its founder’s works— hardly strong going!

I hope, in closing, I have drove the point home: there is no sort of logical necessity for Hindus to avail themselves of a fall-doctrine, nor is such a doctrine a serious position within established Hindu thought.

— सर्वोत्तम
