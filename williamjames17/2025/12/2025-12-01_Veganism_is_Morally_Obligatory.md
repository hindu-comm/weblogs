I was originally going to title this “Why Veganism is Obviously Correct”, though I later decided, due to how indebted the case here is to my own (hopefully not excessively) idiosyncratic metaethics, to exercise some epistemic humility and soften my phrasing. Despite this, I do hope that my presentation will go some way in illustrating that my belief in this is a defensible one. Or, at the absolute least, a defensible one given my prior commitments. Of course, to understand my position, it is necessary to *spell out* said prior commitments. A short while ago, I did just this in a piece on what I consider to be the four axioms implicit in my metaethical intuitions. In that piece, I formulated them specifically insofar as they foreground *proscribability*. More generally, they can be formulated as follows:

1.  *Behaviours are such that individuals will seek to either prevent or
    facilitate their occurrences in accordance with whether they
    reinforce or weaken the integrity of their selves.*

2.  *Insofar as the spontaneous attribution of intentional states onto
    others is integral to the functioning of selfhood itself, the self
    is intersubjectively constituted.*

3.  *Insofar as the self is intersubjectively constituted, behaviours
    not directed towards oneself, or even behaviours initiated by
    oneself, can nevertheless be detrimental to that self’s integrity,
    and thus be such that any rational individual would act to prevent
    their occurrence in themselves and in others, to the extent they are
    able to.*

4.  *For any behaviour X, X is either ‘right’ or ‘wrong’ depending on
    whether any individual A who accurately represents their
    intersubjective constitution to themselves facilitates or prevents
    it.*

It is worth clarifying that this is not, in the slightest, intended to be a form of ethical egoism*.* What I’m suggesting here is *not* that morality is constituted by an individual’s conscious arbitration of their own self-interest relative to the other individuals in their environment. What I’m saying is actually that, for an act to *be* moral, it has to conform to the logic internal to the structure of what it means to adopt the intentional stance vis-à-vis a prospective “other.” Because selves are intersubjectively constituted, *because to be a self is always already to be a self-among-selves, to act at all is to act under obligation. Action and obligation are logically codependent.* When I talk about an individual who “accurately represents their intersubjective constitution to themselves”, what I mean is an individual who does not artificially mutilate their capacity to adopt the intentional stance under conditions when they would, ordinarily, spontaneously do so. Any such subject is capable of rationally determining how they are to act under their given conditions in view of what is truly entailed by attributing intentionality to another individual. This, in itself, is the point of determination for the entirety of the moral law, as far as I intuitively understand it. Given this overview of my commitments, it’s quite easy to progress to the foundational reason why I take veganism to be morally obligatory. The crux of the matter can be encapsulated in a single principle we can call VP:

> **VP**: *There is no way to knowingly engage in or financially support
> animal slaughter, absent countervailing conditions, and maintain an
> otherwise consistent ethic, other than by effectively mutilating one’s
> capacity to adopt the intentional stance and recognise conditions
> under which adopting it is warranted.*

It is worth clarifying here what I mean by “absent countervailing conditions.” Like any sane individual, I subscribe to the principle of ought implies can. Indeed, it is implicit within the four principles I formulated! There isn't any meaningful sense in which something can be morally obligatory for an individual if they are incapable of fulfilling it in their own circumstances, because an obligation has no relevance outside of how we can actually act. Hence, ‘countervailing conditions’ are simply those wherein an individual is incapable of acting such that abiding by veganism is possible for them. For me, however, and a significant subset of the population of the West, such conditions do not obtain. Veganism is, with perhaps some effort, an actionable possibility for us. For those of us who are *not* vegan, then, does the contention of VP hold up? I think it is relatively easy to establish that it does. All that *is* actually required for it to obtain is the following:

> **VP Auxiliary 1:** *A significant portion of the organisms within the
> kingdom* Animalia *demonstrate behaviours adequate to the adoption of
> the intentional stance relative to them.*
>
> **VP Auxiliary 2**: *Absent innate or acquired defects in one’s
> capacity to attribute intentional states onto behaviourally suitables
> entities, not doing under standard conditions indicates a voluntary
> suspension of that capacity for an external purpose.*
>
> **VP Auxiliary 3**: *Individuals who effect a continuous or repeated
> suspension of that capacity, especially when it serves an external
> purpose such as assuaging cognitive dissonance, can become habituated
> to said suspension.*

None of these is, I believe, particularly controversial. The first one certainly isn’t— its truth would be borne out by even cursory observations of the relevant organisms. The second is derived by a simple process of elimination, while the third relies on a well-established psychological mechanism. What most carnists *would* dispute is, I believe, the moral relevance of these in conjunction, and hence, of VP. “Why does it matter that I attenuate my capacity to attribute intentional states to animals when I financially support their slaughter? That fact in itself seems insufficient to foreground a supposed obligation to avoid doing so. It’s not like I do that with other humans!”

Nevertheless, this objection misses the point. The true issue implied by VP is *what kind of person you are choosing to be in doing that.* To mutilate one’s capacity to adopt the intentional stance is to detune oneself from the course of action appropriate to a given circumstance by artificially convincing oneself that conditions directly relevant to what course of action to pursue *do not actually obtain*. This, in turn, effectively renders one’s capacity to recognise what *does* obtain systemically unreliable, as you will continually output indifference and doubt when neither is, in fact, warranted. There isn’t necessarily a difference *in kind* between the degree of intentionality ordinarily imputed to an animal and that imputed to another human. This amounts to a self-inflicted systemic impairment in your capacity to recognise the presence of conditions directly relevant to making correct moral judgements. If to act morally is to act in accordance with what it is for me to acknowledge you *as a you and not an it*, then becoming muddled on the conditions under which we recognise “you-ness”, to whatever degree, is a catastrophic failure. While no reasonable person would say that a person incapable of picking out morally relevant facts due to adventitious circumstances not of their making was inherently evil, let me ask you this: what about a person who *chooses* that for their own benefit?
