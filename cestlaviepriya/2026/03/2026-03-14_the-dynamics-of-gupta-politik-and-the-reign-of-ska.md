The Dynamics of Gupta Politik and the Reign of Skandagupta

**“…verily the victory has been achieved by him; (and) whom the goddess of fortune and splendour of her own accord selected as her husband …” – Junāgaḍha Rock Inscription of Skandagupta.**

In his relatively short reign, Skandagupta (~ 455-467 CE), the indomitable Gupta emperor not only defeated the Hūṇa(s) in battle but continued to successfully keep them restricted to the western frontiers of the empire for the rest of his rule. While this victory — endowed on him by the “the goddess of fortune and splendour (Lakṣmī / Śrī) of her own accord” — is rightfully the most renowned event of his reign, trouble had been brewing beneath this glory; the king was fighting the enemies outside and challenges inside. His succession was characterised as much by his valour as by the undercurrent of unfulfilled aspirations, infighting and soaring ambitions that challenged Skandagupta’s claim of the Lion Throne of the Gupta Empire, making his situation precarious, his victories hard-earned and his reign all the more fascinating to study and analyse.

![](https://cestlaviepriya.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/esedsd-removebg-preview-removebg-preview-removebg-preview-removebg-preview-1-imageonline.co-merged.jpg?w=999)

**Left — Gold coin of Skandagupta, depicting himself on the obverse. Right — Gold coin now attributed to Candragupta III, depicting himself on the obverse (Tandon, 2014) .**

Amidst these, some patterns emerge with regard to the political system of the Gupta Imperium which on one hand made the Gupta(s) more resilient in facing the challenges to thier hegemony, providing India with a long, stable rule for more than two centuries and on the other, they caused conditions that made the empire prone to internal power-struggles. There were some common factors, prominent from the early days of the Gupa Empire that set the stage on which the political rivalries in Skandagupta’s reign played out: the Gupta meritocracy, the role of the Houses of Nāga(s) and Vākāṭaka(s), and a peculiar history of power-struggles that emerged from the seat of Gupta Viceroyalty in Vidiśā. Together, they resulted in Skandagupta struggling on multiple fronts.

**The Gupta Meritocracy**

**§ Samudragupta**

The foremost factor was precedent set in the early days of the Gupta polity by the Gupta suzerain Candragupta I (~ 315-335 CE), the first one to claim the title of ‘Mahārājādhirāja’ (The Great King of Kings – The Prayāga Pillar Inscription). The king by choosing his son Samudragupta (r. ~ 335-375 CE) as his successor proclaimed that primogeniture would be neither necessary nor enough to be the Gupta successor and that only he who was “the best fitted to rule” would ascend the throne. The Prayāga Praśasti (Allāhābād Pillar Inscription) of Samudragupta gives a vivid description of his succession. This was a pivotal moment in the Indian history for his reign was to transform the Gupta(s) from a kingdom to an empire. It mentions the ‘tense atmosphere’ of the assembly in which the royal declaration was made. While it was ‘received with cheers by most of those present in the court, it caused great discontent and heart-burning to the rival candidates of the royal family’ (Majumdar, 1970 : 7). Samudragupta was “…being looked at with envy by the faces, melancholy through the rejection of themselves, of others of equal birth” (Raychaudhuri, 1923: 292).

> **The record describes “how in a full session of the royal court
> Candragupta I embraced his son Samudragupta, and declared, in accents
> surcharged with emotion: Thou art worthy, rule this whole world.”**
>
> **(Majumdar, 1970: 7)**

The manner and the tone of events described tells us that this succession caused ‘deep disappointment to the rival princes who coveted the throne’ and therefore, the possibility of Samudragupta facing discontent in his early rule cannot be ruled out; especially since, it is also very likely that he was not the eldest son of Candragupta I. Some opinions even go as far as to suggest a revolt of his brothers (ibid) but without any concrete evidence to their support, such opinions are merely exaggerations at this point. However, Samudragupta was indeed chosen the next ruler of the Gupta(s) amongst many ambitious contenders. This opposition probably was also one of the many factors that affected and inspired his ambitions for pan-India conquests.

**§ Candragupta II**

With the recent identification of Rāmagupta with Kāca, it is now known that the Gupta ruler after Samudragupta also had to prove himself worthy of kingship. The topic has been discussed in depth in a previous post on this blog — [The Elusive Kācha-Rāmagupta](https://cestlaviepriya.wordpress.com/2022/08/30/the-elusive-gupta-king/) — here it is sufficient to say that since Rāmagupta proved insufficient against the Śaka menace, a younger son of Samudragupta, Candragupta (Candragupta II Vikramāditya r. ~375-415 CE) took matters in his own hands, shrewdly handled the Śaka(s) and by permanently removing the threat, eventually ascended the Gupta throne. This was yet another instance of the most able amongst the Gupta lineage claiming the highest seat of the empire.

There are many hints in the literature which when studied along with the interpretation of numismatical and inscriptional evidence suggest that Rāmagupta was not popular even when he ascended the throne. There is also the possibility that Samudragupta had also wanted Candragupta to succeed him (see the sections in the above mentioned blogpost for detailed discussion on this topic). In fact, in Bihār Pillar Inscription, Skandagupta himself informs that Candragupta was accepted by his father (Tandon, 2014). And it seems that instead of opposing Rāmagupta, Candragupta chose to forego his claim to avoid a war of succession or he decided to bide his time till Rāmagupta committed an error. Either way, he did not had to wait for long. We do not know what hapenned to Rāmagupta later but it is important to note that there is no evidence of his violent end at the hands of his brother. As far as the current knowledge goes, Candragupta II continued to successfully rule without any other hindrance that we know of.

**It is important to note that even when a Gupta prince like Rāmagupta claimed the throne merely on the basis of birth without having the capability to actually defend the empire, it usually resulted with another younger brother with the required capabilties ascending the throne instead by proving himself in the battle against the enemies. There are no instances — especially in the hey days of the empire — **of**** **an incapable monarch reigning for a long period. The Gupta meritocracy made sure that the highest seat did not remain with the unworthy for long.**

**§ Kumāragupta I**

It is presumed that the succession of Kumāragupta I Mahendrāditya (r. ~415-455 CE) was without any rivalry. However, even if not a contentious succession, analyzing the inscriptions reveals the possibility of yet another power-struggle, this time between Kumāragupta andhis brother Govindagupta. As Govindagupta’s role was more concerned with the politics of the Viceroyalty of Vidiśā, it will be discussed in the relevant section below.

**The Nāga(s)**

The Nāga dynasty was an ancient ruling house that came into prominence on the political map of India probably towards the close of 2nd c. C.E. Their origin is uncertain but it is known that the Nāga(s) were there when the Kuṣāṇa power was in decline and it is a strong possibility that they were responsible for finishing off the remnants of the Kuṣāṇa(s) in India. As a note on the Nāga(s) has also been posted on this blog — [The Nāga(s) in Ancient India](https://cestlaviepriya.wordpress.com/2020/05/25/the-naga-dynasties-in-ancient-india/) — here it suffices to say that the Nāga(s) were indeed an illustrious royal house and multiple kings of this dynasty were ruling at various centres in ancient India like Mathurā, Vidiśā (Besnagar in Madhya Pradesh), Padmāvatī (Pawaya in Madhya Pradesh) and Kāntipuri (Probably Kutwar in Gwalior, Madhya Pradesh). Their presence in multiple independent centres also suggests that they probably were a confederation and that this confederation may have battled Samudragupta soon after his ascension in which he defeated them (Chhabra & Gai, 1981: 9-10). In order to secure his conquests and his western frontier where the Śaka(s) still had strong presence, albeit as vassals, Samudragupta got the Nāga Princess Kuberanāgā married to Candragupta, son and later successor of Samudragupta.

Important to note that the Nāga(s) were not only the precursors to the Gupta(s) but to the House of the Vākāṭaka(s) (discussed in the following section) as well. In fact, the Nāga-Vākāṭaka connection was much older than that of the Gupta-Vākāṭaka(s). The Bhāraśiva lineage of the Nāga(s) of Padmāvatī had already been ruling the region when the Vākāṭaka(s) rose to prominence in Central India and the Deccan. The Bhāraśiva ruler Bhavanāga was powerful enough to have many Aśvamedha sacrifices completed during his kingship. He must have been in a strong position for successful completion of sacrifice of this magnitude multiple times. The daughter of the same Bhāraśiva ruler was married to Gautamīputra, the son of Vākāṭaka king Pravarasena I. The significance of this marriage for the Vākāṭaka(s) can be understood from the fact that it was meticulously recorded in royal inscriptions of the Vākāṭaka(s) probably due to a firm foundation which a connection with the Nāga power provided to the Vākāṭaka(s).

**“This event was so important in the history of Vākāṭaka dynasty that it was incorporated in their dynastic history and repeated in all the official deeds of the Vākāṭaka(s).”**

Since Candragupta II was married to a Nāga princess Kuberanāgā, he had the support of these Nāga(s) who despite their subjugation by Samudragupta remained a local force to be reckoned with against the Śaka(s), the former arch-enemies of the Nāga(s) (Bakker, 2022; 6). As mentioned above, this could have been the primary goal for Samudragupta for this marital alliance with the conquered Nāga(s).

**The Vākāṭaka(s)**

The Vākāṭaka dynasty was significant on its own with its imposing rise in the Central India and its influence spanning even further south but together with the Gupta(s), they dominated the Indian political scene from late 4^(th) to late 5^(th) century CE. There is, however a perception that considerd their relations as comprising merely of friendly dynamics despite the fact that the Gupta(s) under Samudragupta had indeed halted the Vākāṭaka(s) ambitions and limited them to the Gupta vassalage. The question then naturally arises is that did it ever went through a rough period especially when the Gupta power was not as imposing as in the early period of their alliance?

These dynamics seem particularly important to Bakker who pointed out some interesting queries regarding the Gupta-Vākāṭaka connection. The two branches of the Vākāṭaka(s) — the Nandivardhana and the Vatsagulmā branch could have had difference of opinion regarding the Gupta(s) considering the dynasty had lost much of its original land to Samudragupta (Bakker, 2022). The Gupta-Vākāṭaka strengthened their connection when Candragupta II gave his daughter Prabhāvatī in marriage to the Vākāṭaka Rudrasena II. But with Rudrasena’s early passing, Prabhāvatī’s regency of her sons — Divākarasena, Dāmodarasena and Pravarasena — became the catalyst in strengthening the bonds even more between the two powers. Thus, analyzing the undercurrents provides us with not so much a unidimentional image not only with regard to the Vākāṭaka court but even in the Gupta politik. From the evidence available, at least two Gupta princes during Prabhāvatī’s time — Govindagupta and Ghaṭotkacagupta — got involved in power-struggle against the ruling Gupta Emperors and Prabhāvatī chose different sides to support each time.

> **“We do not know how much resistance there was against Prabhāvatī’s
> regency over her sons and whether there was resentment from the side
> of these sons against their mother, who seemingly indefinitely
> postponed (from c. ad 405 to 419) their ascension to the throne.”**
>
> **Bakker, 2022**.

On one hand, this tension due to Prabhāvatī’s regency could very well be considered an internal struggle of the Vākāṭaka(s) but Prabhāvatī being a Gupta princess meant that there was a strong possibility of the Gupta interference in Vākāṭaka affairs which one can assume ruffled up feathers in the Vākāṭaka court.

**The Viceroyalty of Vidiśā**

**§ Rāmagupta**

As a result of the conquests of Samudragupta, a major portion of Eastern Mālwā was annexed by the Gupta(s). From the evidence that we have — inscriptional and numismatic — Rāmagupta, son of Samudragupta was the first Governor of Eraṇ at Vidiśā. Since then, for a long time in Gupta politik, Vidiśā was to remain the seat of Gupta Viceroyalty and a Gupta prince was usually appointed to govern the region.

The significance of this seat in the central-west regions of India cannot be overstated. Vidiśā was at the crossroads of major ancient Indian routes — religious, trade and military — and being the capital of Eastern Mālwā in earlier times (Ākara) (Moti Chandra, 1977: 23-24) only enhanced its importance. It connected major western ports like Bhṛgukaccha and Sūrapāraka to important religious and commercial cities like Mathurā. It was en route to other major cities in the region like Māhiṣmatī, Ujjain in Central India, and to Prayāga and Kauśāmbī in the north. Thus, it was connected to the core of the Gupta territories both in the east and west. Its close proximity to the kingdom of the Vākāṭaka(s) and the influence of the Nāga(s) in the region made it more prone to political struggles in the region.

**With creation of such a power centre and a Gupta prince usually in control of such a significant position, it usually resulted in soaring ambitions of the viceroy.**

Predictably, Rāmagupta probably since the beginning of his viceroyalty was eyeing the Gupta throne post-Samudragupta. He had indeed signaled this by minting his own copper-coinage with the title of Mahārāja (the title was reserved for vassal kings and governors, thus lower in hierarchy to the Gupta Emperor who was called Mahārājādhirāja i.e. the great king of kings) based on the coinage of the Nāga kings and the local Mālava rulers who ruled the region prior to the Gupta(s) (Bajpai, 1961; 54). There was no precedent of a Gupta prince minting coins, it is therefore reasonable to assume that this was an act of protest — to mark his position and to finance his claim — expressing that the succession would not go uncontested.

As mentioned before, Rāmagupta’s efforts bore fruit and he did manage to succeed Samudragupta but his ineffectiveness in handling the Śaka(s) cost him the Gupta throne for the situation demanded a level of astuteness and valour that Rāmagupta seemingly lacked. His younger brother Candragupta proved himself and captured the Gupta kingship and Rāmagupta was lost in time. It is very likely that there were multiple governors on the seat of Vidiśā, and not all could have been of royal descent but it is certain that Gupta princes were given this responsibility and most of the time, it eventually resulted in internal power struggles in the Gupta polity.

**§ Govindagupta**

It seems post-Rāmagupta, the Gupta Emperor Candragupta II appointed Mahārāja Govindagupta, son by his chief queen Dhruvadevī / Dhruvasvāminī whose Vaiśālī Clay seal suggest that he may have been the heir-apparent (Bakker, 2022).

**“An inscription of a feudatory chieftain (bhūmipati) of Skandagupta, Prabhākara, found in Daśapura (modern Mandasor) in western Mālwā (dating from AD 467/68), makes it probable that Govinda was governor of the western division of Candragupta’s empire, i.e. Mālwā (Bakker, 2022).”**

Interestingly, despite pledging allegiance to the Gupta monarch (Skandagupta) and singing praises to Candragupta II and Govindagupta, the inscription is silent on Kumāragupta I (the previous Gupta Emperor and brother of Govindagupta). As per the inscription, the commander of Govinda’s army in this period was one Vāyurakṣita, whose son Dattabhaṭa was also appointed in the same position by Prabhākara. It is known that during Kumāragupta’s reign, feudatory family of the Varmans or Early Aulikaras were in charge of Daśapura. For Bakker, all this evidence combined suggested that Kumāragupta’s succession had not been without a struggle either.

> **“The contender for the imperial throne had again been an elder
> brother, the viceroy of Vidiśā, viz. Govindagupta. His general,
> Vāyurakṣita, thus turned out to have been on the losing side.”**
>
> **(Bakker, 2022)**

After Candragupta II solidified his control and thus his alliance with the Vākāṭaka(s) by marrying his daughter Prabhāvatī to Rudrasena II, a new angle had been created in the Vidiśā politics born from the proximity of the Vidiśā court to the Vākāṭaka kingdom. It is well known that Prabhāvatīguptā had close relations with her half-brother and the Gupta monarch Kumāragupta I. It is then expected that Govindagupta was not supported in his political adventures by the Vākāṭaka(s) under Prabhāvatī’s regency, however, there are hints that the Vākāṭaka prince Divākarasena did side with Govindagupta. As mentioned above, Prabhāvatī had three sons: Divākarasena, the elder son and heir-apparent, Pravarasena and Damodarasena. Fascinatingly, “in the aftermath of the power struggle between Govinda and Kumāragupta… Divākarasena, vanished from the scene (Bakker, 2022).” It would not be wrong to assume that Divākarasena supported Govindagupta and eventually lost the Vākāṭaka succession to his younger brother Pravarasena.

**Skandagupta and the Power Struggles**

**§ The Question of Legitimacy**

Amongst the successions and reigns of most powerful Gupta monarchs, the reign of Skandagupta had all of these aforementioned factors play out in the open to make him one of the most affected by the internal power-struggles in addition to facing major external threat of the Hunnic invasions and on a constant vigil due to the vulnerability of Indian western frontier. Recent consensus seems to suggest that threat to Skandagupta’s accession was more pronounced because he was most probably not the rightful heir to the throne. Purugupta, the son of Mahādevī Anantadevī (Mahādevī ws the title for the Chief Queen) must have been the heir-apparent. Skandagupta was indeed a son of Kumāragupta but it is noticeable that ‘his mother is not identified by name in any known text or inscription, suggesting that he was, at best, the son of a minor queen of Kumāragupta I, or more probably the son of a woman who was not a queen at all (Tandon, 2014).’And it is even probable that he was a son from a mistress, a concubine or maybe even a slave-girl (ibid), thus, an uncomfortable legitimacy or even a case for illegitimacy in any of these scenarios, the first and only such instance in the history of Gupta polity.

However, ever since this opinion started gaining traction, other scholars like Raychaudhuri from the very beginning expressed their disagreement citing the fact that there was no rule prohibiting the mention of non-Mahādevī(s) in inscriptions (Raychaudhuri, 1923 : 290). Kuberanāgā, mentioned above was one such prominent example. She was not a Mahādevī of Candragupta II and yet, has been mentioned in many inscriptions of her daughter, the Vākāṭaka queen Prabhāvatīguptā (ibid). Yaśomatī, the mother of Harṣavardhan (r. 606–647 CE), the Puśyabhūti Emperor was similarly mentioned in one inscription while omitted from the other (ibid).

**“The omission of the name of Skanda’s mother from inscriptions is, at best, an argumentum ex silentio which can only be accepted if it can be proved that the mention of the name of a Mahādevī was compulsory and that the mention of the name of an ordinary queen was prohibited (Raychaudhuri, 1923 : 291).”**

Add to it the fact that the Gupta successions had a history of being decided on merit, the ablest prince being eventually chosen for the Gupta throne. Therefore, merely being a son of a minor queen was not a compelling enough reason to be considered an illegitimate heir. However, this question of legitimacy cannot be completely brushed aside as irrelevant either. The Gupta Emperors are almost always known to have mentioned the name of their mothers. Despite providing detailed inscriptions, Skandagupta chose to stay silent about her mother’s name while at the same time, mentioning her in emotionally charged verses. And so it seems, that he indeed wasthe son from a either very minor-queen, concubine or even a slave-girl. But important to note that despite the questionable legitimacy, Skandagupta did eventually ascend the throne, the Gupta meritocracy was again a deciding factor. It was not merely the question whether Skandagupta was a legitimate heir or not but more importantly, there were other legitimate heirs claiming the Gupta throne.

A much more concrete cause signifying Skandagupta’s uncomfortable rise can be seen in his inscriptions i.e. the non-mention of any approval from his father Kumāragupta I to accede the throne; a fact that most certainly would have been mentioned if any such special favour had indeed been received like in case of the Prayāga Praśasti of Samudragupta. It can be argued that this is also argumentum ex silentio but Tandon is right in pointing out that to publicize his legitimacy by declaring his father’s approval clearly would have been to his advantage and therefore its absence suggests contentious circumstances.

More importantly, it seems unlikely that it would have been possible for Skandagupta to claim the throne had Kumāragupta I announced the succession by naming any of his other sons. So, it is likely that Kumāragupta I died before making any royal declaration in this matter. There have been opinions that the later years of Kumāragupta I rule saw upheaval but inscription tells us that Skandagupta ‘re-established the Fortune of the Dynasty which had turned adrift when his father had repaired to Heaven (emphasis mine).’ And so it seems there were no major conflicts (there could have been other irritants like the curious Puṣyamitra-s) during the last years of Kumāragupta and it is only after his passing away that difficulties arose and confronting them was how Skandagupta proved his mettle by the force of his arm and armies rescuing ‘a tottering empire and lineage from destruction (Tandon, 2014)’ ; these lines also prove that in the early period of his reign, the major threat was indeed external since challenge from other Gupta princes would not mean threat to the Gupta lineage.

**“These verses from the Jūnāgaḍha Inscription explain his immediate threat i.e. the ‘barbarians’ outside — (Skandagupta) whose fame, moreover, even (his) enemies, in the countries of the Mlêchchhas . . . having (their) pride broken down to the very root, announce with the words: **‘**verily the victory has been achieved by him…(and) whom the goddess of fortune and splendour of her own accord selected as her husband**’** …” (ibid.).**

****§**** **Ghaṭotkacagupta : The Vākāṭaka-Nāga Support**

However, the immediate threat being external did not mean that internal challenges were absent. A section of scholarly opinion attempts to find hints of real life events in the metaphoric details mentioned in Skandagupta’s inscriptions — (Skandagupta) ‘saw his mother who was in tears just as Kṛṣṇa approached Devakī when he had slain his foe’ — suggesting that the foe that had arisen against the Gupta power and made it totter to its foundations was some relative of his (Chhabra & Gai, 1981: 80).

Bakker goes even further in favour of a protracted war of succession that lasted possibly a decade (Bakker, 2015: 25) and identifies the uncle with whom Skandagupta fought as Ghaṭotkacagupta, another Gupta prince, a much younger brother of Kumāragupta I. How plausible this scenario is and what role did Ghaṭotkachagupta play against Skandagupta will be understood from the analysis below.

The combined evidence from the Tumain and the Kevala Narasiṃha Temple Inscription confirms that Ghaṭotkaca was the son of Candragupta II and therefore, a brother, albeit a very younger brother of Kumāragupta I. He was posted as the governor of Airikina (Eraṇa), and as stated before, this was the seat of Gupta Viceroyalty of Vidiśā in Mālwā (Tandon, 2014). Due to his seals discovered from Basāḍh (Vaiśālī), it is even suggested that he may have been a (yuvarāja / heir-apparent) like Govindagupta but there is no confirm evidence for this. Being a much younger brother to Kumāragupta I meant that he was very likely the same age as his nephew and Gupta Emperor Skandagupta. Numismatical evidence for Ghaṭotkaca exists of ‘two gold coins of the Archer type carrying the name Ghaṭo under the king’s arm and these are assumed to be issues of this prince (ibid.). Based on these gold coins, some authors suggest that Ghaṭotkaca had imperial ambitions (ibid.). And as mentioned before, in his analysis of the Bhītarī Pillar Inscription, Bakker took it as a reference to Ghaṭotkaca where Skandagupta, after winning over his enemies, is told to have approached his mother who was in tears ‘just as Kṛṣṇa approached Devakī when he had slain his foe.’ As per this suggestion, if the comparison of Skandagupta and his mother to Kṛṣṇa and Devakī has any meaning at all, ‘the foe that had arisen against the Gupta power and made it totter to its foundations was some relative of his through his mother, presumably her brother.’

But, it is equally important to note that the uncle of Kṛṣṇa, Kaṁsa in this metaphor was a maternal uncle, unlike Ghaṭotkacha (ibid.), a significant difference that certainly Gupta(s) were aware of and therefore an interpretation of these lines would be too simplistic since Ghaṭotkaca was a paternal uncle.

Nonetheless, Bakker contextualised his opinion regarding Ghaṭotkaca’s ambitions by his interpretation of the Kevala Narasiṃha Temple Inscription. It tells us that Ghaṭotkaca, son of Candragupta II (and as mentioned above, a much younger brother of Kumāragupta since the inscription suggests that he was born after his sister Prabhāvatī’s marriage to Rudrasena II) was married to the daughter of Prabhāvatī, possibly named Atibhāvatī. Interestingly, it is an instance of marriage between an uncle and niece and suggests a strong possibility that it was done with the goal of giving Vākāṭaka support for the cause of Ghaṭotkacagupta. Though we can never be sure whether it was done merely with the aim of supporting Ghaṭotkaca or more with the aim of contesting Skandagupta and Ghaṭotkaca became a mere instrument to it.

**Verse 13 of the inscription says that ‘after he (Ghaṭotkaca) had seen that niece (bhāgineyī) (of his) \[. . .\], who was a palace Lakṣmī, \[. . .\] (This) king paid his respects (to her) and married her’ (Bakker, 2022). Verse 17 of the inscription becomes specifically important for this topic. It says: “… he of desirable appearance \[…\] by/with Indra, \[…\] (her) brother brought the proud woman \[…\], back to his own residence with force.”**

As per Bakker’s interpretation, “the one of desirable appearance is Ghaṭotkacagupta, who, presumably, has joined Indra in the sky. The brother is Pravarasena, the Vākāṭaka king and brother of Ghaṭotkaca’s wife (“the proud woman”) Atibhāvatī, and he brings his sister home after her widowhood. Thus, it could suggest that Ghaṭotkaca tried to protest Skandagupta’s succession and lost his life in the struggle and Skandagupta later acceded the throne (Tandon, 2014). Tandon rejects this based on his study of the numismatical evidence. Because if that was the case then Ghaṭotkaca could not have been the principal opponent with whom Skandagupta fought what Bakker himself called a protracted war of succession since the Ghaṭo gold coins are ‘too heavy and large’ (141.2 grains and 135.5 grains) to be issued around the time of Kumāragupta’s death (ibid.). Evidently considerable time had passed since Skandagupta’s accession when these coins were issued.

It is important to remember that Skandagupta’s early gold-coins are about 132 grains and only reach 144 grains later in his reign. The Ghaṭo gold coin being 141 grains gives strong credence to the possibility that Ghaṭotkacagupta very well survived unlike Bakker’s interpretation of that fragmentary verse 17 of the Kevala Narasiṃha Temple Inscription. And as mentioned above, it is reasonable to assume since Ghaṭotkacagupta was a much younger brother to Kumāragupta and thus even by the age criteria, it is quite possible that he survived Skandagupta’s death and then issued those coins.

Even the inscription of Skandagupta states that “when (his) father had attained the skies, \[he\] conquered (his) enemies by the strength of (his) arm, and established again *the ruined fortunes of (his) lineage* (emphasis mine).” Tandon rightly points out that Ghaṭotkaca’s accession, as much undesirable to Skandagupta as could be but it could not have been considered a ruination of the fortunes of the lineage (ibid.). It is indeed possible that Ghaṭotkaca tried but was unsuccessful in stopping Skandagupta from claiming Gupta Kingship. But it does not necessarily mean that he lost his life in this struggle. Pravarasena taking his sister back to his residence can simply mean troubled relations amidst cotentions and his unwillingness to openly support the cause of Ghaṭotkaca anymore. Pravarasena’s rule anyway had ended by the time Skandagupta succeeded. The possibility of Pravarasena’s frustration with exaggerated influence of his mother, the dowager queen Prabhāvatī also cannot be denied.

It even seems that the Vākāṭaka(s) under Prabhāvatīguptā attempted to provide legitimacy to the cause of Ghaṭotkaca by retrospectively giving much more importance to the early pre-imperial Gupta king Ghaṭotkaca (r. ~ 280/300 -319 CE). In two records of Prabhāvatī, Ghaṭotkaca is said to be the first Gupta king (Majumdar, 1970: 2-3) unlike the usual Śrīgupta (r. ~ 240/280 CE). Adding to this contrivance is the location of these records: typically the regions ruled by the Vākāṭaka(s); for example in a record from Rewā, the Gupta family is traced back to Ghaṭotkaca, and not to Śrīgupta (ibid). As mentioned, it is possible that these attempts were made primarily at the behest of Prabhāvatīguptā and Pravarasena, her son and later the King of the Vākāṭaka(s), who initially may have been on-board with this, and not outright against it.

Ghaṭotkaca certainly gained from the support of Prabhāvatī in his earlier attempts before Skandagupta acceeded but post that period, neither Prabhāvatī nor Pravarasena were in power. In such circumstances, will it be wrong to assume that Ghaṭotkaca was given support from the Nāga(s)? The reason for analyzing Nāga(s) role in conflict against Skandagupta stems from some curious verses in his Jūnāgaḍha inscription.

**It says that “he (Skandagupta) forged an order with an effigy, namely, Garuḍa, which rendered devoid of poison, the Serpent (Bhujaṅga) Rulers, who had uplifted their hoods in pride and arrogance (Chhabra & Gai, 1981**: **80****).”**

Since bhujaṅga is synonymous with Nāga (serpent), one obvious conclusion from these lines is that the Gupta(s) under Skandagupta humbled a revolt of the Nāga(s). It is indeed possible that this could be a generic reference to Gupta enemies since the royal Gupta emblem was Garuḍa. However, it is probable that this revolt was spearheaded by Ghaṭotkaca on confidence of the Nāga backing. Ghaṭotkaca afterall had married the Vākāṭaka princess with Nāga ancestry due to her mother. The reasoning behind the Gupta-Nāga martial alliances since the time of Samudragupta had been to secure their backing. It is then quite important to consider the Nāga role in this conflict. Ghaṭotkaca could not have continued to register his protest against the Gupta Emperor by issuing the gold coins if he had no one backing his goals. And considering the evidence from the the Kevala Narasiṃha Temple Inscription, the Vākāṭaka(s) were no longer providing that support.

Analysing the events hitherto, the most notable personality in these conflicts was certainly Prabhāvatīgupta. It is therefore not at all surprising that after ‘the death of this formidable lady (c. ad 443) the Gupta-Vākāṭaka relations quickly deteriorated (Bakker, 2022).’ The grand Gupta-Vākāṭaka alliance was thriving mostly in the period of Prabhāvatīgupta’s regency which ushered in three decades of unparallelled cultural bloom (ibid.).

**The Indore Plates of Pravarasena II issued in his 23^(rd) regnal year from the royal camp (vāsaka) in Tripurī on the northern bank of the Narmadā are very interesting in this regard. Important to note that the issuing region was actually in the Gupta territory.**

The fact that these were issuedquite soon after Prabhāvatīgupta’s death in ~ 443 CE also seem to suggest that this could have been a daring move from Pravarasena against the Gupta(s) since this camp was in the Gupta territory. Mirashi connected this with Bhītarī Inscription of Skandagupta and the enemies mentioned there in. However, it cannot be said with any certainty that the camp mentioned was even a military one. Regardless of that, it is a curious evidence for even though there seems to be no conclusive proof of any rivalry or animosity between Pravarasena II (r. 420 – 455 CE) and Kumāragupta I, the fact that the Vākāṭaka king camped in the Gupta territory and issued inscriptions from there shortly after the death the dowager-queen Prabhāvatīguptā in ~ 443 CE seems too significant to be merely a coincidence or a normal political activity. At least it does give credence to the possibility of rising tensions in the Gupta-Vākāṭaka relations which only got evevn more weak with time.

The fracture in this alliance disadvantaged both the parties involved. There is no doubt about the loss of favour the Vākāṭaka(s) must have had in Skandagupta’s reign. The loss of ‘the Mālawā connection’ from losing the Vākāṭaka influence at the Court of Vidiśā proved ‘disastrous for the Eastern Vākāṭaka(s) who soon became subordinate to their relatives, the other Vākāṭaka branch of Vatsagulmā (Bakker, 2022). The Gupta Empire also ended up with a major drawback from the lack of a trusted ally in the Central-West India. The situation must have become precarious for Skandagupta, especially with the Hūṇa(s) still lingering in the frontiers. This does not mean that the Vākāṭaka(s) actively went against the Gupta Empire. It does however, mean that the weakening of the Gupta-Vākāṭaka(s) required more vigorous vigilance and cognizance from the Gupta side, thus making the role of other vassals in the region prominent in the politics of the time. One of the reason for the rise of the House of Aulikara(s) for example, could be traced to and explained with the vacuum created by the tensions between the Gupta(s) on one side and the Vākāṭaka(s)-Nāga(s) on the other.

**Candragupta III**

There is a curious sequence of coins, earlier attributed to Candragupta II but with deeper analysis, now considered belonging to another Candragupta i.e. Candragupta III. They mostly consist of the Archer type and some recently discovered Horseman type. Tandon has presented in his study that these Candragupta III coins cover a relatively long period starting with the death of Kumāragupta I and since many of them actually belong to the heavy suvarṇa (gold) weight standard, they seem to overlap with the period after this standard was introduced (Tandon, 2014). He mentions 53 coins in the series, thus outnumbering the Ghaṭo coins of Ghaṭotkacgupta by a good margin.

Based on the standard of both timing and quantity, this find is as curious as it is significant for it provides strong evidence in support of another ruler named Candragupta who not only apparently succeeded Kumāragupta I but continued to issue heavy weight standard coins even after the accession of Skandagupta.

**Tandon in his initial researches thus, understood that this new ruler Candragupta III and Skandagupta reigned at least somewhat in parallel (ibid.) for quite some time. But, more analysis later confirmed that the events were more complicated than previously understood.**

It will be much better to quote Tandon on the details of this coinage in full: “First properly identified as a distinct group by P.L. Gupta, and subsequently studied by Nisar Ahmad and Ellen Raven, the coins are mostly of the Archer type, although two coins of the Horseman type have also recently been identified. The Archer types are similar to the coins of Candragupta II in that they carry the legend candra under the king’s arm on the obverse and the title śrī vikrama on the reverse. This is why they were initially thought to be coins of Candragupta II. However, they are distinguished from the coins of Candragupta II by their weight and style. The primary distinguishing feature of the coins is their weight, which, for most coins, lies in between the weights of the coins of Kumāragupta I and the later, heavy-weight coins of Skandagupta. Thus they are considerably heavier than the coins of Candragupta II, which were lighter even than the coins of Kumāragupta I. There are also elements of style that point to the intermediate dating (~ 450-455 CE) of these coins, argued quite convincingly by Raven in her paper (ibid.).”

**Purugupta**

As to the question of identifying this mysterious Candragupta III, could the answer be hiding in the plain sight? We do know for certain of a Gupta ruler who, even though has left us with no coins, seals or inscriptions of his own time, his emphatic evidence is nonetheless found in inscription of his grandson who later became a Gupta ruler as well. The ruler in question is Purugupta (r. 467 – 473 CE). In the Bhītarī silver-copper seal of his grandson Kumāragupta III (r. ~ 530-540 CE), he is mentioned as ‘mahārājadhirāja’ a royal epithet used only for the emperors in the Gupta inscriptions.

The Nālandā clay sealings of his sons Narasiṁhagupta and Budhagupta and his grandson Kumaragupta III all provide confirmation of his rulership of the Gupta(s). The timing, quantity and the weightage of the Candragupta III coins can be explained through Purugupta who until now was thought to have succeeded Skandagupta in ~ 467 CE. Tandon, in fact argues that Purugupta, not Skandagupta, was the immediate successor of his father Kumāragupta I. Most authors have until now presumed that Skandagupta succeeded Kumāragupta I (Tandon, 2014).

**“Five of his (Purugupta) direct descendants (three sons, a grandson and a great-grandson) also occupied the throne, indicating that his was the principal (and only known) dynastic line. It stands to reason that he in fact occupied the throne, most probably as the direct successor of his father, Kumāragupta I. But virtually all scholars assume that Kumāragupta̍s successor was Skandagupta.”**

An earlier find of a coin with the title vikramāditya was once thought to belong to Purugupta. There even were earlier attempts by some scholars that identified Skandagupta with Purugupta based on the fact that both used the title of vikramāditya, however further research confirmed that the Purugupta Vikramāditya coin actually belonged to Gupta Emperor Budhagupta. (ibid.). Similarly, Tandon argues that amongst the two titles that were considered to have been used by Skandagupta — Kramāditya & Vikramāditya — only Kramāditya was used by Skandagupta and his Vikramāditya coinage was infact issued posthumously.

**More interestingly, there is an eight year gap between the last known date for Kumāragupta I (Dāmodarpur Copper Plate 448 CE) and first known date for Skandagupta (Jūnāgaḍh Inscription 456 CE) (ibid.). Thereforem there is indeed a strong possibility that the immediate Gupta successor was Candragupta III (probably Purugupta).**

However, if Candragupta III was indeed Purugupta, it is reasonable to wonder if there is any evidence to corroborate it? Tandon is certain that there is indeed. He mentions a 6^(th) century Buddhist text called ‘The life of Vasubandhu’ by Paramārtha mentions King Vikramāditya of Ayodhyā. He is told to have been a patron of Vasubandhu who was an esteemed Buddhist sage. Vikramāditya, we are told sent his queen and his son, crown prince Bālāditya to Vasubandhu for education. When Bālāditya ascended the throne, he invited Vasubandhu to come to Ayodhyā. The most significant connection here is the name Bālāditya which has been confirmed to be the biruda of Narasiṃhagupta, the Gupta ruler. And the seal inscription from Bhītarī confirms that Purugupta was the father of Narasiṃhagupta. It thus makes sense for us to consider that Vikramāditya was none other than Purugupta.

**Therefore, it is possible that Candragupta III was not some unbeknownst king that suddenly emerged out of the blue but it was Purugupta whose lineage was to carry forward the Gupta dynasty unlike Skandagupta who died without any known descendant.**

**§ The Question of Power-Struggle**

The question then arises about the power-struggle between Skanda and Puru. But scholars like P. L. Gupta, Willis have pointed out that there could not have been a power-struggle between them to begin with since “Skandagupta, a shrewd statesman…would have never allowed him (Purugupta) or his descendants to survive, who if alive, could not but be a constant danger to his life and throne.” So, it does seem that after Kumāragupta passed away without a royal declaration of his successor (he may have wished Skandagupta to be the heir but we can only assume), Purugupta-Candragupta III ascended the Gupta throne and him and his descendants continued to flourish in the Gupta heartland and Skandagupta mostly dominated the Gupta regions in the west. The next question to ask would be why Purugupta adopted the name Candragupta? For this, Tandon suggests that this simply could have been done to glorify himself with the name of his grandfather Candragupta II. A tradition does seemingly developed in the Gupta polity where grandsons took the names of their iluustrious grandfathers: Candragupta II and and Kumāragupta II before and after Purugupta respectively did the same. Then why Purugupta’s descendants chose to forego the use of Candragupta is another relevant question. To this, Tandon assumes that this could have been done to either distinguish him from the earlier Candragupta(s) or was done with the aim of emphasising their royal ancestry and claim to the throne after legendary King Puru (Tandon, 2014).

**“..he (Purugupta) was known as Vikramāditya, thereby providing an explanation for the biruda found on the coins of Candragupta III: śrī vikrama and śrīrajitavikrama (ibid.).”**

**The Reign of Skandagupta: A New Interpretation**

To add more depth to this analysis, in another recent research, Pankaj Tandon (2017) presented more fascinating details. It appears that in the coinage so far accepted as belonging to Candragupta III, a noticeable feature is the presence of peculiar reverse symbols identified as ‘the circle type’ and ‘the diamond type’ which he, based on the opinion of another scholars (Gupta and Srivastava) considers to actually represent the mint marks (ibid.).

All of the Altar coins of the series carry the circle symbol while the coin with the śrīvatsa symbol carry the diamond symbol. The coins showed further variance that can be divided between the king wearing the tunic type or the dhoti type. Tandon studied the three evidences of the Bhītarī Pillar Inscription, Candragupta III coinage and three particular Skandagupta Archer type coins (based on factors like the mint marks, the clothing pattern of the king, their weightage) and came to another conclusion, changing his earlier stance that Skandagupta and Candragupta III ruled for some time in parallel. In his analysis, it appears that for some time, some Gupta mints fell into the hands of the Hūṇa adversaries (ibid.). It would be suitable to quote him in full regarding his conclusions which even though in his own words are ‘speculative’ but nonetheless ‘accounts for several things we know about Gupta history and about the coins’:

**“Skandagupta is an illegitimate son, but he shows military acumen and so his father puts him in charge of the army. He effectively protects the empire from an enemy we can call the Puṣyamitras. When Kumāragupta dies, the heir apparent inherits the throne. I believe this was Purugupta, but**…**that is not crucial to the argument. The heir takes the name Candragupta after his illustrious grandfather (much like his grandfather did himself) and starts issuing coins in that name and with the epithet śrī vikrama on the reverse. His half-brother Skandagupta continues in his military role. A few years later, after Candragupta III has issued at least three series of coins, and the weight of the Gupta dināra has risen to around 8.55 gm, the empire comes under attack again, and this time the enemy makes substantial gains. Perhaps Skandagupta is busy further west and the king leads the defense of the heartland. But he loses and perhaps gets killed in the process. Skandagupta at this point seizes (or is given?) the throne, and starts to issue coins in his name, with a weight of approximately 8.5 gm. The enemy (the Hūṇas perhaps?) occupy a major city and start issuing coins in the Gupta idiom from the city mint, although they remove the Vaiṣṇava symbol (the cakra) on the coins and replace it with a symbol more meaningful to their Persian-inspired religion: the fire altar. Their coins are also somewhat cruder, as their die-cutters are not quite as skilled as the Gupta celators. Skandagupta eventually mounts a counter-attack and, after a reasonably long period of time (judging by the somewhat lengthy sequence of Altar coins) succeeds in pushing out the occupiers. In other words, he restores the ruined fortunes of his family. He re-takes the city with its mint and issues some coins in his name from there (Tandon, 2017).”**

However, there is one major problem regarding the argument of Candragupta III dying few years after his rule, and that is the (so far) unique śrīvatsa coin of Candragupta III. Weighing 9.1 gm, it is difficult to explain its presence if If Candragupta III died at the end of the cakra phase of his coinage, where the maximum coin weight was around 8.6 gm (Tandon, 2017). It could either be that “Candragupta III did not die, but retreated to a safer place in the empire. Skandagupta seized power in part of the empire, probably the west, and started issuing coins” or may be the “unidentified enemy (the Hūṇas?) issued this coin (from the diamond mint) after they lost control of the circle mint to Skandagupta.” An analysis of the same evidence therefore suggests that the battle against the Hūṇas Skandagupta fought indeed took place somewhere deep in the Gupta territory. To quote Tandon:

“…some have taken the reference to the river Ganges in the Bhītarī pillar inscription to indicate that the battle took place on the banks of that river, possibly even at Bhītarī, which is near Varanasi, because of its possible importance to the Guptas. In a recent paper, I have argued that the Hūṇas issued Archer style gold coins probably in the Varanasi area later in the fifth century or early in the sixth, which makes this location all the more plausible for the earlier conflict with Candragupta III and Skandagupta as well. A battle deep in the Gupta heartland seems to agree more with the phrases that Skandagupta uses, such as “the ruined fortunes of (his) lineage; and … (his) lineage that had been made to totter” than would a battle somewhere on the periphery of the empire. It also makes more sense that, since Candragupta III apparently used only two (the “circle” and the “diamond”), the mints would be located deep within the empire (Tandon, 2017).”

**Overview**

From the details analysed, it is clear that there were multiple factors particular to the Gupta polity that eventually converged to make the reign of Skandagupta as one of the most distressed one amongst the reigns of the most powerful Gupta monarchs. The twin factors that contributed most to it was the Viceroyalty of Vidiśā on one hand and his own uncomfortable legitimacy the other which made Ghaṭotkacgupta an irritant and Purugupta his rival. However, despite these struggles, Skandagupta seemed to have tolerated these internal challenges to his authority, evidenced from the gold coinage issued from these parties.

Though, Tandon argues that if the events did actually happened as assumed, it was Skandagupta who was the challenger to Purugupta and not the other way around but it is possible that Kumāragupta wanted Skanda as his heir due to his acumen and since he died before making his declaration, Puru claimed the throne and Skanda had to accept his reality due to his illegitimate birth. And that is why, what Purugupta did would still be considered a challenge to Skandagupta because by the evidence at hand, it seems Skandagupta has been handling the threats empire faced since the ending years of the reign of this father and was therefore, based on the Gupta history, he was well within his rights to assume that he would eventually sit on the Gupta throne.

**In the end, Purugupta-Candragupta III certainly fell short of what was required from a Gupta Emperor, noticed in the gravity of the phrases Skandagupta mentions like “the ruined fortunes of (his) lineage; and … (his) lineage that had been made to totter” ; an interesting parallel with the Rāmagupta-Candragupta II struggle saga.**

Thus, none of these challengers seemed to have actually dominated over him in the long run, thus Skanda maintained his control strong enough to not allow any tangible losses to his authority. Because even if, Purugupta-Candragupta III supposedly passed away in his struggle against the Hunnic invasions, neither his descendants nor Ghaṭotkacagupta could overpower him. This is noticeable from his handling the Nāga interference if we are to believe the bhujaṅga (serpent) identification with the Nāga(s). It is interesting to wonder about the causes that resulted in this policy of tolerance of these nuisances that seemed to have infested his reign almost perennially. The point to be noted in this regard is that with his shrewdness and his military acumen, if Skandagupta wanted, he could have dominated over Candragupta III but he instead allowed himself to remain in the shadow of his half-brother. As mentioned, above was it due to the circumstances of his birth? Why did he tolerate the existence of Ghaṭotkaca who later went on to issue gold coins in his name? Was it because of the threat to the western frontier due to the Hūṇa(s) that took up most of his attention and resources? And to what extent the fracture in the Gupta-Vākāṭaka relations was responsible for this restrain?All these questions are worth pondering over.

So far, there does not seem to be proof of any violent clashes with these challengers to his kingship; though, it is quite expected considering the dynamics of the Gupta history which unlike the Haryaṅka Magadhan period did not feature any violent internal struggles. Would it be wrong to conclude that the stability that the Gupta(s) were known for was to an extent precisely because of the absence of any major violent internal clashes?

It does seem like the priority for Skandagupta was to keep the boundaries of the empire intact. However, noticing his inscriptions, there seems to be hints of personal issues lingering underneath the security reasons. The reference to the story of Kṛṣṇa and Devakī in the Bhītarī Pillar Inscription suggests a special attachment to his mother, probably more strengthened due to the possibility of may be some kind of unjust treatment meted out to his mother. In the Junāgaḍha Rock Inscription of Skandagupta, his insistence in pointing out the manujendra-putras as disappointed princes is noticeable. The epithet ‘amalātmā’ (undefiled soul) applied to him in the Bhītarī Insciption (Raychaudhuri, 1923: 292) also suggest that Skandagupta felt strongly about his perception in public as a worthy successor of Gupta legacy, both militarily and morally.
